# Chapter 9: Development of the Nervous System

**Chapter Overview:** The brain is not a static network but a plastic, living organ that continuously changes in response to genetic programs and experience. This chapter traces neurodevelopment from a single fertilized egg to a functional adult brain, emphasizing (1) the remarkable nature of the process, (2) the critical role of experience, and (3) the consequences of developmental errors. It culminates with two major neurodevelopmental disorders: autism and Williams syndrome.

---

## The Case of Genie (Introductory Case Study)

**Key facts:**
- Admitted to hospital at age **13**; height 1.35 m, weight 28.1 kg.
- Isolated from age **20 months** — kept tied to a potty in a small, dark, closed room; transferred to a covered crib and **straitjacket** at night.
- Father beat her for making any sound; father and brother rarely spoke to her but sometimes **barked at her like dogs**.
- Mother was almost totally blind; permitted only minutes per day with Genie; fed only cereal or baby food (no solid food allowed).
- On admission: almost no sounds, totally incapable of speech, could not stand erect, could not chew solid food, could not control bladder or bowels.
- After rescue: could not react to temperature extremes; silent tantrums (flailing, spitting, scratching, urinating on herself); easily terrified (dogs, men in khaki); could speak only short poorly-pronounced phrases.
- Outcome: currently living in **a home for retarded adults** (Rymer, 1993; Curtiss, 1977).
- **Significance:** Demonstrates the critical role of early experience in human neurodevelopment; illustrates sensitive/critical period concepts.

## 9.1 Phases of Neurodevelopment

### Overview

**Neurodevelopment** requires more than cell multiplication. Three processes beyond cell division must occur:
1. **Differentiation** — cells become specific types (muscle, multipolar neuron, glial, etc.)
2. **Migration and aggregation** — cells move to appropriate sites and align with neighbors
3. **Synapse formation** — cells establish functional relations with other cells

**The five phases of neurodevelopment (in chronological order):**
1. Induction of the neural plate
2. Neural proliferation
3. Migration and aggregation
4. Axon growth and synapse formation
5. Neuron death and synapse rearrangement

---

### Phase 1: Induction of the Neural Plate

**Timeline:** ~3 weeks after conception.

- **Neural plate** — a small patch of **ectodermal** tissue on the **dorsal surface** of the developing embryo; the first major stage of neurodevelopment in all vertebrates.
- Three embryonic cell layers: **ectoderm** (outermost), **mesoderm** (middle), **endoderm** (innermost).
- The neural plate is induced by chemical signals from an area of the underlying **mesoderm** called the **organizer** (Placzek & Briscoe, 2005).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT (Organizer concept):** Dorsal mesoderm tissue from a donor embryo implanted beneath the ventral ectoderm of a host embryo induces an **extra neural plate** on the ventral surface of the host. Demonstrates the organizer function (Muñoz-Sanjuán & Brivanlou, 2002).

**Cell potency — exam-critical distinctions:**

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Totipotent** | Earliest embryonic cells; can become ANY cell type in the body |
| **Multipotent** | Neural plate cells; can become most mature nervous system cell types but NOT other body cells |
| **Embryonic stem cells** | Neural plate cells meeting two criteria: (1) unlimited self-renewal capacity in appropriate culture and (2) ability to differentiate into different mature cell types |
| **Glial stem cells** | Neural tube cells specified as future glial cells; still multipotent and self-renewing |
| **Neural stem cells** | Neural tube cells specified as future neurons; still multipotent and self-renewing |

**Why stem cells self-renew:** Each stem cell division produces two DIFFERENT daughter cells — one that eventually becomes a body cell and one that becomes another stem cell (Spradling & Zheng, 2007). Errors eventually accumulate and disrupt this process.

**Structural sequence from neural plate:**
1. Neural plate folds → **neural groove**
2. Lips of neural groove fuse → **neural tube** (~day 24)
3. Inside of tube → becomes **cerebral ventricles** and **spinal canal**
4. By **40 days after conception**: three anterior swellings → **forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain**

- **Neural crest** — cells that break off from the neural tube during formation; located just dorsal to the tube; give rise to neurons and glial cells of the **peripheral nervous system** (Jessen & Mirsky, 2005).

---

### Phase 2: Neural Proliferation

- Once neural tube is formed, cells **proliferate** (increase greatly in number).
- Proliferation is NOT simultaneous or equal throughout the tube.
- Most cell division occurs in the **ventricular zone** — region adjacent to the ventricle (fluid-filled center of the tube).
- Each species has a characteristic proliferation pattern responsible for brain shape.
- Controlled by two **organizer areas** in the neural tube:
  - **Floor plate** — midline of the anterior (ventral) surface
  - **Roof plate** — midline of the dorsal surface
  (Chizhikou & Millen, 2004)

---

### Phase 3: Migration and Aggregation

#### Migration

- After cell division in the ventricular zone, cells migrate to target locations.
- Migrating cells are **immature** — lacking axons and dendrites.
- Two governing factors: **time** and **location** — subtypes arise on a precise schedule and migrate to prescribed destinations (Okano & Temple, 2009).

**Two types of migration (by direction):**

| Type | Direction |
|---|---|
| **Radial migration** | Straight outward from ventricular zone toward the outer wall of the tube |
| **Tangential migration** | At right angles to radial migration; parallel to tube walls |

Most cells use BOTH types to reach their target (Hatten, 2002).

**Two methods of migration (by mechanism):**

| Method | Mechanism | Directions possible |
|---|---|---|
| **Somal translocation** | Extension grows from cell in migration direction, explores for cues; cell body moves into extension; trailing processes retract (Marin, Valdeolmillos, & Moya, 2006) | Radial OR tangential |
| **Glia-mediated migration** | Cells move along the **radial glial cell** network (Campbell & Gotz, 2002); used once tube walls thicken | Radial ONLY |

**Cortical migration pattern:**
- Orderly waves from **deeper to more superficial layers** = **inside-out pattern** (Cooper, 2008).
- Each wave migrates THROUGH already-formed lower cortical layers before reaching destination.
- Many cortical cells also engage in long tangential migrations.
- Migration of **interneurons** is particularly complex (Wonders & Anderson, 2006).
- Guidance molecules (attractant and repulsive) discovered (Marin & Rubenstein, 2003); some released by glial cells (Auld, 2001).

#### Aggregation

- **Aggregation** — migrated neurons align with other neurons at the same location to form specific nervous system structures.
- Both migration and aggregation are mediated by **cell-adhesion molecules (CAMs)** — on neuron surfaces; recognize and adhere to molecules on other cells (Dalva, McClelland, & Kayser, 2007; Fuerst & Burgess, 2009).
- Elimination of just ONE type of CAM in a knockout mouse has **devastating effects** on brain development (DiCicco-Bloom, 2006; Lien et al., 2006) — CAM abnormalities may cause some neurological disorders.
- **Gap junctions** (connexin tubes between adjacent neurons allowing cytoplasm exchange) are especially prevalent during development and facilitate migration and aggregation (Elias & Kriegstein, 2008; Montoro & Yuste, 2004).

---

### Phase 4: Axon Growth and Synapse Formation

#### Axon Growth

- After neurons migrate and aggregate, **axons and dendrites** begin to grow.
- **Growth cone** — amoebalike structure at the growing tip of an axon or dendrite.
- **Filopodia** — fingerlike cytoplasmic extensions of growth cones; extend and retract as if searching for the correct route (Wen & Zheng, 2006).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Roger Sperry (early 1940s):** Cut optic nerves of frogs + rotated eyeballs 180°, then waited for regeneration of retinal ganglion axons. (Frogs, unlike mammals, regenerate retinal ganglion cells.) Behavioral result: frogs struck FORWARD when a lure was dangled BEHIND them (visual world rotated 180°). Same result in frogs with rotated eye but intact nerve. Neuroanatomical studies confirmed every retinal ganglion cell regrew to the SAME optic tectum point it had originally connected to. Led to the **chemoaffinity hypothesis**.

- **Chemoaffinity hypothesis** (Sperry, 1963): Each postsynaptic surface releases a **specific chemical label**; each growing axon is attracted by this label to its postsynaptic target during both development and regeneration.

**Limitations of chemoaffinity hypothesis:**
- Some axons follow circuitous routes to their targets, not direct paths.
- In regeneration studies (half retina or half tectum destroyed), axons fill available space in orderly fashion rather than returning to original sites — NOT what point-to-point chemoaffinity would predict.

**Revised/current model:**
- Growth cones are guided by a **series of chemical signals along the route** (not just a single attractant at the target) (Imai et al., 2009; Miyamichi & Luo, 2009).
- Additional guidance from **adjacent growing axons**.
- **Pioneer growth cones** — the first growth cones on a new route; follow guidance molecules and blaze a trail.
- **Fasciculation** — subsequent axons follow paths established by pioneer growth cones.

**Topographic mapping and ephrins:**
- **Topographic gradient hypothesis** — axons from one topographic surface are guided to targets by **two intersecting signal gradients** on the terminal surface (e.g., anterior-posterior AND medial-lateral) (Flanagan, 2006; McLaughlin & O'Leary, 2005).
- **Ephrins** — guidance molecules with strongest evidence for signal gradient involvement:
  - **Ephrin-A** = medial-lateral gradient on developing retina
  - **Ephrin-B** = dorsal-ventral gradient on developing retina
- Each retinal point has a unique combination of ephrin-A and ephrin-B concentrations, allowing precise topographic guidance.
- Evolutionary rationale: topographic maps minimize the volume of neural connections; richly connected neurons should be as close as possible (Chklovskii & Koulakov, 2004).

#### Synapse Formation (Synaptogenesis)

- **Synaptogenesis** — the formation of new synapses.
- Growing an axon = single-neuron activity; creating a synapse = requires **coordinated activity in at least two neurons**.
- Key discovery: synaptogenesis requires **glial cells**, specifically **astrocytes** (Allen & Barres, 2005; Shors, 2009).

> **KEY FINDING:** Retinal ganglion cells in culture form **7 TIMES MORE SYNAPSES** when astrocytes are present. Synapses are quickly lost when astrocytes are removed.

- Early view: astrocytes supply **cholesterol** needed during synapse formation (Mauch et al., 2001; Pfreiger, 2002).
- Current view: astrocytes play extensive role by **processing, transferring, and storing information** supplied by neurons (Perea, Navarrete, & Araque, 2009).
- In vitro: any neuron will form synapses with any other neuron ("promiscuity"), but synapses that do not function appropriately are subsequently eliminated (Waites, Craig, & Garner, 2005).

---

### Phase 5: Neuron Death and Synapse Rearrangement

#### Neuron Death

- **Neuron death is a normal, important part of neurodevelopment.**
- ~**50% MORE** neurons produced than required; large-scale death occurs in waves.

| Type | Process | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Necrosis** | Passive cell death; cells break apart, spill contents | Potentially harmful **inflammation** |
| **Apoptosis** | Active, genetically programmed; DNA cleaved and packaged in membranes before breakup; attracts scavenger **microglia** | Safe, orderly, no inflammation (Freeman, 2006) |

**Dark side of apoptosis:**
- Blocked apoptosis programs → **cancer**
- Inappropriately activated programs → **neurodegenerative disease** (Haase et al., 2008)

**Two triggers of apoptosis in developing neurons (Miguel-Aliaga & Thor, 2009):**
1. **Genetic programming** — groups of neurons die together after fulfilling functions, without obvious external stimulus (Chao, Ma, & Shen, 2009).
2. **Failure to receive life-preserving chemicals from target cells** (Deppmann et al., 2008; Innocenti & Price, 2005).

**Evidence for target-derived survival signals:**
- Grafting an **extra target structure** (e.g., extra limb) to embryo before synaptogenesis → **reduces** neuron death in neurons projecting to that area.
- Destroying some neurons projecting into an area → **increases** survival of remaining neurons (competition for trophic factors).

- **Neurotrophins** — life-preserving chemicals supplied by target cells; promote growth and survival, function as axon guidance molecules, stimulate synaptogenesis.
- **Nerve growth factor (NGF)** — the FIRST neurotrophin to be isolated (Levi-Montalcini, 1952, 1975).

#### Synapse Rearrangement

- Neurons with **incorrect connections** are especially likely to die.
- Vacated postsynaptic space is filled by **sprouting axon terminals** of surviving neurons.
- Net effect: each neuron's output becomes focused on a **smaller number** of postsynaptic cells → increased **selectivity** of transmission.

## 9.2 Postnatal Cerebral Development in Human Infants

### Postnatal Growth of the Human Brain

**Unique feature of humans:** Brain develops **more slowly** than any other species; full maturity not reached until **late adolescence** (Blakemore, 2008).

- Brain volume **quadruples** between birth and adulthood (Johnson, 2001).
- This increase is NOT from new neurons (exception: olfactory bulb and hippocampus continue adding neurons).
- **All neurons of the adult human brain are present by the 7th month of prenatal development.**
- Postnatal growth results from:
  1. **Synaptogenesis**
  2. **Myelination of axons**
  3. **Increased branching of dendrites**

**Synaptogenesis timeline (exam-critical):**

| Region | Peak synaptogenesis | Maximum density | Decline to adult level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary visual cortex | 4th postnatal month | 7th–8th postnatal month (150% of adult) | ~3 years of age |
| Primary auditory cortex | 4th postnatal month | 7th–8th postnatal month | ~3 years of age |
| Prefrontal cortex | Steady rate | ~2nd postnatal year | Adolescence |

**Myelination timeline:**
- Sensory areas: First few months after birth.
- Motor areas: Shortly after sensory.
- Prefrontal cortex: Continues **into adulthood** (Fields, 2008a, 2008b).
- Myelination parallels functional development (Nagy, Westerberg, & Klinsberg, 2004).

**Dendritic branching:**
- Follows inside-out pattern (deeper to superficial), mirroring migration.
- Mature dendrites can reconfigure within **seconds** (Alvarez & Sabatini, 2007).

**Regressive changes:**
- Overproduction of synapses followed by periods of synaptic loss — may underlie greater plasticity of the young brain.

**Gray vs. White matter (structural MRI):**
- **White matter:** Grows slowly and steadily until early adulthood.
- **Gray matter:** Inverted U — exceeds adult levels, then decreases (Amso & Casey, 2006).
- Sensory and motor areas reach mature form **before** cognitive/prefrontal areas (Casey et al., 2005).

---

### Development of the Prefrontal Cortex

- **Last brain region to reach maturity** (Casey, Giedd, & Thomas, 2000).
- Believed largely responsible for the course of human **cognitive development**.

**Four cognitive functions of the adult prefrontal cortex:**
1. **Working memory** — keeping information accessible during a task
2. **Planning and executing sequences of actions**
3. **Inhibiting currently-inappropriate responses**
4. **Following rules for social behavior** (Bunge & Zelazo, 2006; Kagan & Baird, 2004)

**Piaget's A-not-B task and perseverative errors:**
- Toy hidden behind one screen → after several trials, moved to other screen (infant watching).
- **7-month-olds** keep reaching for the old (previously correct) screen = **perseverative error**.
- **Perseveration** — continuing to make a formerly correct response when it is currently incorrect.
- Error occurs between **7 and 12 months**, resolves thereafter (Diamond, 1985).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Diamond (1991) [Comparative study]:**
> 1. Infant (not adult) monkeys make the same perseverative error on Piaget's test.
> 2. Adult monkeys with bilateral **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex** lesions make the same perseverative errors as infant monkeys.
> 3. Control adult monkeys with **hippocampal** or **posterior parietal cortex** lesions do NOT make this error.
> **Conclusion:** Prefrontal cortex development (specifically working memory and response inhibition functions) directly underlies the developmental timeline of perseverative errors.

## 9.3 Effects of Experience on Early Development, Maintenance, and Reorganization of Neural Circuits

### Core Concepts

- **Permissive experiences** — necessary for genetic programs to be expressed; do not contribute direction.
- **Instructive experiences** — contribute to the direction of development (Chalupa & Huberman, 2004).

**Critical vs. Sensitive Periods:**

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Critical period** | Interval where experience is ABSOLUTELY ESSENTIAL for development; NO effect outside interval |
| **Sensitive period** | Interval where experience has its GREATEST effect; can still have weak effects outside interval |

- The **vast majority** of experiential effects involve **sensitive periods**, not critical periods (Morishita & Hensch, 2008; Thomas & Johnson, 2008).
- General principle: **"Use it or lose it"** — neural circuits not used after formation do not survive and function normally (Hockfield & Kalb, 1993; Kalil, 1989).

---

### Early Studies: Deprivation and Enrichment

**Sensory deprivation (dark-rearing):**
- Rats reared in dark from birth →
  - Fewer synapses in primary visual cortex
  - Fewer dendritic spines in primary visual cortex
  - Deficits in depth and pattern vision as adults

**Enrichment studies:**
- Rats in enriched (complex) group cages vs. solitary barren cages →
  - **Thicker cortex**
  - More **dendritic spines**
  - More **synapses per neuron** (Sale, Berardi, & Maffei, 2008)

---

### Competitive Nature of Experience: Ocular Dominance Columns

- **Ocular dominance columns** — alternating columns in **layer IV** of primary visual cortex, each receiving input predominantly from one eye.
- Almost fully developed at birth in many species (Feller & Scanziani, 2005).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Hubel, Wiesel, & LeVay (1977); Hata & Stryker (1994):**
> Early monocular deprivation (one eye covered) for a few days:
> - **Deprived eye:** columns narrow; ability to activate visual cortex DECREASES.
> - **Non-deprived eye:** columns widen; ability to activate visual cortex INCREASES.
> If BOTH eyes are covered: effect does NOT occur.
> **Key mechanism:** Changes in **synaptic input into layer IV** of primary visual cortex.
> This is a **sensitive period** effect (not critical period): monocular deprivation in adulthood has modest effects (Hofer et al., 2006).

> **KEY FINDING — Antonini & Stryker (1993):** A few days of monocular deprivation cause **massive decrease in axonal branching** of lateral geniculate nucleus neurons projecting to layer IV from the deprived eye. Demonstrates structural changes occur within DAYS.

---

### Effects of Experience on Topographic Sensory Cortex Maps

> **KEY EXPERIMENT 1 — Roe et al. (1990) [Ferret rerouting]:** Surgically rerouted retinal ganglion axons of ferrets to synapse in the **medial geniculate nucleus** (auditory thalamus) instead of lateral geniculate nucleus. Result: **auditory cortex** developed a **retinotopic organization** due to visual input. Demonstrates that experience drives cortical organization across modalities.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT 2 — Knudsen & Brainard (1991) [Barn owl prisms]:** Raised owls with prisms shifting visual world **23° to the right**. The **auditory spatial map in the tectum** was also shifted 23° to the right — objects were heard to be where they were seen. Cross-modal experience reorganizes topographic maps.

> **KEY FINDING 3 — Early music training (Münte, Altenmüller, & Jänke, 2002):** Early musical training **expands the area of auditory cortex** responding to complex musical tones.

---

### Experience Fine-Tunes Neurodevelopment

- Long before the nervous system is fully developed, neurons begin firing **spontaneously** (Blankenship & Feller, 2010) and interact with the environment.
- Resulting neural activity patterns **fine-tune subsequent developmental stages** (Huberman, Feller, & Chapman, 2008).
- Experience can influence **gene expression** (Fagiolini, Jensen, & Champagne, 2009; Majdan & Shatz, 2006).
- This fine-tuning constitutes the **critical final phase** of normal development.

## 9.4 Neuroplasticity in Adults

- Historical view: neuroplasticity restricted to the developmental period; adult brains "set in their ways."
- Current consensus: **mature brains are continually changing and adapting** (Abraham, 2008; Gogolla, Galimberti, & Caroni, 2007; McCoy, Huang, & Philpot, 2009).

---

### Neurogenesis in Adult Mammals

- **Neurogenesis** — the growth of new neurons.
- Former dogma (pre-1980s): Neurons die throughout life and are NEVER replaced in adults.

**Historical sequence of discoveries overthrowing this dogma:**

| Study | Finding |
|---|---|
| Goldman & Nottebohm (1983) | Brain structures for **singing** in **songbirds** grow before mating season due to new neuron production |
| Cameron et al. (1993) | Adult neurogenesis confirmed in **rat hippocampus** using immunohistochemical markers |
| Subsequent rodent studies | New neurons also added to adult rat **olfactory bulbs** |
| Gould et al. (1999) | Claimed neurogenesis in adult rat **cortex** — later shown to be **erroneous** (Au & Fishell, 2006; Bhardwaj et al., 2006; Rakic, 2006) |
| Kornack & Rakic (1999) | Adult neurogenesis confirmed in **primate hippocampus** |
| Eriksson et al. (1998) | Adult neurogenesis confirmed in **human hippocampus** |

**In adult mammals, confirmed substantial neurogenesis in only two areas:**
1. **Olfactory bulbs**
2. **Hippocampuses**

**Sources and cell types:**

| Region | Source of new neurons | Final cell type |
|---|---|---|
| **Olfactory bulbs** | Adult neural stem cells in **subventricular zone of lateral ventricles** → migrate to olfactory bulbs (Lledo & Saghatelyan, 2006; Sawamoto et al., 2006; Curtis et al., 2007) | **Interneurons** |
| **Hippocampus** | Created near final location in **dentate gyrus** (Kempermann et al., 2004) | **Granule cells** in dentate gyrus |

**Scale:** ~**2,000 new neurons per hour** added to adult hippocampus (West, Slomianka, & Gunderson, 1991).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Kempermann & Gage (1999):** Adult rats in **enriched environments** → **60% more new hippocampal neurons** than controls. However, the effect depends largely on **increased exercise** (not enrichment per se) (Farmer et al., 2004; Van Praag et al., 1999). Implication: exercise as treatment for memory disorders. Aerobic exercise improves cognitive function in older adults (Hertzog et al., 2008).

New adult-generated neurons: survive, integrate into circuits, and conduct neural signals (Doetsch & Hen, 2005; Kempermann, Wiskott, & Gage, 2004; Toni et al., 2008).

---

### Effects of Experience on Reorganization of Adult Cortex

**Documented adult cortical reorganization:**
- **Mühlnickel et al. (1998):** **Tinnitus** produces major reorganization of primary **auditory cortex**.
- **Elbert et al. (1995):** String musicians (left-hand fingering — e.g., violin) have **enlarged hand-representation** in **right somatosensory cortex**.
- **Rossini et al. (1994):** Anesthetizing the 2nd and 4th fingers → reduced their representation in contralateral somatosensory cortex.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Hofer et al. (2005):** Elimination of visual input to one eye in ADULT mice:
> - Reduced size of that eye's ocular dominance columns.
> - If the mice had PREVIOUSLY experienced deprivation in the same eye, the reduction occurred **more quickly and was more enduring**.
> **Interpretation:** Once the brain has adapted to abnormal conditions, it acquires **enhanced ability** to adapt to the same conditions again — a form of plasticity memory.

Possible mechanisms: experience modifies cortical **synapses, boutons, and dendritic spines** (Holtmaat & Svoboda, 2009).

## 9.5 Disorders of Neurodevelopment: Autism and Williams Syndrome

### Autism

**Definition:** A complex neurodevelopmental disorder; apparent before age **3**; does not increase in severity after that age.

**Three CORE symptoms** (Baron-Cohen & Belmonte, 2005):
1. Reduced ability to interpret **emotions and intentions of others**
2. Reduced capacity for **social interaction and communication**
3. **Preoccupation with a single subject or activity**

Note: All three are NOT present in every case (Happé, Ronald, & Plomin, 2008).

**Epidemiological facts (high MCQ frequency):**
- **80%** of those with autism are **male**
- **50%** suffer **mental retardation** (Dawson et al., 2007)
- **35%** have **seizures**
- Older mothers more likely to have autistic children; if mother is **under 30**, risk increases if **father is over 40** (Shelton, Tancredi, & Hertz-Picciotto, 2010)
- Prevalence: previously <1 in 1,000; now CDC estimates **1 in 166 births**
- Autism spectrum disorders = **most prevalent childhood neurological disorders**

**The Case of Alex:**
- Age 7; **echolalia** (repeats almost everything heard); obsessed with trucks, spaghetti, chocolate ice cream; can identify tiny trucks in magazines; can use the Internet but has severe mental retardation.
- Demonstrates autism as heterogeneous: impaired in some areas, relatively preserved in others.

**Early warning signs of autism spectrum disorders:**
- Language delays: no babbling by **12 months**, no words by **16 months**, no self-initiated meaningful phrases by **24 months**
- Social delays: no smiles by **9 months**, no communicative gestures by **12 months**
- Ear anomalies: square-shaped ears, positioned too low, rotated backward with tops flopped over

#### Autism as Heterogeneous Disorder

- Individuals with mental retardation often perform well on: **rote memory, jigsaw puzzles, music, and art**.
- Good spellers; large vocabularies; can read aloud what they do not understand.
- CANNOT: use intonation to convey emotion, coordinate facial expression with speech, speak metaphorically.

#### Autism Spectrum Disorders

- **Autism spectrum disorders** — group of related disorders; used because of great variability among cases.
- **Asperger's syndrome** — mild autism spectrum disorder; **cognitive and linguistic functions well preserved**.

#### Autistic Savants

- **Savants** — intellectually handicapped individuals with amazing specific cognitive or artistic abilities.
- **~10%** of individuals with autism display savant abilities.
- **~50%** of all savants are diagnosed with autism.
- Savant abilities: feats of memory, naming day of the week for any date, identifying prime numbers, drawing, playing instruments.

**Cases of Amazing Savant Abilities:**
- One savant: told time to the **exact second** without a watch — even while asleep.
- **Tom:** Blind, could not tie his shoes, no musical training; could play the most difficult piano piece after hearing it **once**, even with back to the piano.
- Autistic twins: could not do simple addition/subtraction; but could name the **day of the week for any date in the last or next 40,000 years**.

**Explanation:** Abilities do not develop through practice; seem to emerge spontaneously. Hypothesized that brain damage leads to **compensatory overdevelopment** in other brain areas (Treffert & Christensen, 2005; Treffert & Wallace, 2002).

#### Genetic Basis of Autism

- **Siblings** of autistic individuals: ~**5%** chance of diagnosis (above general population; below 50% for single dominant gene or 25% for single recessive gene).
- If one **monozygotic (MZ) twin** is diagnosed, the concordance rate is substantially elevated (typically cited as ~60–90%).
- Genetic factors clearly influence development (Rodier, 2000); likely polygenic/multifactorial.

---

### Williams Syndrome

- **Williams syndrome** — a genetic neurodevelopmental disorder caused by a **microdeletion on chromosome 7** (~25–30 genes deleted).
- Considered in contrast to autism because the two disorders are **similar in some respects and opposite in others**.

**Cognitive profile:**

| Domain | Williams Syndrome |
|---|---|
| Language/verbal ability | **Relatively preserved and fluent** |
| Social interaction | **Highly social**, even with strangers; excessive sociability |
| Facial recognition | Relatively strong |
| Music | Strong interest and ability |
| Visuospatial construction | **Severely impaired** (cannot draw or copy patterns) |
| Mathematical reasoning | Impaired |
| Empathy | **Heightened** |
| IQ | Typically mild to moderate intellectual disability (~50–70) |

**Contrast with Autism (exam-critical table):**

| Feature | Autism | Williams Syndrome |
|---|---|---|
| Social behavior | Severely reduced/withdrawn | Highly social/excessive |
| Language | Often severely impaired | Relatively preserved |
| Visuospatial | Can be relatively preserved | Severely impaired |
| Empathy | Reduced | Heightened |
| Musical ability | Sometimes savant | Generally strong |
| Genetic basis | Multifactorial/polygenic | Chromosome 7 microdeletion |
| Overall IQ range | Highly variable; 50% with retardation | Usually mild-moderate retardation |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Zygote** | Single cell formed from ovum + sperm; starting point of development |
| **Neural plate** | Patch of ectodermal tissue on dorsal surface of embryo; first major stage of neurodevelopment in all vertebrates; appears ~3 weeks after conception |
| **Ectoderm** | Outermost embryonic cell layer; gives rise to nervous system and skin |
| **Mesoderm** | Middle embryonic cell layer; gives rise to muscles, bones, connective tissue |
| **Endoderm** | Innermost embryonic cell layer; gives rise to digestive and respiratory systems |
| **Organizer** | Area of dorsal mesoderm releasing chemical signals that induce neural plate development |
| **Totipotent** | Ability of earliest embryonic cells to develop into ANY type of cell in the body |
| **Multipotent** | Ability of neural plate cells to develop into most mature nervous system cell types but not other body cells |
| **Embryonic stem cells** | Cells of the neural plate; unlimited self-renewal capacity AND ability to differentiate into different mature cell types |
| **Glial stem cells** | Neural tube cells specified as future glial cells; still multipotent and self-renewing |
| **Neural stem cells** | Neural tube cells specified as future neurons; still multipotent and self-renewing |
| **Neural groove** | Intermediate structure when neural plate folds; precursor to neural tube |
| **Neural tube** | Structure formed when lips of neural groove fuse; gives rise to brain and spinal cord |
| **Cerebral ventricles** | Fluid-filled spaces inside the brain; derived from the inside of the neural tube |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **The five phases of neurodevelopment in order:** (1) Induction of neural plate, (2) neural proliferation, (3) migration and aggregation, (4) axon growth and synapse formation, (5) neuron death and synapse rearrangement.

2. **~50% MORE neurons produced than required** during development; excess removed by apoptosis. Apoptosis = active/programmed/safe/orderly (no inflammation). Necrosis = passive/causes inflammation. Blocked apoptosis → cancer; inappropriate apoptosis → neurodegeneration.

3. **Sperry's chemoaffinity hypothesis (1963):** Each postsynaptic surface releases a specific chemical label; growing axons are attracted to their target by this label. Tested by rotating frog eyes 180° and cutting/regenerating optic nerves. Later revised to serial guidance molecule model + fasciculation by pioneer axons.

4. **Synaptogenesis requires astrocytes:** Retinal ganglion cells form **7 times more synapses** with astrocytes present; synapses are lost when astrocytes removed.

5. **NGF** was the FIRST neurotrophin isolated (Levi-Montalcini, 1952). Neurotrophins promote survival, guide axon growth, and stimulate synaptogenesis.

6. **All adult human brain neurons are present by the 7th prenatal month.** Postnatal brain growth = synaptogenesis + myelination + dendritic branching. Exceptions: olfactory bulb and hippocampus add new neurons in adulthood.

7. **Adult neurogenesis** confirmed in two regions only: **olfactory bulbs** (new interneurons; source = subventricular zone) and **hippocampuses** (new granule cells; source = dentate gyrus). Enriched environments → 60% more hippocampal neurons, but effect depends on **exercise**, not enrichment. Estimated rate: ~2,000 new neurons per hour in hippocampus.

8. **Piaget's A-not-B / perseverative error:** Occurs in 7–12-month-old infants; attributed to immature **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex** (Diamond, 1991). Adult monkeys with bilateral dorsolateral prefrontal cortex lesions = same error; hippocampal or posterior parietal lesions do NOT produce this error.

9. **Monocular deprivation** during sensitive period → deprived eye columns narrow, non-deprived eye columns widen (layer IV of primary visual cortex). Competitive process: no effect if BOTH eyes are covered. Antonini & Stryker (1993): structural axonal branching changes occur within DAYS.

10. **Autism statistics:** Apparent before age **3**; **80% male**; **50% mental retardation**; **35% seizures**; prevalence = **1 in 166 births** (CDC). Most prevalent childhood neurological disorder. Three core symptoms: reduced emotion/intention interpretation, reduced social interaction/communication, single-topic preoccupation.

11. **Savants:** ~**10%** of autistic individuals; ~**50%** of savants are autistic. Abilities emerge spontaneously, not through practice; hypothesized compensatory overdevelopment.

12. **Critical vs. sensitive periods:** Vast majority of experiential effects use **sensitive** periods (not critical). If only one eye is covered = harm; if both covered = no harm (competitive experience-dependence).

13. **Prefrontal cortex is the last brain region to mature.** Gray matter: inverted-U curve (exceeds adult level, then decreases). Sensory/motor areas mature before cognitive/prefrontal areas. Prefrontal myelination continues into adulthood.

14. **Ephrins:** Ephrin-A = medial-lateral gradient; Ephrin-B = dorsal-ventral gradient on developing retina. Unique combination at each retinal point enables topographic guidance (topographic gradient hypothesis).

15. **Genie case:** Isolated from age 20 months to 13 years; severe, irreversible developmental deficits despite later intervention. Demonstrates sensitive periods in human language and social development. Currently in a home for retarded adults (Rymer, 1993; Curtiss, 1977).

16. **Inside-out pattern of cortical development:** Each successive wave of cortical neurons migrates THROUGH already-formed deeper layers to reach more superficial layers; progresses from deep to superficial.

17. **Williams syndrome vs. autism:** Williams = chromosome 7 microdeletion; strong language and social skills but severe visuospatial deficits. Autism = multifactorial; poor social skills but visuospatial may be preserved. The two represent contrasting neurodevelopmental profiles.

18. **Totipotent vs. multipotent:** Totipotent = can become ANY cell type; multipotent = can become most nervous system cell types but not other body cells. Neural plate cells transition from totipotent to multipotent. Embryonic stem cells meet two criteria: unlimited self-renewal AND multipotency.
