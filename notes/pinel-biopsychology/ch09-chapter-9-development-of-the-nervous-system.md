# Chapter 9: Development of the Nervous System

**Overview:** The brain is a plastic, living organ that changes with genetic programs and experience. Traces neurodevelopment from fertilized egg to adult brain, emphasizing the role of experience and the consequences of errors, culminating in autism and Williams syndrome.

---

## The Case of Genie

- Admitted at age **13** (1.35 m, 28.1 kg). Isolated from age **20 months**: tied to a potty in a dark room, in a covered crib and **straitjacket** at night; father beat her for sounds and sometimes barked at her like a dog; near-blind mother had minutes a day; fed only cereal/baby food.
- On admission: nearly silent, no speech, couldn't stand erect, chew solid food, or control bladder/bowels. After rescue: silent tantrums, easily terrified, only short poorly-pronounced phrases. Outcome: lives in **a home for retarded adults** (Rymer, 1993; Curtiss, 1977).
- **Significance:** the critical role of early experience; illustrates sensitive/critical periods.

## 9.1 Phases of Neurodevelopment

Beyond cell division, three processes occur: **differentiation** (cells become specific types), **migration and aggregation** (cells move and align), **synapse formation**.

**Five phases (in order):** (1) induction of the neural plate; (2) neural proliferation; (3) migration and aggregation; (4) axon growth and synapse formation; (5) neuron death and synapse rearrangement.

### Phase 1: Induction of the Neural Plate
- **~3 weeks** after conception. **Neural plate** = a patch of **ectodermal** tissue on the **dorsal surface**; first major stage in all vertebrates.
- Three embryonic layers: **ectoderm** (outer), **mesoderm** (middle), **endoderm** (inner). Neural plate is induced by signals from a mesoderm area, the **organizer** (Placzek & Briscoe, 2005).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT:** dorsal mesoderm implanted under a host's ventral ectoderm induces an **extra neural plate** — demonstrates the organizer (Muñoz-Sanjuán & Brivanlou, 2002).

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Totipotent** | Earliest cells; become ANY body cell |
| **Multipotent** | Neural plate cells; become most nervous-system cells but not other body cells |
| **Embryonic stem cells** | Neural plate cells with (1) unlimited self-renewal and (2) ability to differentiate |
| **Glial stem cells** | Tube cells specified as future glia; still multipotent/self-renewing |
| **Neural stem cells** | Tube cells specified as future neurons; still multipotent/self-renewing |

- **Self-renewal:** each stem cell division yields two different daughters — one becomes a body cell, one stays a stem cell (Spradling & Zheng, 2007).
- **Sequence:** neural plate folds → **neural groove** → fuses into **neural tube** (~day 24); tube interior → **cerebral ventricles** + spinal canal; by **40 days** three swellings → forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain.
- **Neural crest**: cells breaking off the tube; give rise to **PNS** neurons and glia (Jessen & Mirsky, 2005).

### Phase 2: Neural Proliferation
- Cells **proliferate** unevenly; most division in the **ventricular zone** (adjacent to the ventricle). Controlled by two organizers: **floor plate** (ventral midline) and **roof plate** (dorsal midline) (Chizhikou & Millen, 2004).

### Phase 3: Migration and Aggregation
- Migrating cells are **immature** (no axons/dendrites); governed by **time** and **location** (Okano & Temple, 2009).

| By direction | |
|---|---|
| **Radial migration** | Straight outward from ventricular zone |
| **Tangential migration** | At right angles, parallel to tube walls |

Most cells use both (Hatten, 2002).

| By mechanism | Mechanism | Directions |
|---|---|---|
| **Somal translocation** | Extension grows, cell body moves into it, trailing processes retract | Radial or tangential |
| **Glia-mediated migration** | Cells move along the **radial glial cell** network (Campbell & Gotz, 2002) | Radial only |

- **Cortical pattern:** orderly waves from deeper to superficial layers = **inside-out pattern** (Cooper, 2008); each wave passes through formed layers. Interneuron migration is especially complex (Wonders & Anderson, 2006); guided by attractant/repulsive molecules.
- **Aggregation**: migrated neurons align to form structures; both migration and aggregation use **cell-adhesion molecules (CAMs)** (Dalva, McClelland, & Kayser, 2007). Eliminating one CAM type devastates brain development. **Gap junctions** (connexin tubes) are prevalent in development and aid migration/aggregation (Elias & Kriegstein, 2008).

### Phase 4: Axon Growth and Synapse Formation

**Axon growth:**
- **Growth cone**: amoebalike tip of a growing axon/dendrite. **Filopodia**: fingerlike extensions that explore the route (Wen & Zheng, 2006).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Roger Sperry (early 1940s):** cut frog optic nerves + rotated eyes 180°. After regeneration, frogs struck FORWARD for a lure dangled BEHIND (world rotated 180°); each retinal ganglion cell regrew to its **original** tectal point → the **chemoaffinity hypothesis**.

- **Chemoaffinity hypothesis** (Sperry, 1963): each postsynaptic surface releases a **specific chemical label** attracting the matching growing axon.
- **Limitations:** some axons take circuitous routes; in half-retina/half-tectum lesions, axons fill space orderly rather than returning to original sites.
- **Revised model:** growth cones follow a **series of signals along the route** (Imai et al., 2009), aided by adjacent axons. **Pioneer growth cones** blaze the trail; **fasciculation** = later axons follow it.
- **Topographic gradient hypothesis**: axons guided by **two intersecting gradients** (e.g., anterior-posterior + medial-lateral) (Flanagan, 2006; McLaughlin & O'Leary, 2005). **Ephrins** are the key molecules: **ephrin-A** = medial-lateral gradient; **ephrin-B** = dorsal-ventral gradient on the developing retina; each point has a unique combination. Rationale: topographic maps minimize connection volume (Chklovskii & Koulakov, 2004).

**Synaptogenesis:**
- **Synaptogenesis** = forming new synapses; requires coordinated activity in ≥2 neurons and depends on **astrocytes** (Allen & Barres, 2005).

> **KEY FINDING:** retinal ganglion cells form **7× more synapses** with astrocytes present; synapses are lost when astrocytes are removed.

- Early view: astrocytes supply **cholesterol** (Mauch et al., 2001). Current: astrocytes process/transfer/store information (Perea, Navarrete, & Araque, 2009). In vitro any neuron forms synapses with any other ("promiscuity"); non-functional ones are later eliminated.

### Phase 5: Neuron Death and Synapse Rearrangement
- Normal: ~**50% more** neurons produced than needed; large-scale death in waves.

| Type | Process | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Necrosis** | Passive; cells break/spill contents | Harmful **inflammation** |
| **Apoptosis** | Active, programmed; DNA packaged before breakup; microglia clean up | Safe, no inflammation (Freeman, 2006) |

- **Dark side:** blocked apoptosis → **cancer**; inappropriate apoptosis → **neurodegeneration** (Haase et al., 2008).
- **Two triggers:** (1) **genetic programming** (neurons die after fulfilling functions); (2) **failure to receive life-preserving chemicals** from target cells (Deppmann et al., 2008).
- Evidence: extra target structure → less death; destroying competing neurons → more survival (competition for trophic factors).
- **Neurotrophins**: target-derived survival chemicals (also guide axons, stimulate synaptogenesis). **Nerve growth factor (NGF)**: the **first** neurotrophin isolated (Levi-Montalcini, 1952, 1975).
- **Synapse rearrangement:** wrongly connected neurons tend to die; vacated space is filled by **sprouting terminals** → output focuses on fewer cells → more **selectivity**.

## 9.2 Postnatal Cerebral Development in Human Infants

- Human brains develop **more slowly** than any other species; full maturity in **late adolescence** (Blakemore, 2008).
- Brain volume **quadruples** birth → adult (Johnson, 2001), NOT from new neurons. **All neurons are present by the 7th prenatal month** (exceptions: olfactory bulb, hippocampus). Growth = (1) **synaptogenesis**, (2) **myelination**, (3) **dendritic branching**.

| Region | Peak synaptogenesis | Max density | Decline to adult |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary visual cortex | 4th month | 7th-8th month (150% of adult) | ~3 years |
| Primary auditory cortex | 4th month | 7th-8th month | ~3 years |
| Prefrontal cortex | Steady | ~2nd year | Adolescence |

- **Myelination:** sensory areas first months; motor areas shortly after; prefrontal **into adulthood** (Fields, 2008); parallels function.
- **Dendritic branching:** inside-out; mature dendrites reconfigure within **seconds** (Alvarez & Sabatini, 2007).
- **Gray vs. white matter (MRI):** white matter grows slowly/steadily to early adulthood; gray matter follows an **inverted U** (exceeds then decreases); sensory/motor areas mature before prefrontal (Casey et al., 2005).

### Development of the Prefrontal Cortex
- **Last region to mature** (Casey, Giedd, & Thomas, 2000); drives cognitive development.
- **Four functions:** (1) **working memory**; (2) **planning/executing action sequences**; (3) **inhibiting inappropriate responses**; (4) **following social rules** (Bunge & Zelazo, 2006).
- **Piaget's A-not-B task:** toy moved to a new screen; **7-month-olds** keep reaching for the old one = **perseverative error**. **Perseveration** = continuing a formerly correct response that is now wrong; occurs **7-12 months** (Diamond, 1985).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Diamond (1991):** (1) infant monkeys make the same error; (2) adult monkeys with bilateral **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex** lesions make it; (3) those with **hippocampal** or **posterior parietal** lesions do NOT. Prefrontal development (working memory/response inhibition) underlies the error's timeline.

## 9.3 Effects of Experience on Neural Circuits

- **Permissive experiences**: needed for genetic programs but don't direct development. **Instructive experiences**: contribute direction (Chalupa & Huberman, 2004).

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Critical period** | Experience ABSOLUTELY ESSENTIAL; no effect outside the interval |
| **Sensitive period** | Experience has GREATEST effect; weak effects still possible outside |

- Most effects involve **sensitive** periods (Morishita & Hensch, 2008). Principle: **"Use it or lose it."**

### Deprivation and Enrichment
- **Dark-rearing** rats: fewer visual cortex synapses/dendritic spines; deficits in depth/pattern vision.
- **Enrichment** rats: thicker cortex, more dendritic spines, more synapses/neuron (Sale, Berardi, & Maffei, 2008).

### Ocular Dominance Columns
- **Ocular dominance columns**: alternating columns in **layer IV** of primary visual cortex, each from one eye; nearly developed at birth.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Hubel, Wiesel, & LeVay (1977); Hata & Stryker (1994):** brief early monocular deprivation → **deprived eye** columns narrow (less cortical activation); **non-deprived eye** columns widen. Covering **both** eyes → no effect. Mechanism: changes in **layer IV synaptic input**. A **sensitive period** effect (adult deprivation has modest effects; Hofer et al., 2006).

> **KEY FINDING — Antonini & Stryker (1993):** a few days of monocular deprivation cause a **massive decrease in axonal branching** of LGN neurons projecting from the deprived eye — structural changes within DAYS.

### Experience and Topographic Maps

> **Roe et al. (1990):** rerouting ferret retinal axons to the **medial geniculate nucleus** made **auditory cortex** develop **retinotopic** organization.

> **Knudsen & Brainard (1991):** owls raised with prisms shifting vision **23° right** had their **tectal auditory spatial map** shift 23° right.

> **Münte, Altenmüller, & Jänke (2002):** early musical training **expands auditory cortex** responding to complex tones.

### Experience Fine-Tunes Development
- Neurons fire **spontaneously** before full development (Blankenship & Feller, 2010); resulting activity **fine-tunes** later stages (Huberman, Feller, & Chapman, 2008) and can influence **gene expression** (Majdan & Shatz, 2006) — the critical final phase.

## 9.4 Neuroplasticity in Adults

- Old view: plasticity is developmental only. Current: **mature brains continually change** (Gogolla, Galimberti, & Caroni, 2007).

### Neurogenesis in Adult Mammals
- **Neurogenesis** = growth of new neurons. Old dogma: no adult replacement.

| Study | Finding |
|---|---|
| Goldman & Nottebohm (1983) | **Songbird** singing structures grow new neurons before mating season |
| Cameron et al. (1993) | Adult neurogenesis confirmed in **rat hippocampus** |
| (rodent studies) | New neurons added to rat **olfactory bulbs** |
| Gould et al. (1999) | Claimed adult **cortical** neurogenesis — later shown **erroneous** (Rakic, 2006) |
| Kornack & Rakic (1999) | Confirmed in **primate hippocampus** |
| Eriksson et al. (1998) | Confirmed in **human hippocampus** |

**Substantial adult neurogenesis confirmed in only two areas:**

| Region | Source | Cell type |
|---|---|---|
| **Olfactory bulbs** | Stem cells in **subventricular zone** of lateral ventricles → migrate (Lledo & Saghatelyan, 2006) | **Interneurons** |
| **Hippocampus** | Created in the **dentate gyrus** (Kempermann et al., 2004) | **Granule cells** |

- **Scale:** ~**2,000 new neurons/hour** in adult hippocampus (West, Slomianka, & Gunderson, 1991).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Kempermann & Gage (1999):** enriched-environment adult rats had **60% more** new hippocampal neurons; the effect depends mainly on **increased exercise** (Van Praag et al., 1999; Farmer et al., 2004). Aerobic exercise improves older-adult cognition (Hertzog et al., 2008).

- New neurons survive, integrate, and conduct signals (Toni et al., 2008).

### Experience and Adult Cortical Reorganization
- **Mühlnickel et al. (1998):** **tinnitus** reorganizes primary **auditory cortex**.
- **Elbert et al. (1995):** string musicians have **enlarged left-hand representation** in **right somatosensory cortex**.
- **Rossini et al. (1994):** anesthetizing fingers shrinks their cortical representation.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT — Hofer et al. (2005):** eliminating one eye's input in ADULT mice shrank its ocular dominance columns; prior deprivation of the same eye made the change **faster and more enduring** — a plasticity "memory."

- Mechanisms: experience modifies **synapses, boutons, dendritic spines** (Holtmaat & Svoboda, 2009).

## 9.5 Disorders of Neurodevelopment: Autism and Williams Syndrome

### Autism
- Complex disorder apparent before age **3**; does not worsen after.
- **Three core symptoms** (Baron-Cohen & Belmonte, 2005): (1) reduced interpretation of others' **emotions/intentions**; (2) reduced **social interaction/communication**; (3) **preoccupation with a single subject/activity**. Not all three are in every case (Happé, Ronald, & Plomin, 2008).

**Epidemiology (high-yield):**
- **80% male**; **50%** with **mental retardation** (Dawson et al., 2007); **35%** have **seizures**.
- Older mothers; if mother **under 30**, risk rises when **father over 40** (Shelton, Tancredi, & Hertz-Picciotto, 2010).
- Prevalence: previously <1 in 1,000; CDC estimate **1 in 166 births**. Autism spectrum = **most prevalent childhood neurological disorders**.

**Case of Alex:** age 7; **echolalia**; obsessed with trucks/spaghetti/chocolate ice cream; uses the Internet but has severe mental retardation — illustrates heterogeneity.

- **Early warning signs:** no babbling by **12 months**, no words by **16 months**, no meaningful phrases by **24 months**; no smiles by **9 months**; no gestures by **12 months**; ear anomalies.
- **Heterogeneity:** often good at **rote memory, jigsaw puzzles, music, art**; large vocabularies; can read aloud what they don't understand; but cannot use intonation for emotion, coordinate facial expression with speech, or speak metaphorically.
- **Autism spectrum disorders** = related group (variable). **Asperger's syndrome** = mild; **cognitive/linguistic functions well preserved**.

**Savants:**
- **Savants** = intellectually handicapped individuals with amazing specific abilities. ~**10%** of autistic individuals are savants; ~**50%** of savants are autistic.
- Examples: telling time to the exact second; **Tom** (blind, untrained) playing a difficult piece after hearing it **once**; twins naming the day of the week for any date over **40,000 years**.
- **Explanation:** abilities emerge spontaneously; hypothesized **compensatory overdevelopment** after damage (Treffert & Christensen, 2005).

**Genetics:** siblings ~**5%** risk; MZ twin concordance substantially elevated (~60-90%); polygenic/multifactorial (Rodier, 2000).

### Williams Syndrome
- **Williams syndrome**: genetic disorder from a **microdeletion on chromosome 7** (~25-30 genes); contrasted with autism (similar in some respects, opposite in others).

| Domain | Williams Syndrome |
|---|---|
| Language/verbal | **Relatively preserved, fluent** |
| Social interaction | **Highly social** (even with strangers) |
| Facial recognition | Relatively strong |
| Music | Strong |
| Visuospatial construction | **Severely impaired** |
| Mathematical reasoning | Impaired |
| Empathy | **Heightened** |
| IQ | Mild-moderate intellectual disability (~50-70) |

| Feature | Autism | Williams Syndrome |
|---|---|---|
| Social behavior | Reduced/withdrawn | Highly social |
| Language | Often severely impaired | Relatively preserved |
| Visuospatial | Can be preserved | Severely impaired |
| Empathy | Reduced | Heightened |
| Musical ability | Sometimes savant | Generally strong |
| Genetic basis | Multifactorial/polygenic | Chromosome 7 microdeletion |
| Overall IQ | Highly variable; 50% with retardation | Usually mild-moderate retardation |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Five phases (in order):** induction → proliferation → migration/aggregation → axon growth/synapse formation → neuron death/synapse rearrangement.

2. **~50% more neurons** produced than needed; excess removed by **apoptosis** (active/safe, no inflammation) vs. **necrosis** (passive/inflammation). Blocked apoptosis → cancer; inappropriate → neurodegeneration.

3. **Sperry's chemoaffinity hypothesis (1963)**: specific chemical labels attract axons; tested by 180° frog eye rotation; later revised to serial guidance + fasciculation.

4. **Synaptogenesis requires astrocytes**: **7× more synapses** with astrocytes present.

5. **NGF** = first neurotrophin (Levi-Montalcini, 1952); neurotrophins promote survival, guide axons, aid synaptogenesis.

6. **All adult human neurons present by the 7th prenatal month**; postnatal growth = synaptogenesis + myelination + dendritic branching (exceptions: olfactory bulb, hippocampus).

7. **Adult neurogenesis** in two regions: **olfactory bulbs** (interneurons; subventricular zone) and **hippocampus** (granule cells; dentate gyrus). Enrichment → 60% more, but driven by **exercise**; ~2,000 new neurons/hour.

8. **A-not-B / perseverative error**: 7-12-month-olds; immature **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex** (Diamond, 1991); hippocampal/parietal lesions don't cause it.

9. **Monocular deprivation** (sensitive period): deprived columns narrow, non-deprived widen (layer IV); no effect if both eyes covered; structural change within DAYS (Antonini & Stryker, 1993).

10. **Autism**: before age **3**; **80% male**; **50% MR**; **35% seizures**; **1 in 166** (CDC); most prevalent childhood neurological disorder; three core symptoms.

11. **Savants**: ~10% of autistic individuals; ~50% of savants autistic; abilities emerge spontaneously (compensatory overdevelopment).

12. **Critical vs. sensitive periods**: most effects use **sensitive** periods; one eye covered = harm, both = no harm (competition).

13. **Prefrontal cortex** matures last; gray matter follows an inverted U; sensory/motor before prefrontal; prefrontal myelination into adulthood.

14. **Ephrins**: ephrin-A = medial-lateral; ephrin-B = dorsal-ventral gradient on the retina (topographic gradient hypothesis).

15. **Genie**: isolated 20 months to 13 years; irreversible deficits despite intervention — sensitive periods in language/social development.

16. **Inside-out pattern**: each cortical wave migrates through formed deeper layers to superficial ones.

17. **Williams syndrome vs. autism**: Williams = chromosome 7 microdeletion, strong language/social but severe visuospatial deficits; autism = multifactorial, poor social, visuospatial may be preserved.

18. **Totipotent vs. multipotent**: totipotent = any cell; multipotent = most NS cells only. Embryonic stem cells = unlimited self-renewal + multipotency.
