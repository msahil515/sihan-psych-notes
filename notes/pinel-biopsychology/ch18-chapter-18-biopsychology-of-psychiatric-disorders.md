# Chapter 18: Biopsychology of Psychiatric Disorders

Biological bases of four disorders: schizophrenia, affective disorders (depression/mania), anxiety disorders, and Tourette syndrome. Covers psychotropic drug discovery, neurochemical theories (dopamine, monoamine), brain pathology, and the limits of pharmacological treatment.

---

## 18.1 Schizophrenia

### What Is Schizophrenia?
- A severe disorder, hard to define (Heinrichs, 2005); symptoms diverse, overlapping, changing; some neurological disorders mimic it. Some say "**the schizophrenias**" (likely several diseases). Symptoms = **positive** and **negative**.

**Positive symptoms** (excess/distortion):

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **Delusions** | Of being controlled, persecution, or grandeur |
| **Hallucinations** | Imaginary voices |
| **Inappropriate affect** | Emotionally inappropriate reactions |
| **Incoherent speech/thought** | Illogical thinking, echolalia, peculiar associations |
| **Odd behavior** | Poor hygiene, catatonia |

- **Echolalia**: repetition of what was just heard.

**Negative symptoms** (loss): **affective flattening**, **alogia** (no speech), **avolition** (no motivation), **anhedonia** (no pleasure).

- **DSM criteria**: any two symptoms for **1 month**; or one if a bizarre delusion or voices.

### Causal Factors
- **Genetic**: general prevalence ~**1%**; close biological relative ~**10%** (even if adopted away). Concordance: **identical twins 45%**, **fraternal 10%**. <100% → experience matters. **Adoption studies**: risk from biological (not adoptive) parents.

> **Gottesman & Bertelsen (1989)**: offspring of identical twins **discordant** for schizophrenia had **equally high** risk from both twins — the non-ill twin still transmits susceptibility.

- **Current view**: people inherit a **susceptibility** activated by experience. **Multiple genes** implicated.
- **Early/environmental factors** altering neurodevelopment: birth complications, infections, autoimmune reactions, toxins, trauma, stress.
- **Neurodevelopmental theory**: schizophrenia from disrupted fetal brain development. **Famine studies** (Dutch 1944-45; Chinese 1959-61): prenatal famine increased adult schizophrenia (St. Clair et al., 2005).

### Discovery of Antischizophrenic Drugs
- **Chlorpromazine**: developed as an **antihistamine**; **1950** a French surgeon noted calming effects; alleviates symptoms (calms agitated, activates blunted); doesn't cure; first **receptor blocker** identified.
- **Reserpine**: from the **snakeroot plant**; depletes dopamine; abandoned (dangerous blood pressure drop).
- **Shared**: effect after **2-3 weeks**; **Parkinson-like motor effects** → suggested a dopamine-related mechanism.

### Dopamine Theory
- **Ehringer & Hornykiewicz (1960)**: Parkinson's striatums are **dopamine-depleted**. → **Original theory**: schizophrenia = **too much dopamine**; drugs decrease it.
- Support: reserpine depletes dopamine; amphetamine/cocaine (can trigger schizophrenic episodes) raise dopamine.

> **Carlsson & Lindqvist (1963)**: chlorpromazine left dopamine **unchanged** but raised **metabolites** → it's a **receptor blocker** (feedback → more release/breakdown). **Revised theory**: hyperactivity **at dopamine receptors**.

- **Creese, Burt, & Snyder (1976)**: neuroleptic potency correlates with dopamine receptor affinity, but **haloperidol** seemed anomalous → **five dopamine receptor subtypes** (Hartmann & Civelli, 1997). **Phenothiazines** (chlorpromazine) bind **D1 + D2**; **butyrophenones** (haloperidol, spiroperidol) bind selectively **D2**. **Snyder (1978)**: **D2** binding highly correlates with potency. **Revised theory**: hyperactivity at **D2 receptors**.

### Limitations of the Dopamine Theory
1. **Other receptors**: glutamate, GABA, serotonin implicated; **LSD/PCP** act via serotonergic/glutaminergic systems. **Atypical neuroleptics** aren't primarily D2 blockers — **clozapine** (first atypical): high D1/D4/serotonin affinity, slight D2; effective in treatment-resistant patients, no parkinsonian effects, but can cause **agranulocytosis**.
2. **Time lag**: D2 blocked within **hours** but relief takes **2-3 weeks** → a slow compensatory change is the real mechanism.
3. **Widespread brain damage**: enlarged ventricles/fissures; reduced volume in ~**50 regions** (Kubicki et al., 2007); gray + white matter, esp. **temporal lobes**; ongoing progression. No specific damage to dopaminergic circuits — unexplained by the theory.
4. **Marginal effectiveness**: chlorpromazine helps only after **6+ months**, ~**1 in 7** patients (Adams et al., 2005); better for **positive** than **negative** symptoms; **74% dropout** at 18 months (Lieberman et al., 2005). Typical → motor side effects; atypical → diabetes/weight gain.

## 18.2 Affective Disorders: Depression and Mania

- **Affective (mood) disorder**: disturbances of mood/emotion.
- **Major depressive disorder**: persistent depression > **2 weeks** disrupting functioning.
- **Mania**: overconfidence, impulsivity, distractibility, high energy.

| Disorder | Description |
|---|---|
| **Bipolar** | Depression + mania |
| **Unipolar** | Depression without mania |
| **Reactive depression** | Triggered by a negative experience |
| **Endogenous depression** | No apparent cause |
| **Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)** | Recurs each winter (reduced sunlight) |
| **Postpartum depression** | After childbirth; ≥1 month; ~10% of deliveries |

- **Epidemiology**: lifetime depression ~**10%**; **unipolar twice as common in women** (no sex difference in bipolar); actual lifetime suicide risk in clinical depression ~**5%** (Bostwick & Pankratz, 2000; the older 15% figure was flawed). Linked to heart disease (adults) and bone loss (women).
- **SAD**: higher in northern US (9%) than Florida (1.5%); **light therapy** helps.

### Causal Factors
- **Genetic**: concordance ~**60%** identical, ~**15%** fraternal; bipolar concordance higher than unipolar; **no specific gene** found.
- **Stress**: evidence linking stress to affective disorders is **sparse**; stress can trigger attacks in already-depressed people; extreme stress more often causes **PTSD**.

### Antidepressant Drugs
1. **MAOIs**: **Iproniazid** (first antidepressant; originally for **tuberculosis**; 1957). Inhibit **monoamine oxidase (MAO)** → raise monoamines. **Cheese effect**: blocking **tyramine** breakdown → BP surges/strokes (cheese, wine, pickles).
2. **Tricyclics**: three-ring structure; **imipramine** (first; first tried as antischizophrenic); block **serotonin + norepinephrine** reuptake; safer than MAOIs.
3. **Selective reuptake inhibitors**: **SSRIs** (late 1980s; **fluoxetine/Prozac** first; no more effective than imipramine; a 2003 suicide-risk warning was unconfirmed and a premature warning **increased** suicide rates 2003-2005, Gibbons et al., 2007); **SNRIs** (e.g., Reboxetine).
4. **Mood stabilizers**: treat depression without inducing mania (or vice versa); mechanism unknown; also treat **epilepsy**. **Lithium** (first; **John Cade, 1954**; guinea-pig "calmness" was actually nausea). Best: mania = **lithium/carbamazepine**; bipolar depression = **lamotrigine**.

- **Effectiveness**: ~50% response vs. 25% placebo (Hollon, Thase, & Markowitz, 2002). **Kirsch et al. (2008)**: no significant benefit over placebo in mild/moderate depression; placebos **82% as effective**.

### Brain Pathology
- Bipolar MRI: reduced overall brain size and amygdala/cingulate/prefrontal cortex (inconsistent across studies). **Two most consistently abnormal structures**: **amygdala** and **anterior cingulate cortex** (and their connections); cell loss also seen in genetically predisposed healthy volunteers.

### Theories
- **Monoamine theory**: depression = **underactivity at serotonergic and noradrenergic synapses** (MAOIs/tricyclics/SSRIs/SNRIs are agonists). Support: receptor **up-regulation** in untreated depressed brains (Nemeroff, 1998). Weak overall.
- **Diathesis-stress model**: a genetic **diathesis** + early-life stress → permanent stress sensitization. Depressed people release more **CRH/ACTH/glucocorticoids**; evidence largely indirect.

### Brain Stimulation
> **Lozano et al. (2008)**: chronic **DBS** in the white matter of the **anterior cingulate gyrus** (ventral to the anterior corpus callosum) in 20 treatment-resistant patients → **60% substantial improvement, 35% symptom-free**, maintained ≥1 year.

## 18.3 Anxiety Disorders

- **Anxiety**: chronic fear without a direct threat. **Anxiety disorder**: anxiety disrupting functioning, with somatic stress reactions (**tachycardia, hypertension**, nausea, sleep disturbance, high glucocorticoids).
- **Most prevalent psychiatric disorders**: ~**17%** lifetime prevalence; **~2× more in females** (Somers et al., 2006).

| Class | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Generalized anxiety disorder** | Anxiety with no obvious precipitating stimulus |
| **Phobic anxiety disorder** | Triggered by specific objects/situations |
| **Panic disorder** | Rapid-onset extreme fear with severe somatic symptoms |
| **OCD** | Recurring obsessions and compulsions (e.g., hand-washing reduces anxiety) |
| **PTSD** | Persistent distress after extreme stress |

- **Agoraphobia**: fear of public/open spaces.

### Etiology
- Experience often apparent; significant **genetic** component (heritability 30-40%); higher MZ than DZ concordance; **no specific genes** found.

### Pharmacological Treatment
1. **Benzodiazepines** (chlordiazepoxide/Librium, diazepam/Valium): **most widely prescribed psychoactive drugs** (~10% of adults); agonists at **GABA-A receptors**; cause **ataxia**, sedation; **highly addictive** (short-term only).
2. **Serotonin agonists**: **buspirone** (selective **5-HT1A** agonist); anxiolytic without ataxia/sedation.
3. **Antidepressants**: high **comorbidity** of anxiety + depression; SSRIs help anxiety, anxiolytics help depression.

### Animal Models
1. **Elevated-plus-maze**: time in **enclosed vs. exposed** arms (Pellow et al., 1985).
2. **Defensive burying test**: time burying a shock source (Treit et al., 1993).
3. **Risk-assessment test**: freezing + risk assessment after cat exposure (Blanchard et al., 1990).
- All validated by **benzodiazepine** reduction of anxiety indices. **Limitation**: may measure benzodiazepine-sensitive anxiety only (buspirone has no reliable effect in the elevated-plus-maze).

### Neural Bases
- Anxiolytics act at **GABA-A** or **serotonin** receptors → implicate GABAergic/serotonergic deficits.
- Implicated structures (same as affective disorders): **amygdala** and **anterior cingulate cortex** — but **no gross structural damage** in anxiety disorders; abnormalities show on **functional imaging** (e.g., increased amygdala activity in spider phobia; Schienle et al., 2005).

## 18.4 Tourette Syndrome

- **Tourette syndrome**: a disorder of **tics** (involuntary, repetitive, stereotyped movements/vocalizations). Begins in childhood/adolescence with simple motor tics; becomes more complex, often peaks then subsides.
- **Complex motor tics**: hitting, touching, hopping, lewd gestures. **Verbal tics**: inarticulate sounds, **coprolalia** (obscenities), **echolalia** (others' words), **palilalia** (own words).
- **Epidemiology**: ~**0.7%** prevalence; **3× more in males**; concordance **55% MZ / 8% DZ** (Pauls, 2001). Comorbid with **ADHD** and/or **OCD**.
- **Tic suppression**: possible with effort; the believed **rebound** is a **myth** — no rebound above baseline (Himle & Woods, 2005).

### Neuropathology
- Studied mainly via brain imaging. Focus on the **caudate nucleus** (striatum): patients have **smaller caudate nuclei**; during suppression, fMRI activity in **prefrontal cortex + caudate** (prefrontal initiates suppression). Also **sensorimotor cortex thinning** (face/mouth/larynx; Sowell et al., 2008). **Gilbert et al. (2006)**: reduced D2 receptor binding in unmedicated adults. Hypothesis: abnormality of the **basal ganglia-thalamus-cortex circuit**, especially the **striatum**.

### Treatment
1. **Education** (patient, family, teachers).
2. **Treat anxiety/depression** first.
3. **Treat tics** with **neuroleptics** (D2 blockers): reduce tics ~**70%**, but benefits modest (side effects → refusal). Consistent with a **striatal dopaminergic abnormality**.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Schizophrenia concordance**: identical 45%, fraternal 10%; close biological relatives ~10%.

2. **Gottesman & Bertelsen (1989)**: discordant identical twins transmit equal risk — non-ill twin still carries susceptibility.

3. **Chlorpromazine** = first antischizophrenic AND first **receptor blocker** identified (originally an antihistamine).

4. **Carlsson & Lindqvist (1963)**: chlorpromazine is a dopamine receptor blocker → revised theory = hyperactivity **at receptors**.

5. **Butyrophenones** (haloperidol, spiroperidol) = selective **D2**; phenothiazines (chlorpromazine) = D1+D2; potency correlates with D2 binding (Snyder, 1978).

6. **Clozapine** = first atypical (D1/D4/serotonin, slight D2); causes agranulocytosis.

7. First drugs: **iproniazid** (MAOI, 1957, for TB); **imipramine** (first tricyclic); **fluoxetine/Prozac** (first SSRI); **lithium** (Cade, 1954).

8. **Cheese effect**: MAOIs + tyramine → BP surge → stroke.

9. **Kirsch et al. (2008)**: antidepressants ≈ placebo in mild/moderate depression (placebo 82% as effective).

10. **Monoamine theory**: underactivity at serotonergic/noradrenergic synapses; receptor up-regulation in untreated patients.

11. **Benzodiazepines** act on **GABA-A** (most prescribed; addictive; cause ataxia); **buspirone** on **5-HT1A** (no ataxia/sedation).

12. **Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent** psychiatric disorders (~17%; 2:1 female).

13. **Tourette**: 0.7% prevalence; 3:1 male; concordance 55%/8%; no suppression rebound; treated with D2 blockers; smaller caudate; basal ganglia-thalamus-cortex circuit.

14. **Lozano et al. (2008)**: anterior cingulate DBS → 60% improved, 35% symptom-free in treatment-resistant depression.

15. **Amygdala and anterior cingulate cortex** abnormal in BOTH affective disorders (structural shrinkage) AND anxiety disorders (functional hyperactivity, no gross damage).
