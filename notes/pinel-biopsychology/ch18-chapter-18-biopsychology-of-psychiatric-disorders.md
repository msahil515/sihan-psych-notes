# Chapter 18: Biopsychology of Psychiatric Disorders

This chapter covers the biological underpinnings of four major psychiatric disorders: schizophrenia, affective disorders (depression and mania), anxiety disorders, and Tourette syndrome. It traces the discovery of psychotropic drugs, develops key neurochemical theories (dopamine theory, monoamine theory), surveys brain pathology findings, and evaluates the limits of current pharmacological treatments.

---

## 18.1 Schizophrenia

### What Is Schizophrenia?

- **Schizophrenia** is a severe psychiatric disorder whose major difficulty is accurate definition (Heinrichs, 2005; Krueger & Markon, 2006).
- Symptoms are complex, diverse, overlap with other psychiatric disorders, and frequently change during the disorder's progression.
- Various neurological disorders (e.g., complex partial epilepsy) can mimic schizophrenic symptoms.
- Some experts use the plural **"the schizophrenias"** (Wong & Van Tol, 2003) to acknowledge that the current diagnosis likely encompasses several different brain diseases.
- Symptoms are classified into **positive symptoms** and **negative symptoms**.

---

### Positive Symptoms of Schizophrenia

Positive symptoms represent an **excess or distortion of normal function**:

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **Delusions** | False beliefs: delusions of being controlled (e.g., "Martians are making me steal"), delusions of persecution (e.g., "My mother is poisoning me"), delusions of grandeur (e.g., "Tiger Woods admires my backswing") |
| **Hallucinations** | Imaginary voices making critical comments or directing behavior |
| **Inappropriate affect** | Failure to react emotionally appropriately to positive or negative events |
| **Incoherent speech/thought** | Illogical thinking, echolalia, peculiar associations among ideas, belief in supernatural forces |
| **Odd behavior** | Difficulty with daily tasks, poor hygiene, talking in rhymes, catatonia (remaining motionless in awkward positions for long periods) |

- **Echolalia**: Vocalized repetition of some or all of what has just been heard (exhibited by many patients with schizophrenia).

---

### Negative Symptoms of Schizophrenia

Negative symptoms represent a **reduction or loss of normal function**:

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **Affective flattening** | Reduction or absence of emotional expression |
| **Alogia** | Reduction or absence of speech |
| **Avolition** | Reduction or absence of motivation |
| **Anhedonia** | Inability to experience pleasure |

---

### DSM Diagnostic Criteria (Tamminga & Holcomb, 2005; Walker et al., 2004)

- Recurrence of **any two symptoms** for **1 month** is sufficient for diagnosis.
- **Only one symptom** is necessary if it is:
  - A particularly bizarre delusion, OR
  - A hallucination that includes voices.

---

### Causal Factors in Schizophrenia

#### Genetic Evidence

- General population prevalence of schizophrenia: **~1%**
- Probability in a **close biological relative** (parent, child, sibling) of a patient: **~10%**, even if the relative was adopted shortly after birth by a healthy family (Kendler & Gruenberg, 1984; Rosenthal et al., 1980).
- **Concordance rates**:
  - **Identical (monozygotic) twins: 45%** (Holzman & Matthyse, 1990; Kallman, 1946)
  - **Fraternal (dizygotic) twins: 10%**
- Concordance < 100% in identical twins indicates that **experience also plays a significant role**.
- **Adoption studies**: Risk of schizophrenia is increased by the biological parents having the disorder, but **not** by adoptive parents having it (Gottesman & Shields, 1982).
- Key study (Gottesman & Bertelsen, 1989): In identical twins **discordant** for schizophrenia (one with, one without), the incidence of schizophrenia was equally high in the offspring of **both** twins — meaning the non-ill twin still carried and transmitted the genetic risk.

> **Current view**: Some people **inherit a potential (susceptibility)** for schizophrenia, which may or may not be activated by experience.

#### Multiple Genes

- Multiple different genes have been linked to schizophrenia (Hall et al., 2009; O'Tuathaigh et al., 2007; Walsh et al., 2008); mechanisms are not yet determined.

#### Early Experiential / Environmental Factors

Factors thought to alter normal neurodevelopment in genetically susceptible individuals:
- Birth complications
- Early infections
- Autoimmune reactions
- Toxins
- Traumatic injury
- Stress (Jarskog, Miyamoto, & Lieberman, 2007; Lenzenweger, 2006)

#### Neurodevelopmental Theory — Famine Studies

- **Neurodevelopmental theory of schizophrenia**: Schizophrenia arises from disruption of normal fetal brain development.
- Supporting evidence: Fetuses whose mothers suffered during two 20th-century famines were **more likely to develop schizophrenia as adults**:
  - The **Nazi-induced Dutch famine of 1944–1945**
  - The **Chinese famine of 1959–1961**
  (Kyle & Pichard, 2006; McClellan, Susser, & King, 2006; St. Clair et al., 2005)

---

### Discovery of the First Antischizophrenic Drugs

#### Chlorpromazine

- Developed as an **antihistamine** by a French drug company.
- In **1950**, a French surgeon noticed it had a calming effect on patients pre-surgically and suggested it might calm psychotic patients.
- This suggestion was incorrect, but subsequent research led to the discovery that **chlorpromazine alleviates schizophrenic symptoms**:
  - Agitated patients were calmed.
  - Emotionally blunted patients were activated.
- **Does NOT cure schizophrenia** but often reduces symptoms enough to allow discharge from institutions.
- First receptor blocker to be identified in psychopharmacology.

#### Reserpine

- Active ingredient of the **snakeroot plant**, long used in India for mental illness.
- An American psychiatrist confirmed its antischizophrenic action.
- No longer used: produces a **dangerous decline in blood pressure** at therapeutic doses.

#### Shared Properties of Chlorpromazine and Reserpine

1. Antischizophrenic effect manifests only after **2–3 weeks** of treatment.
2. Associated with **motor effects resembling Parkinson's disease**: tremors at rest, muscular rigidity, decreased voluntary movement.

These similarities suggested they acted through the same mechanism, related to Parkinson's disease.

---

### Dopamine Theory of Schizophrenia

#### Origins (1960)

- **Key finding (Ehringer & Hornykiewicz, 1960)**: The striatums (caudates + putamens) of persons dying of Parkinson's disease were **depleted of dopamine**.
- Because antischizophrenic drugs produced Parkinson-like side effects, researchers hypothesized that these drugs reduced dopaminergic transmission.
- **Original dopamine theory**: Schizophrenia is caused by **too much dopamine**; antischizophrenic drugs work by **decreasing dopamine levels**.

#### Supporting Facts for Original Theory

1. **Reserpine** depletes the brain of dopamine (and other monoamines) by breaking down synaptic vesicles.
2. **Amphetamine and cocaine** — drugs that can trigger schizophrenic episodes in healthy subjects — increase extracellular dopamine and monoamine levels.

#### Carlsson and Lindqvist (1963) — Revision

- Expected chlorpromazine to deplete dopamine like reserpine.
- Found instead: extracellular dopamine levels were **unchanged**; extracellular **dopamine metabolites were increased**.
- Concluded: chlorpromazine acts as a **receptor blocker** (binds to dopamine receptors without activating them, preventing dopamine from activating them).
- Mechanism: Receptor blockade sends a **feedback signal** to presynaptic neurons → increases dopamine release → dopamine is broken down in synapses → metabolite levels rise.
- **Revised theory**: Schizophrenia is caused by **high levels of activity at dopamine receptors**, not high dopamine levels per se.
- Chlorpromazine was the **first receptor blocker to be identified**.

#### Snyder et al. — D2 Receptor Theory (mid-1970s)

- **Creese, Burt, & Snyder (1976)**: Tested the affinity of various neuroleptics for dopamine receptors using radioactively labeled dopamine in calf striatal membrane samples.
- General finding: Clinically effective neuroleptics had **high affinity for dopamine receptors**; ineffective drugs had low affinity.
- Exception: **Haloperidol** — one of the most potent antischizophrenic drugs — had **relatively low affinity** for dopamine receptors in these assays.
- Resolution: **Five subtypes of dopamine receptors** were identified (Hartmann & Civelli, 1997):
  - **Phenothiazines** (e.g., chlorpromazine) bind to both **D1 and D2** receptors.
  - **Butyrophenones** (e.g., haloperidol) bind selectively to **D2** receptors, not D1.
- **Revised theory**: Schizophrenia is caused by **hyperactivity specifically at D2 receptors**.
- **Snyder (1978)**: Confirmed that the degree to which neuroleptics (**= antischizophrenic drugs**) bind to D2 receptors is **highly correlated** with their antischizophrenic potency.
- Example: **Spiroperidol** (a butyrophenone) — greatest affinity for D2, most potent antischizophrenic effect.

> **Key Table: Development of the Dopamine Theory**

| Period | Key Event |
|---|---|
| Early 1950s | Antischizophrenic effects of chlorpromazine and reserpine documented; linked to parkinsonian side effects |
| Late 1950s | Dopamine depletion found in Parkinson's disease brains |
| Early 1960s | Hypothesis: schizophrenia = excessive dopaminergic activity |
| 1960s–early 1970s | Chlorpromazine and neuroleptics found to be receptor blockers at dopamine synapses |
| Mid-1970s | Affinity for dopamine receptors only roughly correlated with antischizophrenic potency |
| Late 1970s | Binding to D2 receptors found to be highly correlated with antischizophrenic potency |
| 1980s–1990s | D2 theory recognized as insufficient to explain all findings |

---

### Neural Basis of Schizophrenia: Limitations of the Dopamine Theory

Four key discoveries challenge the D2-only version:

#### 1. Receptors Other Than D2 Are Involved

- Other neurotransmitters implicated in schizophrenia:
  - **Glutamate** (Javitt & Coyle, 2004; Tuominen et al., 2005)
  - **GABA** (Lewis, Hashimoto, & Volk, 2005)
  - **Serotonin** (Sawa & Snyder, 2002)
- Evidence from hallucinogens:
  - **LSD** and **PCP (phencyclidine)** produce schizophrenia-like symptoms by acting on **serotonergic and glutaminergic** transmission (González-Maeso & Sealfon, 2009).
- **Atypical neuroleptics**: Antischizophrenic drugs that are **not primarily D2 receptor blockers**.
  - **Clozapine** — first atypical neuroleptic: high affinity for D1, D4, and serotonin receptors, but only **slight affinity for D2**.
  - Clozapine advantages: effective in treatment-resistant patients; does not produce parkinsonian side effects.
  - Clozapine disadvantage: produces a **severe blood disorder (agranulocytosis)** in some patients.

#### 2. It Takes Weeks for Neuroleptics to Work

- Neuroleptics block D2 receptors within **hours**, but symptom relief takes **2–3 weeks**.
- This time discrepancy indicates that D2 blockade alone is not the specific therapeutic mechanism.
- True mechanism: D2 blockade triggers some **slow-developing compensatory change** in the brain.

#### 3. Schizophrenia Is Associated with Widespread Brain Damage

- Brain imaging studies: **enlarged ventricles and fissures** (= reduced brain size) were among the first findings.
- Meta-analysis (Kubicki et al., 2007): Reduced volume in **50 different brain regions**.
- Damage found in both **gray and white matter**.
- Most consistently in the **temporal lobes** (Honea et al., 2005).
- Thompson et al. (2001): Structural MRIs of adolescents with schizophrenia documented cortical gray matter loss.
- Postmortem studies: widespread **neuron loss** and abnormalities of neuron structure and circuitry (Walker et al., 2004).
- Developmental progression (meta-analyses of Steen et al., 2006; Vita et al., 2006):
  1. Extensive brain damage exists at **first medical treatment**.
  2. Subsequent scans reveal **ongoing development** of brain damage.
  3. Different areas damaged at **different rates** (Vidal et al., 2006).
- Problem for dopamine theory: **little evidence of specific damage to dopaminergic circuits**; the diffuse pattern of damage has no explanation within the dopamine theory.

#### 4. Neuroleptics Are Only Marginally Effective

- Meta-analysis (Adams et al., 2005) re-evaluating chlorpromazine vs. placebo:
  - Clear beneficial effects only after **6+ months**.
  - Only about **1 in 7 patients** was substantially helped.
- Neuroleptics are much more effective against **positive symptoms** than **negative symptoms** (Murphy et al., 2006).
- Side effects of **typical neuroleptics**: Motor disturbances (Parkinsonian symptoms).
- Side effects of **atypical neuroleptics**: Diabetes, weight gain, fat regulation problems (Melkersson & Dahl, 2004).
- In an 18-month comparison study (Lieberman et al., 2005): Overall **dropout rate was 74%** regardless of typical vs. atypical.
- Meta-analysis (Leucht et al., 2004): Patients equally likely to refuse typical and atypical neuroleptics.

## 18.2 Affective Disorders: Depression and Mania

### Major Categories of Affective Disorders

- **Affective disorder**: Any psychiatric disorder characterized by disturbances of mood or emotion. Also called **mood disorders**.
- **Clinical depression / major depressive disorder**: Extreme, persistent depression lasting more than **2 weeks**, disrupting daily functioning (sleep, eating, hygiene, social contact, work).
- **Mania**: Characterized by overconfidence, impulsivity, distractibility, and high energy. The opposite of depression.
  - Mild mania: talkative, energetic, impulsive, positive, confident; can be productive.
  - Full-blown mania: grandiosity + high energy + distractibility + impulsivity → unfinished projects, unpaid bills, broken relationships.

| Disorder | Description |
|---|---|
| **Bipolar affective disorder** | Depressive patients who also experience periods of mania |
| **Unipolar affective disorder** | Depressive patients who do not experience mania |
| **Reactive depression** | Depression triggered by a negative experience (death, job loss) |
| **Endogenous depression** | Depression with no apparent cause |
| **Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)** | Attacks of depression and lethargy recurring every winter, triggered by reduced sunlight |
| **Postpartum depression** | Intense, sustained depression following childbirth; diagnosed if lasting ≥ 1 month; usually resolves within 3 months; ~10% of deliveries |

#### Epidemiology of Affective Disorders

- Lifetime probability of clinical depression: **~10%** of the population.
- **Unipolar** disorder diagnosed **twice as often in women** as men (Altemus, 2006; Bale, 2006; Hyde, Mezulis, & Abramson, 2008); reason unclear.
- **No sex difference** in bipolar affective disorder.
- Suicide rate: Guze & Robins (1970) meta-analysis reported 15% lifetime suicide risk (widely cited but flawed); Bostwick & Pankratz (2000) found the actual lifetime risk of suicide in clinical depression is approximately **5%**.
- In adults: affective disorders associated with **heart disease** (Frasure-Smith & Lespérance, 2005; Miller & Blackwell, 2006).
- In adult women: associated with **bone loss** (Eskandari et al., 2007).

#### SAD: Evidence for Sunlight Trigger

1. Incidence higher in **northern US (9%)** than in **Florida (1.5%)**, where winter days are longer/brighter (Modell et al., 2005).
2. **Light therapy** is often effective in reducing symptoms (Lam et al., 2006).

---

### Causal Factors in Affective Disorders

#### Genetic Factors

- Twin study concordance rates:
  - **Identical twins: ~60%**
  - **Fraternal twins: ~15%**
  - Whether reared together or apart.
- Tendency for affected twins to share the same type (unipolar or bipolar).
- **Concordance rates for bipolar disorders are higher** than for unipolar.
- **No specific gene** has been linked to affective disorders (Berton & Nestler, 2006).

#### Role of Stress (Diathesis-Stress Model)

- Depression is often described as a **stress-related disorder** (Bale, 2005).
- Good evidence linking stress to affective disorders is **sparse**.
- Some studies show stressful events can **trigger attacks** in already-depressed individuals (Brown, 1993).
- Childhood sexual abuse has only a **slight effect** on adult depression development (Nelson et al., 2002).
- Extreme stress more commonly produces **posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD)**, not depression.

---

### Discovery of Antidepressant Drugs

Four major classes:

#### 1. Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs)

- **Iproniazid**: First antidepressant drug; originally developed for **tuberculosis** (failed).
- Antidepressant potential noticed because TB patients seemed less concerned about their illness.
- First marketed as an antidepressant in **1957**.
- **Mechanism**: Inhibits **monoamine oxidase (MAO)** — the enzyme that breaks down monoamine neurotransmitters (e.g., norepinephrine, serotonin) in the cytoplasm → increases monoamine levels.
- Major side effect: **Cheese effect** — MAO inhibitors prevent tyramine breakdown in the liver; consuming tyramine-rich foods (cheese, wine, pickles) can cause dangerous blood pressure surges and **strokes** (Youdim, Edmonson, & Tipton, 2006).

#### 2. Tricyclic Antidepressants

- Named for their **three-ring chemical structure**.
- **Imipramine**: First tricyclic antidepressant; initially tested as an antischizophrenic (had no effect on schizophrenia but helped some depressed patients).
- **Mechanism**: Block the reuptake of both **serotonin and norepinephrine** → increases their brain levels.
- Safer alternative to MAOIs.

#### 3. Selective Monoamine-Reuptake Inhibitors

- **SSRIs (Selective Serotonin-Reuptake Inhibitors)**: Introduced in the **late 1980s**.
  - Serotonin agonists that block **reuptake of serotonin** from synapses.
  - **Fluoxetine (Prozac)**: First SSRI developed.
  - Others: Paxil, Zoloft, Luvox, Remeron.
  - Prozac is structurally a slight variation of imipramine; **no more effective than imipramine** in treating depression.
  - Popularity: few side effects; claimed to act against a wide range of psychological disorders.
  - 2003 warning about increased suicide risk was **not confirmed** in subsequent studies (Bridge et al., 2007; Simon et al., 2006; Tiihonen, 2006); premature warning led to decreased SSRI prescriptions and **markedly increased suicide rates 2003–2005** (Gibbons et al., 2007).
- **SNRIs (Selective Norepinephrine-Reuptake Inhibitors)**: e.g., Reboxetine — as effective as SSRIs in treating depression.
- Drugs blocking reuptake of **more than one monoamine**: e.g., Wellbutrin, Effexor.

#### 4. Mood Stabilizers

- Developed because antidepressants in **bipolar patients** can trigger bouts of mania.
- **Mood stabilizers**: Drugs that act against depression without increasing mania, or against mania without increasing depression (Bourin & Prica, 2007).
- Mechanism of action: **unknown**; also effective in treating **epilepsy**.
- **Lithium**: First mood stabilizer; a simple metallic ion.
  - Discovery (John Cade, Australian psychiatrist): Mixed urine of manic patients with lithium (to form a soluble salt) and injected it into guinea pigs to test for mania induction. Lithium control group was also "calmer" — but the inactivity was actually **extreme nausea** from high lithium doses, not calmness.
  - In **1954**, Cade tried lithium on 10 manic patients; apparent therapeutic effect.
  - Not widely accepted initially (poor scientific credentials, unpatentable ion).
  - Confirmed as effective against mania without increasing depression in the **late 1960s**.
- Best current treatments (Bourin & Prica, 2007):
  - Mania: **lithium** and **carbamazepine** (anti-epileptic)
  - Bipolar depression: **lamotrigine** (anti-epileptic)

#### Effectiveness of Antidepressants

- Hollon, Thase, and Markowitz (2002): MAOIs, tricyclics, and selective monoamine-reuptake inhibitors all showed similar results — **~50% improved**, but **25% control (placebo) improved**, so **only 25% net benefit**.
- **Kirsch et al. (2008)** meta-analysis (all FDA-registered evaluation studies of the 4 most common antidepressants):
  - No significant improvement over placebo in **mildly or moderately depressed** patients.
  - Only slight improvement in the **severely depressed**.
  - Overall: placebos were **82% as effective** as the actual drugs.

---

### Brain Pathology and Affective Disorders

- MRI studies of **bipolar patients**: Reductions in overall brain size (Frazier et al., 2005) and size of amygdala, cingulate cortex, prefrontal cortex (Savitz & Drevets, 2009; Delbello et al., 2004).
- Pattern is **inconsistent** across studies (McDonald et al., 2004) — suggests current diagnosis may not represent a single homogeneous disorder (Fountoulakis et al., 2008).
- **Two most consistently abnormal structures** in affective disorders:
  1. **Amygdala** (Gotlib & Hamilton, 2008; Savitz & Drevets, 2009)
  2. **Anterior cingulate cortex** (Fountoulakis et al., 2008; Greicius et al., 2007)
  - Connections **between** these two structures are also disturbed (Matthews et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2009).
  - Structural MRIs of healthy volunteers with genetic predisposition to depression show **cell loss in anterior cingulate and amygdala**.

---

### Theories of Depression

#### Monoamine Theory of Depression

- **Monoamine theory of depression**: Depression is associated with **underactivity at serotonergic and noradrenergic synapses**.
- Based on the fact that MAOIs, tricyclics, SSRIs, and SNRIs are all agonists at serotonin, norepinephrine, or both.
- Autopsy support (Nemeroff, 1998): Norepinephrine and serotonin receptors were found to be **more numerous** in brains of deceased untreated depressed individuals.
  - Interpretation: **Up-regulation** — when insufficient neurotransmitter is released, the number of receptors increases compensatorily.
- **Weakness**: Few depressed patients benefit substantially from monoamine agonists; overall support is weak.

#### Diathesis-Stress Model of Depression

- **Diathesis**: A genetic susceptibility to a disorder, incapable of initiating it by itself.
- **Central idea**: If susceptible individuals are exposed to **early-life stress**, their systems become permanently sensitized and overreact to mild stressors for the rest of their lives (Gillespie & Nemeroff, 2007; Pariante & Lightman, 2008).
- Evidence: Depressed individuals synthesize more **hypothalamic corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH)** and release more **ACTH** from the anterior pituitary and more **glucocorticoids** from the adrenal cortex.
- Limitation: Evidence is largely indirect; based on patients' unconfirmed recollections; no evidence of excessive early stress in majority of depressed patients (Monroe & Reid, 2008).

---

### Treatment of Depression with Brain Stimulation

- **Chronic deep brain stimulation (DBS)** via implanted electrodes: Demonstrated significant therapeutic effect in treatment-resistant depressed patients.
- **Lozano et al. (2008)**: Implanted stimulation electrode tip into **white matter of the anterior cingulate gyrus just ventral to the anterior end of the corpus callosum**.
- Continual pulses of electrical stimulation (undetectable by patients).
- Study: 20 patients who had repeatedly failed conventional treatments.
  - **60% showed substantial improvements**
  - **35% were largely symptom free**
  - Improvements maintained for at least **1 year** (study duration).
- Requires careful double-blind evaluation before wider recommendation.

## 18.3 Anxiety Disorders

### Definition and Overview

- **Anxiety**: Chronic fear that persists in the absence of any direct threat; a common psychological correlate of stress.
- Adaptive if it motivates effective coping; pathological when it disrupts normal functioning.
- **Anxiety disorder**: Anxiety severe enough to disrupt normal functioning.
- All anxiety disorders associated with:
  - Feelings of anxiety (fear, worry, despondency)
  - Physiological stress reactions: **tachycardia**, **hypertension**, nausea, breathing difficulty, sleep disturbances, high glucocorticoid levels.
- Prevalence: Meta-analysis of 46 studies (Somers et al., 2006) — **~17% of people** suffer from an anxiety disorder at some point; incidence **~twice as great in females**.
- Anxiety disorders are the **most prevalent of all psychiatric disorders**.

---

### Five Classes of Anxiety Disorders

| Class | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Generalized anxiety disorder** | Stress responses and extreme anxiety in the absence of any obvious precipitating stimulus |
| **Phobic anxiety disorder** | Anxiety triggered by specific objects (e.g., birds, spiders) or situations (e.g., crowds, darkness) |
| **Panic disorder** | Rapid-onset attacks of extreme fear with severe somatic symptoms (choking, heart palpitations, shortness of breath); can be a component of generalized or phobic anxiety, or a separate disorder |
| **Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)** | Frequently recurring, uncontrollable, anxiety-producing thoughts (obsessions) and impulses (compulsions); compulsive behaviors (e.g., hand-washing) reduce associated anxiety |
| **Posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD)** | Persistent pattern of psychological distress following exposure to extreme stress (war, sexual assault) (McNally, 2003; McNally, Bryant, & Ehlers, 2003; Newport & Nemeroff, 2000) |

- **Agoraphobia**: The pathological fear of public places and open spaces (the phobic anxiety disorder case M.R. suffered from).

---

### Etiology of Anxiety Disorders

- Role of **experience** is often apparent — anxiety frequently triggered by identifiable stressful events and focused on particular objects/situations (Anagnostaras, Craske, & Fanselow, 1999).
- Significant **genetic component**: heritability estimates 30%–40% (Leonardo & Hen, 2006).
- Concordance rates substantially higher in identical twins than fraternal twins.
- **No specific genes** yet linked to anxiety disorders (Gordon & Hen, 2004).
- Timing and focus of anxiety disorders often reflect the particular **experiences** of the patient (Gross & Hen, 2004).

---

### Pharmacological Treatment of Anxiety Disorders

Three drug categories:

#### 1. Benzodiazepines

- Examples: **Chlordiazepoxide (Librium)**, **diazepam (Valium)**.
- Also prescribed as: hypnotics (sleep-inducing), anticonvulsants, muscle relaxants.
- **Most widely prescribed psychoactive drugs** — approximately **10% of adult North Americans** currently taking them.
- **Mechanism**: Agonistic action on **GABA-A receptors**.
- Adverse effects: sedation, **ataxia** (disruption of motor activity), tremor, nausea, withdrawal rebound anxiety.
- Major problem: **highly addictive** — should be prescribed for **short-term use only** (Gray & McNaughton, 2000).

#### 2. Serotonin Agonists

- **Buspirone**: Widely used; selective agonist at the **5-HT1A receptor** subtype.
- Does not function as an SSRI.
- Advantage over benzodiazepines: **specific** — produces anxiolytic effects **without** ataxia, muscle relaxation, or sedation.
- Side effects: dizziness, nausea, headache, insomnia.

#### 3. Antidepressant Drugs

- **Comorbidity** of anxiety disorders and depression: over half of patients with affective disorder had also been diagnosed with an anxiety disorder (Simon et al., 2004).
- Comorbidity exists because both involve heightened emotional response to stress (Morilak & Frazer, 2004).
- SSRIs are often effective against anxiety disorders; anxiolytics often effective against depression.

---

### Animal Models of Anxiety

Three validated animal models (Gray & McNaughton, 2000; Green, 1991; Treit, 1985):

#### 1. Elevated-Plus-Maze

- Rats placed on a plus-sign-shaped maze **50 cm above the floor**.
- Two arms have sides (enclosed); two have no sides (exposed).
- **Measure of anxiety**: proportion of time spent in **enclosed arms** rather than exposed arms (Pellow et al., 1985).

#### 2. Defensive Burying Test

- Rats shocked by a wire-wrapped wooden dowel on the wall of a familiar chamber.
- **Measure of anxiety**: time rats spend spraying bedding material at the shock source with forward head/forepaw thrusting movements (Treit et al., 1993).

#### 3. Risk-Assessment Test

- After brief cat exposure in a burrow system, rats flee to burrows and freeze.
- Then engage in **risk-assessment behaviors**: scanning from burrow entrance, exploring in cautious stretched posture (Blanchard et al., 1990; Blanchard, Blanchard, & Rodgers, 1991).
- **Measures of anxiety**: time spent in freezing and risk assessment.

#### Validation and Limitation

- All three models validated by demonstrations that **benzodiazepines reduce anxiety indices**; non-anxiolytics usually do not.
- Major limitation: These models may measure **benzodiazepine-sensitive anxiety** rather than anxiety in general.
- Example: Buspirone does **not** have a reliable anxiolytic effect in the elevated-plus-maze (a non-GABAergic anxiolytic).

---

### Neural Bases of Anxiety Disorders

- Current theories based heavily on **analysis of therapeutic drug effects**.
- Anxiolytic drugs are agonists at either:
  - **GABA-A receptors** (e.g., benzodiazepines) → implicates GABAergic deficits.
  - **Serotonin receptors** (e.g., buspirone, Prozac, Paxil) → implicates serotonergic deficits.
- **Brain structures implicated** in anxiety disorders (same as affective disorders):
  - **Amygdala**
  - **Anterior cingulate cortex**
- Key distinction: In affective disorders, these structures show **shrinkage (structural damage)**; in anxiety disorders, there is typically **no gross structural damage** — abnormalities are found in **functional imaging** (abnormal activity patterns during emotional tasks).
- Example study (Schienle et al., 2005): fMRI showed **increased amygdala activity** in patients with spider phobia when viewing photographs of spiders.

## 18.4 Tourette Syndrome

### What Is Tourette Syndrome?

- **Tourette syndrome**: A disorder of **tics** — involuntary, repetitive, stereotyped movements or vocalizations (Gerard & Peterson, 2003).
- Typically begins in **childhood or early adolescence** with **simple motor tics** (e.g., eye blinking, head movements).
- Symptoms tend to become more **complex and severe** with age.
- Symptoms often **reach a peak** after a few years then gradually subside as the patient matures.

#### Common Complex Motor Tics

- Hitting, touching objects, squatting, hopping, twirling, lewd gestures.

#### Verbal Tics

| Type | Definition |
|---|---|
| Inarticulate sounds | Barking, coughing, grunting |
| **Coprolalia** | Uttering obscenities (involuntary) |
| **Echolalia** | Repetition of another person's words |
| **Palilalia** | Repetition of one's own words |

#### Epidemiology

- Prevalence: **~0.7% of the population**.
- **3 times more frequent in males** than females.
- Major genetic component:
  - Concordance rate: **55% in identical twins**, **8% in fraternal twins** (Pauls, 2001).

#### Comorbidities

- Some patients also display **attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)**, **obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)**, or both (Sheppard et al., 1999).

#### Tic Suppression

- Tics can be **temporarily suppressed** with concentration and effort.
- Common misconception (Marcks et al., 2004): 77% of medical professionals believed tic suppression is inevitably followed by a **rebound** (tics become worse after suppression).
- This has been **proven false** — no rebound above baseline observed following tic suppression (Himle & Woods, 2005; Meidinger et al., 2005).

---

### Neuropathology of Tourette Syndrome

- Well-defined disorder with clearly observable symptoms → neuropathology more amenable to study.
- Impediments: no animal model; no strong link to a single gene; symptoms often subside with age, so few postmortem studies.
- Study is based **almost exclusively on brain-imaging studies** (difficult because patients must remain motionless; often studied in adults where tics have largely subsided).

#### Key Neural Findings

- Most research focused on the **caudate nucleus** (part of the striatum):
  - Patients tend to have **smaller caudate nuclei**.
  - During tic suppression: fMRI activity recorded in **both prefrontal cortex and caudate nuclei** (Albin & Mink, 2006; Gerard & Peterson, 2003).
  - Hypothesized mechanism: **prefrontal cortex** initiates tic suppression by acting on **caudate nuclei**.
- Brain damage is more widespread than just the caudate:
  - MRI study of children (Sowell et al., 2008): documented **thinning in sensorimotor cortex**, particularly in areas controlling **face, mouth, and larynx (voice box)**.
- **Gilbert et al. (2006)**: Binding of extracellular D2 receptors was **reduced** in brains of adult Tourette patients who had **never taken D2-receptor-blocking medication**.
- Current hypothesis: Tourette syndrome involves an abnormality of the **basal ganglia–thalamus–cortex feedback circuit**; the **striatum** is especially implicated as the target of many dopaminergic projections into the basal ganglia.

---

### Treatment of Tourette Syndrome

Treatment sequence:

1. **Education**: Patient, family members, friends, and teachers educated about the nature of Tourette syndrome.
2. **Treat ancillary emotional problems**: Anxiety and depression addressed first.
3. **Treat tics**: Typically with **neuroleptics** (D2 receptor blockers, the same drugs used for schizophrenia).

#### Neuroleptics for Tics

- Can reduce tics by approximately **70%**.
- In practice, benefits are **modest** because patients (especially children) often refuse treatment due to adverse side effects (weight gain, fatigue, dry mouth).
- Efficacy of D2 blockers is consistent with the hypothesis that Tourette syndrome involves a **dopaminergic abnormality in the striatum**.

## Key Studies / Experiments

> **KEY STUDY — Ehringer & Hornykiewicz (1960)**
> - **Method**: Post-mortem analysis of brains from deceased Parkinson's disease patients.
> - **Finding**: Striatums (caudate + putamen) were depleted of dopamine.
> - **Significance**: Inspired the dopamine theory of schizophrenia.

> **KEY STUDY — Carlsson & Lindqvist (1963)**
> - **Method**: Assessed effects of chlorpromazine on extracellular dopamine and its metabolites.
> - **Finding**: Dopamine levels unchanged by chlorpromazine; metabolite levels increased.
> - **Conclusion**: Chlorpromazine is a dopamine **receptor blocker** (first identified receptor blocker); blocks dopamine binding, triggering feedback-driven dopamine release and breakdown.

> **KEY STUDY — Creese, Burt, & Snyder (1976)**
> - **Method**: Radioactively labeled dopamine was added to calf striatal membrane samples; drug ability to compete with dopamine for receptor binding was measured.
> - **Finding**: Clinically effective neuroleptics had high affinity for dopamine receptors; haloperidol appeared anomalous.
> - **Significance**: Led to the discovery of D2-selective butyrophenone binding and the D2 revision of the dopamine theory.

> **KEY STUDY — Gottesman & Bertelsen (1989)**
> - **Method**: Compared offspring of identical twins discordant for schizophrenia.
> - **Finding**: The incidence of schizophrenia was equally high in the offspring of the twin WITH and WITHOUT schizophrenia.
> - **Significance**: Demonstrates that non-ill twins can still carry and transmit the genetic risk without expressing the disorder.

> **KEY STUDY — Adams et al. (2005)**
> - **Method**: Meta-analysis of all studies comparing chlorpromazine to placebo.
> - **Finding**: Clear benefits only after 6+ months; only ~1 in 7 patients substantially helped.

> **KEY STUDY — Kirsch et al. (2008)**
> - **Method**: Meta-analysis of all FDA-registered evaluation studies of the 4 most common antidepressants.
> - **Finding**: No significant improvement over placebo in mildly/moderately depressed patients; placebos were 82% as effective as actual drugs.

> **KEY STUDY — Lozano et al. (2008)**
> - **Method**: Implanted chronic bilateral deep brain stimulation electrodes into white matter of anterior cingulate gyrus (just ventral to anterior corpus callosum) in 20 treatment-resistant depressed patients.
> - **Finding**: 60% showed substantial improvement; 35% were largely symptom-free; maintained for at least 1 year.

> **KEY STUDY — Thompson et al. (2001)**
> - **Method**: Structural MRIs of adolescents with schizophrenia.
> - **Finding**: Documented progressive cortical gray matter loss across multiple regions; greatest in certain cortical areas (shown with color-coded maps, dark blue = normal, red = greatest loss).

> **KEY STUDY — Himle & Woods (2005) / Meidinger et al. (2005)**
> - **Finding**: No rebound in tic frequency was observed following voluntary tic suppression periods in children with Tourette syndrome (refuting the widely believed rebound myth).

> **KEY STUDY — Somers et al. (2006)**
> - **Method**: Meta-analysis of 46 studies on anxiety disorder prevalence.
> - **Finding**: ~17% of people suffer from an anxiety disorder at some point; incidence about twice as great in females.

> **KEY STUDY — Famine Studies (Dutch 1944–1945 / Chinese 1959–1961)**
> - Kyle & Pichard (2006); McClellan, Susser, & King (2006); St. Clair et al. (2005)
> - **Finding**: Fetuses whose mothers suffered during these famines had an increased likelihood of developing schizophrenia as adults — supports the neurodevelopmental theory of schizophrenia.

| Term | Definition |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Concordance rates for schizophrenia**: Identical twins = 45%; fraternal twins = 10%; close biological relatives = ~10%.

2. **Gottesman & Bertelsen (1989)**: Offspring of discordant identical twins (one with, one without schizophrenia) had equal risk — the non-ill twin still carries and transmits genetic susceptibility.

3. **Chlorpromazine** was the first antischizophrenic drug AND the first receptor blocker ever identified; developed originally as an antihistamine.

4. **Carlsson and Lindqvist (1963)** discovered chlorpromazine is a dopamine receptor blocker (not a dopamine depleter), leading to the revised dopamine theory: schizophrenia = hyperactivity AT dopamine receptors.

5. **D2 receptor specificity**: Butyrophenones (e.g., haloperidol, spiroperidol) bind D2 but not D1; phenothiazines (e.g., chlorpromazine) bind both D1 and D2. Clinical potency correlates with D2 binding (Snyder, 1978).

6. **Clozapine** is the first atypical neuroleptic; has affinity for D1, D4, and serotonin receptors but minimal D2 affinity; causes agranulocytosis.

7. **Iproniazid** = first antidepressant (MAOI, 1957, originally for tuberculosis); **imipramine** = first tricyclic antidepressant (originally thought to be antischizophrenic); **fluoxetine (Prozac)** = first SSRI; **lithium (John Cade, 1954)** = first mood stabilizer.

8. **Cheese effect**: MAOIs prevent tyramine breakdown → eating tyramine-rich foods (cheese, wine, pickles) → dangerous blood pressure surges → stroke risk.

9. **Kirsch et al. (2008)**: Antidepressants are not significantly better than placebo in mild/moderate depression; placebos are 82% as effective. Only slight benefit in severe depression.

10. **Monoamine theory of depression**: Underactivity at serotonergic and noradrenergic synapses; evidence includes up-regulation of receptors in deceased untreated depressed patients (Nemeroff, 1998).

11. **Benzodiazepines** act on **GABA-A receptors**; are the most widely prescribed psychoactive drugs (~10% of adult North Americans); highly addictive; cause ataxia; should be short-term only. Buspirone acts on **5-HT1A receptors** without causing ataxia/sedation.

12. **Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent of all psychiatric disorders** (~17% lifetime prevalence; 2:1 female:male ratio) (Somers et al., 2006).

13. **Tourette syndrome**: 0.7% prevalence; 3:1 male:female; concordance 55% MZ / 8% DZ; tic suppression does NOT cause a rebound (Himle & Woods, 2005); treated with D2 receptor blockers (neuroleptics); involves smaller caudate nucleus and abnormality of the basal ganglia–thalamus–cortex circuit.

14. **Lozano et al. (2008) DBS study**: Stimulation of white matter of the anterior cingulate gyrus (ventral to anterior corpus callosum) produced 60% substantial improvement and 35% symptom-free outcomes in treatment-resistant depressed patients.

15. **Amygdala and anterior cingulate cortex** are abnormal in BOTH affective disorders (structural shrinkage) AND anxiety disorders (functional hyperactivity on fMRI without structural gross damage).
