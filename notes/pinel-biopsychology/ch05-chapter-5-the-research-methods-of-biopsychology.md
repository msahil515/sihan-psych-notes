# Chapter 5: The Research Methods of Biopsychology

**Overview:** Techniques for studying the nervous system and behavior. Part One: nervous-system methods (neuroimaging, psychophysiological recording, invasive methods, pharmacology, genetic engineering). Part Two: behavioral methods (neuropsychological testing, behavioral paradigms). Most research methods are also used clinically.

---

## PART ONE: METHODS OF STUDYING THE NERVOUS SYSTEM

## 5.1 Visualizing and Stimulating the Living Human Brain

- Before the **early 1970s**, imaging the living human brain was impossible. Conventional X-rays are useless (brain structures absorb X-rays similarly; overlapping structures lose information).

### The Ironic Case of Professor P.
- **Professor P.**, a **behavioral neuroscientist**, had a tumor on his right **auditory-vestibular cranial nerve (VIII)**. His workup shows research methods used clinically:

| Clinical Test | Method | Assessed |
|--------------|--------|-----------------|
| Audiological assessment | Detect sounds of varying volume/pitch | Hearing |
| Auditory evoked potential | EEG evoked in auditory cortex by clicks | Auditory cortex |
| Vestibular (caloric) test | Cold water in ear | Vestibular nerve |
| Somatosensory evoked potential | Cheek prick; somatosensory cortex EEG | Intraoperative baseline |
| Facial nerve (VII) monitoring | Cheek electrodes → loudspeaker | Protect facial nerve in surgery |

- Risk of VIII tumor removal: damaging the **facial nerve (VII)** → right-side facial sagging.

> **Clinical Implication:** EEG, evoked potentials, and stimulation monitoring are used in diagnosis and treatment.

### Contrast X-Rays
- **Contrast X-ray techniques**: inject a substance absorbing X-rays differently from surrounding tissue.
- **Cerebral angiography**: **radio-opaque dye** into a **cerebral artery** to visualize cerebral circulation. Uses: localize **vascular damage**; tumors via vessel **displacement**.

### X-Ray Computed Tomography (CT)
- **Computed tomography (CT)**: computer-assisted X-ray imaging; introduced **early 1970s**.
- Procedure: head in a cylinder; X-ray tube rotates around one level → computer builds a **CT scan** of a horizontal section; repeats at successive levels (**8-9 sections**) → 3D representation.

### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
- **MRI**: high-resolution images from waves **hydrogen atoms emit** when activated by **radio-frequency waves in a magnetic field**.
- Clearer than CT; superior **spatial resolution** (ability to detect spatial-location differences); 2D and 3D images. Clinical use: monitoring tumor growth.

### Positron Emission Tomography (PET)
- **PET**: the **first** technique giving **functional** (activity) images.
- 2-DG procedure: **radioactive 2-deoxyglucose (2-DG)** injected into the **carotid artery**; 2-DG resembles **glucose** and is taken up by **active cells** but **cannot be metabolized** → accumulates (Barros, Porras, & Bittner, 2005). PET maps **radioactivity** per **voxel (volume pixel)** — not a direct tissue image; must be superimposed on a brain image.

> **Key Study:** Phelps & Mazziotta (1985) — PET during tasks; high activity in occipital visual cortex during visual scanning.

### Functional MRI (fMRI)
- **fMRI**: images the **increase in oxygenated blood** to active areas; most influential tool of **cognitive neuroscience** (Poldrack, 2008).
- Mechanism (Raichle & Mintun, 2006): active areas take up **more oxygenated blood than needed**; oxygenated blood has **magnetic properties** → the **BOLD signal** (Blood-Oxygen-Level-Dependent).
- **Four advantages over PET:** (1) nothing injected; (2) **structural + functional** in one image; (3) better spatial resolution; (4) whole-brain 3D activity. Clinically used for presurgical mapping and diagnostics (Holdsworth & Bammer, 2008).
- **Limitations:** BOLD is **NOT neural activity** (complex, variable relationship); **too slow** (2-3 s/image vs. millisecond APs); often misrepresented as "pictures of neural activity."

> **Key Study:** Kiehl et al. (1999) — fMRI: cortical areas more active when identifying words vs. asterisk control.

### Magnetoencephalography (MEG)
- **MEG**: measures **scalp magnetic field changes** from neural activity. **Advantage**: superior **temporal resolution**. Like PET/fMRI, shows **correlation, not causation**.

### Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)
- **TMS**: a **magnetic field under a skull coil** disrupts an underlying cortical area (Pascual-Leone, Walsh, & Rothwell, 2000) — a **"virtual lesion"** that temporarily turns the area off.
- **Purpose**: overcomes the **causation problem** — PET/fMRI/MEG show only correlation, whereas TMS tests whether disrupting an area **causes** a behavioral change (Rorden & Karnath, 2004; Sack, 2006). Open questions: safety, depth, mechanism.

| Method | Images | Invasive? | Spatial | Temporal | Causation? |
|--------|--------|-----------|-------------|--------------|---------------------|
| CT | Structural | No (radiation) | Moderate | N/A | No |
| MRI | Structural | No | High | N/A | No |
| PET | Functional | Yes (injection) | Moderate | Low (~30 s) | No |
| fMRI | Functional + Structural | No | High | Low (2-3 s) | No |
| MEG | Functional | No | Low-moderate | High (ms) | No |
| TMS | Stimulation/disruption | No | Moderate | High (ms) | Yes |

## 5.2 Recording Human Psychophysiological Activity

- **Psychophysiological recording**: non-invasive recording from the **body surface**.
- Five measures: brain (**scalp EEG**); somatic NS (**EMG**, **EOG**); autonomic NS (**skin conductance**, **cardiovascular activity**).

### Scalp Electroencephalography (EEG)
- **EEG**: gross brain electrical activity from scalp electrodes; reflects the **sum** of APs, PSPs, and signals from skin/muscle/blood/eyes.
- Value: correlation with **states of consciousness** and **cerebral pathology** (e.g., epilepsy).

| Wave | Frequency | Amplitude | Correlate |
|------|-----------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Alpha** | 8-12 Hz | High | Relaxed wakefulness |
| Beta | >13 Hz | Low | Aroused/alert |
| Theta | 4-7 Hz | Medium | Drowsy; memory tasks (anterior cingulate) |
| Delta | <4 Hz | Very high | Deep sleep |

**Event-Related Potentials (ERPs):** EEG waves accompanying psychological events. **Sensory evoked potential** = cortical EEG change from a momentary stimulus. Components: **Signal** + **Noise** (noise often masks signal).

**Signal averaging:** record ~1,000 responses; average per time point → random noise cancels → **average evoked potential (AEP)**, characterized by **direction** and **latency**.

**Key ERP components:**
- **P300 wave**: positive wave ~**300 ms** after a **meaningful** stimulus (Friedman, Cycowicz, & Gaeta, 2001); positive waves shown as downward deflections.
- **Far-field potentials**: small waves in the **first few ms**; from **brainstem sensory nuclei**; **not influenced by stimulus meaning**.

**Spatial resolution:** traditionally poor; Gevins et al. (1995) color-code evoked EEG on a 3D MRI surface.

> **Key Study (Gevins et al., 1995):** theta during a memory task peaked in the **anterior cingulate cortex**.

### Muscle Tension (EMG)
- **Electromyography** → **electromyogram (EMG)** from skin electrodes. Fibers contract **all-or-none**; anxious/aroused people show **high resting muscle tension** → index of **arousal**.
- **Raw EMG**: amplitude rises with contraction. **Integrated EMG**: total activity per unit time (smooth curve).

### Eye Movement (EOG)
- **Electrooculography** → **electrooculogram (EOG)**. Basis: steady **front-positive/back-negative** eyeball potential. Electrodes each side = **horizontal**; above/below = **vertical**.

### Skin Conductance
- Emotion increases skin's electrical conductance. **Skin conductance level (SCL)**: background level. **Skin conductance response (SCR)**: transient change with discrete experiences. Basis: **sweat glands** (most responsive: hands, feet, armpits, forehead).

### Cardiovascular Activity
1. **Heart rate**: **electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG)** from chest electrodes; normal rest ~**70 bpm**.
2. **Blood pressure**: **systolic** (peak, contraction) / **diastolic** (minimum, relaxation) in **mmHg**; normal ~**130/70**; **hypertension** = chronic >**140/90**; measured by **sphygmomanometer**.
3. **Blood volume**: **plethysmography** (e.g., **genital engorgement** in sexual arousal) via **strain gauge** or **light absorption**.

## 5.3 Invasive Physiological Research Methods

- Mostly in **lab animals**; three categories (lesion, electrical stimulation, recording); prerequisite = **stereotaxic surgery**.

### Stereotaxic Surgery
- **Stereotaxic surgery**: precisely positions devices in brain depths using:
  1. **Stereotaxic atlas**: 2D frontal maps; distances in mm from a **reference point** (rats: **bregma**, where two skull **sutures** meet).
  2. **Stereotaxic instrument**: **head holder** + **electrode holder**; three axes (anterior-posterior, dorsal-ventral, lateral-medial).

### Lesion Methods
- **Lesion**: brain part removed/damaged; behavior then assessed.

| Type | Method | Target | Notes |
|------|--------|--------|-------|
| **Aspiration lesion** | Suction via glass pipette | Cortical tissue | White matter/vessels preserved |
| **Radio-frequency lesion** | High-frequency current heats tissue | Small subcortical structures | Size set by current/electrode |
| **Knife cut (sectioning)** | Blade swings out | Nerves/tracts | Minimal surrounding damage |
| **Cryogenic blockade** | Cryoprobe cools neurons below threshold | Any target | No structural damage = **reversible lesion** |

- **Reversible lesions** also via **local anesthetics** (e.g., **lidocaine**; Floresco, Seamans, & Phillips, 1997).

**Cautions:** structures are tightly packed, so lesions rarely hit only the target. **Oversimplification trap**: naming an imprecise lesion after the target leads to (1) attributing all effects to it, (2) wrongly concluding it is uninvolved in unaffected behaviors.

**Bilateral vs. unilateral:** unilateral effects are often mild; **bilateral** effects are **much more severe**; most studies use **bilateral** lesions.

### Electrical Stimulation
- Via **bipolar electrode** (two insulated wires wound together). Weak pulses increase nearby firing; effects usually **opposite to a lesion**; can elicit eating, drinking, attacking, copulating, sleeping. Depends on tip location, current parameters, environment.

### Invasive Electrophysiological Recording

| Method | Electrode | Records | Animal State |
|--------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Intracellular unit** | Inside neuron | Graded membrane potential | Immobilized |
| **Extracellular unit** | Next to neuron | APs only | Freely moving possible |
| **Multiple-unit** | Large tip | Total APs/time (integrated) | Freely moving |
| **Invasive EEG** | Implanted (skull/wire) | General electrical activity | Freely moving |

- Modern extracellular: simultaneous recording from up to **~100 neurons** (Nicolelis & Ribeiro, 2006).

## 5.4 Pharmacological Research Methods

- **Main psychopharmacology strategy**: give drugs that **increase or decrease** specific neurotransmitter effects; observe behavior.

### Routes of Administration

| Route | Abbrev. | Site |
|-------|-------------|------|
| Intraperitoneal | **IP** | Abdominal peritoneal cavity |
| Intramuscular | **IM** | Large muscle |
| Subcutaneous | **SC** | Fatty tissue under skin |
| Intravenous | **IV** | Surface vein |
| Intragastric | IG | Stomach (tube) |
| Oral | — | Ingested |
| Intracerebral | — | Into brain via cannula |

- **Problem**: many drugs don't cross the **blood-brain barrier**. **Solution**: a **cannula** (hollow tube) stereotaxically implanted for direct delivery.

### Selective Chemical Lesions

| Neurotoxin | Selectivity | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Kainic acid** | Cell bodies at tip | Destroys cell bodies; spares passing axons |
| **Ibotenic acid** | Cell bodies at tip | Destroys cell bodies; spares passing axons |
| **6-hydroxydopamine (6-OHDA)** | **Norepinephrine/dopamine** neurons | Destroys catecholaminergic neurons only |

### Measuring Chemical Activity
**1. 2-DG technique (animal):** inject **radioactive 2-DG** → active neurons accumulate it (can't metabolize) → kill animal, slice brain → **autoradiography** (photographic emulsion) → active areas appear **black**. (PET = same principle in living humans.)

**2. Cerebral dialysis:** measures **extracellular neurochemical concentration** in **behaving animals** (Robinson & Justice, 1991) via an implanted **semipermeable** tube; collected chemicals analyzed (often by **chromatograph**).

### Locating Neurotransmitters and Receptors
Both expose brain slices to a **labeled ligand**.

**1. Immunocytochemistry:** locate neuroproteins by labeling their **antibodies** with dye/radioactivity. **Antigen** = foreign protein triggering antibody production. Indirect NT location: target the **synthesis enzymes** (e.g., labeled antibody to **tyrosine hydroxylase** locates **dopaminergic neurons** in the substantia nigra).

**2. In situ hybridization:** label artificial **complementary mRNA** strands → they bind target **mRNA** in neurons → marks neurons synthesizing the target protein (localizes **mRNA expression**).

## 5.5 Genetic Engineering

### Gene Knockout
- **Gene knockout techniques**: create organisms **lacking a gene** (Eisener-Dorman, Lawrence, & Bolivar, 2008). **Knockout mice** = favored subjects.

> **KEY STUDY: Melanopsin Knockout Mice** (Ruby et al., 2002; Hattar et al., 2003)
> - **Melanopsin**: protein in retinal neurons projecting to the **hypothalamic circadian clock**; implicated in light-based circadian regulation.
> - **Findings:** knockout **reduced but did not eliminate** clock responses and circadian adjustment to light.
> - **Conclusion:** melanopsin contributes but is **not the only factor**.

**Key generalization:** "one gene, one behavior" is FALSE; each behavior is controlled by **many genes interacting with each other and with experience**; knockout typically **reduces but does not eliminate** a behavior.

### Gene Replacement
- **Gene replacement techniques**: swap one gene for another. **Transgenic mice**: contain **another species' genetic material**.

> **KEY STUDY: Schizophrenia Transgenic Mice** (Shen et al., 2008)
> - Inserted a **defective human schizophrenia gene** (from a high-incidence Scottish family) into mice.
> - **Findings:** **reduced cerebral cortex**, **enlarged ventricles**, schizophrenia-like behaviors.
> - **Conclusion:** the gene is a **causal factor** in the familial schizophrenia.

- Genes can be given a chemical **switch** to turn on/off at a chosen developmental point. Future goal: **gene therapy** (as yet unrealized).

### Fluorescence and the Brainbow
- **Green fluorescent protein (GFP)**: fluoresces **green under blue light**; first isolated from a **jellyfish** by **Shimomura, Johnson, and Saiga (1962)**; gene cloned early 1990s; **Nobel Prize in Chemistry 2008** (Chalfie, Shimomura, Tsien).
- Strategy: express GFP in **only target cells**. **First neuronal use:** Chalfie et al. (1994) — GFP in roundworm **C. elegans** touch-receptor neurons → glowed green.
- **Brainbow (Livet et al., 2007):** Tsien (1998) found GFP variants fluoresce in **different colors**; introducing **cyan, yellow, and blue** proteins into developing mouse neurons gives each neuron a **unique color** → **trace axonal pathways**.

## PART TWO: BEHAVIORAL RESEARCH METHODS

- Objectives: **control, simplify, objectify**.
- **Behavioral paradigm**: procedures to (1) **produce** and (2) **objectively measure** a behavioral phenomenon.

## 5.6 Neuropsychological Testing

- **Neurologist**: assesses simple sensory/motor functions. **Neuropsychologist**: assesses subtle perceptual, emotional, motivational, cognitive functions.
- **Three benefits**: (1) assist **diagnosis** when other tests are equivocal; (2) basis for **counseling/care**; (3) **evaluate treatment** effectiveness and side effects.

### Three Phases

| Phase | Era | Goal | Method | Limitation |
|-------|-----|------|--------|-----------|
| **Single-test** | Pre-1950s | Detect brain damage; structural vs. functional | One test | No single test covers all symptoms |
| **Standardized battery** | 1960s | Identify brain-damaged patients | Battery (e.g., Halstead-Reitan); score < cutoff = damage | Poor at neurological vs. psychiatric |
| **Customized battery** | From 1960s (current) | Characterize **nature** of deficits | Common battery + patient-specific tests | Needs more skill |

- **Halstead-Reitan Battery**: most widely used standardized battery; aggregate score below a **cutoff** = brain damage.
- **Customized approach**: common battery first, then targeted tests (e.g., memory deficit → short- and long-term memory tests). Differs by (1) measuring functions from **modern theories**; (2) assessing **cognitive strategy**, not just score; (3) requiring **more skill**.

### Common Battery Tests

**Intelligence:**
- IQ is a **poor measure** of brain damage but included for context.
- **WAIS (Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale)**: first published **1955**; standardized **1981** on **1,880 U.S. citizens**, ages **16-71**; given first (IQ aids interpretation); **11 original subtests** (Information, Comprehension, Arithmetic, Similarities, Digit Span, Vocabulary, Digit Symbol, Picture Completion, Block Design, Picture Arrangement, Object Assembly); later version 15 subtests.
- **Low verbal scores → left hemisphere damage**; **low performance scores → right hemisphere damage**.

**Memory:**
- WAIS often misses memory deficits. **Information subtest**: general-knowledge memory. **Digit span**: most widely used **short-term memory** test; normal span = **7**; rarely disrupted by damage.

**Language:**
- Inferred from low verbal scores, or the **Token Test**: **20 tokens** (2 shapes, 2 sizes, 5 colors: white, black, yellow, green, red); instructions of increasing complexity ("Touch a red square" → "Touch the small red circle then the large green square").

**Language lateralization:** one hemisphere dominates language; **left** in most people, **right** in some (Ch. 16); tests determine which dominates per patient.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **CT vs. MRI**: CT = computer-assisted X-ray (early 1970s); MRI = hydrogen atoms in a magnetic field; MRI has higher spatial resolution and 3D.

2. **PET**: radioactive 2-DG into the carotid artery; taken up by active neurons but not metabolized; accumulates; **first functional** imaging.

3. **fMRI's four advantages over PET**: no injection; structural + functional; better spatial resolution; whole-brain 3D.

4. **BOLD signal**: what fMRI measures; NOT direct neural activity; 2-3 s/image (too slow for ms events).

5. **TMS** is the only method that can **demonstrate causation** ("virtual lesion"); PET/fMRI/MEG show correlation only.

6. **MEG**: superior **temporal resolution**; lower spatial resolution.

7. **P300**: positive deflection ~300 ms; only for **meaningful** stimuli. **Far-field potentials** (first ms) from brainstem; meaning-independent.

8. **Alpha waves**: 8-12 Hz, high amplitude; **relaxed wakefulness**.

9. **Signal averaging**: cancels random noise; the evoked potential survives.

10. **Kainic/ibotenic acid** destroy cell bodies, spare axons; **6-OHDA** destroys NE/dopamine neurons; **cryogenic blockade** = reversible lesion (also via lidocaine).

11. **Bilateral > unilateral** lesions in severity; most studies use bilateral.

12. **Gene knockout**: typically **reduces but does not eliminate** a behavior; behaviors are polygenic × experience.

13. **GFP/Brainbow**: GFP from jellyfish (Shimomura et al., 1962); Nobel 2008 (Chalfie, Shimomura, Tsien); Brainbow (Livet et al., 2007) = unique color per neuron.

14. **Three testing phases**: single-test (pre-1950s); Halstead-Reitan standardized battery (1960s); customized battery (current).

15. **WAIS**: published 1955; standardized 1981 (1,880 citizens, 16-71); 11 subtests. Low verbal = left hemisphere; low performance = right hemisphere. Digit span normal = 7, rarely disrupted.
