# Chapter 5: The Research Methods of Biopsychology

**Chapter Overview:** This chapter surveys the day-to-day techniques biopsychologists use to study both the nervous system and behavior. It is organized around biopsychology's intrinsic duality: Part One covers methods for studying the nervous system (neuroimaging, psychophysiological recording, invasive physiological methods, pharmacological methods, and genetic engineering); Part Two covers behavioral research methods (neuropsychological testing and behavioral paradigms). Most methods used in research are also employed clinically for diagnosis and treatment.

## PART ONE: METHODS OF STUDYING THE NERVOUS SYSTEM

---

## 5.1 Methods of Visualizing and Stimulating the Living Human Brain

- Prior to the **early 1970s**, biopsychological research was impeded by the inability to obtain images of the **living human brain**.
- Conventional X-ray photography is next to useless for brain imaging: brain structures differ only slightly from one another in X-ray absorption; overlapping structures leave little information in the beam.

---

### The Ironic Case of Professor P.

- **Professor P.** was a **behavioral neuroscientist** with a brain tumor on his right **auditory-vestibular cranial nerve (cranial nerve VIII)**.
- His pre-surgical workup demonstrated that biopsychological research methods are also used clinically:

| Clinical Test | Method | What It Assessed |
|--------------|--------|-----------------|
| Audiological assessment | Detect sounds of various volumes/pitches | Hearing ability |
| Auditory evoked potential | EEG signals evoked in auditory cortex by clicks | Auditory cortex function |
| Vestibular function (caloric test) | Cold water injected into ear | Vestibular nerve function |
| Somatosensory evoked potential baseline | Skin of cheek pricked; EEG of somatosensory cortex recorded | Baseline for intraoperative monitoring |
| Intraoperative facial nerve (VII) monitoring | Cheek electrodes; cortical responses fed to loudspeaker | Protect facial nerve during tumor removal |

- Risk of auditory nerve (VIII) tumor removal: damaging the **facial nerve (VII)**, causing right-side facial sagging.

> **Clinical Implication:** Many biopsychological research methods (EEG, evoked potentials, electrical stimulation monitoring) are used in clinical diagnosis and treatment.

---

### Contrast X-Rays

- **Contrast X-ray techniques**: inject a substance into one body compartment that absorbs X-rays either **less than or more than** surrounding tissue, heightening contrast during X-ray photography.
- **Cerebral angiography**: contrast X-ray technique; a **radio-opaque dye** is infused into a **cerebral artery** to visualize the **cerebral circulatory system** during X-ray photography.
  - Primary use: localizing **vascular damage**.
  - Secondary use: tumor localization via blood vessel **displacement from normal position**.

---

### X-Ray Computed Tomography (CT)

- **Computed tomography (CT)**: computer-assisted X-ray procedure to visualize the brain and other internal structures of the living body.
- Introduced **early 1970s**; revolutionized the study of the living human brain.

**Procedure:**
1. Patient's head positioned in center of a large cylinder.
2. X-ray tube rotates around the head at one horizontal level, projecting beam to an X-ray detector on the opposite side.
3. Computer combines many individual X-ray photographs to generate a **CT scan** of one horizontal brain section.
4. Tube and detector move to next level along the body axis; process repeats.
5. **8-9 horizontal sections** typically obtained; combined into a **3D representation**.

---

### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

- **Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)**: procedure constructing high-resolution images from the measurement of waves that **hydrogen atoms emit** when activated by **radio-frequency waves in a magnetic field**.
- Stimulated by CT's success; provides **clearer images** than CT.
- **Spatial resolution**: the ability to detect and represent differences in spatial location — MRI has superior spatial resolution vs. CT.
- Produces 2D and 3D images.
- Clinical use: monitoring tumor growth over time (sequential structural MRI scans).

---

### Positron Emission Tomography (PET)

- **Positron emission tomography (PET)**: the **first** brain-imaging technique to provide **functional brain images** (images of brain activity) rather than structural images.

**Standard 2-DG procedure:**
1. **Radioactive 2-deoxyglucose (2-DG)** injected into patient's **carotid artery** (feeds the ipsilateral cerebral hemisphere).
2. 2-DG is similar to **glucose** (primary metabolic fuel of the brain); rapidly taken up by **active, energy-consuming cells**.
3. Unlike glucose, **2-DG cannot be metabolized** — accumulates in active neurons (or associated astrocytes: Barros, Porras, & Bittner, 2005) until gradually broken down.
4. PET scan = color-coded image of **radioactivity levels** in various parts of one horizontal brain level.
5. If scan taken during 30 seconds of reading: indicates **most active areas** during that task.

- **Voxels (volume pixels)**: tiny cubic units composing a PET scan; a PET scan is a colored map of radioactivity per voxel — NOT a direct image of brain tissue.
- To map voxels onto brain structures: PET scan must be **superimposed on a brain image**.

> **Key Study:** Phelps & Mazziotta (1985) — Series of PET scans during different cognitive/perceptual tasks; high activity (reds and yellows) in visual cortex of occipital lobe during visual scanning.

---

### Functional MRI (fMRI)

- **Functional MRI (fMRI)**: produces images representing the **increase in oxygen flow in the blood** to active brain areas.
- Most influential tool of **cognitive neuroscience** (Poldrack, 2008); widely used for medical diagnosis (Holdsworth & Bammer, 2008).

**Mechanism (Raichle & Mintun, 2006):**
1. Active brain areas take up **more oxygenated blood than needed** for energy; oxygenated blood accumulates in active areas.
2. Oxygenated blood has **magnetic properties** (oxygen influences effect of magnetic fields on blood iron).
- Signal recorded: **BOLD signal** (Blood-Oxygen-Level-Dependent signal).

**Four advantages of fMRI over PET:**
1. **Nothing injected** into subject.
2. Provides **structural AND functional** information in same image.
3. **Better spatial resolution**.
4. Can produce **3D images** of activity over entire brain.

**Critical limitations:**
- BOLD signal is **NOT neural activity** — relationship is complex and variable (Bartels et al., 2008; Ekstrom et al., 2009; Goense & Logothetis, 2008; Shmuel & Leopold, 2009; Zhang et al., 2009).
- **Too slow** to capture many neural responses: takes **2-3 seconds** per image; action potentials occur in **milliseconds** (Dobbs, 2005; Poldrack, 2008).
- Images often misleadingly presented as "pictures of neural activity" (especially in popular press).

> **Key Study:** Kiehl et al. (1999) — fMRI showing cortical areas more active when subjects identified which letter strings were words vs. asterisk control condition.

---

### Magnetoencephalography (MEG)

- **Magnetoencephalography (MEG)**: measures changes in **magnetic fields on the scalp surface** produced by changes in underlying neural activity.
- **Major advantage over fMRI**: **superior temporal resolution** — can record fast changes in neural activity.
- Shared weakness with PET and fMRI: shows **correlation**, cannot prove **causation**.

---

### Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)

- **Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)**: creates a **magnetic field under a coil** positioned next to the skull, affecting activity in an underlying cortical area (Fitzpatrick & Rothman, 2000; Pascual-Leone, Walsh, & Rothwell, 2000).
- Effect: **temporarily turns off** (disrupts) part of the brain; then effects on cognition and behavior are assessed.
- Often called a **"virtual lesion"** technique.
- **Critical purpose**: overcomes the **causation problem** of brain-imaging studies.
  - PET, fMRI, MEG can only show **correlation** between brain and cognitive activity.
  - TMS tests whether disrupting a brain area **actually causes** a behavioral change.
- Still has open questions: safety, depth of effect, mechanisms of neural disruption (Allen et al., 2007; Bestmann, 2007; Wagner et al., 2007).
- References for causation issue: Rorden & Karnath (2004); Sack (2006).

**Brain Imaging Methods Comparison:**

| Method | Images | Invasive? | Spatial Res. | Temporal Res. | Can Prove Causation? |
|--------|--------|-----------|-------------|--------------|---------------------|
| CT | Structural | No (radiation) | Moderate | N/A | No |
| MRI | Structural | No | High | N/A | No |
| PET | Functional | Yes (radioactive injection) | Moderate | Low (~30 s) | No |
| fMRI | Functional + Structural | No | High | Low (2-3 s) | No |
| MEG | Functional | No | Low-moderate | High (ms) | No |
| TMS | Stimulation/disruption | No | Moderate | High (ms effects) | Yes (disruption) |

## 5.2 Recording Human Psychophysiological Activity

- **Psychophysiological recording methods**: recording physiological activity from the **surface of the human body** (non-invasive).

**Five major measures:**
- Brain activity: **scalp EEG** (1)
- Somatic nervous system: **muscle tension (EMG)** and **eye movement (EOG)** (2)
- Autonomic nervous system: **skin conductance** and **cardiovascular activity** (2)

---

### Scalp Electroencephalography (EEG)

- **Electroencephalogram (EEG)**: measure of **gross electrical activity of the brain**.
- Device: **electroencephalograph (EEG machine)**; technique: **electroencephalography**.
- Electrodes: disk-shaped (~half size of a dime), taped to scalp.
- EEG signal reflects the **sum** of: action potentials, postsynaptic potentials, plus electrical signals from skin, muscles, blood, eyes.
- Utility: not a clear view of single-neuron activity — value lies in correlation with **states of consciousness** and **cerebral pathology** (e.g., epilepsy).
- Signals decrease in amplitude from source — multi-site recording allows rough source localization.

**EEG Wave Forms:**

| Wave | Frequency | Amplitude | Psychological Correlate |
|------|-----------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Alpha waves** | 8-12 Hz | High | Relaxed wakefulness |
| Beta waves | >13 Hz | Low | Aroused/alert |
| Theta waves | 4-7 Hz | Medium | Drowsy; also memory tasks (anterior cingulate) |
| Delta waves | <4 Hz | Very high | Deep sleep |

**Event-Related Potentials (ERPs):**
- **Event-related potentials (ERPs)**: EEG waves accompanying particular psychological events.
- **Sensory evoked potential**: change in cortical EEG elicited by momentary sensory stimulus presentation.
- Components: **Signal** (response to stimulus) + **Noise** (ongoing background EEG).
- Problem: noise often masks the signal.

**Signal Averaging:**
- Record response to a stimulus ~1,000 times; computer calculates mean at each time point.
- Random noise cancels out; consistent signal remains.
- Result: **Average evoked potential (AEP)**.
- Each AEP wave characterized by **direction** (positive/negative) and **latency** (ms after stimulus).

**Key ERP Components:**
- **P300 wave**: positive wave ~**300 ms** after a stimulus that has **meaning for the subject** (e.g., signals an imminent reward; requires a response). Reference: Friedman, Cycowicz, & Gaeta (2001). By convention, positive EEG waves shown as **downward deflections**.
- **Far-field potentials**: small waves in the **first few ms** after stimulus; originate in **brainstem sensory nuclei**; **NOT influenced by stimulus meaning**.

**Spatial resolution of EEG:**
- Traditional: poor spatial resolution.
- Modern advance (Gevins et al., 1995): amplitude of evoked EEG signals color-coded and plotted on surface of 3D MRI scan.

> **Key Study (Gevins et al., 1995):** Distribution of theta waves during a memory task plotted on 3D MRI showed highest incidence of theta waves in the **anterior cingulate cortex**.

---

### Muscle Tension (EMG)

- **Electromyography**: measures muscle tension; record = **electromyogram (EMG)**.
- Electrodes taped to skin over the muscle of interest.
- Each skeletal muscle fiber contracts in an **all-or-none fashion**; muscle tone maintained by a few fibers contracting at any time.
- Psychological relevance: anxious/aroused individuals show **high resting muscle tension** — EMG is an indicator of **psychological arousal**.
- **Raw EMG signal**: increased contraction = increased amplitude.
- **Integrated EMG signal**: total EMG activity per unit time (e.g., 0.1-s intervals); smooth curve.

---

### Eye Movement (EOG)

- **Electrooculography**: records eye movements; record = **electrooculogram (EOG)**.
- Basis: **steady potential difference between front (positive) and back (negative) of the eyeball**.
- When eye moves, change in electrical potential detectable across periorbital electrodes.
- Electrode placement:
  - Each side of eye: **horizontal movements**.
  - Above and below eye: **vertical movements**.

---

### Skin Conductance

- Emotional experiences associated with **increases in skin's ability to conduct electricity**.
- **Skin conductance level (SCL)**: background level of skin conductance associated with a particular situation.
- **Skin conductance response (SCR)**: transient change in skin conductance associated with discrete experiences.
- Physiological basis: incompletely understood; **sweat glands** implicated.
  - Main function of sweat glands: body cooling; but they activate in emotional situations.
  - Most emotionally responsive sweat glands: **hands, feet, armpits, forehead**.

---

### Cardiovascular Activity

- The cardiovascular system: blood vessels + heart.
- Functions: distribute O2/nutrients, remove metabolic wastes, transmit chemical messages.

**Three psychophysiological cardiovascular measures:**

**1. Heart Rate:**
- **Electrocardiogram (ECG, or EKG from German)**: electrical signal of each heartbeat; recorded from chest electrodes.
- Normal resting heart rate: **~70 beats per minute**.

**2. Blood Pressure:**
- **Systolic pressure**: peak pressure during heart **contraction** (systoles).
- **Diastolic pressure**: minimum pressure during **relaxation** (diastoles).
- Expressed as systolic/diastolic in **mmHg**.
- Normal resting BP: **~130/70 mmHg**.
- **Hypertension**: chronic BP > **140/90 mmHg**; serious health hazard.
- **Sphygmomanometer**: clinical BP device (hollow cuff + rubber bulb + pressure gauge; sphygmos = pulse).

**3. Blood Volume:**
- **Plethysmography**: techniques for measuring changes in **blood volume** in a particular body part (plethysmos = enlargement).
- Best-known example: **genital engorgement** with sexual arousal (both males and females).
- Methods:
  - **Strain gauge** wrapped around target tissue.
  - **Light absorption** through tissue (more blood = more light absorbed).

## 5.3 Invasive Physiological Research Methods

- Used primarily in **laboratory animal** research.
- Three categories: **lesion methods**, **electrical stimulation**, **invasive recording methods**.
- Prerequisite: **stereotaxic surgery**.

---

### Stereotaxic Surgery

- **Stereotaxic surgery**: precisely positions experimental devices in the **depths of the brain**.
- Required elements:
  1. **Stereotaxic atlas**: directions to target (like a geographic atlas but 3D).
  2. **Stereotaxic instrument**: device for getting there.

**Stereotaxic atlas:**
- Series of 2D frontal brain slice maps; distances in mm from a **reference point**.
- Rat atlases: reference point = **bregma** (point on skull top where two major **sutures** intersect).

**Stereotaxic instrument:**
- **Head holder**: fixes brain in prescribed position and orientation.
- **Electrode holder**: holds the device to be inserted.
- Three axes of movement: **anterior-posterior, dorsal-ventral, lateral-medial**.
- Example: rat amygdala implanted at 2.8 mm posterior to bregma, 4.5 mm lateral, 8.5 mm ventral; electrode anchored with stainless steel screws and dental acrylic.

---

### Lesion Methods

- **Lesion**: part of brain removed, damaged, or destroyed; then behavior assessed to infer function.
- **Four types:**

| Type | Method | Target | Notes |
|------|--------|--------|-------|
| **Aspiration lesion** | Suction through glass pipette | Accessible cortical tissue | White matter more resistant to suction; vessels preserved |
| **Radio-frequency lesion** | High-frequency current heats tissue | Small subcortical structures | Size/shape determined by current duration, intensity, electrode configuration |
| **Knife cut (sectioning)** | Blade swings out from stereotaxically positioned device | Nerves or tracts | Eliminates conduction with minimal surrounding damage |
| **Cryogenic blockade** | Coolant through cryoprobe cools neurons below firing threshold | Any stereotaxic target | No structural damage; neurons resume firing when warmed; = **reversible lesion** |

- **Reversible lesions** also achievable via microinjections of **local anesthetics** (e.g., **lidocaine**; Floresco, Seamans, & Phillips, 1997).

**Interpreting Lesion Effects — Cautions:**
- Brain structures are small, convoluted, and tightly packed; virtually impossible to destroy only the target.
- **Oversimplification trap**: calling an imprecise lesion by the target structure's name leads to:
  1. Attributing all effects to the named structure.
  2. Concluding the named structure is not involved in unaffected behaviors (because the lesion was incomplete).

**Bilateral vs. Unilateral Lesions:**
- **Unilateral**: restricted to one brain half; effects often mild or hard to detect.
- **Bilateral**: both sides; **much more severe** behavioral effects.
- Most experimental studies use **bilateral lesions**.

---

### Electrical Stimulation

- Delivered via **bipolar electrode** (two insulated wires wound together, cut at end).
- Weak current pulses = immediate increase in firing of nearby neurons.
- Effects: usually **opposite to lesion** of same site.
- Can elicit: eating, drinking, attacking, copulating, sleeping.
- Response depends on: **electrode tip location**, **current parameters**, **test environment**.

---

### Invasive Electrophysiological Recording Methods

**Four methods:**

| Method | Electrode | Records | Animal State |
|--------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Intracellular unit recording** | Tip inside neuron | Graded membrane potential fluctuations | Chemically immobilized (movement impossible) |
| **Extracellular unit recording** | Tip in extracellular fluid next to neuron | Action potentials only (no membrane potential) | Can be freely moving (flexible microelectrodes) |
| **Multiple-unit recording** | Large tip (many neurons) | Total action potentials per unit time via integrating circuit | Can be freely moving |
| **Invasive EEG recording** | Implanted (skull screws = cortical; wire electrodes = subcortical) | General electrical brain activity | Can be freely moving |

- Modern extracellular capability: simultaneously record from up to **~100 neurons** by analyzing correlations among several implanted electrodes (Nicolelis & Ribeiro, 2006).

---

## 5.4 Pharmacological Research Methods

- **Major strategy of psychopharmacology**: administer drugs that **increase or decrease effects of particular neurotransmitters** and observe behavioral consequences.

---

### Routes of Drug Administration

| Route | Abbreviation | Site |
|-------|-------------|------|
| Intraperitoneal | **IP** | Peritoneal cavity of abdomen |
| Intramuscular | **IM** | Large muscle |
| Subcutaneous | **SC** | Fatty tissue beneath skin |
| Intravenous | **IV** | Large surface vein |
| Intragastric | IG | Stomach (via tube) |
| Oral | — | Ingested |
| Intracerebral (cannula) | — | Directly into brain via stereotaxic cannula |

- **Problem with peripheral routes**: many drugs do not pass through the **blood-brain barrier**.
- **Solution**: **cannula** (fine hollow tube) stereotaxically implanted in brain; drugs administered in small amounts directly.

---

### Selective Chemical Lesions

- Regular lesions affect **all neurons** in target area; selective chemical lesions use **neurotoxins** with affinity for specific neural components.

**Three key selective neurotoxins:**

| Neurotoxin | Selectivity | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Kainic acid** | Cell bodies at cannula tip | Destroys cell bodies; spares passing axons |
| **Ibotenic acid** | Cell bodies at cannula tip | Destroys cell bodies; spares passing axons |
| **6-hydroxydopamine (6-OHDA)** | Only neurons releasing **norepinephrine** or **dopamine** | Destroys catecholaminergic neurons; leaves others intact |

---

### Measuring Chemical Activity of the Brain

**1. The 2-Deoxyglucose (2-DG) Technique (animal version):**
1. Animal injected with **radioactive 2-DG**.
2. Placed in test situation for activity of interest.
3. Active neurons absorb 2-DG (like glucose) but **cannot metabolize it** — accumulates.
4. Animal **killed**; brain removed and sliced.
5. Slices subjected to **autoradiography**: coated with photographic emulsion, stored in dark, then developed.
6. High radioactivity areas appear as **black spots** (density can be color-coded).

Compare with PET: PET uses same 2-DG principle but in **living humans**.

**2. Cerebral Dialysis:**
- **Cerebral dialysis**: measures **extracellular concentration of specific neurochemicals** in **behaving animals** without killing them (Robinson & Justice, 1991).
- Fine tube with **semipermeable section** implanted in brain structure of interest.
- Extracellular chemicals **diffuse into tube** and are collected for:
  - Freezing and storage for later analysis, OR
  - Direct delivery to a **chromatograph** (device measuring chemical constituents of liquids/gases).

---

### Locating Neurotransmitters and Receptors in the Brain

Both techniques involve exposing brain slices to a **labeled ligand** (a molecule that binds to the target molecule).

**1. Immunocytochemistry:**
- **Antigen**: foreign protein that triggers immune reaction (antibody production).
- **Immunocytochemistry**: locates neuroproteins in brain by labeling their **antibodies with dye or radioactive element** and exposing brain slices to labeled antibodies.
- Dye/radioactivity accumulation marks the **target neuroprotein's location**.
- Indirect neurotransmitter location: bind to the **enzymes** required for neurotransmitter synthesis (only synthesizing neurons have all those enzymes).
- Example: labeled antibody to **tyrosine hydroxylase** (enzyme converting tyrosine to L-dopa) locates **dopaminergic neurons** in the substantia nigra.

**2. In Situ Hybridization:**
- Based on the fact that all peptides/proteins are transcribed from **mRNA sequences**.
- **Procedure:**
  1. Artificially create hybrid mRNA strands with **complementary base sequences** to the target mRNA.
  2. Label hybrid strands with **dye or radioactive element**.
  3. Expose brain slices to labeled hybrid strands.
  4. Hybrid strands **bind to complementary mRNA** in neurons — marks neurons that synthesize the target neuroprotein.
- Localizes **mRNA expression** (i.e., where a protein is being synthesized).

## 5.5 Genetic Engineering

---

### Gene Knockout Techniques

- **Gene knockout techniques**: create organisms **lacking a particular gene** under investigation (Eisener-Dorman, Lawrence, & Bolivar, 2008).
- **Knockout mice**: favored mammalian subjects; gene for a specific protein deleted.

> **KEY STUDY: Melanopsin Knockout Mice**
> - **Researchers:** Ruby et al. (2002); Hattar et al. (2003)
> - **Gene knocked out:** Gene for **melanopsin** synthesis
> - **Background:** Melanopsin is a protein in some mammalian **retinal neurons** that project to the **hypothalamic circadian clock mechanism**; implicated in regulating circadian rhythms by light.
> - **Findings:**
>   1. Knockout **reduced but did not eliminate** clock responses to light changes.
>   2. **Impaired but did not eliminate** ability to adjust circadian rhythms to light-dark changes.
> - **Conclusion:** Melanopsin contributes to circadian rhythm control by light but is **not the only factor**.

**Critical generalization from gene knockout studies:**
- "Each behavior is controlled by a **single gene**" is FALSE.
- Reality: each behavior is controlled by **many genes interacting with one another and with experience**.
- Typical finding: gene knockout **reduces but does not eliminate** a behavior.

---

### Gene Replacement Techniques

- **Gene replacement techniques**: replace one gene with another.
- **Transgenic mice**: mice containing **genetic material of another species** (e.g., a defective human gene).

> **KEY STUDY: Schizophrenia Transgenic Mice**
> - **Researcher:** Shen et al. (2008)
> - **Method:** Inserted a **defective human gene** associated with schizophrenia in a Scottish family (with unusually high incidence) into mice.
> - **Findings:** Transgenic mice showed:
>   - **Reduced cerebral cortex** and **enlarged ventricles** (cerebral abnormalities).
>   - Abnormal behaviors reminiscent of human schizophrenia.
> - **Conclusion:** Confirmed the defective gene was a **causal factor** in the familial Scottish schizophrenia.

**Additional technique:**
- Replace a gene with one identical except for added bases that act as a **switch** — gene turns on/off in response to specific chemicals.
- Allows activation/suppression of gene at a **particular developmental point**.
- Future goal: **gene therapy** for neurological disease (replacing faulty genes); as yet unrealized.

---

### Fantastic Fluorescence and the Brainbow

**Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP):**
- **Green fluorescent protein (GFP)**: protein exhibiting **bright green fluorescence** under **blue light**.
- First isolated by **Shimomura, Johnson, and Saiga (1962)** from a **jellyfish** found off the west coast of North America.
- **Nobel Prize in Chemistry 2008**: Martin Chalfie, Osamu Shimomura, and Roger Y. Tsien.
- GFP gene identified and **cloned in the early 1990s**.
- General strategy: activate GFP gene in **only the target cells** for visualization.
  - Either insert GFP gene only in target cells, OR
  - Introduce in all cells but express only in target cells.
- **First use in neurons:** Chalfie et al. (1994) — introduced GFP gene into transparent roundworm **Caenorhabditis elegans** in chromosomal area controlling **touch receptor neuron** development — neurons glowed green.

**Brainbow (Livet et al., 2007):**
- **Tsien (1998)**: minor alterations to GFP gene produce proteins fluorescing in **different colors**.
- **Livet et al. (2007)**: introduced mutated genes for **cyan, yellow, and blue** fluorescent proteins into genomes of developing mice; expressed in developing neurons.
- Each neuron produces **different amounts of the three proteins** = unique distinctive color per neuron (analogous to a color printer using three inks).
- Because each neuron has a unique color, **axonal pathways can be traced** through the cellular morass.
- Technique named **"brainbow"**.

  2. A method for **objectively measuring** it.

## 5.6 Neuropsychological Testing

- **Neurologist**: assesses simple sensory and motor functions.
- **Neuropsychologist**: assesses subtle perceptual, emotional, motivational, and cognitive functions.
- Neuropsychological testing is **time-consuming**; prescribed for only a small proportion of brain-damaged patients.

**Three benefits of neuropsychological testing for brain-damaged patients:**
1. **Assist in diagnosis** (especially when imaging, EEG, and neurological tests are equivocal).
2. Provides basis for **counseling and care**.
3. Provides basis for **evaluating treatment effectiveness** and seriousness of side effects.

---

### Three Phases of Neuropsychological Testing

| Phase | Era | Goal | Method | Limitation |
|-------|-----|------|--------|-----------|
| **Single-test approach** | Pre-1950s | Detect brain damage; differentiate structural vs. functional | One test | No single test sensitive to all varied brain damage symptoms |
| **Standardized-test-battery approach** | 1960s (predominant) | Identify brain-damaged patients | Standardized battery (e.g., Halstead-Reitan); aggregate score < cutoff = brain damage | Poor at distinguishing neurological from psychiatric patients |
| **Customized-test-battery approach** | From 1960s (now predominant) | Characterize the **nature** of each patient's deficits | Common battery for all + patient-specific tests | Requires more skill and knowledge from neuropsychologist |

**Halstead-Reitan Neuropsychological Test Battery:**
- Most widely used standardized battery.
- Tests that brain-damaged patients tend to perform poorly on.
- All scores added to form a single **aggregate score**.
- Score below a designated **cutoff** = diagnosis of brain damage.

**Customized-test-battery approach — how it works:**
1. Common battery for all patients (general symptoms).
2. Customized tests selected based on initial results (characterize specific deficits).
   - Example: if common battery reveals memory problem → specific short-term and long-term memory tests administered.

**Three ways customized tests differ from earlier tests:**
1. Designed to measure functions **spotlighted by modern theories** (e.g., short-term vs. long-term memory are distinct mechanisms — both must be tested).
2. Assessment of **cognitive strategy** employed, not just overall score (brain damage can change strategy without lowering the score).
3. Requires **more skill and knowledge** from the neuropsychologist to select the right battery and identify qualitative differences.

---

### Tests of the Common Neuropsychological Test Battery

#### Intelligence

- Overall **IQ is a notoriously poor measure** of brain damage — yet always included for context.
- **Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (WAIS)**:
  - First published: **1955**.
  - Standardized: **1981** on a sample of **1,880 U.S. citizens**, ages **16-71**.
  - Why administered first: IQ level helps interpret subsequent test results.
  - **11 original subtests**: Information, Comprehension, Arithmetic, Similarities, Digit Span, Vocabulary, Digit Symbol, Picture Completion, Block Design, Picture Arrangement, Object Assembly.
  - Later version: **15 subtests**.
  - Lateralization clue from WAIS:
    - **Low verbal subtest scores** → associated with **left hemisphere damage**.
    - **Low performance subtest scores** → associated with **right hemisphere damage**.

#### Memory

- WAIS weakness: often fails to detect memory deficits despite memory-specific subtests.
- **Information subtest**: memory for general knowledge (e.g., "Who is Queen Elizabeth?").
- **Digit span subtest**: most widely used test of **short-term memory**; identifies longest sequence of random digits correctly repeated 50% of the time.
  - Normal digit span: **7**.
- Both information and digit span are among the **least likely to be disrupted** by brain damage.
- Memory problems often reported by patient or family rather than detected by WAIS.

#### Language

- If WAIS taken: language deficits inferred from low aggregate **verbal subtest scores**.
- If WAIS not taken: **Token Test** for quick language screening.

**Token Test:**
- **20 tokens**: 2 shapes (squares, circles), 2 sizes (large, small), 5 colors (white, black, yellow, green, red).
- Placed on table in front of subject.
- Progression:
  1. Simple instructions: "Touch a red square."
  2. More complex: "Touch the small, red circle and then the large, green square."
  3. Subject reads instructions aloud and follows them.

#### Language Lateralization

- It is usual for one hemisphere to participate more in language-related activities (**language lateralization**).
- In **most people**: **left hemisphere is dominant** for language.
- In **some people**: **right hemisphere is dominant** (see Chapter 16).
- Tests of language lateralization determine which hemisphere is dominant in each patient.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **CT vs. MRI**: CT = X-ray based (computer-assisted), introduced early 1970s; MRI = hydrogen atoms excited by radio-frequency waves in magnetic field; MRI has higher spatial resolution and 3D capability.

2. **PET mechanism**: radioactive 2-DG injected into carotid artery; taken up by active neurons (like glucose) but not metabolized; accumulates; PET images radioactivity levels. First functional brain imaging technique.

3. **fMRI vs. PET — 4 advantages of fMRI**: (1) no injection, (2) both structural and functional, (3) better spatial resolution, (4) whole-brain 3D imaging.

4. **BOLD signal**: Blood-Oxygen-Level-Dependent signal; what fMRI actually measures. It is NOT a direct measure of neural activity; relationship is complex and variable. fMRI takes 2-3 seconds per image (too slow for millisecond neural events).

5. **TMS is the only method that can demonstrate causation**: PET, fMRI, MEG show correlation only. TMS temporarily disrupts a cortical area ("virtual lesion") and tests whether the disruption actually causes the behavioral change.

6. **MEG's key advantage**: superior temporal resolution (records fast changes in neural activity); spatial resolution is lower.

7. **P300 wave**: positive EEG deflection at ~300 ms; only appears if stimulus has meaning for the subject. Far-field potentials (first few ms) originate in brainstem; not influenced by stimulus meaning.

8. **Alpha waves**: 8-12 Hz, high amplitude; associated with **relaxed wakefulness** (not aroused, not asleep).

9. **Signal averaging**: cancels random noise by averaging many stimulus presentations; the consistent evoked potential survives; random EEG averages to zero.

10. **Kainic/ibotenic acid** destroys cell bodies, spares passing axons. **6-OHDA** selectively destroys norepinephrine and dopamine neurons. Cryogenic blockade = reversible lesion (no structural damage; also achievable with lidocaine).

11. **Bilateral > unilateral lesions**: behavioral effects of bilateral lesions are much more severe; most experimental studies use bilateral lesions.

12. **Gene knockout pattern**: knockout of a single gene typically **reduces but does not eliminate** a behavior; behavior is controlled by many genes interacting with experience (not single-gene control).

13. **GFP and Brainbow**: GFP isolated from jellyfish by Shimomura, Johnson, and Saiga (1962); Nobel 2008 (Chalfie, Shimomura, Tsien). Brainbow by Livet et al. (2007): three fluorescent proteins give each neuron a unique color for axon tracing.

14. **Three phases of neuropsychological testing**: (1) Single-test (pre-1950s; detect brain damage vs. functional), (2) Halstead-Reitan standardized battery (1960s; aggregate score), (3) Customized-test-battery (current; characterize nature of deficits).

15. **WAIS facts**: first published 1955, standardized 1981, 1,880 U.S. citizens aged 16-71, 11 original subtests. Low verbal scores = left hemisphere damage; low performance scores = right hemisphere damage. Digit span (normal = 7) rarely disrupted by brain damage.
