# Chapter 12: Hunger, Eating, and Health

**Overview:** Physiology and psychology of hunger and eating: digestion and energy metabolism, set-point vs. positive-incentive theories, the brain's role in feeding, GI satiety signals, hunger/satiety peptides, body weight regulation, and obesity. High-yield MCQ chapter with many named syndromes, researchers, and cases.

## 12.1 Digestion, Energy Storage, and Utilization

### The Gastrointestinal Tract
- **Digestion** = food broken down and absorbed. Sequence:
1. Chewing mixes food with **saliva** (lubricates, begins digestion).
2. Swallowing → **esophagus** → stomach.
3. **Stomach**: storage reservoir; **hydrochloric acid** breaks food into particles; **pepsin** starts protein → amino acids.
4. Empties via the **pyloric sphincter** into the **duodenum** (small intestine; primary absorption site).
5. Duodenal enzymes (from **gall bladder**, **pancreas**) break proteins → amino acids, starches → simple sugars; absorbed into blood → liver.
6. **Bile** (made in liver, stored in gall bladder, released into duodenum) emulsifies fats; emulsified fat enters the **lymphatic system**.
7. **Large intestine** absorbs remaining water/electrolytes; rest ejected.

### Energy Storage
- Digestion delivers three forms: **lipids** (fats), **amino acids** (from proteins), **glucose** (simple sugar from carbohydrates; main directly usable fuel).

| Form | Storage Site | % of reserves |
|------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **Fats** | Adipose tissue | ~85% |
| **Proteins** | Muscle | ~14.5% |
| **Glycogen** | Liver and muscles | ~0.5% |

- Adult body-weight changes = largely changes in stored **fat**.
- **Why fat over glycogen:** (1) fat stores ~**2× the energy per gram**; (2) **glycogen holds water** (all-glycogen storage → body weight likely >275 kg).

### Three Phases of Energy Metabolism
**Energy metabolism** = chemical changes making energy available.

| Phase | Definition | Hormones |
|-------|------------|--------------------|
| **Cephalic** | Preparatory; begins with sight/smell/thought of food | High insulin, low glucagon |
| **Absorptive** | Meal energy meets immediate needs | High insulin, low glucagon |
| **Fasting** | Body draws on reserves; ends with next cephalic phase | High glucagon, low insulin |

- During rapid weight gain, people may skip the fasting phase. Controlled by two pancreatic hormones: **insulin** and **glucagon**.

**Insulin** (cephalic/absorptive): promotes glucose use; converts fuels to storable forms (glucose→glycogen/fat; amino acids→proteins); stores them. Cephalic role: lower bloodborne fuels in anticipation; absorptive role: minimize fuel increase.

**Glucagon** (fasting): without high insulin, glucose is **spared for the brain** (brain doesn't need insulin for glucose uptake); promotes glycogen/protein → glucose (**gluconeogenesis** = protein→glucose); releases **free fatty acids** (primary fasting fuel); converts them to **ketones** (used by muscle, and by the **brain** in prolonged fasting).

## 12.2 Set Points versus Positive Incentives

### Set-Point Assumption
- **Set-point assumption** (Assanand, Pinel, & Lehman, 1998): hunger = energy deficit; eating restores resources to the **energy set point**; eating ends at **satiation** (thermostat analogy).

| Component | Function | Thermostat |
|-----------|----------|--------------------|
| **Set-point mechanism** | Defines the set point | Setting |
| **Detector mechanism** | Detects deviations | Thermometer |
| **Effector mechanism** | Eliminates deviations | Heater |

- All are **negative feedback systems** maintaining **homeostasis** (stable internal environment). With a plastic brain, there is no logical imperative for set-point regulation.

### Glucostatic and Lipostatic Theories (1940s-50s)
- **Glucostatic theory**: eating maintains a **blood glucose set point** (short-term regulation; meal initiation/termination).
- **Lipostatic theory**: eating maintains a **body-fat set point** (long-term regulation; supported by constancy of adult weight). The two were viewed as complementary.

### Problems with Set-Point Theories
1. **Obesity epidemic** shouldn't occur (de Castro & Plunkett, 2002).
2. **Evolutionary mismatch**: unpredictable ancestral food supply favored eating surpluses; evolution should **prevent** deficits, not just respond (Pinel, Assanand, & Lehman, 2000).
3. **Unconfirmed predictions**: natural glucose drops large enough to induce eating are rare; ~65% of US adults start meals with excess fat; high-calorie premeal drinks don't reliably cut meal size; **beliefs** about calories matter more than actual calories (Lowe, 1993).
4. **Ignores taste, learning, social influence** (e.g., eating dessert when full).

### Positive-Incentive Perspective
- **Positive-incentive theory** (Bolles, Booth, Collier, Rolls, Toates, 1980-81; Berridge, 2004): we are **drawn to eat by anticipated pleasure**, not driven by deficits.
- **Positive-incentive value** = anticipated pleasure of a behavior; eating is controlled like sexual behavior.
- **Factors influencing positive-incentive value**: flavor; learned effects; time since eating; gut contents; presence of others; blood glucose. Strength: many factors interact via their combined effect on positive-incentive value.

## 12.3 What, When, and How Much We Eat

### What We Eat
- **Species-typical preferences**: prefer **sweet/fatty** (energy/nutrient-rich) and **salty** (sodium); avoid **bitter** (toxins).
- **Learned preferences/aversions**: prefer tastes followed by calories; avoid tastes followed by illness; learn from **conspecifics** (rats prefer flavors from mother's milk and other rats' breath; Galef, 1995). **Galef & Wright (1995)**: group-reared rats more likely to learn a healthy diet.

| Nutrient | Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|
| **Sodium** | Deficiency → **immediate, compelling** preference (no learning; Rowland, 1990) |
| **Other vitamins/minerals** | Must be **learned** via positive effects (no detectable taste) |

> **KEY STUDY: Harris et al. (1933):** thiamine-deficient rats given 2 diets (1 complete) mostly learned the complete one; given **10 diets** (1 with thiamine), few learned. More options → harder to learn what's beneficial — explains dietary deficiencies in food-varied societies.

### When We Eat
- **Collier (1986)**: most mammals choose **many small meals** with continuous access; fewer/larger meals only when initiation is costly.
- Humans eat at culturally set times → most likely to feel hungry then; missing a meal can cause **malaise**.
- **Premeal hunger** (Woods, 1991): meals are **homeostasis-disturbing**; the **cephalic phase** releases insulin to soften the influx → **premeal pangs reflect cephalic preparation, not energy deficit**. Hunger is caused by **expectation of food**.

> **KEY STUDY: Weingarten (1983):** rats got 6 meals/day signaled by a **buzzer-and-light CS**. Later, with food always available, the CS triggered eating **even after a recent meal** in non-deprived rats — hunger from **expectation**, not deficit (strong Pavlovian support for positive-incentive theory).

### How Much We Eat
- **Satiety** = state causing eating to stop while food remains. Signals depend on **volume** and **nutritive density** (calories/volume).

> **KEY STUDY: Weingarten & Kulikovsky (1989) — Sham Eating:** food is chewed/swallowed but exits via an esophageal tube (no energy added). Rats sham-eating a **familiar** diet ate normal-sized first meals (increasing on later days); those sham-eating an **unfamiliar** diet ate **large amounts immediately**. Meal size depends on **prior experience** with a food's effects, not immediate gut/blood signals. (Set-point would predict all huge meals.)

- **Appetizer effect**: small pre-meal food **increases** hunger (effective at eliciting cephalic responses).
- **Serving size**: larger servings → more eaten (Geier, Rozin, & Doros, 2006).
- **Social**: **Redd & de Castro (1992)** — people ate **60% more** with others; social factors can also reduce intake (slenderness ideals); females more influenced.

> **KEY STUDY: Rogers & Blundell (1980) — Cafeteria Diet:** a **cafeteria diet** (varied palatable foods) raised rat caloric intake **84%** and body weight **49%** at 120 days — contradicts rigid energy set points.

- **Sensory-specific satiety**: while eating one food, all foods' positive-incentive value declines slightly, but the **eaten food's value plummets**.

> **KEY STUDY: Rolls et al. (1981):** subjects rated 8 foods, ate one, re-rated; the **eaten food's** rating dropped most; they ate a second meal **unless it was the same food**.

> **KEY STUDY: Booth (1981):** after a large meal — (1) **immediate** sensory-specific palatability drop for similar-flavor foods (taste receptors); (2) **~30 min later** general drop for **all** foods (postingestive consequences).

- **Rolls (1990)**: **brief** effects (within a meal) and **enduring** effects (meal to meal); rice, bread, potatoes, sweets, salads resist long-term sensory-specific satiety.
- **Two adaptive consequences**: (1) encourages a **varied diet**; (2) encourages eating a lot when food is plentiful (Raynor & Epstein, 2001).

## 12.4 Physiological Research on Hunger and Satiety

### Blood Glucose
- Direct links between blood glucose and eating have largely failed.

> **KEY STUDY: Campfield & Smith (1990):** in free-feeding rats, blood glucose dropped ~**8%** about **10 minutes before a meal**, preceded by an **insulin rise** — an actively produced cephalic-phase response, not a declining-reserve signal.

**Five reasons the premeal glucose drop does NOT support glucostatic theory:**
1. Drops don't always precede eating (e.g., serving palatable food unexpectedly; Strubbe & Steffens, 1977).
2. The drop is **preceded by an insulin rise** (anticipatory).
3. If no meal is served, glucose returns to baseline.
4. Glucose in CNS extracellular fluid stays constant even when blood glucose drops (Seeley & Woods, 2003).
5. Insulin injections induce eating only with ~**50%** glucose reduction (Rowland, 1981); large glucose infusions don't suppress eating (Geiselman, 1987).

### Myth of Hypothalamic Hunger/Satiety Centers
- **Old theory (1950s)**: **VMH (ventromedial hypothalamus)** = satiety center; **LH (lateral hypothalamus)** = feeding center. Incorrect but productive.

**VMH:**
> **Hetherington & Ranson (1940):** bilateral VMH lesions → **hyperphagia** and extreme obesity (**VMH syndrome**).

| Phase | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| **Dynamic phase** | Immediate; grossly excessive eating, rapid gain; weeks |
| **Static phase** | Consumption stabilizes; obese weight **defended** |

- **Paradox**: VMH-lesioned rats eat more of palatable food but are **less willing to work** for it (Teitelbaum, 1957) or eat slightly unpalatable food (Miller, Bailey, & Stevenson, 1950). **Weingarten, Chang, & Jarvie (1983)**: their finickiness is a **consequence of obesity**, not the lesion.

**LH:**
> **Anand & Brobeck (1951):** bilateral LH lesions → **aphagia** (even reversed VMH hyperphagia).

> **Teitelbaum & Epstein (1962):** aphagia accompanied by **adipsia** (no drinking); partial recovery with tube feeding (first wet palatable foods, then dry pellets if water available).

**Reinterpretation:**
- **VMH is metabolic, not a satiety center:** lesions raise insulin → ↑**lipogenesis**, ↓**lipolysis** (Powley et al., 1980); rats overeat **because they become obese** (calories shunted to fat). Also, large lesions damage the **ventral noradrenergic bundle** and **paraventricular nuclei** fibers; lesions of either alone produce hyperphagia/obesity (Gold et al., 1977; Leibowitz, Hammer, & Chang, 1981).
- **LH is not a feeding center:** lesions cause broad motor disturbances and general unresponsiveness to **all** sensory input.

### Role of the GI Tract in Satiety
> **Cannon & Washburn (1912):** Washburn swallowed a balloon; hunger pangs coincided with stomach contractions → theory: hunger = contractions, satiety = distension. Undermined by stomach-removal patients who still felt normal hunger/satiety.

> **KEY STUDY: Koopmans (1981):** an extra stomach/intestine transplanted (blood-connected, no nerves, no absorption). Food in it decreased eating in proportion to **caloric content and volume** → the satiety signal is **bloodborne** and a **chemical released from the stomach** (not neural, not an absorbed nutrient).

### Hunger and Satiety Peptides
- **Peptides** = short amino acid chains acting as hormones/neurotransmitters; food triggers GI release.

> **KEY STUDY: Gibbs, Young, & Smith (1973):** injecting the gut peptide **cholecystokinin (CCK)** → rats ate **smaller meals** → circulating gut peptides signal satiety.

- **Satiety peptides** (decrease appetite): **CCK** (first; causes aversion/nausea at high doses), **bombesin**, **glucagon**, **alpha-MSH**, **somatostatin** (a dozen+).
- **Hunger peptides** (increase appetite; made mainly in the brain/hypothalamus): **neuropeptide Y**, **galanin**, **orexin-A**, **ghrelin**.
- **Impacts**: (1) the system reacts to **many signals**; (2) many peptide receptors are in the **hypothalamus** (renewed interest); (3) hypothalamic circuits are **only one part** of a larger system (Berthoud & Morrison, 2008).

### Serotonin and Satiety
- **Serotonin** (monoamine) plays a role in satiety. Three properties (Blundell & Halford, 1998): (1) **resists cafeteria diets**; (2) reduces **amount per meal** (not meal number); (3) shifts preference **away from fat**.
- **Serotonin agonists** (fenfluramine, dexfenfluramine, fluoxetine) reduce hunger/eating/weight.

### Prader-Willi Syndrome
- **Prader-Willi syndrome**: from deletion/disruption of **paternal chromosome 15** (Goldstone, 2004).
- **Core features**: **insatiable hunger** (hyperphagia), **little/no satiety**, **slow metabolism** (acts perpetually starving).
- **Other features**: **hypotonia** (weak muscles, esp. infancy), small hands/feet, infant feeding difficulty, tantrums, compulsivity, skin picking, moderate intellectual disability, psychiatric problems.
- **Prognosis (untreated)**: extreme obesity; early death (diabetes, heart disease); some die from gorging.
- **Case of Miss A. (Martin et al., 1998)**: tube-fed infant (weak sucking); age 3.5 sudden **voracious appetite**; managed by locked-away food and a strict diet; tantrums triggered by **any environmental change**.

## 12.5 Body Weight: Set Points versus Settling Points

### Set-Point Assumptions
- Folk belief: a body-fat set point pulls weight back via hunger changes (Assanand et al., 1998).

**Three challenges:**

#### 1. Variability of Body Weight
- Adults show **large, lasting weight changes** (Booth, 2004); the **obesity epidemic** refutes rigid regulation (Rosenheck, 2008); many avoid obesity only by **resisting** urges to eat.

#### 2. Ad Libitum Eating is Unhealthy

> **KEY STUDY: Okinawa (Kagawa, 1978):** Okinawans ate ~**20% fewer calories** (school children **38%** fewer); much lower morbidity/mortality and all aging-related diseases. Death rates vs. rest of Japan: **stroke 59%, cancer 69%, heart disease 59%**; up to **40× more** centenarians. (Correlational.)

> **KEY STUDY: Weindruch et al. (1986):** mice with **25%, 55%, or 65%** caloric restriction lived longer and healthier; the **65%** group lived **67% longer**, with lowest cancer and best immunity. Benefits may begin even later in life.

- **Nuance**: benefits may not be entirely from **fat loss** — **excess energy consumption itself** may accelerate aging (Lane, Ingram, & Roth, 2002). Calorie restriction also reduces epileptic seizures (Maalouf et al., 2008) and improves elderly memory (Witte et al., 2009).

#### 3. Energy Utilization Efficiency
- The body controls fat partly by **changing energy efficiency**: as fat **declines**, energy use becomes more **efficient** (limits loss); as fat **increases**, efficiency **decreases** (limits gain). **Rothwell & Stock (1982)**: obese cafeteria-diet rats had resting expenditure **45% greater**. Explains why diets plateau.
- **Diet-induced thermogenesis**: efficiency adjustment via body temperature (more fat → higher temperature → more energy used; Lazar, 2008).
- **Basal metabolic rate (BMR)**: resting energy use; large individual differences. Slim heavy-eaters may still pay a health cost.

### Settling-Point Model
- Critics (Booth, Fuller, & Lewis, 1981; Wirtshafter & Davis, 1977): **settling point** = the **natural equilibrium** of factors affecting weight — not a defended target. Provides **loose homeostatic regulation** without a set point.

| Feature | Set-Point | Settling-Point |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Mechanism | Set point + detector + effector | Equilibrium of interacting factors |
| Response to deviation | Returns to set point | Limits further change |
| Stability | Rigidly defended | Stable while factors are stable |
| Flexibility | Low | High |
| Analogy | Thermostat | Leaky barrel |

- **Leaky-barrel**: water (fat) level = input (eating) vs. leakage (energy use); no target. A neuron's **resting potential** is a biological settling point.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Fat vs. glycogen**: fat stores ~2× energy/gram and holds no water (glycogen holds water). Fat = 85%, glycogen = 0.5% of reserves.

2. **Three metabolic phases**: cephalic + absorptive = high insulin/low glucagon; fasting = high glucagon/low insulin. Brain gets glucose without insulin in fasting.

3. **Gluconeogenesis** = protein → glucose; fasting phase.

4. **VMH lesions (Hetherington & Ranson, 1940)** → hyperphagia/obesity (dynamic + static phases; weight defended); paradoxically won't work for food or eat unpalatable food.

5. **VMH syndrome is metabolic**: lesions raise insulin → ↑lipogenesis, ↓lipolysis → overeat **because** obese; also from ventral noradrenergic bundle and paraventricular nuclei damage.

6. **LH lesions (Anand & Brobeck, 1951)** → aphagia + adipsia (Teitelbaum & Epstein, 1962); general unresponsiveness, not feeding-specific.

7. **CCK** = first gut peptide shown to reduce meal size (Gibbs et al., 1973); aversion at high doses.

8. **Hunger peptides** = neuropeptide Y, galanin, orexin-A, ghrelin (brain). **Satiety peptides** = CCK, bombesin, glucagon, alpha-MSH, somatostatin (GI).

9. **Prader-Willi**: paternal chromosome 15 deletion; insatiable hunger, no satiety, slow metabolism, hypotonia; fatal if untreated.

10. **Sensory-specific satiety**: eaten food's value plummets, others decline slightly; encourages variety + maximum intake in abundance; brief vs. enduring effects (Rolls, 1990).

11. **Positive-incentive vs. set-point**: incentive = anticipated pleasure; explains appetizer effect, conditioned hunger, sensory-specific satiety, cafeteria diet, social eating.

12. **Weingarten (1983)**: buzzer/light CS triggered eating in non-deprived, recently fed rats — hunger from expectation.

13. **Serotonin**: reduces meal **size** (not frequency), resists cafeteria diets, shifts from fat; agonists reduce weight.

14. **Calorie restriction**: 25-65% restriction improves health/longevity (Weindruch et al., 1986: 65% → +67% lifespan); benefits may not depend on fat loss.

15. **Settling-point vs. set-point**: settling point = equilibrium of factors (leaky barrel), feedback limits change; set point = active return to a fixed target (thermostat). Settling-point fits the evidence better.
