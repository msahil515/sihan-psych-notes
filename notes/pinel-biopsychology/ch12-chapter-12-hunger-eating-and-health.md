# Chapter 12: Hunger, Eating, and Health

**Overview:** This chapter covers the physiology and psychology of hunger and eating, from digestion and energy metabolism through competing theories (set-point vs. positive-incentive), the brain's role in feeding behavior, gastrointestinal satiety signals, hunger/satiety peptides, body weight regulation, and the neuroscience of obesity. It is a high-yield MCQ chapter for biopsychology entrance exams, with numerous named syndromes, researchers, experiments, and clinical cases.

## 12.1 Digestion, Energy Storage, and Energy Utilization

### The Gastrointestinal Tract

- The gut is conceptually a tube with two holes: food enters via the mouth (with teeth) and waste exits the anus.
- **Digestion** = the process by which food is broken down and absorbed; strictly speaking, food has not been consumed until it has been digested.

**Steps in Digestion (sequence — high MCQ target):**

1. Chewing breaks up food and mixes it with **saliva** (lubricates food, begins digestion).
2. Swallowing moves food down the **esophagus** to the stomach.
3. The **stomach** serves primarily as a storage reservoir; **hydrochloric acid** breaks food into small particles; **pepsin** begins breaking proteins into amino acids.
4. The stomach empties through the **pyloric sphincter** into the **duodenum** (upper portion of the small intestine — the primary site of nutrient absorption).
5. Digestive enzymes in the duodenum (many from the **gall bladder** and **pancreas**) break proteins to amino acids and starches/complex sugars to simple sugars; these pass through the duodenum wall into the bloodstream, carried to the liver.
6. **Bile** (manufactured in the liver, stored in the gall bladder, released into the duodenum) emulsifies fats (breaks them into droplets); emulsified fat cannot pass through the duodenum wall and is carried by small ducts into the **lymphatic system**.
7. Most remaining water and electrolytes are absorbed in the **large intestine**; remainder ejected from the anus.

### Energy Storage in the Body

- Digestion delivers energy in three forms:
  1. **Lipids** (fats)
  2. **Amino acids** (breakdown products of proteins)
  3. **Glucose** (a simple sugar; breakdown product of complex carbohydrates — starches and sugars; the body's main directly utilizable energy source)

- Energy stored in three forms:

| Form | Primary Storage Site | % of Body Energy Reserves |
|------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **Fats** | Adipose tissue | ~85% |
| **Proteins** | Muscle | ~14.5% |
| **Glycogen** | Liver and muscles | ~0.5% |

- **Changes in adult body weight are largely a consequence of changes in stored body fat.**

**Why fat is the primary energy storage form (not glycogen):**
1. 1 gram of fat stores almost **twice as much energy** as 1 gram of glycogen.
2. **Glycogen attracts and holds substantial quantities of water** — storing all fat calories as glycogen would result in body weight likely exceeding 275 kg (600 lbs).

### Three Phases of Energy Metabolism

**Energy metabolism** = the chemical changes by which energy is made available for an organism's use.

| Phase | Definition | Dominant Hormones |
|-------|------------|--------------------|
| **Cephalic phase** | Preparatory phase; begins with sight, smell, or thought of food; ends when food starts to be absorbed | High insulin, low glucagon |
| **Absorptive phase** | Energy absorbed from the meal meets the body's immediate energy needs | High insulin, low glucagon |
| **Fasting phase** | Unstored energy from previous meal is exhausted; body withdraws energy from reserves; ends with next cephalic phase | High glucagon, low insulin |

- During rapid weight gain, people often go directly from one absorptive phase into the next cephalic phase, **skipping the fasting phase entirely**.
- Flow of energy during the three phases is controlled by two pancreatic hormones: **insulin** and **glucagon**.

#### Insulin (cephalic and absorptive phases — high insulin, low glucagon)

1. Promotes use of **glucose** as the primary energy source.
2. Promotes conversion of bloodborne fuels to storable forms: glucose → glycogen and fat; amino acids → proteins.
3. Promotes storage of glycogen in liver/muscle, fat in adipose tissue, proteins in muscle.
- Cephalic phase function: **lower bloodborne fuels in anticipation** of the impending influx.
- Absorptive phase function: **minimize increasing fuel levels** by utilizing and storing them.

#### Glucagon (fasting phase — high glucagon, low insulin)

- Without high insulin, glucose has difficulty entering most body cells → glucose is **spared for the brain** (insulin not required for glucose to enter most brain cells).
- Low insulin promotes conversion of glycogen and protein to glucose.
- **Gluconeogenesis** = conversion of protein to glucose; occurs in the fasting phase.
- High glucagon: promotes release of **free fatty acids** from adipose tissue → the body's primary fuel in fasting.
- High glucagon: stimulates conversion of free fatty acids to **ketones** → used by muscles as energy during fasting.
- After prolonged fasting: the **brain begins to use ketones** as well, further conserving glucose.

## 12.2 Theories of Hunger and Eating: Set Points versus Positive Incentives

### The Set-Point Assumption

- **Set-point assumption** (Assanand, Pinel, & Lehman, 1998a, 1998b): hunger is attributed to an energy deficit; eating returns energy resources to their optimal level — the **energy set point**.
- After a meal, energy resources are near set point; they decline as the body uses energy; when they fall far enough below the set point, **hunger** motivates eating; the meal continues until the energy level returns to the set point (**satiation**).
- Model analogy: a thermostat-regulated heating system.

**Three components of all set-point systems:**

| Component | Function | Thermostat Analogy |
|-----------|----------|--------------------|
| **Set-point mechanism** | Defines the set point | Thermostat setting |
| **Detector mechanism** | Detects deviations from the set point | Thermometer |
| **Effector mechanism** | Acts to eliminate deviations | Heater |

- All set-point systems are **negative feedback systems**: feedback from changes in one direction elicits compensatory effects in the opposite direction.
- **Homeostasis** = a stable internal environment; critical for mammalian survival (Wenning, 1999).
- Set-point systems combine negative feedback with a fixed set point to keep internal environment at the prescribed level.
- Note: set-point regulation seemed necessary when the adult brain was assumed to be immutable; since we now know the brain is plastic, there is no longer a logical imperative for set-point regulation of eating.

### Glucostatic and Lipostatic Set-Point Theories (1940s–1950s)

#### Glucostatic Theory
- **Glucostatic theory** = eating is regulated by a system designed to maintain a **blood glucose set point**.
- Become hungry when blood glucose falls significantly below the set point; become satiated when eating returns glucose to the set point.
- Rationale: glucose is the brain's primary fuel.
- Various versions proposed by multiple researchers in the 1940s–1950s.

#### Lipostatic Theory
- **Lipostatic theory** = each person has a **set point for body fat**; deviations produce compensatory adjustments in eating to return fat to the set point.
- Most cited support: the relative constancy of adult body weight.
- Various forms proposed in the 1940s–1950s.

**Integrated view (dominant in 1950s):**
- Glucostatic = **short-term** regulation (meal initiation and termination).
- Lipostatic = **long-term** regulation.
- The two were considered complementary, not mutually exclusive.

### Problems with Set-Point Theories of Hunger and Eating

1. **The obesity epidemic**: should not occur if eating were regulated by a set point. (de Castro & Plunkett, 2002)

2. **Inconsistency with evolutionary pressures**: ancestors faced inconsistent, unpredictable food supplies; survival required eating large quantities when available (banking calories as body fat). Any ancestor whose hunger stopped when immediate energy needs were met would not have survived prolonged food scarcity. Evolution should favor a system that **prevents** energy deficits, not merely responds to them (Pinel, Assanand, & Lehman, 2000).

3. **Major predictions unconfirmed**:
   - Blood glucose reductions large enough to reliably induce eating rarely occur naturally.
   - ~65% of U.S. adults have a significant excess of fat deposits when they begin a meal.
   - High-calorie premeal drinks do not reliably reduce subsequent meal size.
   - Beliefs about caloric content of a premeal drink often influence meal size **more** than actual caloric content (Lowe, 1993).

4. **Failure to account for taste, learning, and social influences**: imagining the sight/smell/taste of preferred food can make someone hungry, even without any energy deficit; people commonly eat a full dessert after a large main course despite feeling full.

### Positive-Incentive Perspective

- **Positive-incentive theory** (Bolles, 1980; Booth, 1981; Collier, 1980; Rolls, 1981; Toates, 1981; reviewed by Berridge, 2004): humans and other animals are not normally driven to eat by internal energy deficits but are **drawn to eat by the anticipated pleasure of eating**.
- **Positive-incentive value** = the anticipated pleasure associated with a behavior.
- Central tenet: eating is controlled in the same way as sexual behavior — not driven by deficit, but by evolved craving and the presence/anticipation of good food.
- Multiple different positive-incentive theories exist; Pinel refers collectively to them as the **positive-incentive perspective**.

**Factors that influence the positive-incentive value of eating (thus determining hunger):**
- Flavor of the food likely to be consumed
- What has been learned about the food's effects (from past experience or from others)
- Amount of time since last eating
- Type and quantity of food currently in the gut
- Whether others are present and eating
- Whether blood glucose levels are within the normal range

**Strength of positive-incentive perspective**: unlike set-point theories, it acknowledges that many factors interact to determine hunger at any time, and suggests this interaction occurs through their combined influence on positive-incentive value (Cabanac, 1971; Palmiter, 2007).

## 12.3 Factors That Determine What, When, and How Much We Eat

### Factors That Determine What We Eat

**Species-typical human taste preferences:**
- **Sweet** and **fatty** tastes: preferred (adaptive — naturally indicate high-energy, vitamin/mineral-rich foods)
- **Salty** tastes: preferred (sodium-rich foods)
- **Bitter** tastes: aversive (often associated with toxins)
- These are superimposed upon by individual learned taste preferences and aversions.

**Learned Taste Preferences and Aversions:**
- Animals learn to **prefer** tastes followed by caloric infusion (Baker & Booth, 1989; Lucas & Sclafani, 1989; Sclafani, 1990).
- Animals learn to **avoid** tastes followed by illness.
- Humans and animals learn from **conspecifics** (members of the same species):
  - Rats learn to prefer flavors experienced in **mother's milk** and smelled on the **breath of other rats** (Galef, 1995, 1996; Galef, Whishkin, & Bielavska, 1997).
  - Many human food preferences are **culturally specific**.
  - **Galef & Wright (1995)**: rats reared in **groups** (vs. isolation) are more likely to learn to eat a healthy diet.

**Learning to Eat Vitamins and Minerals — Two Mechanisms:**

| Nutrient | Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|
| **Sodium** | Deficiency produces an **immediate, compelling preference** for the taste of sodium salt (no learning required) (Rowland, 1990) |
| **All other vitamins/minerals** | Must be **learned** by experiencing positive effects; these nutrients normally have no detectable taste; animals develop aversion to the deficient diet and preference for the complete diet over days |

- Example (thiamine/Vitamin B1): Thiamine-deficient rats develop aversion to their deficient diet; when offered two new diets (one with, one without thiamine), they learn to prefer the thiamine-rich diet as it becomes associated with improved health.

> **KEY STUDY: Harris et al. (1933) — Thiamine Choice with Multiple Options**
> - **Method**: Thiamine-deficient rats offered 2 new diets (one complete, one deficient): nearly all learned to eat the complete diet.
> - Same rats offered **10 new diets** (only one containing thiamine): few developed a preference for the correct diet.
> - **Conclusion**: The more dietary options available, the harder for the body to learn which foods are beneficial — explaining why dietary deficiencies are prevalent in industrialized societies with vast food variety.

**Why dietary deficiencies persist in industrialized societies:**
1. Manufacturers produce foods with preferred tastes but lacking many needed nutrients.
2. The large number of substances consumed daily makes it difficult to learn which foods are beneficial (Harris et al., 1933 principle).

### Factors That Influence When We Eat

- **Collier (1986)**: most mammals choose to eat **many small meals (snacks)** each day when they have ready access to continuous food supply. They opt for fewer large meals only when there are physical costs to meal initiation (e.g., traveling far to reach food).
- Humans tend to eat a few large meals at regular times — influenced by cultural norms, work schedules, family routines, personal preferences, wealth.
- Each person's regular mealtimes are the times when they are **most likely to feel hungry**; missing a scheduled meal can cause **malaise** (headache, nausea, inability to concentrate).

**Premeal Hunger (Woods, 1991; Woods & Ramsay, 2000; Woods & Strubbe, 1994):**
- Eating meals is **homeostasis-disturbing**: it causes a large influx of fuels into the bloodstream.
- Before a meal, when the usual mealtime approaches, the body enters the **cephalic phase**: releases insulin → reduces blood glucose (softening the impact of the upcoming influx).
- **Premeal hunger pangs = sensations of the body's cephalic-phase preparation for the expected meal, not cries for energy.**
- Mealtime hunger is caused by the **expectation of food**, not by an energy deficit.
- Evidence: the author experienced noontime hunger when eating at 12:05 daily, then lost those pangs when his schedule changed — hunger shifted to the new mealtime.

> **KEY STUDY: Weingarten (1983, 1984, 1985) — Pavlovian Conditioning of Hunger**
> - **Method**: Rats given 6 meals/day at irregular intervals; each meal was signaled by a **buzzer-and-light conditional stimulus (CS)**. Conditioning: 11 days. Test phase: food continuously available (no deprivation).
> - **Finding**: Rats began eating each time the buzzer and light were presented — **even if they had recently completed a meal** and were never food-deprived.
> - **Conclusion**: Hunger is often caused by the **expectation of food**, not by an energy deficit. Provides strong Pavlovian support for the positive-incentive perspective.

### Factors That Influence How Much We Eat

**Satiety** = the motivational state that causes a person to stop eating when food is still available.

**Satiety Signals:**
- Depend on both **volume** and **nutritive density** (calories per unit volume) of food.
- Food in the gut and glucose entering the blood can induce satiety signals that inhibit further consumption.
- Rats can learn to adjust volume consumed to maintain caloric intake, but rarely compensate fully if nutritive density is reduced by >50% or palatability changes drastically.

> **KEY STUDY: Weingarten & Kulikovsky (1989) — Sham Eating**
> - **Sham eating** = food is chewed and swallowed but exits via an implanted esophageal tube rather than reaching the stomach (adds no energy to the body).
> - **Method**: Two groups of rats: one sham-ate a **familiar** diet; one sham-ate an **unfamiliar** diet.
> - **Finding**:
>   - Familiar-food group: first sham meal = same size as normal meals; sham meal size **increased on subsequent days**.
>   - Unfamiliar-food group: sham ate **large quantities from the very start**.
> - **Conclusion**: The amount eaten is influenced largely by **previous experience with the food's physiological effects**, not by the food's immediate effect on the body. Gut/blood satiety signals are not necessary to terminate a meal initially.
> - **Set-point prediction** (not confirmed): all sham-eaten meals should be huge (since no energy is added to the body).

**Appetizer Effect:**
- **Appetizer effect** = small amounts of food consumed before a meal **increase** hunger rather than reducing it.
- Mechanism: small food amounts are particularly effective at eliciting cephalic-phase responses.

**Serving Size:**
- Larger serving sizes → greater consumption (Geier, Rozin, & Doros, 2006). Even larger spoons lead to greater eating.

**Social Influences on Satiety:**
- **Redd & de Castro (1992)**: subjects consumed **60% more** when eating with others than alone.
- Laboratory rats also eat substantially more when fed in groups.
- Social factors can also **reduce** consumption: conforming to cultural ideals of slenderness; avoiding appearing gluttonous.
- Females are more strongly influenced by these social pressures than males.

> **KEY STUDY: Rogers & Blundell (1980) — Cafeteria Diet**
> - **Cafeteria diet** = varied diet of highly palatable foods.
> - Adult rats offered bread and chocolate in addition to normal lab diet: increased caloric intake by **84%**; after 120 days, body weight increased by **49%**.
> - Directly contradicts the idea that satiety is rigidly controlled by internal energy set points.

**Sensory-Specific Satiety:**
- **Sensory-specific satiety** = as you eat one food, the positive-incentive value of all foods declines slightly, but the positive-incentive value of **that particular food plummets**. Satiety is to a large degree specific to the sensory properties of the food consumed.

> **KEY STUDY: Rolls et al. (1981) — Sensory-Specific Satiety in Humans**
> - **Method**: Subjects rated palatability of 8 different foods, ate a meal of one, then re-rated all 8.
> - **Finding**: Rating of the **eaten food** declined substantially more than ratings of the other seven foods. When offered an unexpected second meal, subjects ate most of it — **unless it was the same food** as the first meal.

> **KEY STUDY: Booth (1981) — Time Course of Sensory-Specific Satiety**
> - **Method**: Subjects rated pleasure from flavor/smell/sight/thought of various foods at different times after consuming a large, high-calorie, high-carbohydrate liquid meal.
> - **Finding**:
>   1. **Immediate**: sensory-specific decrease in palatability of foods with similar flavor (from taste receptor signals).
>   2. **~30 minutes later**: general decrease in palatability of **all substances** (from postingestive consequences of eating).

**Rolls (1990, 1986) — Two types of sensory-specific satiety effects:**
1. **Brief effects**: influence selection of foods **within a single meal**.
2. **Enduring effects**: influence selection of foods **from meal to meal**.
- Some foods are relatively immune to long-lasting sensory-specific satiety: **rice, bread, potatoes, sweets, green salads** (can be eaten almost every day with little decline in palatability).

**Two adaptive consequences of sensory-specific satiety:**
1. **Encourages a varied diet** (prevents malnutrition from eating only one preferred food).
2. **Encourages eating a lot when food is plentiful** — an animal sated on one food will eat again when encountering a different food; helps maximize intake during times of abundance (Raynor & Epstein, 2001).

## 12.4 Physiological Research on Hunger and Satiety

### Role of Blood Glucose Levels in Hunger and Satiety

- Efforts to directly link blood glucose to eating have been largely unsuccessful.
- Renewed interest in the 1990s with continuous blood glucose monitoring technology.

> **KEY STUDY: Campfield & Smith (1990) — Premeal Blood Glucose Dynamics**
> - **Method**: Rats with free access to food and water; blood glucose continuously monitored via chronic intravenous catheter.
> - **Baseline**: blood glucose rarely fluctuated more than 2%.
> - **Finding**: About **10 minutes before a meal**, glucose suddenly dropped about **8%** — this drop was preceded by an **increase in blood insulin levels**.
> - **Interpretation**: The premeal glucose drop is actively produced by rising insulin (the body's anticipatory cephalic-phase response), not a reflection of gradually declining energy reserves.

**Five reasons the premeal blood glucose drop does NOT support the glucostatic theory:**
1. It is easy to construct situations where blood glucose drops do NOT precede eating (e.g., unexpectedly serving highly palatable food — Strubbe & Steffens, 1977).
2. The premeal glucose drop is typically **preceded by an insulin rise** — indicating the body anticipates the meal and initiates the drop, rather than a deficit causing the hunger.
3. If an expected meal is not served, blood glucose levels **return to their previous homeostatic level**.
4. Glucose in the **extracellular fluids surrounding CNS neurons** stays relatively constant even when blood glucose drops (Seeley & Woods, 2003).
5. Insulin injections do not reliably induce eating unless they reduce blood glucose by **50%** (Rowland, 1981); large premeal glucose infusions do **not suppress eating** (Geiselman, 1987).

### Myth of Hypothalamic Hunger and Satiety Centers

**Old theory (1950s):** eating behavior controlled by two hypothalamic regions.
- **Ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH)** = satiety center
- **Lateral hypothalamus (LH)** = feeding center

This theory proved **incorrect** but stimulated important discoveries.

#### VMH and the VMH Syndrome

> **KEY STUDY: Hetherington & Ranson (1940) — VMH Lesions**
> - **Method**: Large bilateral electrolytic lesions to the ventromedial hypothalamus in rats.
> - **Finding**: Produced **hyperphagia** (excessive eating) and extreme obesity — the **VMH syndrome**.

**VMH Syndrome — Two Phases:**

| Phase | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| **Dynamic phase** | Begins immediately post-surgery; grossly excessive eating; rapid weight gain; lasts several weeks |
| **Static phase** | Consumption gradually declines to a level that maintains stable obesity; the animal **defends this elevated weight** (loses excess if force-fed, regains lost weight after deprivation) |

**Paradox of VMH-lesioned rats:**
- Eat far more than normal when palatable food is freely available.
- Yet less willing to **work** for food (Teitelbaum, 1957).
- Less willing to eat **slightly unpalatable food** (Miller, Bailey, & Stevenson, 1950).
- **Weingarten, Chang, & Jarvie (1983)**: finicky eating of VMH-lesioned rats is a **consequence of their obesity**, not a primary effect of the lesion — unlesioned rats of equal obesity show the same finickiness.

#### LH and the LH Syndrome

> **KEY STUDY: Anand & Brobeck (1951) — LH Lesions**
> - **Method**: Bilateral electrolytic lesions to the lateral hypothalamus.
> - **Finding**: Produced **aphagia** (complete cessation of eating). Even VMH-lesioned hyperphagic rats became aphagic when LH lesions were added.
> - **Conclusion (later disproven)**: LH = feeding center.

> **KEY STUDY: Teitelbaum & Epstein (1962) — LH Syndrome Features**
> - **Findings**:
>   1. Aphagia was accompanied by **adipsia** (complete cessation of drinking).
>   2. LH-lesioned rats show **partial recovery** if kept alive by tube feeding: first eat wet, palatable foods (e.g., chocolate chip cookies soaked in milk); eventually eat dry pellets if water is concurrently available.

#### Reinterpretation of VMH and LH Lesion Effects

**Against the VMH as a satiety center — two lines of evidence:**

1. **VMH's primary role is in energy metabolism, not eating per se:**
   - VMH lesions increase blood insulin levels → increased **lipogenesis** (fat production) + decreased **lipolysis** (fat breakdown) (Powley et al., 1980).
   - Calories ingested by VMH-lesioned rats are converted to fat at a high rate → they must keep eating to ensure enough calories in blood to meet immediate needs.
   - They overeat **because they become obese** (not obese because they overeat).
   - Analogy: "like misers who run to the bank each time they make a bit of money and deposit it in a savings account from which withdrawals cannot be made."

2. **Many VMH effects are due to collateral damage to nearby structures:**
   - The **ventral noradrenergic bundle** courses past the VMH and is damaged by large electrolytic lesions.
   - Fibers projecting from the **paraventricular nuclei** of the hypothalamus are also damaged.
   - Bilateral lesions of the **noradrenergic bundle** (Gold et al., 1977) or the **paraventricular nuclei** (Leibowitz, Hammer, & Chang, 1981) alone produce hyperphagia and obesity — just like VMH lesions.

**Against the LH as a feeding center:**
- LH lesions produce a **wide range of severe motor disturbances** and a **general lack of responsiveness to all sensory input** (not just food and drink).
- The LH is not a center specifically dedicated to feeding.

### Role of the Gastrointestinal Tract in Satiety

> **KEY STUDY: Cannon & Washburn (1912) — Stomach Contractions and Hunger**
> - **Method**: Washburn swallowed an empty balloon on a tube; Cannon pumped air in and connected tube to a water-filled glass U-tube to record contractions.
> - **Finding**: Washburn reported a pang of hunger each time a large stomach contraction was recorded.
> - **Theory derived**: Hunger = feeling of stomach contractions (empty stomach); satiety = feeling of stomach distension.
> - **Undermined by**: Human patients who had their stomachs surgically removed (esophagus connected directly to duodenum) still reported normal hunger and satiety, and maintained normal body weight by eating more meals of smaller size.

> **KEY STUDY: Koopmans (1981) — Transplanted Stomach**
> - **Method**: An extra stomach and length of intestine transplanted into rats; major blood vessels of the implant connected to the recipient's circulatory system. Food injected into transplanted stomach and kept there by a noose around the pyloric sphincter (no neural connections, no nutrient absorption from stomach).
> - **Finding**: Food in the transplanted stomach decreased eating in proportion to both **caloric content** and **volume**.
> - **Significance**: No functional nerves in the transplant → satiety signal reached the brain **through the blood** (bloodborne). Nutrients not absorbed from stomach → signal was a **chemical released from the stomach** in response to caloric value and volume, not an absorbed nutrient.

### Hunger and Satiety Peptides

- **Peptides** = short chains of amino acids; can function as hormones and neurotransmitters.
- Ingested food interacts with receptors in the GI tract → tract releases peptides into the bloodstream.

> **KEY STUDY: Gibbs, Young, & Smith (1973) — CCK as Satiety Signal**
> - **Method**: Injected **cholecystokinin (CCK)** — a gut peptide — into hungry rats.
> - **Finding**: Rats ate **smaller meals**.
> - **Hypothesis generated**: Circulating gut peptides inform the brain about the quantity and nature of food in the GI tract and play a role in satiety (Badman & Flier, 2005; Flier, 2006).

**Satiety Peptides (decrease appetite):**
- **Cholecystokinin (CCK)** — first discovered; also causes conditioned taste aversion and nausea at high doses, but reduces eating at doses below those that cause aversion
- **Bombesin**
- **Glucagon**
- **Alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (alpha-MSH)**
- **Somatostatin**
- (A dozen or more reported; bind to receptors in the hypothalamus and other brain areas involved in energy metabolism)

**Hunger Peptides (increase appetite):**
- Synthesized primarily in the brain, particularly the hypothalamus.
- **Neuropeptide Y**
- **Galanin**
- **Orexin-A**
- **Ghrelin**
(Baird, Gray, & Fischer, 2006; Olszewski, Schiöth & Levine, 2008; Williams et al., 2004)

**Two major impacts of the hunger/satiety peptide discoveries:**
1. Sheer number indicates the eating control system reacts to **many different signals** — not just one or two (Nogueiras & Tschöp, 2005; Schwartz & Azzara, 2004).
2. Many peptides have receptors in the **hypothalamus** → renewed interest in hypothalamic involvement; stimulated by findings that microinjection of gut peptides into hypothalamic sites has major effects on eating.
3. General consensus: hypothalamic circuits are **only one part** of a much larger system (Berthoud & Morrison, 2008; Cone, 2005).

### Serotonin and Satiety

- **Serotonin** = a monoaminergic neurotransmitter; plays a role in satiety.

**Three major properties of serotonin-produced satiety in rats (Blundell & Halford, 1998):**
1. Caused rats to **resist the powerful attraction of highly palatable cafeteria diets**.
2. Reduced the **amount consumed per meal** rather than reducing the number of meals (Clifton, 2000).
3. Associated with a **shift in food preferences away from fatty foods**.

**Clinical relevance:**
- **Serotonin agonists** (fenfluramine, dexfenfluramine, fluoxetine) reduce hunger, eating, and body weight under some conditions (Blundell & Halford, 1998; De Vry & Schreiber, 2000).

### Prader-Willi Syndrome

- **Prader-Willi syndrome** = a chromosomal disorder considered potentially critical for understanding hunger/satiety neural mechanisms (Goldstone, 2004).

**Cause:** Accident of chromosomal replication that **deletes or disrupts a section of chromosome 15 from the father** (paternal chromosome 15).

**Core features:**
- **Insatiable hunger** (hyperphagia)
- **Little or no satiety**
- Exceptionally **slow metabolism**
- Patient acts as though perpetually starving

**Other clinical features:**
- Weak muscles (**hypotonia** — especially in infancy)
- Small hands and feet
- Feeding difficulties in infancy (weak sucking reflex)
- Tantrums
- Compulsivity
- Skin picking
- Moderate intellectual disability
- Psychiatric problems

**Prognosis (untreated):** Most become extremely obese; often die in early adulthood from diabetes, heart disease, or other obesity-related disorders; some have died from gorging until the stomach splits open.

**Case of Miss A. (Martin et al., 1998):**
- Born with little muscle tone; tube-fed due to weak sucking reflex.
- Age 2: hypotonia resolved; characteristic deformities and developmental delays appeared.
- Age 3.5: sudden onset of **voracious appetite**; rapid weight gain.
- Management: strict low-calorie diet; all food locked away.
- Major behavioral problem: tantrums triggered by **any change in environment** (e.g., a substitute teacher).
- Excellent family/medical care minimized obesity-related complications.
- The genetic cause (paternal chromosome 15 deletion) has provided clues about genetic factors in appetite regulation.

## 12.5 Body Weight Regulation: Set Points versus Settling Points

### Set-Point Assumptions about Body Weight and Eating

**Folk belief**: body weight is regulated by a body-fat set point (Assanand, Pinel, & Lehman, 1998a, 1998b): below set point → hunger increases → eating increases → weight returns to set point; above set point → hunger decreases → eating decreases → weight returns to set point.

**Three lines of evidence challenging set-point theories of body weight:**

#### 1. Variability of Body Weight
- A true set-point mechanism should make large weight changes virtually impossible in adults.
- Yet many adults experience **large and lasting changes in body weight** (Booth, 2004).
- The **obesity epidemic in fast-food societies** further refutes rigid set-point regulation (Rosenheck, 2008).
- Set-point theories imply that eating whenever hungry is the best way to maintain weight — yet many people avoid obesity only by **resisting urges to eat**.

#### 2. Set Points and Health: Ad Libitum Eating is Unhealthy
- One implication of set-point theory: each person's set point is optimal for health ("listen to your body"). Experimental results indicate this is false.

> **KEY STUDY: Okinawa Calorie Restriction Study (Kagawa, 1978)**
> - Adult Okinawans consumed on average **20% fewer calories** than other adult Japanese.
> - Okinawan school children consumed **38% fewer calories** than recommended by health officials.
> - Result: substantially lower rates of morbidity, mortality, and **all aging-related diseases** in Okinawa vs. rest of Japan.
> - Death rates from **stroke: 59%**, **cancer: 69%**, **heart disease: 59%** of rates in rest of Japan.
> - Proportion living past **100 years** was up to **40 times greater** than in various other Japanese regions.
> - Limitation: correlational study, not a controlled experiment (possibly confounded by diet quality, lifestyle).

> **KEY STUDY: Weindruch et al. (1986) — Caloric Restriction in Mice**
> - **Method**: Groups of mice had caloric intake of a well-balanced commercial diet reduced by **25%, 55%, or 65%** after weaning. One group ate ad libitum.
> - **Finding**: All levels of restriction improved health and increased longevity; greatest benefits with greatest restriction.
> - Mice with **65% reduction** (consuming least): lowest incidence of cancer, best immune responses, greatest maximum lifespan — lived **67% longer** than ad libitum mice.
> - Benefits of caloric restriction may occur even when initiated later in life (Mair et al., 2003; Vaupel, Carey, & Christensen, 2003).

**Important nuance on calorie restriction:**
- Health benefits may NOT be entirely attributable to **loss of body fat** — in some studies, health improved even without reduced body fat; no consistent correlation between weight loss and health improvements.
- This suggests **excessive energy consumption itself** (independent of fat accumulation) may accelerate aging (Lane, Ingram, & Roth, 2002; Prolla & Mattson, 2001).

**Additional calorie restriction findings:**
- Reduces seizure susceptibility in human epileptics (Maalouf, Rho, & Mattson, 2008).
- Improves memory in the elderly (Witte et al., 2009).

#### 3. Regulation of Body Weight by Changes in Energy Utilization Efficiency
- Body controls fat levels partly by **changing the efficiency with which it uses energy** (not only by changing how much is eaten):
  - As body fat **declines**: energy is used more **efficiently** → limits further weight loss (Martin, White, & Hulsey, 1991).
  - As body fat **increases**: efficiency of energy utilization **decreases** → limits further weight gain.
- **Rothwell & Stock (1982)**: obese cafeteria-diet rats had resting energy expenditure **45% greater** than control rats.
- This explains why weight-loss diets become progressively less effective: initial large losses → progressively declining rate → equilibrium is reached and little further loss occurs.

**Diet-induced thermogenesis:**
- **Diet-induced thermogenesis** = the mechanism by which the body adjusts energy utilization efficiency in response to body fat levels.
- Increases in body fat → increased body temperature → additional energy required to maintain temperature (and vice versa) (Lazar, 2008).

**Basal metabolic rate (BMR):**
- **Basal metabolic rate (BMR)** = the rate at which energy is utilized to maintain bodily processes when resting.
- Major individual differences exist in BMR and in the ability to adjust metabolic rate in response to body fat changes.
- People who remain slim despite gluttonous eating may still pay a health cost (overeating may be harmful even without observable obesity).

### Set Points and Settling Points in Weight Control

**Settling-point model** (critics of set-point model: Booth, Fuller, & Lewis, 1981; Wirtshafter & Davis, 1977):

- **Settling point** = the level at which the various factors influencing body weight achieve a **natural equilibrium** — not a fixed defended target but a dynamic balance.
- As body-fat levels increase, changes occur that tend to limit further increases until balance is achieved between all factors encouraging and discouraging weight gain.
- Provides **loose homeostatic regulation** without a set-point mechanism or a mechanism for returning weight to a specific value.

**Key comparison:**

| Feature | Set-Point Model | Settling-Point Model |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Regulatory mechanism | Fixed set point + detector + effector | Equilibrium of multiple interacting factors |
| Response to deviation | Negative feedback returns weight to set point | Negative feedback merely limits further change in same direction |
| Body weight stability | Rigidly defended | Stable only while influencing factors are stable |
| Flexibility | Low | High |
| Classic analogy | Thermostat | Leaky barrel |

**Leaky-barrel model (Pinel's analogy):**
- Amount of water in the barrel (body fat level) = determined by rate of water input (eating) balanced against rate of leakage (energy utilization).
- No target level — just a settling point based on input and output rates.

**Biological example of a settling point:**
- A neuron's **resting potential** is described as a biological settling point (see Chapter 4).

| **Set-point mechanism** | The component of a set-point system that defines the set point (e.g., thermostat setting) |

| **Effector mechanism** | The component that acts to eliminate deviations from the set point (e.g., heater) |
| **Glucostatic theory** | Theory that eating is regulated by a blood glucose set point; accounts for short-term hunger/satiety |
| **Lipostatic theory** | Theory that eating is regulated by a body-fat set point; accounts for long-term weight regulation |
| **Positive-incentive theory** | Theory that eating is controlled by the anticipated pleasure (positive-incentive value) of eating, not by energy deficits |
| **Positive-incentive value** | The anticipated pleasure associated with a behavior; drives eating according to positive-incentive theory |

| **Orexin-A** | A hunger peptide (also involved in wakefulness/arousal) |

| **Ad libitum** | Free-feeding; eating as much as desired without restriction |
| **Diet-induced thermogenesis** | The mechanism by which the body adjusts energy utilization efficiency in response to changes in body fat level; increased fat → increased temperature → more energy used |
| **Basal metabolic rate (BMR)** | The rate at which energy is utilized to maintain bodily processes when the body is at rest |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Fat vs. Glycogen energy storage**: Fat stores ~2x energy per gram AND does not hold water (glycogen holds water). Fat = 85%, glycogen = only 0.5% of body energy reserves. Changes in adult body weight = largely changes in stored fat.

2. **Three phases of energy metabolism** (cephalic, absorptive, fasting): Know which hormones dominate each phase, what each hormone promotes/inhibits, and what fuels are used. Cephalic and absorptive = high insulin/low glucagon; fasting = high glucagon/low insulin. Brain gets glucose in fasting (insulin not needed for brain glucose uptake).

3. **Gluconeogenesis** = protein → glucose conversion; occurs in the fasting phase (low insulin, high glucagon).

4. **VMH lesions (Hetherington & Ranson, 1940)** produce hyperphagia and obesity — VMH syndrome with dynamic phase (excessive eating) and static phase (stable obesity, weight defended). VMH-lesioned rats are paradoxically less willing to work for food or eat unpalatable food.

5. **VMH syndrome is primarily metabolic**: VMH lesions increase insulin → lipogenesis ↑, lipolysis ↓ → rats overeat because they become obese (not obese because they overeat). Damage to ventral noradrenergic bundle and paraventricular nuclei also contributes.

6. **LH lesions (Anand & Brobeck, 1951)** produce aphagia AND adipsia (Teitelbaum & Epstein, 1962). Partial recovery with tube feeding. LH lesions cause general motor/sensory unresponsiveness, not feeding-specific damage. The concept of the LH as a dedicated feeding center has been abandoned.

7. **CCK (cholecystokinin)** was the first gut peptide shown to reduce meal size (Gibbs, Young, & Smith, 1973). It is a satiety peptide at low doses, but causes conditioned taste aversion and nausea at higher doses.

8. **Hunger peptides** = neuropeptide Y, galanin, orexin-A, ghrelin (synthesized mainly in the brain/hypothalamus). Satiety peptides = CCK, bombesin, glucagon, alpha-MSH, somatostatin (many from the GI tract).

9. **Prader-Willi syndrome**: caused by deletion/disruption of paternal chromosome 15. Key features: insatiable hunger, absent satiety, slow metabolism, hypotonia, small hands/feet, tantrums, compulsivity, skin picking, intellectual disability. Fatal if untreated (obesity, diabetes, heart disease).

10. **Sensory-specific satiety**: the positive-incentive value of the specific food consumed plummets, while other foods decline only slightly. Adaptive: encourages dietary variety (prevents malnutrition) and maximum intake during food abundance. Rolls (1990): brief effects (within a meal) and enduring effects (meal to meal).

11. **Positive-incentive theory vs. set-point theory**: positive-incentive = eating driven by anticipated pleasure; set-point = eating driven by energy deficit. Positive-incentive explains: appetizer effect, Pavlovian conditioning of hunger, sensory-specific satiety, cafeteria diet effects, social eating. Key proponents: Bolles, Booth, Collier, Rolls, Toates (1980–1981); Berridge (2004).

12. **Weingarten's Pavlovian conditioning study (1983)**: buzzer/light CS paired with meals caused rats to eat when CS was presented — even without food deprivation, even right after a meal. Demonstrates hunger is an expectation-driven state, not an energy-deficit signal.

13. **Serotonin and satiety**: serotonin reduces meal SIZE (not frequency), causes resistance to cafeteria diets, and shifts preference away from fat. Serotonin agonists (fenfluramine, dexfenfluramine, fluoxetine) reduce hunger and body weight.

14. **Calorie restriction**: in >12 mammalian species, 25–65% caloric restriction consistently improves health and extends lifespan. Weindruch et al. (1986): 65% restriction in mice → 67% longer maximum lifespan, lowest cancer, best immune function. Benefits may not depend entirely on fat loss — excess energy consumption itself may accelerate aging.

15. **Settling-point model vs. set-point model**: settling point = natural equilibrium of all factors affecting body weight (leaky barrel analogy); no active defense of a fixed target. In settling-point model, negative feedback limits further change; in set-point model, negative feedback triggers return to the set point. The settling-point model is more consistent with the evidence.
