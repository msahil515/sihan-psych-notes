# Chapter 16: Lateralization, Language, and the Split Brain

The forebrain has two hemispheres connected by the **cerebral commissures** (chiefly the **corpus callosum**). They differ in function: **lateralization of function**. Covers its discovery, study methods, split-brain research, brain asymmetries, and evolution of lateralization and language.

---

## 16.1 Cerebral Lateralization: Introduction

- Brain shows **bilateral duplication**: two hemispheres connected only by **cerebral commissures** (**corpus callosum** ~200 million axons; anterior, hippocampal, posterior commissures; massa intermedia).
- **Lateralization of function**: differential specialization of the hemispheres.

### Discovery
- **Marc Dax (1836)**: noted that all ~40 of his patients with **speech problems** had left-hemisphere damage; report ignored.
- **Paul Broca (1861-1864)**: aphasic patients with **left inferior prefrontal** lesions → **Broca's area** (anterior to the left face motor area). **Aphasia** = deficit producing/comprehending language.
- **Hugo-Karl Liepmann (early 1900s)**: **apraxia** (difficulty making requested movements out of context, though spontaneous movement is fine) is also tied to **left-hemisphere** damage despite bilateral symptoms.
- **Cerebral dominance**: the (oversimplified) idea that one hemisphere (usually left = **dominant**) controls all complex processes; the other = **minor**.

### Tests of Lateralization
1. **Unilateral lesion studies**: compare left vs. right damage effects.
2. **Sodium amytal (Wada) test** (Wada, 1949): inject **sodium amytal** into one **carotid artery** to anesthetize that hemisphere; anesthetizing the **speech-dominant** (usually left) hemisphere causes **complete mutism** 1-2 min. **Invasive**; presurgical.
3. **Dichotic listening test** (Kimura, 1961): **noninvasive**; digit pairs played simultaneously to each ear; most report more from the **right ear** (~55% vs. 45%) → **left-hemisphere language dominance**. The superior ear reflects the **contralateral** hemisphere (stronger contralateral connections).
4. **Functional imaging** (PET, fMRI): far greater **left-hemisphere** activity on language tasks.

### Speech Laterality and Handedness

| Study | Population | Findings |
|---|---|---|
| Russell & Espir (1961) | WWII brain damage | ~60% of dextrals with LH lesions aphasic; ~2% with RH; sinestrals more variable |
| Penfield & Roberts (1959) | Unilateral excisions | Similar |

- **Dextrals** = right-handers; **sinestrals** = left-handers. Left hemisphere is language-dominant in almost all dextrals and a majority of sinestrals; sinestrals are **more variable**.
- **Milner (1974)** (sodium amytal): **92%** of right-handers (no early LH damage) had left-hemisphere speech; **69%** of left-handed/ambidextrous; only **30%** with early LH damage. Early damage can shift lateralization (neuroplasticity).

### Sex Differences
- **McGlone (1977, 1980)**: male stroke victims **3× more likely** to be aphasic; male LH strokes hurt WAIS **verbal** subtests, RH strokes hurt **performance** subtests; females showed no significant left/right difference → claimed **male brains more lateralized**.
- **Contested**: **Sommer et al. (2004)** meta-analysis of 14 imaging studies found **no significant sex effect** on language lateralization.

## 16.2 The Split Brain

### The Corpus Callosum Paradox
- Early 1950s: the corpus callosum's size/position implied importance, yet cutting it (in animals, and in **agenesis**) seemed to have no behavioral effect. **Roger Sperry** investigated.

> **KEY STUDY: Myers & Sperry (1953)** — cats: transect both **optic chiasm** and **corpus callosum**, patch one eye → all visual info restricted to one hemisphere. Phase 1 (first eye patched): one hemisphere learned a visual discrimination as fast as two. Phase 2 (patch switched): controls stayed ~100% accurate; **experimental cats dropped to 50% (chance)** and relearned from scratch.
> **Conclusions**: (1) the brain can act as **two separate brains**; (2) the corpus callosum **transfers information between hemispheres**.

- Confirmed in monkeys (fine tactual/motor discriminations don't transfer — these fibers are **contralateral**).

### Commissurotomy in Humans
- Epileptic discharge spreads via the corpus callosum. **Vogel and Bogen** began **commissurotomy** (transect corpus callosum, smaller commissures intact) for intractable epilepsy; benefit was greater than expected (many had no further major convulsions). Evaluated by **Sperry and Gazzaniga** → **Sperry's Nobel Prize 1981**.
- **Testing**: patients fixate center; stimuli flashed for **0.1 s** to one visual field. **Left visual field → right hemisphere**; **right visual field → left hemisphere**. Tactual/motor tasks done under a ledge.

### Independent Hemispheric Function
- **Left hemisphere**: speaks; right visual field + right hand. **Right hemisphere**: cannot speak (understands simple instructions); left visual field + left hand.

| Input | Hemisphere | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Right visual field / right hand | Left | Names it OR selects with right hand |
| Left visual field / left hand | Right | Can't name it; left hand selects correct object; left hemisphere claims not to know |

- An object flashed to the **left visual field** → patient (left hemisphere speaking) says **nothing appeared**, yet the left hand fetches the matching object.

### Cross-Cuing
- Hemispheres can communicate (1) **neurally via brain stem** or (2) externally via **cross-cuing** (behavioral cues).
> **Gazzaniga (1967)**: a color shown to the left visual field; the right hemisphere frowned at a wrong verbal guess, and the left hemisphere self-corrected — a confound to control.

### Doing Two Things at Once
- Each hemisphere learns independently; e.g., pencil (left field) + orange (right field) → left hand grabs pencil, right hand grabs orange; left hemisphere reports "two oranges."
- **Helping-hand phenomenon**: the left hand (right hemisphere) redirects the right hand to the correct object.
- **Attention**: independent foci; split-brain patients find a target **faster** than controls via two parallel searches (Luck et al., 1989).
- **Chimeric figures test** (Levy, Trevarthen, & Sperry, 1972): fused half-faces; each hemisphere does independent **visual completion**. Verbal description = the **right-visual-field** half (left hemisphere); pointing = the **left-visual-field** half (right hemisphere).

### The Z Lens
- **Z lens (Zaidel, 1975)**: a contact lens **opaque on one side**; moves with the eye, restricting input to one hemisphere regardless of eye movement → allows scanning complex material (solves the 0.1-s limit).

### Dual Functioning and Conflict
- Usually the left hemisphere dominates daily activity; rarely the right takes an autonomous role.

> **Case of Peter**: complex partial seizures from age 8; commissurotomy at 20 (LH language-dominant). Post-surgery couldn't respond with the left body to verbal commands; his right hemisphere acted with **intent** (left hand turned off TV, struck a relative), while his left hemisphere had no idea why (Joseph, 1988).

- **Sperry, Zaidel, & Zaidel (1979)**: emotion-charged images to the right hemisphere produced emotionally appropriate **speech** (left hemisphere) even though it couldn't identify the image — **emotional** (not visual) information crossed.
- **Weissman & Banich (2000)**: simple tasks → one hemisphere; complex tasks → both. So imaging may show bilateral activity for hard tasks; **elderly** people show less lateralization (recruit both hemispheres).

## 16.3 Differences between Left and Right Hemispheres

- Most research targets the **right hemisphere's** special abilities. **Lateralization is statistical, not absolute** — no function is exclusive to one hemisphere (even language: 55:45 ear advantage; right hemisphere understands at a preschool level; Gazzaniga, 1998).

| Function | Left Dominance | Right Dominance |
|---|---|---|
| Vision | Words, letters | Faces, geometric patterns, emotional expression |
| Audition | Language sounds | Nonlanguage sounds, music |
| Touch | Verbal memory | Tactile patterns, Braille |
| Movement | Complex/ipsilateral movement | Movement in spatial patterns |
| Memory | Speech, reading, writing, arithmetic | Nonverbal memory, emotional content |
| Spatial ability | — | Mental rotation, geometry, direction, distance |

1. **Ipsilateral movement**: the left hemisphere has stronger ipsilateral motor control; left-hemisphere lesions more likely to impair left-hand movements (Haaland & Harrington, 1996).
2. **Spatial ability (right)**: **Levy (1969)** — split-brain patients better at a 3D→2D task with the right hemisphere (rapid, silent) than left (hesitant, verbal). Consistent with **contralateral neglect** from **right-hemisphere** damage.
3. **Emotion (right)**: right hemisphere superior at **identifying facial expressions** (Bowers et al., 1985).
4. **Music (right)**: **Kimura (1964)** dichotic test — right ear (left hemisphere) better for **digits**, left ear (right hemisphere) better for **melodies**.
5. **Memory**: left = verbal/episodic meaning; right = nonverbal/perceptual. Alternative: hemispheres differ by **material**, not process (Wolford, Miller, & Gazzaniga, 2004).
6. **Left-hemisphere interpreter**: a mechanism that seeks patterns/meaning.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT (Metcalfe, Funnell, & Gazzaniga, 1995):** predict which light (top 80%, bottom 20%) comes on. Intact controls and split-brain **left hemisphere** sought a nonexistent rule → only **68%**. The **right hemisphere** (like animals) always picked the top light → **80%**. The interpreter can hinder performance.

### What Is Lateralized?
- Old view (broad clusters per hemisphere) is **too simplistic**; categories like "language" comprise dozens of processes with exceptions (e.g., right hemisphere better at speech **intonation** and **speaker identity**; Beeman & Chiarello, 1998). Current approach: break tasks into **constituent processes** and lateralize each.

### Anatomical Asymmetries
- Arise from interhemispheric **gene expression** differences (Sun et al., 2005).

| Area | Location | Significance | Larger hemisphere |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Frontal operculum** | Anterior to face motor area | Site of Broca's area (left) | Left in total volume (buried in sulci) |
| **Planum temporale** | Posterior lateral fissure, temporal lobe | Language comprehension; "Wernicke's area" | **Left** (~65% of brains) |
| **Heschl's gyrus** | Lateral fissure, anterior to planum temporale | Primary auditory cortex | **Right** |

- **Geschwind & Levitsky (1968)**: planum temporale larger on the left in ~65%. **Critical**: this asymmetry is **NOT correlated** with degree of language lateralization. Similar asymmetries exist in **non-humans**.
- **Perfect pitch** (Schlaug et al., 1995): musicians with perfect pitch have greater left planum temporale.
- **Galuske et al. (2000)** (Wernicke's area): columns same diameter but **~20% farther apart** in the left, with **longer connecting axons** — possibly a language-processing adaptation.

## 16.4 Evolution of Lateralization and Language

### Three Theories
1. **Analytic-synthetic theory**: left = **analytic/sequential**; right = **synthetic/holistic (gestalts)**. **Criticism**: too vague to test.
2. **Motor theory** (Kimura, 1979): the left hemisphere is specialized for **fine motor control** (speech is one category). Support: aphasia often co-occurs with motor deficits. **Criticism**: doesn't explain why motor became lateralized.
3. **Linguistic theory**: the left hemisphere is specialized for **language itself**, regardless of modality. Based on **ASL** users with left-hemisphere damage.

> **Case of W.L.**: congenitally deaf, right-handed; large **left frontotemporoparietal stroke** → **sign language aphasia** (errors using/understanding ASL) but **intact pantomime** and general cognition (Corina et al., 1992) → supports the linguistic theory.

### When Did Lateralization Evolve?
- Not uniquely human: lateralization exists across **all five vertebrate classes**; may date to ~**500 million years ago** (MacNeilage, Rogers, & Vallortigara, 2009). **Right-handedness** likely from a right-sided **feeding** preference. Left-hemisphere communication dominance also in **birdsong** and **dog/monkey** conspecific calls.

### Survival Advantages
1. **Efficiency**: concentrating a function in one hemisphere (one skilled hand > two moderate ones).
2. **Parallel processing**: two processes done simultaneously if lateralized differently.

### Evolution of Human Language
- **Human language**: a virtually **limitless** number of ideas from a **finite set of elements** (Hauser, Chomsky, & Fitch, 2005). Other species don't recombine signals creatively. A near-instinct: at **10 months** infants distinguish **all** human-language sounds; at **30 months** they use complete sentences and **500+ words** (Golinkoff & Hirsh-Pasek, 2006), the plastic brain narrowing to the native language(s).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Marc Dax (1836)** first noted left-hemisphere speech damage (ignored); **Broca (1861-1864)** formally established it (Broca's area).

2. **Sodium amytal (Wada) test** (Wada, 1949): carotid injection; anesthetizing the dominant (left) hemisphere causes 1-2 min **mutism**.

3. **Dichotic listening (Kimura, 1961)**: superior ear = contralateral dominance; **right-ear advantage** (~55:45) = left-hemisphere language.

4. **Myers & Sperry (1953)**: corpus callosum **transfers information**; each hemisphere can act as a separate brain (cats; chiasm + callosum cut).

5. **Corpus callosum** (~200 million axons) is the largest commissure; cut in **commissurotomy** for epilepsy.

6. Split-brain: **left hemisphere speaks**; **right cannot** (preschool comprehension). **Left visual field → right hemisphere**, **right field → left hemisphere**.

7. **Cross-cuing**: hemispheres communicate via behavioral cues — a confound.

8. **Z lens (Zaidel, 1975)**: restricts input to one hemisphere despite eye movement; solves the 0.1-s limit.

9. **Left-hemisphere interpreter** (Gazzaniga): seeks patterns; the left hemisphere scores 68% vs. the right's 80% on random prediction.

10. **Lateralization is statistical, not absolute** (even language: 55:45).

11. **Planum temporale** (Wernicke's) larger left in ~65% (Geschwind & Levitsky, 1968), but **not correlated** with language lateralization; **Heschl's gyrus** larger right; perfect-pitch musicians have greater left planum temporale (Schlaug et al., 1995).

12. **McGlone (1977, 1980)**: males 3× more aphasic → "male brains more lateralized"; **Sommer et al. (2004)** found no significant sex effect.

13. Three evolution theories: **analytic-synthetic** (vague), **motor** (Kimura, 1979; fine motor), **linguistic** (language per se; supported by W.L.'s sign language aphasia with intact pantomime).

14. Lateralization is **not uniquely human** (all five vertebrate classes; ~500 mya); right-handedness from right-sided feeding; left-hemisphere communication in birdsong/primate calls.

15. **Apraxia** (Liepmann): left-hemisphere damage, bilateral symptoms; movement impaired on command not spontaneously — with aphasia, basis for **cerebral dominance**.
