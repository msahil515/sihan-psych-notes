# Chapter 16: Lateralization, Language, and the Split Brain

The human forebrain is divided into two largely separate cerebral hemispheres connected by the cerebral commissures, most notably the corpus callosum. Although the hemispheres appear similar, they differ substantially in function — a phenomenon called **lateralization of function**. This chapter covers the discovery of lateralization, methods of studying it, findings from split-brain research, anatomical brain asymmetries, and evolutionary theories of how lateralization and language arose.

---

## 16.1 Cerebral Lateralization of Function: Introduction

### Background and Historical Context

- The human brain reflects the general principle of **bilateral duplication**: two hemispheres, left and right, entirely separate except for the **cerebral commissures** connecting them.
- The major commissures include:
  - **Corpus callosum** (largest; ~200 million axons)
  - Anterior commissure
  - Hippocampal commissure
  - Posterior commissure
  - Massa intermedia
- Despite their similar gross appearance, the hemispheres differ markedly in function.
- **Lateralization of function**: the differential specialization of the left and right cerebral hemispheres.

### Discovery of the Specific Contributions of Left-Hemisphere Damage to Aphasia and Apraxia

#### Marc Dax (1836)
- **Marc Dax**: unknown country doctor; presented a short report at a medical society meeting in France in **1836** — his only scientific presentation.
- Observed that among ~40 brain-damaged patients with **speech problems**, not a single one had damage restricted to the right hemisphere.
- Report aroused little interest; Dax died the following year.
- Significance: first recorded clinical observation of left-hemisphere dominance for speech.
- Most contemporaries still believed the brain acted as a whole (anti-localizationist view).

#### Paul Broca (1861–1864)
- **Paul Broca**: reported postmortem examination of two **aphasic** patients whose left-hemisphere lesions involved frontal cortex just anterior to the face area of the primary motor cortex.
- **Aphasia**: a brain-damage-produced deficit in the ability to **produce or comprehend language**.
- By 1864, Broca had examined 9 aphasic patients, all with damage to the **inferior prefrontal cortex of the left hemisphere** — thereafter called **Broca's area**.
- Broca's area location: inferior left prefrontal cortex, just anterior to the face area of the left primary motor cortex (within the frontal lobe, above the lateral fissure).

#### Hugo-Karl Liepmann (early 1900s)
- **Hugo-Karl Liepmann**: discovered that **apraxia**, like aphasia, is almost always associated with **left-hemisphere damage**, even though its symptoms are bilateral.
- **Apraxia**: difficulty performing movements when asked to do so out of context, despite no difficulty performing those same movements spontaneously.
- Combined evidence of left-hemisphere role in both language and voluntary movement led to the concept of **cerebral dominance**.

#### Concept of Cerebral Dominance
- **Cerebral dominance**: the idea that one hemisphere (usually the left) assumes the dominant role in controlling all complex behavioral and cognitive processes; the other plays a minor role.
- Left hemisphere = **dominant hemisphere**; right hemisphere = **minor hemisphere**.
- Later shown to be an oversimplification: both hemispheres have important, distinct specializations.

---

### Tests of Cerebral Lateralization

Four major methods (a fifth — split-brain studies — is covered in section 16.2):

#### 1. Unilateral Lesion Studies (Earliest Method)
- Compare effects of left-hemisphere vs. right-hemisphere damage on behavior.
- Historical foundation for discovering language lateralization.

#### 2. Sodium Amytal Test (Wada Test)
- **Sodium amytal test** (also called the **Wada test**): devised by **Wada (1949)**.
- Administered prior to neurosurgery to map language lateralization, so the surgeon avoids damaging language cortex.
- **Procedure**: inject a small amount of **sodium amytal** (a barbiturate anesthetic) into the **carotid artery** on one side of the neck.
  - Anesthetizes the ipsilateral hemisphere for a few minutes.
  - Patient is asked to recite well-known sequences (alphabet, days of the week, months of the year) and name pictures.
  - Then injected on the other side; test is repeated.
- **Results**:
  - When the **speech-dominant hemisphere** (usually left) is anesthetized: patient is rendered **completely mute** for 1-2 minutes; after recovery, there are errors of serial order and naming.
  - When the **minor speech hemisphere** (usually right) is anesthetized: mutism often does NOT occur; errors are few.
- Note: This is an **invasive** test; used clinically in presurgical evaluation.

#### 3. Dichotic Listening Test
- Devised by **Kimura (1961)**.
- **Noninvasive**; can be administered to healthy individuals.
- **Procedure**: three pairs of spoken digits are presented via earphones; each pair is presented simultaneously — one digit to each ear (e.g., 3, 9, 2 to one ear; 1, 6, 4 to the other). The participant reports all digits heard.
- **Finding**: most people report slightly more digits from the **right ear** (indicative of **left-hemisphere specialization for language**).
- Right-ear advantage = ~55% vs. 45% for left ear (not absolute, statistical).
- Patients with right-hemisphere language specialization (identified by sodium amytal) perform better with the **left ear**.
- **Explanation (Kimura)**: although sounds from each ear project to both hemispheres, **contralateral connections are stronger** and take precedence when two different sounds compete for the same cortical auditory centers. Therefore, the superior ear reflects the specialization of the **contralateral hemisphere**.

#### 4. Functional Brain Imaging
- **PET** (positron emission tomography) and **fMRI** (functional magnetic resonance imaging) used to monitor brain activity during tasks.
- On language tests: far greater activity in **left hemisphere** than right hemisphere typically revealed (Martin, 2003).

### Discovery of the Relation between Speech Laterality and Handedness

#### Key Clinical Studies
- Two large-scale lesion studies clarified the link between language lateralization and handedness:

| Study | Population | Findings |
|---|---|---|
| Russell & Espir (1961) | Military personnel with WWII brain damage | ~60% of dextrals with left-hemisphere lesions had aphasia; ~2% with right-hemisphere lesions; ~30% of sinestrals with left-hemisphere lesions; ~24% with right-hemisphere lesions |
| Penfield & Roberts (1959) | Neurological patients with unilateral excisions | Similar proportions |

- **Dextrals**: right-handers. **Sinestrals**: left-handers.
- Left hemisphere dominant for language in **almost all dextrals** and **majority of sinestrals**.
- Sinestrals are **more variable** (less predictable) in hemisphere of language lateralization than dextrals.

#### Milner (1974) — Sodium Amytal Test Data
- Almost all right-handed patients **without** early left-hemisphere damage had **left-hemisphere speech** (92%).
- Most left-handed and ambidextrous patients without early left-hemisphere damage had **left-hemisphere speech** (69%).
- **Early left-hemisphere damage** decreases left-hemisphere speech specialization in left-handed/ambidextrous patients — only 30% retained left-hemisphere speech.
- Interpretation caveat: sodium amytal tests are only administered to those with brain dysfunction; early damage can shift lateralization; Milner's figures likely **underestimate** left-hemisphere speech prevalence in the general healthy population.
- **Neuroplasticity implication**: early brain damage can cause the lateralization of speech to shift to the other hemisphere (Maratsos & Matheny, 1994; Stiles, 1998).

---

### Sex Differences in Brain Lateralization

#### McGlone's Studies (1977, 1980)
- **McGlone**: studied unilateral stroke victims.
- Key findings:
  1. Male victims of unilateral strokes were **three times more likely** to suffer from aphasia than female victims.
  2. Male victims of **left-hemisphere strokes** had deficits on the WAIS **verbal subtests**.
  3. Male victims of **right-hemisphere strokes** had deficits on the WAIS **performance subtests**.
  4. Female victims showed **no significant differences** between the disruptive effects of left- and right-hemisphere strokes on WAIS performance.
- **Conclusion (McGlone)**: male brains are **more lateralized** than female brains.

#### Status of the Sex-Difference Hypothesis
- McGlone's hypothesis was widely embraced but **support has been mixed**:
  - Some researchers failed to confirm sex differences in effects of unilateral lesions (Inglis & Lawson, 1982).
  - A few fMRI studies suggested females use both hemispheres more for language (Jaeger et al., 1998; Kansaku, Yamaura, & Kitazawa, 2000).
  - A **meta-analysis of 14 functional brain-imaging studies** (Sommer et al., 2004) did **NOT find a significant effect of sex** on language lateralization.
- Conclusion: the sex-difference hypothesis is still contested.

## 16.2 The Split Brain

### The Corpus Callosum Paradox

- In the early 1950s, the **corpus callosum** presented a paradox:
  - Its size (~200 million axons) and central position between the hemispheres implied an extremely important function.
  - Yet research in the 1930s–1940s showed cutting it in monkeys and other species had no observable effect on behavior.
  - Human patients born without a corpus callosum (**agenesis of the corpus callosum**) or with it damaged appeared quite normal.
- **Roger Sperry** (also known for eye-rotation experiments in Chapter 9) and colleagues were intrigued by this paradox.

---

### Groundbreaking Experiment of Myers and Sperry (1953)

> **KEY STUDY: Myers & Sperry (1953)**
> - **Species**: cats
> - **Design**: four groups:
>   1. Key experimental group: both **optic chiasm** and **corpus callosum** transected; one eye patched.
>   2. Control: only optic chiasm transected.
>   3. Control: only corpus callosum transected.
>   4. Unlesioned control group.
> - **Task**: visual discrimination — circle vs. square panel; press the correct one for food reward.
> - **Method to isolate one hemisphere**: transect (completely cut through) both the optic chiasm AND the corpus callosum; patch one eye. This restricts all visual information to the hemisphere **ipsilateral** to the uncovered eye.
>   - Two routes by which visual info can cross from one eye to the contralateral hemisphere: (1) via corpus callosum, (2) via optic chiasm — both were cut.
> - **Results — Phase 1 (patch on first eye)**:
>   - All groups learned the visual discrimination at normal rates.
>   - Cutting the optic chiasm produced a **scotoma** (area of blindness) involving the entire medial half of each retina, but one hemisphere alone could learn the task as rapidly as two hemispheres.
> - **Results — Phase 2 (patch transferred to other eye)**:
>   - Control cats (any combination of intact, chiasm-only cut, or callosum-only cut): **continued at ~100% accuracy** — memory transferred normally.
>   - Key experimental cats (both optic chiasm AND corpus callosum cut): performance **dropped immediately to 50% (baseline/chance)**; had to relearn the task with no savings — as if they had never seen it before.
> - **Conclusions**:
>   1. The cat brain has the capacity to act as **two separate brains**.
>   2. The function of the corpus callosum is to **transmit (transfer) information between the two hemispheres**.

- Confirmed in multiple species (monkeys, etc.): split-brain monkeys cannot transfer fine tactual discriminations or fine motor responses from one hand to the other (all somatosensory and motor fibers for fine discrimination are **contralateral**).

---

### Commissurotomy in Human Epileptics

- Epileptic discharges often spread from one hemisphere to the other **through the corpus callosum**.
- Early attempt by **Van Wagenen & Herren (1940)** failed, presumably due to incomplete transections.
- **Vogel and Bogen**: neurosurgeons who initiated a program of **commissurotomy** for severe, intractable epilepsy.
- **Commissurotomy**: surgical procedure that typically involves transecting the corpus callosum while leaving the smaller commissures intact.
- **Rationale**: if discharge is limited to the hemisphere of origin, severity of convulsions might be reduced.
- **Outcome**: therapeutic benefits were greater than anticipated — many commissurotomized patients experienced **no further major convulsions**.
- Neuropsychological evaluation of Vogel and Bogen's patients was conducted by **Sperry and Gazzaniga**.
- This work was a major factor in **Sperry being awarded the Nobel Prize in 1981**.

#### Testing Method for Human Split-Brain Patients
- Could NOT use optic chiasm transection (would produce scotoma).
- Instead: patients fixated on the center of a display screen; visual stimuli flashed onto the left or right side for **0.1 second** (long enough to perceive, short enough to preclude confounding eye movements).
  - Stimuli in **left visual field** → transmitted to **right visual cortex**.
  - Stimuli in **right visual field** → transmitted to **left visual cortex**.
- Fine tactual and motor tasks performed by each hand under a ledge (so the ipsilateral hemisphere could not monitor via vision).

---

### Evidence That the Hemispheres of Split-Brain Patients Can Function Independently

#### Key Asymmetry: Left vs. Right Hemisphere in Split-Brain Humans
- Unlike split-brain lab animals (whose hemispheres are relatively equal), the hemispheres of split-brain humans differ substantially:
  - **Left hemisphere**: capable of **speech**; verbally adept; receives sensory info from right visual field and right hand; controls fine motor of right hand.
  - **Right hemisphere**: understands a few simple instructions but **cannot speak**; receives sensory info from left visual field and left hand; controls fine motor of left hand.

#### How Each Hemisphere Responds to Input

| Input | Identifying Hemisphere | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Object in right visual field / right hand | Left hemisphere | Can name it verbally OR select it with right hand |
| Object in left visual field / left hand | Right hemisphere | Cannot verbally name it; left hand can select the correct object from test items; patient (left hemisphere) claims not to know what it is |

- Split-brain patients asked to name an object flashed in the **left visual field** will claim **nothing appeared on the screen** (it is the left hemisphere speaking, and it did not receive the information).
- When an object is placed in the **left hand**: patient is aware something is there (crude tactual info via ipsilateral somatosensory fibers) but **cannot say what it is**; yet the left hand (right hemisphere) can accurately fetch a matching object.

---

### Cross-Cuing

- Although split-brain hemispheres have no direct neural communication, they can communicate:
  1. **Neurally via indirect pathways through the brain stem**.
  2. **Externally via cross-cuing**.
- **Cross-cuing**: a process by which one hemisphere signals information to the other hemisphere through external behavioral cues.

> **Example (Gazzaniga, 1967)**: Red or green stimulus presented to the left visual field; patient asked to verbally report the color. Initially at chance (50%). Performance improved because:
> - The right hemisphere saw the color AND heard the left hemisphere's guess.
> - If the left hemisphere guessed wrong, the right hemisphere caused a frown/head shake.
> - The left hemisphere detected this behavioral feedback and self-corrected its verbal answer.
> - This cross-cuing mimicked neural transfer — researchers had to be alert to this artifact.

- Key lesson: split-brain patients have various behavioral strategies at their command; obtaining a clean neurological description is difficult.

---

### Doing Two Things at Once

- Because each hemisphere functions independently, split-brain patients can **learn two different things simultaneously**.

> **Experiment**: Two different visual stimuli flashed simultaneously — pencil in left visual field (right hemisphere), orange in right visual field (left hemisphere). Patient asked to reach with each hand into separate bags and grasp the object on the screen.
> - Left hemisphere reports "Two oranges" (all it knows is what it saw: an orange).
> - When hands are withdrawn: right hand holds an orange (correct for left hemisphere); left hand holds a pencil (correct for right hemisphere).
> - Two hemispheres learned two different things at the same time.

- **Helping-hand phenomenon**: When presented with conflicting stimuli, the right hemisphere (controlling the left hand) may physically grab the right hand and redirect it to the object the right hemisphere saw — demonstrating intent and understanding on the part of the right hemisphere.

- **Attention**: each hemisphere of split-brain patients can maintain an **independent focus of attention** (Gazzaniga, 2005). Split-brain patients can find a visual target in an array more **quickly** than healthy controls (Luck et al., 1989) — because two split hemispheres conduct two independent parallel searches.

- **Chimeric figures test** (Levy, Trevarthen, & Sperry, 1972):
  - **Chimeric figures**: photographs composed of fused-together half-faces of two different people, flashed at the fixation point.
  - Named after **Chimera**: mythical monster composed of parts of different animals.
  - Each hemisphere simultaneously and independently engaged in **visual completion** — filling in the incomplete half face to perceive a complete, bilaterally symmetrical face.
  - When asked to describe what they saw: patients described the completed version of the half presented to the **right visual field** (left hemisphere, which can speak).
  - When asked to point to the face from photographs: patients pointed to the completed version of the half presented to the **left visual field** (right hemisphere, which directed the pointing hand).

---

### The Z Lens

- Early split-brain visual studies were limited: stimuli needing more than **0.1 second** to perceive could not be studied (eye movements would defeat the lateralization).
- **Zaidel (1975)**: developed the **Z lens** (also called the Zaidel lens) to resolve this.
- **Z lens**: a contact lens that is **opaque on one side** (left or right). Because it moves with the eye, it permits visual input to enter only one hemisphere regardless of eye movement, allowing the patient to scan complex visual material (e.g., pages of a book).
- Used to compare left and right hemisphere abilities on various tests, including comprehension of spoken language (verbal material was presented to both ears; the Z lens controlled which hemisphere directed the response).

---

### Dual Mental Functioning and Conflict in Split-Brain Patients

#### General Pattern
- In most split-brain patients, the right hemisphere does not have a strong independent will; the left hemisphere controls most everyday activities.
- However, in a few patients, the right hemisphere takes a more active, autonomous role, causing serious conflicts.

#### The Case of Peter, the Split-Brain Patient Tormented by Conflict

- **Peter**: began having **complex partial seizures** at age 8; antiepileptic medication ineffective; received commissurotomy at age 20.
- Pre-surgery sodium amytal test: **left-hemisphere dominant** for language.
- Post-surgery: unlike most split-brain patients, **could not respond with the left side of his body to verbal input**.
  - Could not make whole-body movements (e.g., "Stand like a boxer") or left-body movements (e.g., "Touch your left ear with your left hand") on verbal command.
  - Peter's left hemisphere often remarked that he **hated the left side of his body**.
- Peter's right hemisphere exhibited **independent, obstinate, and sometimes mischievous behavior**:
  - Left hand would turn off TV shows the left hemisphere was enjoying.
  - Left leg would not walk in the intended direction.
  - Left arm would perform socially unacceptable acts (e.g., striking a relative).
- In the lab: left hemisphere sometimes became angry with the left hand — swore at it, struck it, tried to force it with the right hand. The left hand usually **resisted** and kept performing under direction of the right hemisphere.
- It was always clear that the **right hemisphere was behaving with intent and understanding**, while the **left hemisphere had no idea** why the left hand was acting as it was (Joseph, 1988).

---

### Independence of Split Hemispheres: Current Perspective

- Classic study: **Sperry, Zaidel, & Zaidel (1979)** used the Z lens to present emotion-charged images to the right hemisphere (relatives, pets, political/historical figures).
  - Patients' behavioral reactions were **emotionally appropriate** — right hemispheres capable of emotional expression.
  - Unexpected: **emotional content of right-hemisphere images was reflected in the patients' speech** (left hemisphere), even though the left hemisphere did not know what the image was.
  - Emotional information could pass from right to left hemisphere; visual information could not.
  - Bizarre result: left hemisphere gave emotionally appropriate verbal responses to images it could not identify.

> **Illustrative exchange (Sperry, Zaidel, & Zaidel, 1979)**: Patient's right hemisphere was shown a photo of his aunt. Left hemisphere (speaking) identified her as female, "a happy person," and then the patient traced an "E" on the back of his right hand with his left index finger and exclaimed, "My aunt, my Aunt Edie." The right hemisphere communicated the identity via a behavioral cue — tracing the first letter of her name.

- **Task difficulty and hemispheric independence** (Weissman & Banich, 2000):
  - Simple tasks: best processed in **one hemisphere** (the specialized one).
  - Complex tasks: require the cognitive power of **both hemispheres**.
  - Implication 1: fMRI lateralization studies may show bilateral activity for difficult tasks even when one hemisphere is truly dominant.
  - Implication 2: **elderly individuals** often display less lateralization — as neural resources decline, both hemispheres must be recruited for most tasks.

## 16.3 Differences between Left and Right Hemispheres

### Overview

- Five methods used to study lateralization: unilateral lesions, sodium amytal, dichotic listening, fMRI/PET, and split-brain studies.
- Most research focused on **uncovering the special abilities of the right hemisphere** (left-hemisphere verbal and motor abilities are readily apparent).
- **Critical point**: lateralization is **statistical, not absolute** — there are no functions that reside exclusively in one hemisphere. Differences represent **slight biases**, not absolute separations.
  - Even language, the most lateralized of all cognitive abilities, is far from completely lateralized: right-ear advantage on dichotic listening is only 55% vs. 45%, and right hemispheres of most split-brain patients can understand many spoken/written words and simple sentences (language ability of the right hemisphere is comparable to that of a preschool child; Gazzaniga, 1998).

---

### Examples of Cerebral Lateralization of Function

#### Table: Abilities Showing Cerebral Lateralization (Table 16.1)

| Function | Left-Hemisphere Dominance | Right-Hemisphere Dominance |
|---|---|---|
| Vision | Words, Letters | Faces, Geometric patterns, Emotional expression |
| Audition | Language sounds | Nonlanguage sounds, Music |
| Touch | Verbal memory, Finding meaning in memories | Tactile patterns, Braille |
| Movement | Complex movement, Ipsilateral movement | Movement in spatial patterns |
| Memory | Speech, Reading, Writing, Arithmetic | Nonverbal memory, Perceptual aspects of memories, Emotional content |
| Language | (as above) | (as above) |
| Spatial ability | — | Mental rotation of shapes, Geometry, Direction, Distance |

#### 1. Superiority of the Left Hemisphere in Controlling Ipsilateral Movement
- When complex, cognitively driven movements are made by one hand, most activation is in the **contralateral hemisphere** (as expected).
- However, **ipsilateral effects** are substantially greater in the **left hemisphere** than in the right (Kim et al., 1993; Haaland & Harrington, 1996).
- Left-hemisphere lesions are more likely than right-hemisphere lesions to produce **ipsilateral motor problems** (e.g., reduce accuracy of left-hand movements).

#### 2. Superiority of the Right Hemisphere in Spatial Ability
- **Levy (1969)**: 3D block placed in right or left hand of split-brain patients; asked to point to 2D unfolded representation.
  - **Right hemisphere superior** on this spatial task.
  - Right hemisphere approach: rapid, silent.
  - Left hemisphere approach: hesitant, accompanied by running verbal commentary that patients found difficult to suppress.
- Confirmed by many subsequent studies (Funnell, Corballis, & Gazzaniga, 1999; Kaiser et al., 2000).
- Consistent with: disorders of spatial perception (e.g., **contralateral neglect**) tend to be associated with **right-hemisphere damage**.

#### 3. Specialization of the Right Hemisphere for Emotion
- Old concept of left-hemisphere dominance wrongly presumed the right hemisphere is uninvolved in emotion.
- Evidence: right hemisphere superior to left in **identifying facial expressions of emotion** (Bowers et al., 1985).

#### 4. Superior Musical Ability of the Right Hemisphere
- **Kimura (1964)**: compared standard dichotic digit test with a dichotic melody test.
  - 20 right-handers; two different melodies played simultaneously, one to each ear; participants identified which melodies they heard.
  - **Right ear (left hemisphere)**: superior in perceiving **digits** (language).
  - **Left ear (right hemisphere)**: superior in perceiving **melodies** (music).
- Consistent with: right temporal lobe lesions are more likely to disrupt music discrimination than left temporal lobe lesions.

#### 5. Hemispheric Differences in Memory
- Both hemispheres can perform memory tasks, but each is better on certain tests:
  - **Left hemisphere**: greater role in **verbal memory** and encoding of episodic memory; "finding meaning in memories."
  - **Right hemisphere**: greater role in **nonverbal memory**; perceptual aspects of memories.
- Alternative framework (Wolford, Miller, & Gazzaniga, 2004): hemispheres have similar abilities expressed differently — link memory to **specific materials** rather than specific processes.

#### 6. The Left-Hemisphere Interpreter
- **Interpreter**: a hypothetical neuronal mechanism in the **left hemisphere** that continuously assesses patterns of events and tries to make sense of them (find meaning and causal explanations).

> **Key Experiment (Metcalfe, Funnell, & Gazzaniga, 1995; Roser & Gazzaniga, 2004)**:
> - Split-brain patients tested: guess which of two lights (top or bottom) would come on. Top light came on 80% of the time in random sequence (unknown to participants).
> - **Intact controls**: discovered the top light came on more often but tried to find a nonexistent pattern-rule — scored only **68%** correct.
> - **Left hemisphere of split-brain patients**: performed like intact controls — tried to find deeper meaning; performed poorly (~68%).
> - **Right hemisphere of split-brain patients**: like animals (rats/pigeons), did NOT try to interpret — simply learned to **always select the top light**, maximizing correct responses to **80%**.
> - Conclusion: the left hemisphere's interpreter can actually hinder optimal performance.

---

### What Is Lateralized: Broad Clusters or Individual Cognitive Processes?

- Early theories: ascribed broad clusters of mental abilities to one hemisphere (language to left, spatial to right, etc.).
- **Current consensus**: this is **too simplistic**.
  - Categories like "language," "spatial ability," "emotion," and "musical ability" each comprise dozens of individual cognitive activities.
  - Notable exceptions exist for ALL broad categories.
  - Example: the right hemisphere is superior at perceiving the **intonation of speech** and the **identity of the speaker** (Beeman & Chiarello, 1998) — yet "language" is supposedly left-lateralized.
- **Current approach**: following cognitive psychologists, researchers break down complex tasks into their **constituent cognitive processes** and determine the laterality of each element separately. The laterality of a task can then be predicted from the specific cognitive elements it comprises.

---

### Anatomical Asymmetries of the Brain

- Many anatomical differences between hemispheres documented; result from **interhemispheric differences in gene expression** (Sun et al., 2005).
- Most research focused on language-relevant cortical areas: **frontal operculum**, **planum temporale**, and **Heschl's gyrus**.

#### Key Language Areas

| Area | Location | Functional Significance | Hemisphere with Larger Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Frontal operculum** | Frontal lobe cortex, just anterior to face area of primary motor cortex | Site of Broca's area in left hemisphere | Right hemisphere on surface; **left hemisphere** in total volume (buried in sulci) |
| **Planum temporale** | Posterior region of the lateral fissure, temporal lobe | Comprehension of language; often called Wernicke's area | **Left hemisphere** (in ~65% of human brains) |
| **Heschl's gyrus** | Lateral fissure, just anterior to planum temporale | Location of primary auditory cortex | **Right hemisphere** (often two Heschl's gyri on right, one on left) |

- **Geschwind & Levitsky (1968)**: documented that planum temporale is larger in the left hemisphere in about 65% of human brains.
- Difficulty: no clear boundaries around these areas; no consensus on definition (Davis et al., 2008; Friederici, 2009; Keller et al., 2009).
- **Critical finding**: the tendency for the planum temporale to be larger in the left hemisphere is **NOT related** to the tendency for the left hemisphere to be dominant for language. People with well-developed planum temporale asymmetries do not tend to have more lateralized language function (Dos Santos Sequeira et al., 2006; Eckert et al., 2006; Jäncke & Steinmetz, 2003).
- Similar planum temporale asymmetries have been reported in **non-humans** (Dehaene-Lambertz, Hertz-Pannier, & Dubois, 2006).

#### Planum Temporale and Perfect Pitch
- **Schlaug et al. (1995)**: used structural MRI to measure planum temporale asymmetry in relation to **perfect pitch** (ability to identify the pitch of individual musical notes without a reference).
- Planum temporale tended to be **larger in the left hemisphere** in musicians **with** perfect pitch than in nonmusicians or musicians **without** perfect pitch.

#### Cellular-Level Asymmetries in Wernicke's Area
- **Galuske et al. (2000)**: compared neuron organization in part of Wernicke's area vs. the same area in the right hemisphere.
- Both hemispheres: organized into regularly spaced **columns of interconnected neurons**, interconnected by medium-range axons.
- Same column **diameter** in both hemispheres; however:
  - Columns are about **20% farther apart** in the left hemisphere.
  - Interconnected by **longer axons** in the left hemisphere.
- This unique columnar organization may be an adaptation for **processing language signals**.

## 16.4 Evolutionary Perspective of Cerebral Lateralization and Language

### Theories of the Evolution of Cerebral Lateralization

Three major theories, each postulating a different fundamental distinction between left and right hemisphere function:

#### 1. Analytic-Synthetic Theory
- **Analytic-synthetic theory of cerebral asymmetry**: two basic modes of thinking — an **analytic mode** and a **synthetic mode** — have become segregated in the left and right hemispheres, respectively.
  - **Left hemisphere**: operates logically, analytically, computer-like; analyzes stimulus information **sequentially**; abstracts relevant details and attaches verbal labels.
  - **Right hemisphere**: primarily a synthesizer; concerned with overall stimulus **configuration**; processes information in terms of **gestalts (wholes)**.
- Popularized by pop psychology.
- **Criticism**: too vague — impossible to specify the degree to which any task requires analytic vs. synthetic processing; not easily subjected to empirical tests.

#### 2. Motor Theory of Cerebral Asymmetry
- **Motor theory of cerebral asymmetry** (Kimura, 1979): the left hemisphere is specialized not specifically for speech but for the **control of fine movements**, of which speech is only one category.
- Support: lesions producing aphasia often produce other motor deficits (Serrien, Ivry, & Swinnen, 2006).
- **Criticism**: does not explain **why** motor function became lateralized in the first place (Beaton, 2003).

#### 3. Linguistic Theory of Cerebral Asymmetry
- **Linguistic theory of cerebral asymmetry**: the primary role of the left hemisphere **is language** (rather than analytic thought or fine motor control being primary).
- Based largely on studies of **deaf people** using **American Sign Language (ASL)** who suffer unilateral brain damage.
- ASL has a structure similar to spoken language.
- Key finding: left-hemisphere damage can disrupt ASL use but **not pantomime gestures** — suggesting the fundamental left-hemisphere specialization is **language** itself, not general motor skill.

> **The Case of W.L., the Man Who Experienced Aphasia for Sign Language**:
> - W.L.: congenitally deaf, right-handed male; raised using ASL.
> - History of cardiovascular disease; admitted with right-side weakness.
> - CT scan: large **left frontotemporoparietal stroke**.
> - Wife noticed uncharacteristic errors in signing and difficulty understanding others' signs.
> - Pre-stroke 2-hour videotape of an interview obtained — valuable baseline.
> - Post-stroke formal testing confirmed: specific loss in ability to use and understand sign language (**sign language aphasia**).
> - W.L. **could** produce and understand complex **pantomime gestures** — ruling out motor/sensory deficits.
> - Cognitive tests: deficit was not due to general cognitive deficits (Corina et al., 1992).
> - Conclusion: supports linguistic theory — the left hemisphere is specialized for **language per se**, regardless of modality (spoken or signed).

### When Did Cerebral Lateralization Evolve?

- Previous assumption: cerebral lateralization was an **exclusive feature of the hominin brain**.
- One version of the motor theory: left-hemisphere specialization for motor control evolved in early hominins for **tool use**, and then language evolved there due to greater motor dexterity.
- **Current evidence**: lateralization of function present in **many vertebrates** that evolved long before humans (Hopkins & Cantalupo, 2008; Hopkins, Russell, & Cantalupo, 2007).
- Suggestion: lateralization may have been present in its basic form when **vertebrates first emerged about 500 million years ago** (MacNeilage, Rogers, & Vallortigara, 2009).
- **Right-handedness**: may have evolved from a preference for using the **right side of the body for feeding** — demonstrated in species from all five vertebrate classes (fishes, reptiles, birds, amphibians, and mammals). Once hands evolved (in monkeys and apes), right-hand preference extended to feeding and other complex tasks.
- **Left-hemisphere specialization for communication**: present before human evolution:
  - Left hemisphere plays dominant role in **birdsong** (Chapter 2).
  - Left hemispheres of **dogs and monkeys** dominant in perception of conspecific calls.

---

### What Are the Survival Advantages of Cerebral Lateralization?

Two fundamental advantages:

1. **Efficiency**: it may be more efficient for neurons performing a particular function to be concentrated in **one hemisphere** (e.g., having one highly skilled hand is often more advantageous than two moderately skilled hands).

2. **Parallel processing**: two different kinds of cognitive processes may be more readily performed **simultaneously** if they are lateralized to different hemispheres (MacNeilage et al., 2009).

- Once some abilities are lateralized, further lateralization may become advantageous (e.g., motor theory: language became lateralized in the left hemisphere because fine motor control was already there).

---

### Evolution of Human Language

- **Human language**: a system allowing a **virtually limitless number of ideas** to be expressed by combining a **finite set of elements** (Hauser, Chomsky, & Fitch, 2005; Wargo, 2008).
- Other species have communication systems, but they cannot compare:
  - Monkeys have distinct warning calls for different threats but do **not combine calls to express new ideas**.
  - Birds and whales sing complex songs but do **not creatively recombine sounds to express new ideas**.
- Language has been called a **human instinct** because it is so readily and universally learned by infants:
  - At **10 months**: infants say little but can distinguish the sounds of **all human languages**.
  - At **30 months**: infants speak in complete sentences and use **over 500 words** (Golinkoff & Hirsh-Pasek, 2006).
  - Over this 20-month period, the plastic infant brain **reorganizes itself** to learn its parents' languages.
  - By 30 months: infants can recognize only the sounds of the language(s) they have been exposed to (text cuts off but implies perceptual narrowing).

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Lateralization of function** | The differential specialization of the left and right cerebral hemispheres for particular functions |
| **Cerebral commissures** | Axon bundles connecting the two cerebral hemispheres (corpus callosum, anterior commissure, hippocampal commissure, posterior commissure, massa intermedia) |
| **Corpus callosum** | The largest cerebral commissure; approximately 200 million axons; connects the two cerebral hemispheres |
| **Aphasia** | A brain-damage-produced deficit in the ability to produce or comprehend language |
| **Apraxia** | Difficulty performing voluntary movements when asked to perform them out of context, even though the same movements can be performed spontaneously |
| **Broca's area** | Area in the inferior left prefrontal cortex, just anterior to the face area of the left primary motor cortex; associated with speech production |
| **Cerebral dominance** | The (historically held) concept that one hemisphere (usually the left) controls all complex behavioral and cognitive processes; now viewed as an oversimplification |
| **Sodium amytal test (Wada test)** | Presurgical test of language lateralization involving injection of sodium amytal into the carotid artery to temporarily anesthetize one hemisphere |
| **Dichotic listening test** | A noninvasive test (Kimura, 1961) in which different stimuli are simultaneously presented to each ear; the superior ear indicates contralateral hemisphere language specialization |
| **Right-ear advantage** | In most people, more digits are correctly identified from the right ear in dichotic listening — indicative of left-hemisphere language dominance |
| **Dextrals** | Right-handers |
| **Sinestrals** | Left-handers |
| **Commissurotomy** | Surgical transection of the corpus callosum (and sometimes other commissures) used to treat intractable epilepsy |
| **Split-brain patient** | A patient whose cerebral hemispheres have been surgically separated via commissurotomy |
| **Scotoma** | An area of blindness; produced by transection of the optic chiasm in the Myers & Sperry experiments |
| **Optic chiasm** | The point at which the optic nerves from each eye partially cross; transected in the Myers & Sperry cat experiments to restrict visual information to one hemisphere |
| **Cross-cuing** | A process by which one hemisphere communicates information to the other through external behavioral cues rather than direct neural connections |
| **Helping-hand phenomenon** | In split-brain patients, when the left hand (right hemisphere) physically intervenes to redirect the right hand (left hemisphere) to correct a perceived error |
| **Chimeric figures test** | A test using photographs composed of fused half-faces of different people, revealing independent simultaneous visual completion by each hemisphere |
| **Visual completion** | The brain's ability to fill in areas of visual information not received (e.g., the blind spot or scotoma) using surrounding information |
| **Z lens (Zaidel lens)** | A contact lens that is opaque on one side, developed by Zaidel (1975) to restrict visual input to one hemisphere regardless of eye movement |
| **Interpreter (left-hemisphere)** | A hypothetical neuronal mechanism in the left hemisphere that continuously assesses patterns of events and tries to make sense of them |
| **Chimera** | In mythology, a monster composed of parts of different animals; the chimeric figures test was named after this |
| **Planum temporale** | An area of temporal lobe cortex in the posterior region of the lateral fissure; thought to play a role in language comprehension; often called Wernicke's area; larger in left hemisphere in ~65% of brains |
| **Heschl's gyrus** | Located in the lateral fissure, just anterior to the planum temporale; location of primary auditory cortex; tends to be larger in the right hemisphere |
| **Frontal operculum** | Area of frontal lobe cortex just anterior to the face area of primary motor cortex; site of Broca's area in the left hemisphere |
| **Wernicke's area** | Refers to the planum temporale; involved in language comprehension |
| **Perfect pitch** | The ability to identify the pitch of individual musical notes; associated with greater left-hemisphere planum temporale size in musicians |
| **Analytic-synthetic theory** | Theory of cerebral asymmetry holding that the left hemisphere processes information analytically and sequentially, while the right hemisphere synthesizes information holistically |
| **Motor theory of cerebral asymmetry** | Theory (Kimura, 1979) that the left hemisphere is specialized for fine motor control, of which speech is a special case |
| **Linguistic theory of cerebral asymmetry** | Theory that the primary specialization of the left hemisphere is language itself, regardless of modality |
| **American Sign Language (ASL)** | A sign language with a structure similar to spoken language; disrupted by left-hemisphere damage (sign language aphasia) |
| **Complex partial seizures** | Seizures involving altered consciousness; presented in the case of Peter |
| **Helping-hand phenomenon** | The left hand of a split-brain patient overriding the right hand's actions to correct a perceived error |
| **Agenesis of the corpus callosum** | Congenital absence of the corpus callosum; individuals may appear quite normal |
| **Intractable epilepsy** | Epilepsy that does not respond to medication; indication for commissurotomy |
| **Bilateral duplication** | The general bodily principle of having two of almost everything, one on each side |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Marc Dax (1836)** was the first to note that all speech-impaired patients had left-hemisphere damage, but his report was ignored; **Broca (1861–1864)** is credited with formally establishing left-hemisphere language dominance via the discovery of Broca's area.

2. The **sodium amytal (Wada) test** was developed by **Wada (1949)** and involves injecting sodium amytal into the carotid artery to temporarily anesthetize one hemisphere; anesthetizing the dominant (left) hemisphere causes complete mutism for 1–2 minutes; used pre-surgically to map language.

3. **Dichotic listening test (Kimura, 1961)**: the superior ear reveals contralateral hemisphere language dominance; most people show a **right-ear advantage** (left-hemisphere language dominance); the right-ear advantage is only ~55% vs. 45% (statistical, not absolute).

4. **Myers & Sperry (1953)** demonstrated that (a) the corpus callosum transfers learned information between hemispheres and (b) each hemisphere can function as an independent brain — done with cats whose optic chiasm and corpus callosum were both transected.

5. The **corpus callosum** (~200 million axons) is the largest cerebral commissure; commissurotomy involves cutting it (and sometimes other commissures) to limit epileptic discharge to one hemisphere.

6. In split-brain patients, the **left hemisphere speaks**; the **right hemisphere cannot speak** but can understand simple language (comparable to preschool level). Objects in the **left visual field** go to the **right hemisphere**; objects in the **right visual field** go to the **left hemisphere**.

7. **Cross-cuing**: split-brain patients can communicate between hemispheres via external behavioral signals (e.g., facial expression, head shake) — a major confound in split-brain research that must be controlled.

8. The **Z lens (Zaidel, 1975)** is an opaque contact lens that restricts visual input to one hemisphere irrespective of eye movements — solved the 0.1-second exposure time limitation of earlier split-brain visual testing.

9. The **left-hemisphere interpreter** (Gazzaniga): a mechanism that continuously seeks patterns and meaning; it leads the left hemisphere to seek nonexistent rules and perform worse (68%) than the right hemisphere (80%) in random-event prediction tasks.

10. **Lateralization is statistical, not absolute**: even language — the most lateralized cognitive ability — shows only a 55:45 right-ear advantage on dichotic listening; right hemispheres of split-brain patients understand many words and simple sentences.

11. **Planum temporale** (Wernicke's area) is larger in the **left** hemisphere in ~65% of brains (Geschwind & Levitsky, 1968), but this asymmetry is **not correlated** with degree of language lateralization. Heschl's gyrus (primary auditory cortex) is larger in the **right** hemisphere. **Musicians with perfect pitch** show greater left-hemisphere planum temporale asymmetry (Schlaug et al., 1995).

12. **McGlone (1977, 1980)**: males are **3 times more likely** to have aphasia after unilateral stroke than females — proposed male brains are more lateralized. However, a meta-analysis by **Sommer et al. (2004)** of 14 imaging studies found **no significant sex effect** on language lateralization.

13. Three theories of why cerebral lateralization evolved: **(1) Analytic-synthetic theory** (left = analytic/sequential; right = holistic/gestalt — too vague for testing); **(2) Motor theory** (Kimura, 1979 — left specialized for fine motor control; language a subset — doesn't explain why motor became lateralized); **(3) Linguistic theory** (left is specialized for language per se — supported by sign language aphasia in patient W.L. who lost ASL but retained pantomime).

14. Cerebral lateralization is **not uniquely human**: it is present across all five vertebrate classes (fishes, reptiles, birds, amphibians, mammals); may have originated ~500 million years ago (MacNeilage et al., 2009). Right-handedness likely evolved from right-sided feeding preferences; left-hemisphere communication dominance also present in birds (birdsong) and primates (conspecific calls).

15. **Apraxia** (Liepmann): associated with left-hemisphere damage despite bilateral symptoms; involves difficulty performing movements on command but NOT in spontaneous context — distinct from aphasia, but both contributed to the concept of **cerebral dominance**.
