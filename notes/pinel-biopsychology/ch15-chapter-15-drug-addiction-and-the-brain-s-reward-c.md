# Chapter 15: Drug Addiction and the Brain's Reward Circuits

Drug addiction is a global public health crisis: in the US alone, over 60 million people are addicted to nicotine, alcohol, or both, and 5.5 million to illegal drugs. This chapter covers core pharmacological principles, the biology of tolerance and withdrawal, profiles of five major addictive drugs, and competing neuroscientific theories of addiction, with emphasis on the brain's reward circuitry and the role of dopamine.

---

## The Case of the Drugged High School Teachers

- Illustrates the cultural hypocrisy around **drug legality vs. drug safety**: teachers concerned about student drug use were drinking whiskey and smoking cigarettes at the same convention.
- Most drug laws in Western countries were enacted in the early 20th century, BEFORE any scientific research on the topic.
- **Key insight (MCQ target):** Legal status is NOT a reliable indicator of a drug's safety or danger.

## 15.1 Basic Principles of Drug Action

### Drug Administration and Absorption

**Psychoactive drugs** are drugs that influence subjective experience and behavior by acting on the nervous system.

There are four main routes of drug administration:

| Route | Mechanism | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Oral ingestion** | Dissolves in stomach fluids; absorbed in intestine into bloodstream. Some drugs (e.g., alcohol) pass through stomach wall directly | Easiest and safest; most unpredictable (absorption affected by food type/amount) |
| **Injection** | SC = subcutaneous (fatty tissue); IM = intramuscular; IV = intravenous (directly into veins) | Strong, fast, predictable; IV preferred by addicts (drug goes directly to brain). IV risk: overdose, impurity/allergy reactions, scarring, collapsed veins |
| **Inhalation** | Absorbed through lung capillaries | Used for anesthetics, tobacco, marijuana. Difficult to regulate dose; chronic use damages lungs |
| **Absorption through mucous membranes** | Nose, mouth, rectum | e.g., cocaine snorted through nasal membranes; damages nasal membranes with chronic use |

### Drug Penetration of the Central Nervous System

- Once in bloodstream, drug is carried to CNS blood vessels.
- The **blood-brain barrier (BBB)** is a protective filter that makes it difficult for many potentially dangerous blood-borne chemicals to pass from CNS blood vessels into neurons.
- Drugs must penetrate this barrier to exert CNS effects.

### Mechanisms of Drug Action

Psychoactive drugs act on the nervous system in several ways (Koob & Bloom, 1988):

1. **Diffuse membrane action**: e.g., alcohol and general anesthetics act broadly on neural membranes throughout the CNS.
2. **Specific synaptic mechanisms**:
   - Binding to particular **synaptic receptors**
   - Influencing **synthesis, transport, release, or deactivation** of specific neurotransmitters
   - Influencing **post-synaptic receptor chain reactions** (intracellular signaling cascades)

### Drug Metabolism and Elimination

- Most drug actions terminated by **liver enzymes**.
- **Drug metabolism**: liver enzymes convert active drugs to nonactive forms (inactive **metabolites**).
- Metabolism often eliminates a drug's lipid solubility so it can no longer penetrate the BBB.
- Small amounts also excreted in: urine, sweat, feces, breath, and **mother's milk**.

### Drug Tolerance

**Drug tolerance** = a state of decreased sensitivity to a drug that develops as a result of prior exposure to it.

**Two ways to demonstrate tolerance:**
1. Same dose produces less effect than before.
2. More drug is needed to produce the same effect.

**Graphically:** Drug tolerance = shift in the **dose-response curve to the RIGHT**.

**Three important points about the specificity of drug tolerance:**

1. **Cross-tolerance**: One drug can produce tolerance to other drugs that act by the same mechanism.
2. **Selective tolerance**: Tolerance often develops to SOME effects of a drug but NOT others. Failing to understand this can be lethal (e.g., becoming tolerant to nausea from tobacco but NOT to cardiovascular effects).
3. **Drug sensitization** (Robinson, 1991): Increasing sensitivity to a drug. Drug tolerance is **not a unitary phenomenon** (Littleton, 2001) — no single mechanism underlies all examples.

**Two categories of drug tolerance:**

| Category | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Metabolic tolerance** | Results from changes that REDUCE THE AMOUNT of drug reaching its sites of action |
| **Functional tolerance** | Results from changes that REDUCE THE REACTIVITY of the sites of action to the drug |

- Tolerance to psychoactive drugs is **largely functional**.
- Functional tolerance mechanisms (Treistman & Martin, 2009):
  - Reduce **number of receptors** for the drug
  - Decrease **efficiency of binding** to existing receptors
  - Diminish **impact of receptor binding** on cell activity
- At least some adaptive neural changes involve **epigenetic mechanisms** affecting gene expression (Wang et al., 2007).

### Drug Withdrawal Effects and Physical Dependence

- **Withdrawal syndrome**: adverse physiological reaction to sudden elimination of a drug after significant prior exposure (usually several days).
- **Key principle**: Withdrawal effects are **virtually always OPPOSITE to the initial effects** of the drug.
  - Examples: withdrawal from **anticonvulsants** → convulsions; withdrawal from **sleeping pills** → insomnia.
- **Physical dependence**: condition in which individuals suffer withdrawal reactions when they stop taking a drug.

**Relationship between tolerance and withdrawal:**
- SAME adaptive neural changes that produce tolerance also produce withdrawal symptoms.
- Mechanism: drug exposure → compensatory neural changes that offset drug effects (= tolerance). When drug is removed, these compensatory changes (unopposed) manifest as withdrawal symptoms opposite to the drug's initial effects.

**Factors determining withdrawal severity:**
- Specific drug
- Duration and degree of preceding drug exposure
- Speed of drug elimination from body
- **Rule:** Longer exposure + greater doses + more rapid elimination = greater withdrawal

### Addiction: What Is It?

**Addict** = habitual drug user who continues to use a drug DESPITE:
- Adverse effects on health and social life
- Repeated efforts to stop (Volkow & Li, 2004)

**Key distinction: addiction is NOT synonymous with physical dependence**

- Common misconception: addicts are trapped in a cycle of drug-taking → withdrawal → drug-taking to relieve withdrawal.
- Evidence AGAINST this: most addicts **renew drug taking after months of enforced abstinence**, long after withdrawal has subsided.
- If withdrawal were the key driver, hospitalization for a few days would cure addiction — it does not.

**Behavioral addictions (same neural mechanisms):**
- Compulsive binge eating (Johnson & Kenny, 2010; Pelchat, 2009)
- Compulsive gambling (Volkow & Wise, 2005)
- Compulsive illicit sex
- All satisfy the same definition of addiction.

## 15.2 Role of Learning in Drug Tolerance

Learning plays a **major role** in drug tolerance. Two key phenomena:
1. **Contingent drug tolerance**
2. **Conditioned drug tolerance**

### Contingent Drug Tolerance

**Contingent drug tolerance** = tolerance develops ONLY to drug effects that are actually **experienced** during drug exposure.

**Method: Before-and-After Design**
- Two groups receive the SAME drug injections and tests.
- Group A: drug BEFORE each test (can experience the drug effect during the test).
- Group B: drug AFTER each test (cannot experience the drug effect during the test).
- Final test: all subjects receive drug then test; compare tolerance between groups.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Pinel, Mana & Kim (1989)**
> - **Species:** Rats
> - **Drug:** Alcohol
> - **Measure:** Anticonvulsant effect (amygdala stimulation to induce convulsions)
> - **Group 1:** Alcohol injection 1 hour BEFORE convulsive amygdala stimulation
> - **Group 2:** Alcohol injection 1 hour AFTER convulsive stimulation
> - **Both groups:** Same dose regimen (one injection every 2 days)
> - **Result:** Group 1 became ALMOST TOTALLY TOLERANT. Group 2 developed NO tolerance, despite identical drug exposure.
> - **Conclusion:** Tolerance is contingent on actually experiencing the drug effect. Mere drug exposure without experiencing the effect is insufficient.
> - Replicated across many species including humans (Poulos & Cappell, 1991; Wolgin & Jakubow, 2003).

### Conditioned Drug Tolerance

**Conditioned drug tolerance** = tolerance effects are maximally expressed ONLY when a drug is administered in the SAME SITUATION in which it was previously administered (McDonald & Siegel, 2004; Mitchell, Basbaum & Fields, 2000).

Also called: **situational specificity of drug tolerance**.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Crowell, Hinson & Siegel (1981)**
> - **Species:** Rats
> - **Drug:** Alcohol
> - **Design:** Both groups received 20 alcohol + 20 saline injections (alternating; 1 injection every other day)
> - **Group 1:** Alcohol in DISTINCTIVE TEST ROOM; saline in colony room
> - **Group 2:** Alcohol in COLONY ROOM; saline in distinctive test room
> - **Test:** Tolerance to hypothermic (temperature-reducing) effects in BOTH environments
> - **Result:** Tolerance only when tested in the SAME environment where alcohol was previously administered. NO tolerance in the alternate environment.
> - **Conclusion:** Drug tolerance is situationally specific.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Siegel et al. (1982) — Overdose Study**
> - **Species:** Heroin-tolerant rats
> - **Manipulation:** High dose of heroin given in NOVEL vs. USUAL injection environment
> - **Result:** Significantly MORE DEATHS in novel environment than in usual environment
> - **Conclusion:** Conditioned tolerance is context-specific. A safe dose in familiar context can be lethal in a new context — explains real-world overdose deaths.

**Siegel's Conditioned Compensatory Response Theory:**

Each drug administration = a **Pavlovian conditioning trial**:
- **Conditional Stimuli (CS)**: Environmental stimuli predicting drug (bars, washrooms, needles, other addicts, etc.)
- **Unconditional Stimuli (US)**: Drug effects
- **Conditioned Compensatory Responses (CCR)**: CS comes to elicit responses **OPPOSITE** to drug effects
- As CCRs grow stronger, they counteract drug effects → situationally specific tolerance
- **Overdose mechanism**: Massive dose in unusual setting → no CCRs → no tolerance protection → lethal dose

**Exteroceptive vs. Interoceptive Conditional Stimuli:**
- **Exteroceptive stimuli**: External, public stimuli (e.g., drug-taking environment)
- **Interoceptive stimuli**: Internal, private stimuli (feelings from drug ritual, early drug effects)
- **Important**: Even **THINKING about a drug** can evoke conditioned compensatory responses

**Situational Specificity of Sensitization:**
- **Anagnostaras & Robinson (1996)**: 10 amphetamine injections greatly increased motor activity — but ONLY when tested in the same environment as injections.

**Ramsay and Woods (1997) — Reconceptualizing Drug Conditioning:**
- Criticized assumption: drug = US; drug effect = UR.
- Their view: **US** = the DISRUPTION of neural functioning directly produced by the drug; **UR** = various COMPENSATORY REACTIONS to that disruption.
- **Predictive principle**: Once US and UR correctly identified, the **conditional response is always SIMILAR to the unconditional response** (not opposite to the drug effect).
- This resolves all confusing cases where conditioned responses seemed to go in the "wrong" direction.

**Distinction: Withdrawal Effects vs. Conditioned Compensatory Responses:**
| Feature | Withdrawal Effects | Conditioned Compensatory Responses |
|---|---|---|
| Direction | Opposite to drug effects | Opposite to drug effects |
| Trigger | Elimination of drug from body | Drug-predictive environmental cues (drug absent) |
| Context dependency | Less context-specific | Highly context-specific |

## 15.3 Five Commonly Abused Drugs

### Tobacco

- **Major psychoactive ingredient**: **Nicotine**
- Also contains ~4,000 chemicals: **tar**
- **Mechanism**: Nicotine acts on **nicotinic cholinergic receptors** in the brain (Benowitz, 2008)
- **Leading preventable cause of death** in Western countries
- US: ~400,000 premature deaths/year (approximately 1 in every 5 deaths) (CDC, 2008b)

**Differential effects (tolerance):**
- Nonsmokers (no tolerance): nausea, vomiting, coughing, sweating, abdominal cramps, dizziness, flushing, diarrhea
- Smokers (tolerant): more relaxed, more alert, less hungry after a cigarette

**Addiction statistics:**
| Statistic | Value |
|---|---|
| % of experimenters who become addicted | ~70% |
| Comparison: alcohol | ~10% |
| Comparison: heroin | ~30% |
| Typical time to develop addiction | Within weeks (Di Franza, 2008) |
| % of quit attempts successful at 2+ years | ~20% |
| Heritability (twin studies) | ~65% (Lerman et al., 1999; True et al., 1999) |

**Withdrawal effects:** Depression, anxiety, restlessness, irritability, constipation, difficulties sleeping and concentrating.

**Smoker's Syndrome:** Chest pain, labored breathing, wheezing, coughing, heightened susceptibility to respiratory infections.

**Health risks of chronic tobacco use:**
- **Respiratory**: pneumonia, **bronchitis** (chronic bronchiole inflammation), **emphysema** (loss of lung elasticity), lung cancer
- **Cancer**: larynx, mouth, esophagus, kidneys, pancreas, bladder, stomach
- **Cardiovascular**: heart attack, stroke
- **Psychological**: Smokers MORE tense than nonsmokers; apparent relaxation is merely temporary reversal of addiction-induced stress (Parrott, 1999). More prone to panic attacks (Zvolensky & Bernstein, 2005).

**Buerger's Disease:**
- Incidence: ~15 per 100,000; mostly male smokers
- Blood vessel constriction especially in legs
- Continued smoking → gangrene → amputation (toes → foot → knee → hip; can affect both legs)
- Stopping smoking halts progression; yet many patients continue to smoke even after amputations (Brecher, 1972)

**Secondhand smoke / Teratogenic effects:**
- **Nicotine is a teratogen**
- Smoking during pregnancy: increased miscarriage, stillbirth, early child death
- Breastfed infants: blood nicotine levels can equal mother's blood levels
- Secondhand smoke: increases heart disease and cancer in non-smokers

**Treatment:**
- Nicotine patches: marginally effective short-term (Shiffman & Ferguson, 2008)
- Smokers who quit before age 30 live almost as long as lifetime non-smokers (Doll et al., 2004)

### Alcohol

**Epidemiology:**
- Involved in >3% of all US deaths (Mokdad et al., 2004)
- ~13 million heavy US users; ~80,000 US deaths/year (CDC, 2008a)

**Pharmacology:**
- Molecules small, soluble in both fat AND water → invades all body tissues
- Classified as a **depressant** (at moderate-high doses depresses neural firing)
- At LOW doses: can stimulate neural firing, facilitate social interaction
- Heritability: ~55% (McGue, 1999); several genes identified (Nurnberger & Bierut, 2007)

**Dose-dependent effects:**
- Moderate doses: cognitive/perceptual/verbal/motor impairment, loss of control
- Blood alcohol 0.5%: risk of death from respiratory depression
- Red facial flush: vasodilation → heat loss → **hypothermia**
- Acts as a **diuretic** (increases urine production by kidneys)

**Tolerance:** Mostly **functional**; liver metabolic tolerance plays only a minor role.

**Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome (Three Phases):**

| Phase | Timing | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1** | 5-6 hours after cessation | Severe tremors, agitation, headache, nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, profuse sweating, hallucinations |
| **Phase 2** | 15-30 hours after cessation | Convulsive activity (defining feature) |
| **Phase 3 (Delirium Tremens / DTs)** | 1-2 days after cessation; lasts 3-4 days | Disturbing hallucinations, bizarre delusions, agitation, confusion, hyperthermia, tachycardia |

- **Convulsions and DTs can be LETHAL** — more dangerous than opiate withdrawal.

**Brain effects of chronic alcohol:**
- **Direct**: Widespread brain damage (Mechtcheriakov et al., 2007)
- **Indirect**:
  - **Korsakoff's syndrome**: via thiamine deficiency; memory loss, sensory/motor dysfunction, severe dementia
  - Increased stroke susceptibility (Rehm, 2006)
  - Reduces calcium ion flow into neurons
  - Interferes with second messengers
  - Disrupts **GABAergic** and **glutaminergic** transmission
  - Triggers **apoptosis** (Farber & Olney, 2003; Ikonomidou et al., 2000)

**Systemic health effects:**
- **Cirrhosis** (liver scarring): major cause of death in heavy drinkers
- Heart muscle erosion → heart attack risk
- Irritates digestive tract → oral/liver cancer, stomach ulcers, pancreatitis, gastritis
- 20,000+ US deaths/year from alcohol-related traffic accidents

**Low-to-moderate drinking risks:**
- Even 1-2 drinks/day: elevated risk of MOST cancers (breast, prostate, ovary, skin) (Allen et al., 2009; Bagnardi et al., 2001)

**Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS):**
- Alcohol is a **teratogen**; penetrates placental membrane
- **Symptoms**: brain damage, mental retardation, poor coordination, poor muscle tone, low birth weight, retarded growth, physical deformity
- No safe TIME or AMOUNT during pregnancy (Farber & Olney, 2003; Guerri, 2002)
- Even moderate drinking: cognitive problems in offspring without full FAS (Korkman et al., 2003)

**Disulfiram (Antabuse):**
- Mechanism: blocks alcohol metabolism → accumulation of **acetaldehyde**
- High acetaldehyde: flushing, dizziness, headache, vomiting, breathing difficulty
- NOT a cure: alcoholics stop taking it when they want to drink
- Useful only in supervised hospital/outpatient settings (Brewer, 2007)

**Alcohol and Heart Disease — Revised Understanding:**
- Fillmore et al. (2006); Stockwell et al. (2007): meta-analysis using LIFETIME abstainers as controls found **moderate drinking does NOT prevent coronary heart disease**.
- Earlier positive findings confounded by ex-drinkers (who quit due to illness) in abstainer groups.

### Marijuana

**Botanical source**: Dried leaves and flowers of **Cannabis sativa** (common hemp plant).

**Routes of administration:**
- Smoking: joint or pipe
- Oral: must be baked in fat-rich substrate for GI absorption

**Active compounds:**
- Primary psychoactive ingredient: **THC (delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol)**
- 80+ **cannabinoids** in marijuana (same chemical class as THC)
- **Hashish**: dried extracted resin (dark, corklike)
- **Hash oil**: further processed; most potent form

**Historical context:**
- Written records: 6,000 years in China (rope, grain, psychoactive/medicinal)
- Medieval Europe: grown primarily for rope manufacture
- American colonies: grown as cash crop; George Washington grew cannabis
- Word "marijuana" and smoking practice introduced to southern US in early 20th century
- 1926 New Orleans newspaper article: misrepresented as causing violent criminal behavior → rapid legislation
- Misclassified as a **narcotic** in many states (incorrectly; narcotics legally = opiates)

**Effects at typical social doses:**
- Increased sense of well-being; restlessness then dreamy relaxation
- Expanded sense of space and time
- Enhanced perception: touch, sight, smell, taste, sound
- Hunger (especially craving for sweets)
- Subtle changes in thought formation and expression
- Observer would NOT notice intoxication at low doses (National Commission on Marijuana and Drug Abuse, 1972)

**Effects at HIGH doses:**
- Short-term memory impairment
- Impaired multi-step task performance
- Slurred speech
- Sense of unreality, emotional intensification, sensory distortion, paranoia, motor impairment

**Addiction potential: LOW**
- Only ~10% of users use daily
- Most try in teens, curtail by 30s-40s (Room et al., 2010)
- Withdrawal symptoms rare except in contrived lab settings with massive oral doses

**Health hazards (documented):**
1. **Respiratory problems** (~10% of chronic regular smokers): cough, bronchitis, asthma
2. **Tachycardia**: single large doses can trigger heart attacks in susceptible individuals

**Brain damage question:**
- Most evidence: **NEGATIVE** (no documented brain damage)
- Exception: **Yücel et al. (2008)** — MRI study: men with extreme exposure (5+ joints/day, ~20 years) had **reduced hippocampus and amygdala volumes**. Needs replication; causation uncertain.

**Memory:**
- Deficits are acute effects that disappear after weeks of abstinence
- General consensus: marijuana NOT associated with substantial permanent memory problems (Grant et al., 2003; Jager et al., 2006; Iversen, 2005)
- Heavy adolescent users: some cognitive deficits, but Pope et al. (2003): lower verbal IQ predisposes to heavy marijuana use (confound)

**Schizophrenia correlation:**
- Best study: Swedish males followed 25 years (Zammit et al., 2002) — higher schizophrenia incidence among heavy users
- BUT: correlation, not causation. Alternative: early-stage schizophrenia may increase susceptibility to marijuana.

**Therapeutic effects of THC:**
- Suppress nausea/vomiting in cancer patients
- Stimulate appetite in AIDS patients (DiMarzo & Matias, 2005)
- Block seizures
- Dilate bronchioles in asthmatics
- Decrease **glaucoma** severity (elevated intraocular fluid pressure)
- Reduce anxiety, some pain, MS symptoms
- Medical use: no documented adverse side effects

**Cannabinoid Receptors:**
| Receptor | Location | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **CB1** | Brain (widespread) | Most prevalent G-protein linked receptor in the brain; mediates most psychoactive effects |
| **CB2** | Brain stem + immune system cells | Van Sickle et al., 2005 |

- **Endocannabinoids**: endogenous cannabinoid neurotransmitters
- First isolated: **Anandamide** ("internal bliss" in Sanskrit) (Nicoll & Alger, 2004)

### Cocaine and Other Stimulants

**Stimulants** = drugs producing general increases in neural and behavioral activity.

**Cocaine preparation:**
- Source: **coca bush** (primarily Peru and Bolivia)
- Traditional: coca paste eaten
- Modern: **cocaine hydrochloride** (white powder; snorted or injected)
- **Crack**: base form via baking soda process; impure, cheap, smokable
- ~36 million Americans have used cocaine or crack (SAMSHA, 2009)

**Psychological effects:**
- Wave of well-being; self-confidence, alertness, energy; friendliness, talkativeness
- Reduced desire for food and sleep

**Cocaine sprees (binges):**
- Extremely high intake maintained for 1-2 days
- Rapidly increasing tolerance to euphoria → escalating doses
- **Cocaine psychosis**: psychotic behavior from cocaine sprees; often **mistakenly diagnosed as paranoid schizophrenia**
- Medical risks during spree: loss of consciousness, seizures, respiratory arrest, heart attack, stroke

**Tolerance and sensitization with cocaine:**
- Tolerance develops to: euphoria
- **Sensitization** develops to: motor and convulsive effects (Robinson & Berridge, 1993)

**Withdrawal from cocaine:** Relatively mild — negative mood swing, insomnia.

**Other stimulants:**

| Drug | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Amphetamine (speed)** | Wide illicit use since 1960s; d-amphetamine (dextroamphetamine); produces **amphetamine psychosis** |
| **Methamphetamine (meth/ice/crystal)** | More potent; smokable crystalline form (Cho, 1990) |
| **MDMA (ecstasy)** | 3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine; taken orally (Baylen & Rosenberg, 2006) |

**Primary mechanism of cocaine action:**
- **Blockade of dopamine transporters** → dopamine accumulates in synapse → prolonged dopaminergic stimulation
- Other stimulants: **increase the release of monoamines** into synapses (Sulzer et al., 2005)

**Long-term health effects:**
- MDMA: neuropsychological test deficits; dopaminergic/serotonergic deficiencies; brain imaging abnormalities
- **Methamphetamine structural brain damage**: MRI study — decreased volume in multiple brain regions after ~10 years of use (Thompson et al., 2004)
- Cardiac: electrocardiographic abnormalities (Haning & Goebert, 2007)
- Prenatal exposure: behavioral, neurological, cardiovascular problems in infants

### The Opiates: Heroin and Morphine

**Source**: **Opium** = dried sap from seed pods of **opium poppy**

**Key psychoactive ingredients of opium:**
- **Morphine** (most potent)
- **Codeine** (weaker relative)

**Opiates** act by binding to receptors for **endorphins** and **enkephalins** (endogenous opiates).

**Historical timeline (MCQ-heavy):**

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| Before 4000 B.C. | Eating opium spreads from Middle East |
| 1644 | Emperor of China bans tobacco; opium smoking increases; British East India Company smuggles opium into China |
| 1803 | Morphine isolated from opium |
| 1830s | Morphine commercially available |
| 1856 | Hypodermic needle invented |
| 1870 | Heroin synthesized (two acetyl groups added to morphine → greatly increased BBB penetration) |
| Early 20th century | Opium legal; found in cakes, candies, wines, medicines (laudanum, Godfrey's Cordial, Mrs. Winslow's Soothing Syrup, Street's Infant Quietness) |
| 1898 | Heroin marketed by **Bayer Drug Company** as "superior aspirin"; no prescription required; falsely claimed NOT addictive |
| 1914 | **Harrison Narcotics Act**: made opium, morphine, cocaine illegal in US — heroin NOT included |
| 1924 | US Congress made heroin illegal (possession, sale, use) |

**Laudanum** = very popular preparation of opium and alcohol.

**Why heroin is more potent than morphine:** Two acetyl groups increase lipid solubility → greater BBB penetration.

**Effects:**
- **Rush** (after IV injection): wave of intense abdominal, orgasmic pleasure
- Evolves into: serene, drowsy euphoria
- Medical uses: analgesic, cough suppressant, antidiarrheal

**Opiate Withdrawal Syndrome:**
- Begins: **6-12 hours after last dose**
- Early signs: restlessness, pacing, watering eyes, running nose, yawning, sweating
- Then: fitful sleep for several hours
- Severe phase: chills, shivering, **gooseflesh** ("going cold turkey"), nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, cramps, dilated pupils, tremor, **muscle spasms** ("kicking the habit")
- **Peak severity**: 2nd-3rd day after last dose
- **Resolution**: by day 7, nearly gone
- **Severity comparison**: comparable to bad flu; NOT as dangerous as barbiturate or alcohol withdrawal (McKim, 1986)

**Direct health hazards (relatively minor):**
- Constipation, pupil constriction, menstrual irregularity, reduced libido
- Many addicts use pure heroin/morphine for years with no serious ill effects
- Opiate addiction more prevalent among **doctors, nurses, and dentists** than other professionals (Brewster, 1986)
- Example: **Dr. William Stewart Halsted** — founder of Johns Hopkins Medical School, "father of modern surgery," addicted to morphine throughout his career

**Indirect health hazards (from illegal status):**
- Poverty, petty crime, prostitution
- AIDS, hepatitis, syphilis, gonorrhea (unsterile needles, unsafe sex)
- Unknown drug purity
- Treatment success rate: only ~10%

**Heroin overdose deaths — three points of confusion:**
1. Many "heroin overdose" deaths actually involve **drug interactions** (alcohol/benzodiazepines with low heroin levels; Darke et al., 2000)
2. Some deaths from **illicit drug purity variation** (toxic adulterants or unexpectedly pure batches)
3. Recent US increase in opiate overdose deaths almost entirely due to **legal synthetic opioid analgesics** (Oxycontin, Lorcet), NOT heroin (Manchikanti, 2007; Paulozzi et al., 2006)

**Treatments for heroin addiction:**

| Treatment | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Methadone** | Opiate; less euphoric than heroin; blocks withdrawal | Most common; but itself an opiate; high dropout; ~10% success (Zador, 2007) |
| **Buprenorphine** | High, long-lasting affinity for opiate receptors; blocks other opiates without powerful euphoria | As effective as methadone (Davids & Gaspar, 2004; Gerra et al., 2004) |
| **Swiss heroin clinics (1994)** | Physician-administered heroin for small fee as part of total treatment package | Uniformly positive results: reduced crime, improved health, many returned to work/family, new addictions declined. Model adopted in Canada, Norway, Netherlands, Germany |

## 15.4 Biopsychological Approaches to Theories of Addiction

### Physical-Dependence and Positive-Incentive Perspectives of Addiction

**Physical-Dependence Theory (traditional/inadequate view):**
- Addiction maintained by need to prevent/alleviate withdrawal symptoms
- Cycle: drug use → tolerance/dependence → withdrawal → more drug to relieve withdrawal
- **Problems:**
  1. Addicts relapse **after months of enforced abstinence** when withdrawal is long gone
  2. Many physically dependent patients (e.g., pain patients on morphine) do NOT become addicts
  3. Some drugs causing physical dependence (e.g., certain antihypertensives) are not addictive

**Positive-Incentive Theory (current, better-supported view):**
- Addiction driven by the **anticipated pleasure** (positive incentive value) of the drug
- Key factor is **craving**, not avoidance of withdrawal
- Addicts return to drugs because they WANT the high, not to flee withdrawal

### From Pleasure to Compulsion: Incentive-Sensitization Theory

**Incentive-Sensitization Theory** (Robinson & Berridge, 1993) — most influential current theory:

Two **distinct brain systems**:

| System | Mediates | Brain Substrate | Changes with Addiction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"Wanting"** (incentive salience) | Drug craving, motivational pull | Mesolimbic dopamine system (VTA → Nucleus Accumbens) | **SENSITIZES** (increases) with repeated use |
| **"Liking"** (hedonic pleasure) | Actual enjoyment from drug | Opioid systems (nucleus accumbens, ventral pallidum) | **HABITUATES** (decreases) with repeated use |

**Key implications:**
- With addiction: wanting INCREASES while liking DECREASES
- Addict desperately craves drug despite getting less pleasure
- Drug-associated cues activate sensitized wanting system → intense craving even without drug
- Mesolimbic dopamine system becomes **sensitized** to drug-related stimuli

**Brain Reward Circuits:**

**Mesolimbic Dopamine System** (central reward pathway):
- **VTA (Ventral Tegmental Area)** → **Nucleus Accumbens (NAc)** → **Prefrontal Cortex**
- All major addictive drugs ultimately increase dopamine in the nucleus accumbens

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Olds & Milner (1954) — Intracranial Self-Stimulation (ICSS)**
> - Rats pressed lever to receive electrical brain stimulation
> - Would press thousands of times per hour, forgoing food, water, and sleep
> - Most reinforcing sites: **lateral hypothalamus, medial forebrain bundle, VTA**
> - Revealed existence of a **brain reward/pleasure system**

**Nucleus Accumbens (NAc):** Critical hub for reward, motivation, addiction; receives dopamine from VTA; projects to motor systems to direct behavior toward rewards.

**Common final pathway of all addictive drugs:**

| Drug | Primary Mechanism | Effect on Dopamine in NAc |
|---|---|---|
| Cocaine | Blocks dopamine transporter | Increased synaptic dopamine |
| Amphetamine | Increases dopamine release | Increased dopamine |
| Heroin/opiates | Disinhibits VTA dopamine neurons | Increased dopamine from VTA |
| Nicotine | Activates nicotinic receptors on VTA neurons | Increased dopamine release |
| Alcohol | Multiple mechanisms (GABA-A, glutamate, opioid receptors) | Increased dopamine |
| Marijuana (THC) | CB1 receptors on GABAergic interneurons → disinhibition of VTA | Increased dopamine |

### Relapse and Its Causes

Three major triggers of relapse (even after prolonged abstinence):

1. **Stress**: activates CRF pathways and HPA axis; sensitizes dopamine reward circuits
2. **Drug-associated cues**: conditioned stimuli (places, people, paraphernalia) trigger intense craving via sensitized mesolimbic dopamine system (**cue-induced craving**)
3. **Priming doses**: small amounts of the drug itself can reinstate full drug-seeking behavior

**Clinical implication:** Successful treatment must address ALL three relapse triggers.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Routes of administration by speed to CNS (fastest to slowest):** IV injection > inhalation > IM injection > SC injection > oral ingestion. IV has greatest risk of overdose, impurity reactions, and collapsed veins.

2. **Drug tolerance = rightward shift in dose-response curve.** Two types: metabolic (less drug reaches target) and functional (target less reactive). Tolerance to psychoactive drugs is LARGELY functional. Cross-tolerance: drugs acting by same mechanism share tolerance. Sensitization = the OPPOSITE of tolerance (increasing sensitivity with repeated exposure; Robinson, 1991).

3. **Withdrawal effects are ALWAYS opposite to initial drug effects** (compensatory neural adaptations underlying both tolerance and withdrawal). The most DANGEROUS withdrawals: alcohol (DTs, convulsions — lethal) and barbiturates. Opiate withdrawal: uncomfortable but not life-threatening (comparable to bad flu; peaks day 2-3, resolves by day 7).

4. **Addiction is NOT synonymous with physical dependence.** Addicts relapse after months of abstinence (withdrawal long over). Positive-incentive theory (anticipated pleasure/craving) better explains addiction than physical-dependence theory (withdrawal avoidance).

5. **Contingent tolerance (Pinel et al., 1989):** Tolerance only to effects actually experienced. Before-and-after design: drug before test → tolerance develops; drug after test → no tolerance, despite identical drug exposure.

6. **Conditioned tolerance (Crowell et al., 1981; Siegel):** Tolerance is situationally specific. Siegel's conditioned compensatory response theory: environmental cues (CS) elicit responses OPPOSITE to drug effects (CCRs) → counteract drug effects → tolerance. Novel environment = no CCRs = overdose risk. Siegel et al. (1982): heroin-tolerant rats die more in novel environment.

7. **Tobacco addiction rate = ~70%** (highest of the five drugs). Heroin ~30%, alcohol ~10%. Nicotine addiction develops within weeks (Di Franza, 2008). Heritability ~65%. Nicotine is a teratogen. Only ~20% of quit attempts succeed at 2+ years. Smokers are MORE tense than nonsmokers; relaxation is reversal of addiction-induced stress (Parrott, 1999).

8. **Alcohol withdrawal has THREE phases: Phase 1 (5-6 hrs: tremors, sweating, hallucinations), Phase 2 (15-30 hrs: convulsions), Phase 3 = Delirium Tremens (1-2 days, lasts 3-4 days: hallucinations, delusions, hyperthermia, tachycardia). DTs and convulsions can be LETHAL.** FAS: no safe time or amount of alcohol during pregnancy. Moderate drinking does NOT protect against heart disease (Fillmore et al., 2006 — correct control group: lifetime abstainers).

9. **CB1 = most prevalent G-protein linked receptor in the brain.** THC acts on CB1. Anandamide = first endocannabinoid (Sanskrit: "internal bliss"). CB2 = brain stem + immune cells. Marijuana addiction potential LOW; ~10% of users use daily. Yücel et al. (2008): extreme use (5+ joints/day, 20 years) linked to reduced hippocampus and amygdala volumes.

10. **Cocaine's primary mechanism = blockade of dopamine transporters** → dopamine accumulates in synapse. Cocaine sensitizes motor/convulsive effects while tolerance develops to euphoria (Robinson & Berridge, 1993). Cocaine psychosis often mistaken for paranoid schizophrenia. Methamphetamine causes structural brain volume loss (Thompson et al., 2004).

11. **Heroin synthesized 1870 (two acetyl groups on morphine = better BBB penetration); marketed by Bayer 1898 as "superior aspirin." NOT included in 1914 Harrison Narcotics Act (falsely claimed non-addictive).** Rush = intense abdominal/orgasmic pleasure then serene drowsy euphoria. "Going cold turkey" = gooseflesh; "kicking the habit" = leg muscle spasms. Methadone and buprenorphine are the two main pharmacological treatments.

12. **Incentive-Sensitization Theory (Robinson & Berridge, 1993):** "Wanting" (incentive salience, mesolimbic dopamine) SENSITIZES with addiction; "Liking" (hedonic pleasure, opioid systems) HABITUATES. Explains compulsive drug seeking despite diminishing pleasure.

13. **Mesolimbic dopamine system = VTA → Nucleus Accumbens → Prefrontal Cortex.** ALL major addictive drugs increase dopamine in the nucleus accumbens. ICSS experiment (Olds & Milner, 1954) revealed brain reward system. Three main relapse triggers: stress, drug-associated cues, priming doses.

14. **Ramsay & Woods (1997):** US in drug conditioning = neural DISRUPTION (not the drug); UR = COMPENSATORY REACTION. Conditional response is always SIMILAR to the UR. Resolves confusion about direction of conditioned drug effects.

15. **Disulfiram (Antabuse):** blocks alcohol metabolism → acetaldehyde accumulation → aversive symptoms. NOT a cure. Buprenorphine: has high, long-lasting affinity for opiate receptors, blocks other opiates without powerful euphoria; as effective as methadone. Swiss heroin clinic model (1994): clinician-administered heroin reduced crime, improved health, and reduced new heroin addictions.
