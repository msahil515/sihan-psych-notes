# Chapter 15: Drug Addiction and the Brain's Reward Circuits

Covers pharmacological principles, the biology of tolerance and withdrawal, five major addictive drugs, and theories of addiction, emphasizing brain reward circuitry and dopamine.

---

## The Case of the Drugged High School Teachers
- Cultural hypocrisy of **drug legality vs. safety**: teachers worried about students were drinking and smoking. Most drug laws predate research.
- **MCQ target:** legal status is NOT a reliable indicator of a drug's safety.

---

## 15.1 Basic Principles of Drug Action

### Administration and Absorption
- **Psychoactive drugs**: influence experience/behavior via the nervous system.

| Route | Mechanism | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Oral ingestion** | Absorbed in intestine (alcohol via stomach wall) | Easiest, safest; most unpredictable |
| **Injection** | SC / IM / IV | Strong, fast; IV preferred by addicts (direct to brain); risks overdose, impurity, collapsed veins |
| **Inhalation** | Lung capillaries | Anesthetics, tobacco, marijuana; hard to dose; lung damage |
| **Mucous membranes** | Nose, mouth, rectum | e.g., snorted cocaine; damages nasal membranes |

### CNS Penetration
- The **blood-brain barrier (BBB)** filters out many blood-borne chemicals; drugs must penetrate it.

### Mechanisms (Koob & Bloom, 1988)
1. **Diffuse membrane action** (alcohol, general anesthetics).
2. **Specific synaptic mechanisms**: bind receptors; affect NT synthesis/transport/release/deactivation; affect post-synaptic signaling cascades.

### Metabolism and Elimination
- Most actions terminated by **liver enzymes** (**drug metabolism** → inactive **metabolites**, often removing lipid solubility). Small amounts excreted in urine, sweat, feces, breath, **mother's milk**.

### Drug Tolerance
- **Drug tolerance**: decreased sensitivity from prior exposure. Shown by less effect at the same dose, or more drug needed. Graphically = **rightward shift in dose-response curve**.
- **Cross-tolerance**: tolerance transfers to drugs with the same mechanism. **Selective tolerance**: to some effects but not others (can be lethal). **Drug sensitization** (Robinson, 1991): increasing sensitivity. Tolerance is **not unitary** (Littleton, 2001).

| Category | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Metabolic tolerance** | Less drug reaches its sites of action |
| **Functional tolerance** | Sites of action become less reactive |

- Psychoactive drug tolerance is **largely functional** (fewer receptors, weaker binding, reduced impact; Treistman & Martin, 2009); some changes are **epigenetic** (Wang et al., 2007).

### Withdrawal and Physical Dependence
- **Withdrawal syndrome**: adverse reaction to sudden drug elimination. **Effects are virtually always OPPOSITE to the drug's initial effects** (anticonvulsant withdrawal → convulsions).
- **Physical dependence**: withdrawal occurs on stopping.
- The **same compensatory neural changes** cause both tolerance and withdrawal. Severity ↑ with longer exposure, greater doses, and faster elimination.

### Addiction
- **Addict**: continues use despite adverse effects and repeated attempts to stop (Volkow & Li, 2004).
- **Addiction ≠ physical dependence**: most addicts relapse after **months of abstinence** (long after withdrawal subsides).
- **Behavioral addictions** (same mechanisms): binge eating (Johnson & Kenny, 2010), gambling, compulsive sex.

## 15.2 Role of Learning in Drug Tolerance

### Contingent Drug Tolerance
- **Contingent drug tolerance**: develops only to drug effects actually **experienced** during exposure. Tested with the **before-and-after design** (drug before vs. after the test).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Pinel, Mana & Kim (1989):** rats, alcohol's anticonvulsant effect. Group 1 (alcohol **before** convulsive amygdala stimulation) became **almost totally tolerant**; Group 2 (alcohol **after**) developed **no tolerance** despite identical exposure. Replicated across species (Poulos & Cappell, 1991).

### Conditioned Drug Tolerance
- **Conditioned drug tolerance** (situational specificity): tolerance maximally expressed only in the **same situation** as prior administrations (McDonald & Siegel, 2004).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Crowell, Hinson & Siegel (1981):** rats got alcohol in one room, saline in another. Tolerance to alcohol's hypothermic effect appeared **only in the alcohol-associated environment**.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Siegel et al. (1982):** heroin-tolerant rats given a high dose in a **novel** vs. usual environment had **significantly more deaths** in the novel one — explains real-world overdoses.

- **Siegel's conditioned compensatory response theory**: each administration is a Pavlovian trial; **CS** (environment, needles, etc.) → **conditioned compensatory responses (CCRs)** **opposite** to drug effects, producing situational tolerance. A massive dose in a novel setting → no CCRs → lethal.
- **Exteroceptive** (external) vs. **interoceptive** (internal) stimuli; even **thinking** about a drug can evoke CCRs.
- **Anagnostaras & Robinson (1996)**: amphetamine sensitization is also situationally specific.
- **Ramsay & Woods (1997)**: the **US** = the drug's **neural disruption**, the **UR** = the **compensatory reaction**; once correctly identified, the conditioned response is always **similar** to the UR.

| Feature | Withdrawal Effects | Conditioned Compensatory Responses |
|---|---|---|
| Direction | Opposite to drug | Opposite to drug |
| Trigger | Drug elimination | Drug-predictive cues (drug absent) |
| Context | Less specific | Highly context-specific |

## 15.3 Five Commonly Abused Drugs

### Tobacco
- Main ingredient: **nicotine** (acts on **nicotinic cholinergic receptors**; Benowitz, 2008); also ~4,000 chemicals = **tar**. **Leading preventable cause of death**; ~400,000 US deaths/year.
- Nonsmokers: nausea, vomiting, dizziness. Smokers (tolerant): relaxed, alert, less hungry.

| Statistic | Value |
|---|---|
| % experimenters who become addicted | ~70% (vs. alcohol ~10%, heroin ~30%) |
| Time to addiction | Within weeks (Di Franza, 2008) |
| Quit attempts successful at 2+ years | ~20% |
| Heritability | ~65% (Lerman et al., 1999) |

- **Withdrawal**: depression, anxiety, irritability, sleep/concentration difficulty.
- **Smoker's syndrome**: chest pain, wheezing, cough, respiratory susceptibility.
- **Risks**: pneumonia, **bronchitis**, **emphysema**, lung/larynx/mouth/esophagus/bladder cancers, heart attack, stroke. Smokers are **more tense** than nonsmokers (Parrott, 1999); more panic attacks (Zvolensky & Bernstein, 2005).
- **Buerger's disease** (~15/100,000, mostly male smokers): vessel constriction → gangrene → amputation; stops if smoking ceases.
- **Nicotine is a teratogen**; pregnancy → miscarriage/stillbirth; secondhand smoke increases heart disease/cancer.
- **Treatment**: nicotine patches marginally effective; quitting before 30 nearly restores life expectancy (Doll et al., 2004).

### Alcohol
- >3% of US deaths; ~13 million heavy users. Small, fat- and water-soluble → invades all tissues. A **depressant** at moderate/high doses; stimulant at low doses. Heritability ~55% (McGue, 1999).
- Effects: cognitive/motor impairment; blood alcohol 0.5% → death from respiratory depression; vasodilation → **hypothermia**; acts as a **diuretic**.
- Tolerance mostly **functional**.

| Phase | Timing | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1** | 5-6 h | Tremors, agitation, nausea, sweating, hallucinations |
| **Phase 2** | 15-30 h | Convulsions |
| **Phase 3 (Delirium Tremens)** | 1-2 days, lasts 3-4 | Hallucinations, delusions, agitation, hyperthermia, tachycardia |

- **Convulsions and DTs can be LETHAL** (more dangerous than opiate withdrawal).
- **Brain effects**: direct widespread damage; indirect **Korsakoff's syndrome** (thiamine deficiency → memory loss/dementia); disrupts **GABAergic/glutaminergic** transmission; triggers **apoptosis**.
- **Systemic**: **cirrhosis** (major death cause), heart damage, oral/liver cancer, ulcers, **pancreatitis**, **gastritis**, 20,000+ traffic deaths/year. Even 1-2 drinks/day raises most cancer risks.
- **Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS)**: alcohol is a **teratogen**; brain damage, mental retardation, poor coordination, low birth weight, deformity; **no safe time or amount**.
- **Disulfiram (Antabuse)**: blocks alcohol metabolism → **acetaldehyde** accumulation → aversive effects; not a cure.
- **Heart disease (revised)**: Fillmore et al. (2006) using **lifetime abstainers** as controls found moderate drinking does NOT prevent coronary disease (earlier findings confounded by sick ex-drinkers).

### Marijuana
- From **Cannabis sativa**; primary ingredient **THC (delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol)**; 80+ **cannabinoids**. **Hashish** = dried resin; **hash oil** = most potent. Historically grown for rope; misclassified as a **narcotic**.
- **Social doses**: well-being, relaxation, expanded time/space sense, enhanced perception, hunger; not obvious to observers. **High doses**: short-term memory impairment, slurred speech, unreality, paranoia, motor impairment.
- **Addiction potential LOW**: ~10% use daily; withdrawal rare.
- **Hazards**: respiratory problems (~10% of chronic smokers); **tachycardia**. Brain damage evidence mostly **negative**; exception **Yücel et al. (2008)** — extreme use (5+ joints/day, ~20 years) linked to **reduced hippocampus/amygdala volumes** (needs replication).
- **Memory** deficits are acute and reversible (Grant et al., 2003). **Schizophrenia**: heavy use correlated (Zammit et al., 2002) but correlation, not causation (early schizophrenia may increase use).
- **Therapeutic THC**: suppresses nausea (cancer), stimulates appetite (AIDS), blocks seizures, dilates bronchioles, decreases **glaucoma** pressure, reduces anxiety/pain/MS symptoms.

| Receptor | Location |
|---|---|
| **CB1** | Brain (widespread); **most prevalent G-protein-linked receptor in the brain**; most psychoactive effects |
| **CB2** | Brain stem + immune cells (Van Sickle et al., 2005) |

- **Endocannabinoids**: endogenous cannabinoids; first isolated = **Anandamide** (Sanskrit "internal bliss"; Nicoll & Alger, 2004).

### Cocaine and Other Stimulants
- **Stimulants**: general increases in neural/behavioral activity. Cocaine from the **coca bush**; **cocaine hydrochloride** (powder); **crack** (smokable base).
- Effects: well-being, confidence, energy; reduced food/sleep desire.
- **Cocaine sprees**: rapid euphoria tolerance → escalating doses; **cocaine psychosis** (often misdiagnosed as **paranoid schizophrenia**); risks: seizures, respiratory arrest, heart attack, stroke.
- **Tolerance** to euphoria, **sensitization** to motor/convulsive effects (Robinson & Berridge, 1993). Withdrawal relatively mild.
- Others: **amphetamine** (amphetamine psychosis), **methamphetamine**, **MDMA (ecstasy)**.
- **Mechanism**: cocaine **blocks dopamine transporters** → dopamine accumulates; other stimulants **increase monoamine release** (Sulzer et al., 2005).
- **Methamphetamine** causes structural brain volume loss after ~10 years (Thompson et al., 2004).

### Opiates: Heroin and Morphine
- **Opium** = dried poppy sap; key ingredients **morphine** (most potent) and **codeine**. Opiates bind **endorphin/enkephalin** receptors.

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| 1803 | Morphine isolated |
| 1856 | Hypodermic needle invented |
| 1870 | Heroin synthesized (two acetyl groups on morphine → more BBB penetration) |
| 1898 | Bayer marketed heroin as "superior aspirin"; claimed non-addictive |
| 1914 | **Harrison Narcotics Act**: opium/morphine/cocaine illegal — **heroin NOT included** |
| 1924 | Heroin made illegal in US |

- **Laudanum** = opium + alcohol preparation. Heroin > morphine due to greater **lipid solubility/BBB penetration**.
- **Effects**: **rush** (intense abdominal/orgasmic pleasure) → serene drowsy euphoria; uses: analgesic, cough suppressant, antidiarrheal.
- **Withdrawal**: begins **6-12 h** after last dose; gooseflesh ("cold turkey"), muscle spasms ("kicking the habit"); peaks day **2-3**, resolves by day 7; comparable to bad flu — **not as dangerous** as alcohol/barbiturate withdrawal.
- **Direct hazards minor**: many use for years with little harm; addiction more prevalent among **doctors, nurses, dentists** (e.g., **Dr. William Stewart Halsted**). **Indirect hazards** (from illegality): crime, AIDS/hepatitis from needles, unknown purity; ~10% treatment success.
- **Overdose confusion**: many "overdoses" are **drug interactions** (Darke et al., 2000); some from purity variation; recent US increase is mostly from **legal synthetic opioids** (Oxycontin), not heroin (Paulozzi et al., 2006).

| Treatment | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Methadone** | Opiate; less euphoric; blocks withdrawal | High dropout; ~10% success (Zador, 2007) |
| **Buprenorphine** | High, long-lasting opiate receptor affinity; blocks others without euphoria | As effective as methadone |
| **Swiss heroin clinics (1994)** | Physician-administered heroin in a treatment package | Reduced crime, improved health, fewer new addictions |

## 15.4 Theories of Addiction

### Physical-Dependence vs. Positive-Incentive
- **Physical-dependence theory** (inadequate): addiction maintained by avoiding withdrawal. **Problems**: relapse after long abstinence; pain patients on morphine rarely become addicts; some dependence-causing drugs aren't addictive.
- **Positive-incentive theory** (better): addiction driven by **anticipated pleasure** and **craving**, not withdrawal avoidance.

### Incentive-Sensitization Theory (Robinson & Berridge, 1993)
Two distinct systems:

| System | Mediates | Substrate | Change with addiction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"Wanting"** (incentive salience) | Craving, motivational pull | Mesolimbic dopamine (VTA → NAc) | **SENSITIZES** |
| **"Liking"** (hedonic pleasure) | Actual enjoyment | Opioid systems | **HABITUATES** |

- Wanting **increases** while liking **decreases** → desperate craving despite less pleasure; drug cues activate the sensitized wanting system.

### Brain Reward Circuits
- **Mesolimbic dopamine system**: **VTA (Ventral Tegmental Area) → Nucleus Accumbens (NAc) → Prefrontal Cortex**; all addictive drugs increase **dopamine in the NAc**.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Olds & Milner (1954) — Intracranial Self-Stimulation (ICSS):** rats pressed a lever thousands of times/hour for brain stimulation, forgoing food/water/sleep; most reinforcing sites = **lateral hypothalamus, medial forebrain bundle, VTA** — revealed a brain reward system.

| Drug | Mechanism | NAc dopamine |
|---|---|---|
| Cocaine | Blocks dopamine transporter | Increased |
| Amphetamine | Increases release | Increased |
| Heroin/opiates | Disinhibits VTA dopamine neurons | Increased |
| Nicotine | Nicotinic receptors on VTA neurons | Increased |
| Alcohol | Multiple (GABA-A, glutamate, opioid) | Increased |
| Marijuana (THC) | CB1 on GABAergic interneurons → disinhibition | Increased |

### Relapse
Three triggers (even after abstinence): (1) **stress** (HPA axis, sensitizes reward circuits); (2) **drug-associated cues** (**cue-induced craving**); (3) **priming doses**. Treatment must address all three.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Routes by CNS speed (fastest→slowest)**: IV > inhalation > IM > SC > oral. IV has the greatest overdose/impurity/vein risk.

2. **Tolerance = rightward dose-response shift**; metabolic vs. functional (psychoactive = largely **functional**); cross-tolerance; **sensitization** = opposite of tolerance.

3. **Withdrawal effects always opposite to the drug's**; **alcohol** (DTs, convulsions) and barbiturates are most dangerous; opiate withdrawal is uncomfortable, not life-threatening (peaks day 2-3).

4. **Addiction ≠ physical dependence**: relapse after long abstinence; positive-incentive theory (craving) > physical-dependence theory.

5. **Contingent tolerance (Pinel et al., 1989)**: only to experienced effects; before-and-after design.

6. **Conditioned tolerance (Crowell et al., 1981; Siegel)**: situationally specific; CCRs oppose drug effects; novel environment → overdose risk (Siegel et al., 1982).

7. **Tobacco addiction ~70%** (highest); ~65% heritable; develops in weeks; smokers more tense than nonsmokers; nicotine is a teratogen.

8. **Alcohol withdrawal**: Phase 1 (5-6 h tremors/hallucinations), Phase 2 (15-30 h convulsions), Phase 3 **DTs** (1-2 days, lasts 3-4); DTs/convulsions LETHAL. FAS: no safe time/amount. Moderate drinking doesn't protect the heart (Fillmore et al., 2006).

9. **CB1 = most prevalent G-protein receptor in the brain**; THC acts on it; anandamide = first endocannabinoid; CB2 = brain stem/immune cells; addiction potential LOW.

10. **Cocaine blocks dopamine transporters**; tolerance to euphoria, sensitization to motor/convulsive effects; cocaine psychosis mistaken for paranoid schizophrenia; methamphetamine causes brain volume loss.

11. **Heroin synthesized 1870; Bayer marketed it 1898; NOT in the 1914 Harrison Act**; rush then drowsy euphoria; methadone and buprenorphine are the main treatments.

12. **Incentive-Sensitization (Robinson & Berridge, 1993)**: "wanting" sensitizes, "liking" habituates → craving despite less pleasure.

13. **Mesolimbic dopamine system = VTA → NAc → PFC**; all addictive drugs raise NAc dopamine; ICSS (Olds & Milner, 1954); relapse triggers = stress, cues, priming.

14. **Ramsay & Woods (1997)**: US = neural disruption, UR = compensatory reaction; conditioned response always similar to UR.

15. **Disulfiram** blocks alcohol metabolism (acetaldehyde); **buprenorphine** blocks opiates without euphoria, as effective as methadone; **Swiss heroin clinics (1994)** reduced crime and new addictions.
