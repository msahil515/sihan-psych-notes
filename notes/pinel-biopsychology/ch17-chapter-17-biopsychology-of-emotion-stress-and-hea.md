# Chapter 17: Biopsychology of Emotion, Stress, and Health

Biological bases of emotion: Darwin's evolutionary theory, neural circuits of fear and aggression, and stress effects on health and immunity. Integrates historical theories (James-Lange, Cannon-Bard), landmark syndromes (Kluver-Bucy, sham rage), and modern cognitive neuroscience, emphasizing the **amygdala** in emotion and fear conditioning.

---

## 17.1 Biopsychology of Emotion: Introduction

### Darwin's Theory (1872)
- ***The Expression of Emotions in Man and Animals*** (Darwin, 1872): emotional expressions are products of **evolution**, shared within a species (anecdotal/interspecies evidence).
- **Three ideas**: (1) expressions evolve from behaviors signaling **what an animal will do next**; (2) if beneficial, they evolve to enhance communication (losing original function); (3) opposite messages use opposite movements — the **principle of antithesis**.
- **Threat displays** evolved so animals could communicate aggression without fighting.

| Signal | Aggression | Submission |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Gulls | Beaks toward each other | Beaks away |
| Primates | Staring | Averting gaze |
| Dogs | Ears forward, hair up, tail up | Ears back, hair down, tail down |

### James-Lange and Cannon-Bard Theories
- **James-Lange (1884)** (William James, Carl Lange; first physiological theory): emotion-inducing stimuli → ANS/somatic responses → these responses **produce** the feeling ("we feel afraid because we tremble"). Predicts **different emotions = different ANS patterns**; experience depends on ANS feedback.
- **Cannon-Bard (~1915)** (Walter Cannon, Philip Bard): stimuli have **two independent effects** — feeling (in brain) and expression (in ANS/somatic NS), as **parallel processes**. Predicts all emotions → **same sympathetic activation**; experience independent of ANS feedback.

| Feature | James-Lange | Cannon-Bard |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Sequence | Body → feeling | Simultaneous, independent |
| ANS specificity | High | None |
| ANS feedback | Necessary | Irrelevant |

- **Both wrong**: against James-Lange — broken-neck patients (no ANS feedback) still have a full emotional range (Lowe & Carroll, 1985); against Cannon-Bard — ANS/somatic responses **can** influence experience.
- **Modern view**: three factors influence each other bidirectionally — perception of stimulus, ANS/somatic responses, experience of emotion.

### Sham Rage (Bard, 1929)
- **Decorticate cats** show **sham rage**: exaggerated, poorly directed aggression to slight provocation. Elicitable down to (not including) the **hypothalamus**; absent if the hypothalamus is removed → **hypothalamus is critical for aggressive expression; cortex inhibits/directs** it.

### Limbic System
- **Papez (1937)**: emotion controlled by interconnected nuclei/tracts ringing the thalamus = the **limbic system** (amygdala, mammillary body, hippocampus, fornix, cingulate gyrus, septum, olfactory bulb, hypothalamus). Expressed via the **hypothalamus**, experienced via the **cortex**. Expanded by **MacLean (1952)**.

### Kluver-Bucy Syndrome (1939)
- Monkeys after **bilateral anterior temporal lobectomy**. Five symptoms: (1) eat almost anything; (2) increased sexual activity toward **inappropriate objects**; (3) **hypermetamorphosis** (repeatedly investigate familiar objects); (4) **oral** investigation; (5) **lack of fear**. Became tame. In primates, mostly from **amygdala** damage (Phelps, 2006).

> **Human case (Marlowe, Mancall, & Thomas, 1985):** flat affect, placidity, oral exploration, sexual "reversal," perseverative imitation.

### Six Early Landmarks

| Event | Date |
|-------|------|
| Phineas Gage | 1848 |
| Darwin's evolution of emotion | 1872 |
| James-Lange and Cannon-Bard | ~1900 |
| Sham rage (Bard) | 1929 |

### Emotions and the ANS
- ANS specificity lies **between** total specificity (James-Lange) and total generality (Cannon-Bard) (Levenson, 1994; Ax, 1955). Sympathetic activation: ↑heart rate, BP, pupil dilation, muscle blood flow, respiration, epinephrine/norepinephrine.

**Polygraphy**: uses ANS indexes; detects **emotions, not lies**; useful but not infallible (mock-crime procedure).

| Technique | Method | Accuracy |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Control-question** | Target vs. control questions; lying = greater sympathetic activation | ~80% (mock crime) |
| **Guilty-knowledge** | Uses info known only to the guilty; differential reactions | 88% (Lykken, 1959) |

> **Lykken (1959):** using only **electrodermal activity** and the guilty-knowledge technique, **88% of mock criminals correctly identified; no innocent subjects judged guilty.**

### Emotions and Facial Expression
- **Paul Ekman** (with Friesen): atlas of expressions; cross-cultural **universality** (most convincing: isolated **New Guinea tribe**, Ekman & Friesen, 1971); similar to primate relatives.
- **Six primary expressions**: surprise, anger, sadness, disgust, fear, **happiness** (only **positive valence**); others are mixtures. Later: contempt, embarrassment. **Emotional valence** = positive/negative character.
- **Pride** (Tracy & Robins): a primary emotion not from a mixture; cross-cultural; has **postural** components.
- **Facial feedback hypothesis**: expressions influence experience.

> **Rutledge & Hupka (1985):** subjects forming happy faces felt happier; angry faces felt angrier.

- **Detecting false expressions**: **microexpressions** (~0.05 s leaks of real emotion; Porter & ten Brinke, 2008); subtle genuine/false differences.
- **Duchenne smile** (Duchenne, 1862; named by Ekman): genuine smile uses **orbicularis oculi** (encircles eye; **involuntary**, only in genuine pleasure) + **zygomaticus major** (lip corners; **voluntary**). False smiles lack the lateral orbicularis oculi.

## 17.2 Fear, Defense, and Aggression

- **Fear**: reaction to threat; motivates defense. **Defensive behaviors**: protect from harm. **Aggressive behaviors**: threaten or harm.
- Barrett (2006): base neural searches on behavioral descriptions, eliciting situations, and adaptive functions — not assuming words like "fear" map to discrete brain entities.

### Models
- **Blanchard & Blanchard colony-intruder model**: alpha male vs. small intruder; alpha uses **lateral attack** (sideways, biting the back), defender pivots to keep frontal orientation.
- **Pellis et al. (1988)** (cats/mice): cats don't "play" — apparent play = **vacillation between attack and defense**; behavior on a **linear scale** from aggression to defense; an antianxiety drug moved cats toward efficient killing.

**Aggressive behaviors:**

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Predatory aggression** | Stalking/killing **other species** for food (rats bite the neck) |
| **Social aggression** | Unprovoked aggression toward a **conspecific** for hierarchy; mostly males; piloerection, lateral attack, back bites |

**Defensive behaviors:**

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Intraspecific defense** | Vs. social aggression: freezing, flight, boxing, back protection |
| **Defensive attacks** | When cornered: lunging, biting at the attacker's **face** |
| **Freezing and flight** | Avoidance responses |
| **Risk assessment** | Gathering information for better defense |
| **Defensive burying** | Spraying dirt to bury dangerous objects |

- **Maternal defensive behaviors** look like male social aggression but are defensive.
- **Target-site concept**: behaviors attack specific body sites while protecting others (aggressor targets the **back**, protects its **face**).
- **Lateral septal lesions** ("septal rage") increase **defensiveness/reactivity**, NOT social aggression (no unprovoked attack).

### Aggression and Testosterone
- Male social aggression reflects **organizational** (perinatal testosterone organizes the nervous system) and **activational** (post-puberty testosterone activates patterns) effects.
- **Rodents**: neonatal castration → adult testosterone fails to induce social aggression; adult castration eliminates it (reversed by replacement).
- **Soma et al. (2005, 2006) conclusions**: (1) testosterone increases social aggression in many species; (2) effect varies by species/season; (3) aggression itself **increases testosterone** (e.g., playing with a gun; Klinesmith et al., 2006) — **bidirectional**; (4) **brain** testosterone levels matter more than blood.
- **Human evidence weak**: aggression doesn't rise with pubertal testosterone; castration doesn't eliminate it; injections don't increase it. Correlations may mean aggression raises testosterone. Most human outbursts are **defensive attacks** (not testosterone-related social aggression); studies often used blood (not brain) levels and conflated aggression types.

## 17.3 Neural Mechanisms of Fear Conditioning

- **Fear conditioning**: pairing a neutral **CS** (tone) with an aversive **US** (foot shock) → CS alone elicits defensive behavior (freezing, startle) and sympathetic responses. Key researcher: **LeDoux**.

### Amygdala and Fear Conditioning
- **Lesion mapping**: lesions of the **medial geniculate nucleus** (auditory thalamic relay) **block** conditioning to a tone; **auditory cortex** lesions do NOT. Critical pathway: **medial geniculate nucleus → amygdala**; amygdala lesions block conditioning.
- **Amygdala** receives input from **all sensory systems**; learns/retains the **emotional significance** of signals.

| Pathway | Route | Mediates |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **Direct** | Medial geniculate → amygdala | Conditioning to **simple sounds** |
| **Indirect (cortical)** | Medial geniculate → auditory cortex → amygdala | Conditioning to **complex sounds** |

| From amygdala | Target | Response |
|---------|--------|-------------------|
| → **Periaqueductal gray (PAG)** | Midbrain | Defensive **behavioral** responses |
| → **Lateral hypothalamus** | Hypothalamus | **Sympathetic** responses |

### Contextual Fear Conditioning and the Hippocampus
- **Contextual fear conditioning**: contexts come to elicit fear by association (e.g., a trail where a bear appeared).
- **Bilateral hippocampal lesions** block fear of the **context** but NOT the explicit **CS** (tone) (Antoniadis & McDonald, 2000).

### Amygdala Complex
- The amygdala = the **amygdala complex** (a dozen+ nuclei). The **lateral nucleus** is critical for **acquisition, storage, and expression** of conditioned fear (Maren & Quirk, 2004). Projections: **prefrontal cortex** (suppresses conditioned fear) and **hippocampus** (contextual learning).

## 17.4 Stress and Health

- **Stress response**: physiological changes from harm/threat. **Stressors**: experiences inducing it; all produce a **core pattern**. **Chronic psychological stress** is most implicated in ill health (Kiecolt-Glaser et al., 2002).

### Selye's Stress Response (1950s)
- **Hans Selye** described its **dual nature**: short-term **adaptive** (energy mobilization), long-term **maladaptive** (enlarged adrenals).
- **System 1 (anterior-pituitary adrenal-cortex)**: stressors → **ACTH** → **glucocorticoids** (the most common physiological stress measure).
- **System 2 (sympathetic NS adrenal-medulla)**: → **epinephrine/norepinephrine** (Selye initially ignored this; both now acknowledged).
- **Cytokines** (1990s): peptide hormones causing inflammation/fever — classified as **major stress hormones**.
- Selye's "same general response" assertion is **partly correct**; responses vary by stressor, timing, and person (e.g., Katz et al., 1970: breast-cancer-surgery patients who minimized worry had lower stress).

### Animal Models
- Extreme-stressor research had **ethical** and **validity** problems.
- **Subordination stress**: chronic conspecific threat; subordinate rodents show smaller testes, shorter lives, lower testosterone, **higher glucocorticoids**.

### Psychosomatic Disorders: Gastric Ulcers
- **Psychosomatic disorders**: medical disorders with psychological causes.
- **Gastric ulcers**: stomach/duodenum lesions; ~500,000 US cases/year. **H. pylori** alone is insufficient — **75% of healthy people** have it without ulcers; psychological treatment helps without reducing H. pylori; **stress is a necessary co-factor**.

### Psychoneuroimmunology
- 1970s discovery: stress increases susceptibility to infection → early 1980s field of **psychoneuroimmunology** (psychology × nervous system × immune system).
- **Antigens**: surface proteins identifying a cell as native or foreign.

| Division | Role | Feature |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Innate immune system** | First-line defense near entry points; general pathogen classes | Rapid; no memory |
| **Adaptive immune system** | Targets specific antigens | Has **memory** |

- **Innate**: **phagocytes** (macrophages, microglia) with **toll-like receptors** → **phagocytosis** + **cytokine** release (inflammation, fever, recruits phagocytes, activates lymphocytes).
- **Adaptive**: **lymphocytes** (from bone marrow/thymus).

| Cell | Immunity | Mechanism |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| **T cells** | **Cell-mediated** | Kill invaded microorganisms/body cells |
| **B cells** | **Antibody-mediated** | Make **antibodies** binding/destroying antigens; produce **memory B cells** |

- **T-reg cells**: destroy self-attacking T cells (prevent **autoimmune diseases** like MS).
- **Immunization**: immunity via vaccination (e.g., smallpox via cowpox). Immune cells connect via synapse-like contacts.

**Stress and immunity — Segerstrom & Miller (2004) meta-analysis (~300 studies):**

| Stress | Duration | Effect |
|----------------|----------|--------------------|
| **Acute** | <100 min (public speaking, competition) | **IMPROVE** immunity (mainly **innate**) |
| **Chronic** | Long-lasting (caregiving, unemployment) | **IMPAIR adaptive** immunity |

- **Eustress** improves functioning; **distress** disrupts it. Cytokines: short-term help fight infection, long-term cause adverse effects.
- **Mechanisms**: T/B cells have **glucocorticoid** receptors; lymphocytes have epinephrine/norepinephrine/glucocorticoid receptors; cytokines also made in the **nervous system**; behavioral routes (diet, sleep, drugs).

> **Cohen et al. (1991):** 394 participants given a respiratory virus or saline; **higher-stress participants developed more colds.**

- **Three evidence types** for stress → infection susceptibility: human correlational (Glaser et al., 1987: more infections during exams); controlled animal experiments; partially controlled human studies.
- Decreased immune function may not raise disease due to **redundancy**, **short duration**, and **compensatory increases**.

### Early Experience of Stress
- Early severe stress → brain/endocrine abnormalities (Teicher et al., 2003); may interact with inherited susceptibility (Ch. 18); often **increases** later stress-response intensity (more glucocorticoid release).
- **Prenatal stress** also has adverse effects (Weinstock, 2008).
- **Rat pup handling (Sapolsky, 1997)**: a few minutes/day has **salutary** effects — adults show **smaller glucocorticoid increases** to stressors.

### Stress and the Hippocampus
- Chronic stress/elevated glucocorticoids damage **hippocampal neurons** (rich in glucocorticoid receptors), affecting spatial memory and contextual fear conditioning, and impairing neuroplasticity.

## 17.5 Brain Mechanisms of Human Emotion
- Modern cognitive neuroscience uses imaging and lesions. **Phineas Gage (1848)**: damage to both **medial prefrontal lobes** (planning + emotion; Vogt, 2005).
- The **amygdala** is central to human emotion, especially **fear** and recognizing **fearful facial expressions**.

> **Case of S.P.**: bilateral amygdala damage; **could not recognize fearful facial expressions** despite recognizing other emotions — confirms the amygdala's specific fear role in humans.

- **Medial prefrontal cortex**: projects to the **lateral amygdala** to **suppress conditioned fear**; damage → personality/emotional-regulation changes (as in Gage).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **James-Lange (1884)**: body responses produce emotion (different emotions = different ANS patterns; first physiological theory).

2. **Cannon-Bard (~1915)**: feeling and expression are parallel/independent; all emotions = same sympathetic activation; feeling independent of ANS feedback.

3. **Sham rage (Bard, 1929)**: decorticate cats; needs the **hypothalamus**; cortex inhibits/directs aggression.

4. **Kluver-Bucy (1939)**: bilateral anterior temporal lobectomy → hyperphagia, hypersexuality (inappropriate objects), hypermetamorphosis, oral tendencies, **lack of fear**; from **amygdala** damage.

5. **Limbic system (Papez, 1937; MacLean, 1952)**: amygdala, hippocampus, mammillary body, fornix, cingulate gyrus, septum, olfactory bulb, hypothalamus.

6. **Duchenne smile**: orbicularis oculi (involuntary, genuine only) + zygomaticus major (voluntary); false smiles lack the lateral orbicularis oculi.

7. **Six primary expressions** (Ekman & Friesen): surprise, anger, sadness, disgust, fear, **happiness** (only positive valence); others are mixtures.

8. **Guilty-knowledge technique (Lykken, 1959)**: 88% accuracy, no false positives; uses electrodermal activity; avoids the control-question problem.

9. **Fear conditioning circuit (LeDoux)**: medial geniculate → direct (simple tones) or cortical (complex sounds) → amygdala; amygdala → PAG (defensive behavior) + lateral hypothalamus (sympathetic); **lateral nucleus** is critical.

10. **Contextual fear conditioning** needs the **hippocampus**: hippocampal lesions block context fear, not CS fear; amygdala lesions block both.

11. **Stress (Selye, 1950s)**: ACTH → glucocorticoids; sympathetic NS → epinephrine/norepinephrine; cytokines added 1990s; glucocorticoids = main measure.

12. **Acute vs. chronic stress (Segerstrom & Miller, 2004)**: acute (<100 min) **improves innate** immunity (eustress); chronic **impairs adaptive** immunity (distress).

13. **H. pylori and ulcers**: insufficient alone (75% of healthy people have it); stress is a necessary co-factor; psychological treatment helps without reducing H. pylori.

14. **Testosterone and aggression**: brain levels matter more than blood; aggression raises testosterone (bidirectional); human outbursts are mostly **defensive attacks** (not testosterone-related).

15. **Psychoneuroimmunology**: emerged early 1980s; Cohen et al. (1991) — higher stress → more colds; effects limited by redundancy, short duration, and compensation.
