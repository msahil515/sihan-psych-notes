# Chapter 17: Biopsychology of Emotion, Stress, and Health

This chapter examines the biological bases of emotion — tracing from Darwin's evolutionary theory through neural circuits of fear and aggression to the effects of stress on health and the immune system. It integrates historical theories (James-Lange, Cannon-Bard), landmark syndromes (Kluver-Bucy, sham rage), and modern cognitive neuroscience of human emotion, with particular emphasis on the amygdala as a central structure for emotional processing and fear conditioning.

---

## 17.1 Biopsychology of Emotion: Introduction

### Evolutionary Perspective

#### Darwin's Theory of the Evolution of Emotion (1872)

- **Key publication:** *The Expression of Emotions in Man and Animals* (Darwin, 1872) — first major event in the biopsychological study of emotion.
- Darwin argued (largely on **anecdotal evidence**) that particular emotional responses (e.g., human facial expressions) tend to accompany the same emotional states in all members of a species.
- Darwin believed expressions of emotion, like other behaviors, are **products of evolution**.
- He compared emotional expressions across different species (**interspecies comparisons**) to develop his evolutionary theory.

**Darwin's Three Main Ideas on the Evolution of Emotional Expression:**

1. Expressions of emotion evolve from behaviors that indicate **what an animal is likely to do next**.
2. If signals provided by such behaviors benefit the displaying animal, they will evolve to **enhance their communicative function** — their original function may be lost.
3. Opposite messages are often signaled by opposite movements and postures: the **principle of antithesis**.

**Example — Threat Displays:**
- Originally, facing enemies, rising up, and exposing weapons were early-stage combat behaviors.
- Once enemies recognized these as signals of impending aggression, a survival advantage accrued to attackers who could **communicate aggression most effectively without actually fighting**.
- Result: elaborate **threat displays** evolved; actual combat declined.

**Principle of Antithesis — Illustration:**

| Signal | Aggression | Submission |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Gulls (beaks) | Pointing beaks at each other | Pointing beaks away |
| Primates (gaze) | Staring | Averting gaze |
| Dogs (posture) | Ears forward, back up, hair up, tail up | Ears back, back down, hair down, tail down |

> Darwin's 1872 book used woodcuts of dogs to illustrate the principle of antithesis in aggressive vs. submissive postures.

### James-Lange and Cannon-Bard Theories

#### James-Lange Theory (1884)

- Proposed **independently** by **William James** and **Carl Lange** in **1884**.
- The **first physiological theory of emotion**.
- **Sequence:** Emotion-inducing stimuli → cortex receives and interprets → changes in visceral organs (via ANS) and skeletal muscles (via somatic NS) → autonomic/somatic responses **trigger** emotional experience in the brain.
- **Key reversal of common sense:** James and Lange argued that ANS activity and behavior (e.g., rapid heartbeat, running away) **produce** the feeling of emotion — not vice versa.
- Classic phrasing: "We feel sorry because we cry, angry because we strike, afraid because we tremble."
- Predicts: **different emotions = different patterns of ANS activity** (high specificity).
- Predicts: emotional experience **depends entirely** on feedback from ANS and somatic NS.

#### Cannon-Bard Theory (c. 1915)

- Proposed by **Walter Cannon** (~1915); extended and promoted by **Philip Bard**.
- **Emotional stimuli have two independent excitatory effects:**
  1. Excite the **feeling of emotion** in the brain.
  2. Excite the **expression of emotion** in the autonomic and somatic nervous systems.
- Emotional experience and emotional expression are **parallel processes with no direct causal relation**.
- Predicts: all emotional stimuli produce the **same general pattern of sympathetic activation** (increased HR, BP, pupil dilation, increased blood flow to muscles, increased respiration, increased epinephrine/norepinephrine from adrenal medulla).
- Predicts: emotional experience is **totally independent** of autonomic/somatic feedback.

#### Comparison: James-Lange vs. Cannon-Bard

| Feature | James-Lange | Cannon-Bard |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Causal sequence | Body responses → emotional feeling | Emotional feeling and body responses occur simultaneously and independently |
| ANS specificity | Different emotions = different ANS patterns (high specificity) | All emotions = same sympathetic activation (no specificity) |
| Role of ANS feedback | Necessary for emotional experience | Irrelevant to emotional experience |

#### Why Both Are Wrong

- **Against James-Lange:** Human patients with broken necks (eliminated autonomic and somatic feedback) are capable of a **full range of emotional experiences** (Lowe & Carroll, 1985) — so ANS feedback is NOT necessary.
- **Against Cannon-Bard:** Numerous reports confirm that autonomic and somatic responses **can influence** emotional experience — so ANS feedback is NOT irrelevant.

#### Modern Biopsychological View

- Each of the three principal factors influences the other two (bidirectional relationships):
  1. Perception of the emotion-inducing stimulus
  2. Autonomic and somatic responses to the stimulus
  3. Experience of the emotion

### Sham Rage

- Discovered by **Bard (1929)**.
- **Decorticate cats** (cortex removed) respond aggressively to the slightest provocation (light touch): arch backs, erect hair, growl, hiss, expose teeth.
- **Sham rage:** The exaggerated, poorly directed aggressive response of decorticate animals.
- Abnormal in two respects: (1) inappropriately **severe**, (2) not **directed at particular targets**.
- Sham rage can be elicited when cerebral hemispheres removed down to (but **not including**) the hypothalamus.
- Cannot be elicited if the **hypothalamus is also removed**.
- **Conclusion (Bard):** Hypothalamus is critical for expression of aggressive responses; cortex functions to **inhibit and direct** these responses.

---

### Limbic System and Emotion

- **Papez (1937)** (pronounced "Payps"): proposed emotional expression is controlled by several **interconnected nuclei and tracts ringing the thalamus**.
- This circuit = **limbic system** (limbic = "border").

**Key Limbic System Structures:**
- Amygdala
- Mammillary body
- Hippocampus
- Fornix
- Cortex of the cingulate gyrus
- Septum
- Olfactory bulb
- Hypothalamus

- Papez proposed: emotional states are **expressed** through action of circuit structures on the **hypothalamus** and **experienced** through their action on the **cortex**.
- Revised and expanded by **Paul MacLean (1952)** → influential **limbic system theory of emotion**.

---

### Kluver-Bucy Syndrome

- Described by **Kluver and Bucy (1939)** in monkeys following **bilateral anterior temporal lobectomy**.

**Five Key Symptoms:**
1. Consumption of almost anything edible (dietary changes/hyperphagia)
2. Increased sexual activity, often directed at **inappropriate objects**
3. Tendency to repeatedly investigate familiar objects (**hypermetamorphosis**)
4. Tendency to investigate objects with the **mouth** (oral tendencies)
5. **Lack of fear** (even toward snakes, which terrify normal monkeys)

- Monkeys previously unhandleable became **tame** subjects after surgery.
- In primates, most symptoms result from damage to the **amygdala** (Phelps, 2006).
- The syndrome has been observed in several species, including humans.

> **Human Case (Marlowe, Mancall, & Thomas, 1985):** Patient with brain infection showed flat affect, placidity, indifference, oral exploration of all objects, sexual "reversal" (advances toward males despite prior heterosexuality), and perseverative imitative behaviors.

### Emotions and the Autonomic Nervous System

#### Emotional Specificity of the ANS

- **James-Lange view:** Different emotions → different ANS patterns → different emotional experiences. (Maximum specificity)
- **Cannon-Bard view:** All emotions → same general sympathetic activation. (No specificity)
- **Experimental evidence:** The truth lies **between** total specificity and total generality (Levenson, 1994; Ax, 1955).

**Sympathetic Activation Components (Cannon-Bard):**
- Increased heart rate
- Increased blood pressure
- Pupil dilation
- Increased blood flow to muscles
- Increased respiration
- Increased release of epinephrine and norepinephrine from adrenal medulla

#### Polygraphy

- **Polygraphy:** Method of interrogation using ANS indexes of emotion to infer truthfulness.
- Important distinction: Polygraphy detects **emotions**, not lies.
- Polygraph tests by skilled examiners: useful but **far from infallible**.

**Mock-Crime Procedure:**
- Volunteer subjects participate in a mock crime → polygraph by examiner unaware of guilt/innocence.

| Technique | Method | Accuracy |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Control-question technique** | Physiological response to target question compared to control questions; assumes lying = greater sympathetic activation. | ~80% in mock-crime studies |
| **Guilty-knowledge technique** | Polygrapher uses info known only to the guilty; assesses differential reactions to actual vs. contrived crime details. Innocent = react same to all; guilty = react differentially. | 88% correct identification (Lykken, 1959) |

> **KEY STUDY — Lykken (1959):** Subjects stole a purse from an office, hid it in a locker. Using only **electrodermal activity** and guilty-knowledge technique: **88% of mock criminals correctly identified; NONE of the innocent subjects judged guilty.**

**Problem with control-question in real life:** "Did you steal that purse?" elicits reactions in ALL suspects (guilty or innocent) → difficult to detect deception. Guilty-knowledge technique circumvents this.

### Emotions and Facial Expression

#### Ekman's Research Program

- **Paul Ekman** and colleagues preeminent in facial expression research (Ekman, 2003).
- Started 1960s by analyzing hundreds of films/photographs of people experiencing real emotions.
- Compiled an atlas of facial expressions associated with different emotions (Ekman & Friesen, 1975).

#### Universality of Facial Expression

- People of different cultures make similar facial expressions in similar situations and correctly identify expressions from other cultures.
- Most convincing study: isolated **New Guinea tribe** (Ekman & Friesen, 1971) with little/no outside contact — still showed and recognized same expressions.
- Some subtle **cultural differences** identified (Russell, Bachorowski, & Fernandez-Dols, 2003).
- Human facial expressions similar to those of **primate relatives** (Parr, Waller, & Fugate, 2005).

#### Six Primary Facial Expressions (Ekman & Friesen)

1. Surprise
2. Anger
3. Sadness
4. Disgust
5. Fear
6. **Happiness** (the only one with **positive emotional valence**)

- All other genuine emotional expressions = **predictable mixtures** of these six primaries.
- Later additions (Ekman, 1992): **contempt** and **embarrassment**.
- **Emotional valence:** The general positive or negative character of an emotion.

#### Pride as a Primary Emotion (Tracy & Robins, 2004, 2007a, 2007b)

- Not creatable from a mixture of other primaries.
- Readily identified across cultures.
- Involves **postural as well as facial** components.
- Expression: small smile, head tilted back slightly, hands on hips or raised above head or clenched fists with arms crossed on chest.

#### Facial Feedback Hypothesis

- **Facial feedback hypothesis:** Our facial expressions influence our emotional experience.
- Supported by evidence (Adelmann & Zajonc, 1989).

> **KEY STUDY — Rutledge and Hupka (1985):**
> - Method: Subjects assumed patterns of facial contractions corresponding to happy or angry faces (without knowing this) while viewing slides.
> - Finding: Slides made subjects feel **more happy and less angry** when making happy faces; **less happy and more angry** when making angry faces.

#### Voluntary Control and Detecting False Expressions

- We can inhibit true facial expressions and substitute false ones.
- Two ways to distinguish genuine from false expressions:

1. **Microexpressions:** Brief (~0.05 second) expressions of the real emotion that break through false ones (Porter & ten Brinke, 2008). Detectable with practice; can be detected without slow-motion photography.
2. Subtle differences between genuine and false expressions detectable by skilled observers.

#### The Duchenne Smile

- First described by French anatomist **Duchenne (1862)**.
- Named the **Duchenne smile** by Ekman (Ekman & Davidson, 1993).
- Genuine smile of enjoyment involves two muscles:

| Muscle | Action | Voluntary Control? |
|--------|--------|--------------------|
| **Orbicularis oculi** | Encircles eye; pulls skin from cheeks/forehead toward eyeball | Normally contracted ONLY by genuine pleasure; **difficult to control voluntarily** |
| **Zygomaticus major** | Pulls lip corners up | Can be controlled **voluntarily** |

- False smiles typically lack contraction of the **lateral portion of orbicularis oculi**.
- "Inertia of the orbicularis oculi in smiling unmasks a false friend."

## 17.2 Fear, Defense, and Aggression

### Definitions

- **Fear:** The emotional reaction to threat; the motivating force for defensive behaviors.
- **Defensive behaviors:** Behaviors whose primary function is to **protect** the organism from threat or harm.
- **Aggressive behaviors:** Behaviors whose primary function is to **threaten or harm**.

> Barrett (2006): Progress in understanding the neural basis of emotion has been limited because neuroscientists have often assumed that words like "fear," "happiness," and "anger" correspond to discrete brain entities. Better approach: base the search for neural mechanisms on thorough behavioral descriptions, the situations that elicit them, and their putative adaptive functions.

---

### Types of Aggressive and Defensive Behaviors

#### Blanchard and Blanchard's Colony-Intruder Model (1989, 1990)

- Study of interactions between the **alpha male** (dominant male) of an established mixed-sex colony and a **small male intruder** in rats.
- Alpha male bites intruder on the back; intruder runs; alpha chases; intruder stops and faces attacker; intruder rears up on hind legs; alpha moves to **lateral orientation** (body perpendicular to front of defender); alpha performs **lateral attack** (moves sideways, crowding and sometimes pushing defender); may lunge and bite at defender's back; defender pivots to maintain frontal orientation.

#### Pellis et al. (1988) — Cats and Mice

- Videotaped cat-mouse interactions; found different cats reacted differently (killers, defensive, apparent "players").
- **Conclusion 1:** Cats do **NOT** play with prey — apparent play = **vacillation between attack and defense**.
- **Conclusion 2:** Cat behavior exists on a **linear scale** from total aggressiveness to total defensiveness.
- **Test:** Antianxiety drug (reduced defensiveness) moved cats toward more efficient killing:
  - Avoiders → played with mice after injection
  - Players → killed mice after injection
  - Killers → killed more quickly after injection

#### Categories of Aggressive and Defensive Behaviors in Rats

Classification based on three criteria: **(1) topography (form), (2) eliciting situations, (3) apparent function.**

**Aggressive Behaviors:**

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Predatory Aggression** | Stalking and killing members of OTHER species for eating. Rats kill prey (mice, frogs) by biting the back of the neck. |
| **Social Aggression** | Unprovoked aggression toward a **conspecific** (same-species member) to establish/alter/maintain social hierarchy. Primarily among males. Characterized by piloerection, lateral attack, bites to defender's back. |

**Defensive Behaviors:**

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Intraspecific Defense** | Defense against social aggression. Characterized by freezing, flight, boxing, and behaviors specifically designed to protect the back from bites. |
| **Defensive Attacks** | Attacks launched when animals are cornered by threatening conspecifics or other species. Include lunging, shrieking, biting attacks directed at the **face** of the attacker. |
| **Freezing and Flight** | Avoidance responses. Wild rat freezes until human penetrates its safety zone, then explodes into flight. |
| **Risk Assessment** | Behaviors to obtain information for more effective defense. E.g., rats chased into burrow by a cat do not emerge until they spend time scanning the environment at the entrance. |
| **Defensive Burying** | Rats/rodents spray sand and dirt ahead with forepaws to bury dangerous objects, drive off predators, construct burrow barriers. |

**Maternal Defensive Behaviors:**
- Behaviors by which mothers protect their young.
- Despite defensive function, similar in appearance to **male social aggression**.

#### Target-Site Concept

- **Target-site concept:** Aggressive and defensive behaviors of an animal are often designed to attack specific sites on another animal's body while protecting specific sites on its own.
- Socially aggressive rat: lateral attack designed to deliver bites to defender's **back** while protecting attacker's own **face** (the likely target of defensive attack).
- Defending rat: boxing and pivoting designed to protect its **back**.

#### Lateral Septum and Aggression

- Lateral septal lesions make laboratory rats notoriously difficult to handle — behavior termed "**septal aggression**" or "**septal rage**."
- However: Rats with lateral septal lesions do **NOT initiate attacks** at an experimenter unless **threatened**.
- Conclusion: Lateral septal lesions increase **defensiveness/reactivity**, NOT social aggression.

---

### Aggression and Testosterone

- Social aggression occurs more commonly among males than females → explained by **organizational** and **activational effects** of testosterone.
- **Organizational effects:** Brief testosterone release around birth in genetic males organizes nervous system along masculine lines, creating potential for male social aggression patterns.
- **Activational effects:** High testosterone levels after puberty **activate** male patterns of social aggression.

**Evidence in Rodents:**
- Neonatal castration of male mice → testosterone injections in adulthood **fail** to induce social aggression.
- Adult castration → **eliminates** social aggression (unless testosterone replacement is given).

**Soma and Colleagues' Major Conclusions (Demas et al., 2005; Soma, 2006):**

1. Testosterone increases social aggression in males of many species; castration largely abolishes it in those same species.
2. In some species, castration has **no effect** on social aggression; in others, reduces it only during breeding season.
3. Engaging in aggressive activity can itself **increase testosterone levels** — e.g., playing with a gun increased testosterone in male college students (Klinesmith, Kasser, & McAndrew, 2006). **Bidirectional relationship.**
4. Blood testosterone level is **NOT the best measure** — what matters more are testosterone levels in **relevant brain areas**. Testosterone can be synthesized at particular brain sites.

**Human Evidence (Weak and Unclear):**
- Aggressive behavior does NOT increase at puberty as blood testosterone increases.
- Castration does NOT eliminate aggressive behavior.
- Testosterone injections elevating blood levels do NOT increase aggressive behavior.
- Some studies: violent male criminals and aggressive athletes tend to have higher testosterone — but this **correlation** may mean aggressive encounters **increase** testosterone, not vice versa.

**Why Human Evidence Is Weak:**
1. Hormonal and neural regulation of aggression in humans may genuinely differ from other mammals.
2. OR: Research is methodologically flawed — typically based on blood/saliva testosterone rather than brain levels; AND researchers often failed to distinguish **social aggression** (related to testosterone in many species) from **defensive attack** (NOT related to testosterone).
3. Most aggressive outbursts in humans are overreactions to real or perceived threat → more appropriately viewed as **defensive attack**, not social aggression.

## 17.3 Neural Mechanisms of Fear Conditioning

### Fear Conditioning

- **Fear conditioning:** Establishment of fear in response to a previously neutral stimulus (**conditional stimulus, CS**) by pairing it with an aversive stimulus (**unconditional stimulus, US**).
- Typical experiment: Rat hears **tone** (CS) → receives mild **electric shock** to feet (US) → after several pairings, tone alone elicits:
  - Defensive behaviors: freezing, increased susceptibility to startle
  - Sympathetic NS responses: increased heart rate and blood pressure
- Key researcher: **LeDoux** and colleagues (Schafe & LeDoux, 2004; Olsson & Phelps, 2007).

---

### Amygdala and Fear Conditioning

**LeDoux's Lesion Mapping:**

- Bilateral lesions of **medial geniculate nucleus** (auditory relay nucleus of thalamus) → **blocked** fear conditioning to a tone.
- Bilateral lesions of **auditory cortex** → did **NOT** block fear conditioning to a tone.
- Critical pathway: **medial geniculate nucleus → amygdala**.
- Lesions of the **amygdala** blocked fear conditioning (like medial geniculate lesions).

**Amygdala's Role:**
- Receives input from **all sensory systems**.
- The structure in which the **emotional significance of sensory signals is learned and retained**.

**Two Pathways: Medial Geniculate Nucleus → Amygdala**

| Pathway | Route | What It Mediates |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **Direct pathway** | Medial geniculate nucleus → amygdala directly | Fear conditioning to **simple sounds** |
| **Indirect (cortical) pathway** | Medial geniculate nucleus → auditory cortex → amygdala (Romanski & LeDoux, 1992) | Fear conditioning to **complex sounds** (Jarrell et al., 1987) |

- If only one pathway is destroyed → conditioning to simple sounds proceeds normally (both capable of mediating).
- Only the **cortical route** mediates conditioning to complex sounds.

**Pathways FROM the Amygdala:**

| Pathway | Target | Response Mediated |
|---------|--------|-------------------|
| → Periaqueductal gray (PAG) of midbrain | Brainstem | Appropriate **defensive behavioral responses** (Bandler & Shipley, 1994) |
| → Lateral hypothalamus | Hypothalamus | Appropriate **sympathetic responses** |

---

### Contextual Fear Conditioning and the Hippocampus

- **Contextual fear conditioning:** Process by which benign contexts (environments) come to elicit fear through association with fear-inducing stimuli.
- Example: Repeatedly encountering a bear on a particular trail → the trail itself elicits fear.

**Two Ways to Produce Contextual Fear Conditioning in the Lab:**
1. Conventional fear conditioning (CS + US) → animal becomes fearful of the **context** (test chamber) as well as the CS (tone).
2. Delivering aversive stimuli in a distinctive context **without any other CS** → animal becomes fearful of that context/chamber.

**Role of the Hippocampus:**
- Hippocampus plays a key role in memory for **spatial location**.
- **Bilateral hippocampal lesions:**
  - **Block** fear response to **context**.
  - Do **NOT** block fear response to the **explicit CS** (e.g., tone).
- Reference: Antoniadis & McDonald (2000).

---

### Amygdala Complex and Fear Conditioning

- The amygdala is **NOT a single structure** — it is the **amygdala complex**: a cluster of many nuclei (a dozen or so major nuclei, divided into subnuclei).
- Each subnucleus is structurally distinct, has different connections (LeDoux, 2000b), and likely has different functions.

**Critical Structure:**
- The **lateral nucleus of the amygdala** (not the entire amygdala) is critically involved in the **acquisition, storage, and expression of conditioned fear** (Kim & Jung, 2006; Maren & Quirk, 2004).

**Projections to the Lateral Nucleus:**

| Structure | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| **Prefrontal cortex** | Acts on lateral nucleus to **suppress** conditioned fear |
| **Hippocampus** | Interacts with lateral nucleus to mediate learning about the **context** of fear-related events |

## 17.4 Stress and Health

### Definition

- **Stress response / Stress:** A cluster of physiological changes produced when the body is exposed to harm or threat.
- **Stressors:** Experiences that induce the stress response.
- All stressors produce the **same core pattern** of physiological changes — whether psychological or physical.
- **Chronic psychological stress** most frequently implicated in ill health (Kiecolt-Glaser et al., 2002; Natelson, 2004).

---

### The Stress Response

#### Hans Selye's Description (1950s)

- Selye (pronounced **SELL-yay**) first described the stress response in the **1950s**.
- Emphasized the **dual nature**:
  - **Short-term:** Adaptive changes helping the animal respond (e.g., mobilization of energy resources).
  - **Long-term:** Maladaptive changes (e.g., enlarged adrenal glands) — de Kloet, Joëls, and Holsboer (2005).

**Two Systems in the Stress Response:**

**System 1 — Anterior-Pituitary Adrenal-Cortex System:**
- Stressors → neural circuits → **ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)** from anterior pituitary → **glucocorticoids** from adrenal cortex → components of stress response.
- **Circulating glucocorticoid levels** = most commonly employed physiological measure of stress.

**System 2 — Sympathetic-Nervous-System Adrenal-Medulla System:**
- Stressors → activate **sympathetic NS** → increased **epinephrine and norepinephrine** from adrenal medulla.
- Selye initially largely ignored this system.
- Modern theories acknowledge **both systems** (Gunnar & Quevedo, 2007; Ulrich-Lai & Herman, 2009).

**Cytokines as Major Stress Hormones (1990s discovery):**
- Brief stressors → increase in blood levels of **cytokines** (peptide hormones released by many cells; participate in physiological and immunological responses; cause inflammation and fever).
- Cytokines classified with adrenal hormones as **major stress hormones**.

**Evaluation of Selye's Major Assertion:**
- Assertion: Both physical AND psychological stressors induce the same general stress response — **partly correct**.
- But: Stress responses are complex and varied depending on the stressor, its timing, the nature of the stressed person, and how they react (Joëls & Baram, 2009; Miller, Chen, & Zhou, 2007).
- Example (Katz et al., 1970): Women awaiting surgery for possible breast cancer who convinced themselves they couldn't have cancer or that worrying was counterproductive showed **lower stress levels**.

---

### Animal Models of Stress

- Early research used extreme stressors (repeated shock, long physical restraint).
- **Two problems** with extreme-stress research:
  1. **Ethical concerns** — many early studies would not be permitted today.
  2. **Scientific validity** — extreme stress masks normal stress-response variations; hard to relate to common human stressors.

**Subordination Stress:**
- **Subordination stress:** Stress when conspecific threat becomes an enduring feature of daily life.
- Studied in social species with stable **dominance hierarchies (pecking orders)**.
- Subordinate male rodents continually attacked by dominant males show:
  - More likely to attack juveniles
  - Smaller testes
  - Shorter life spans
  - Lower blood testosterone levels
  - Higher blood glucocorticoid levels

---

### Psychosomatic Disorders: The Case of Gastric Ulcers

- **Psychosomatic disorders:** Medical disorders that have psychological causes.
- So many adverse effects of stress on health documented that most, if not all, disorders may now be considered psychosomatic (Miller & Blackwell, 2006).
- **Gastric ulcers:** Painful lesions to the lining of the stomach and duodenum; potentially life-threatening; ~500,000 new cases/year in the USA.

**The H. pylori Controversy:**
- Claim: *Helicobacter pylori* bacteria are responsible for all gastric ulcers (except those from NSAIDs like aspirin) — Blaser (1996).
- **But:** H. pylori alone is insufficient:
  - **75% of healthy individuals** show signs of H. pylori infection but do NOT develop ulcers.
  - **Psychological treatments** help gastric ulcers without reducing H. pylori signs.
  - **Stress** appears to be a necessary co-factor increasing the stomach wall's susceptibility to H. pylori damage.
  - Gastric ulcers occur more commonly in people in **stressful situations**; stressors produce gastric ulcers in laboratory animals.

---

### Psychoneuroimmunology: Stress, the Immune System, and the Brain

- **Discovery in the 1970s:** Stress can increase susceptibility to infectious diseases.
- This led in the **early 1980s** to the new field of **psychoneuroimmunology** — the study of interactions among psychological factors, the nervous system, and the immune system (Fleshner & Laudenslager, 2004).

#### The Immune System

- **Antigens:** Protein molecules on the surface of a cell that identify it as native or foreign. The immune system must distinguish foreign from body cells before acting.

**Two Divisions of the Mammalian Immune System:**

| Division | Role | Key Feature |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Innate immune system** | First line of defense; acts near entry points; attacks general classes of molecules produced by various pathogens | Rapid; no memory required |
| **Adaptive immune system** | Targeted defense; binds to specific antigens on foreign cells; destroys them or marks for destruction | Has **memory** — once pathogens recognized and destroyed, promptly eliminated if they invade again |

**Innate Immune System Mechanism:**
- Activated by **toll-like receptors** (structurally similar to "toll" receptor discovered in fruit flies).
- **Phagocytes** (cells that destroy/ingest pathogens — e.g., macrophages, microglia) have toll-like receptors.
- Upon binding to pathogens → two responses:
  1. **Phagocytosis:** Destruction and ingestion of foreign matter (bacteria).
  2. Release of **cytokines** → inflammatory response (swelling, redness, fever, body aches, flu-like symptoms) + attracts more phagocytes + activates lymphocytes.

**Adaptive Immune System:**
- **Lymphocytes:** Specialized white blood cells produced in bone marrow and thymus; stored in lymphatic system until activated.
- Two general types:

| Cell Type | Immunity Type | Mechanism |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| **T lymphocytes (T cells)** | **Cell-mediated immunity** | Kill microorganisms or body cells they have invaded |
| **B lymphocytes (B cells)** | **Antibody-mediated immunity** | Produce antibodies that bind to and destroy foreign antigens |

**Cell-Mediated Immunity (T cells) — Step by Step:**
1. Macrophage ingests foreign microorganism.
2. Macrophage displays microorganism's **antigens** on its cell membrane surface.
3. This attracts **T cells** — each has two receptor types: one for macrophage surface molecules, one for a specific foreign antigen.
4. T cell with appropriate receptor binds to infected macrophage → series of reactions initiated.
5. Bound T cell multiplies → creates more T cells with specific receptor to destroy all invaders with target antigens and all infected body cells.

**Antibody-Mediated Immunity (B cells) — Step by Step:**
1. **B cell** binds to a foreign antigen via appropriate receptor.
2. B cell multiplies and synthesizes a lethal form of its receptor molecules = **antibodies**.
3. Antibodies released into intracellular fluid → bind to foreign antigens → **destroy or deactivate** microorganisms.
4. **Memory B cells** for the specific antigen are also produced — long-lived; accelerate antibody-mediated immunity upon subsequent infection.

**T-reg Cells (Regulatory T Cells):**
- Sometimes T cells mistakenly attack the body's own tissue → **autoimmune diseases** (e.g., T cells attacking myelin → multiple sclerosis).
- **T-reg cells:** Identify and destroy T cells engaged in self-attacks. Potentially of major clinical significance (Fehervari & Sakaguchi, 2006).

**Vaccination:**
- Adaptive immune memory gives vaccinations their **prophylactic (preventive)** effect.
- Vaccination = administering a weakened form of a virus → adaptive immune system prepared if virus later invades.
- **Immunization:** The process of creating immunity through vaccination.
- Example: Smallpox largely eradicated by vaccination with weaker **cowpox** virus.

**Immune Synapses:**
- High-resolution microscopy revealed connections between immune cells resembling **synapses** (Biber et al., 2007; Davis, 2006).

#### What Effect Does Stress Have on Immune Function?

> **Evolutionary challenge:** If stress always disrupts immune function, this is a maladaptive response. How could it have evolved? (Segerstrom, 2007) — this challenges the widespread belief that stress always disrupts immunity.

**KEY META-ANALYSIS — Segerstrom and Miller (2004):**
- Reviewed ~300 previous studies of stress and immune function.

| Type of Stress | Duration | Effect on Immunity |
|----------------|----------|--------------------|
| **Acute stressors** | Less than 100 minutes (public speaking, athletic competition, musical performance) | **IMPROVE** immune function — mainly **innate immune system** (rapid mobilization) |
| **Chronic stressors** | Long-lasting (caring for ill relative, unemployment) | **IMPAIR adaptive immune system** |

- **Eustress:** Stress that **improves** health or other aspects of functioning.
- **Distress:** Stress that **disrupts** health or other aspects of functioning.

**Cytokines and the Bidirectional Role:**
- Short-term cytokine-induced inflammatory responses → **help** the body combat infection.
- Long-term cytokine release → associated with a variety of **adverse health consequences** (Robles, Glaser, & Kiecolt-Glaser, 2005).

**How Does Stress Influence Immune Function?**
- Mechanisms are numerous and complex.
- Both T cells and B cells have receptors for **glucocorticoids**.
- Lymphocytes have receptors for **epinephrine, norepinephrine, and glucocorticoids**.
- Many neuropeptides released by neurons are also released by immune system cells.
- Cytokines (originally thought to be produced only by immune cells) are also produced in the **nervous system** (Salzet, Vieau, & Day, 2000).
- **Behavioral routes:** Stressed people change diet, exercise, sleep, drug use — all can influence immune function.
- Example: Wolf et al. (2007) — stress in mothers aggravates asthmatic symptoms in their children; asthma in children increases stress in their mothers.

**Why Decreased Immune Function May NOT Increase Infectious Disease:**
1. Immune system has many **redundant components** — disruption of one may have little effect.
2. Stress-produced changes may be **too short-lived** to substantially affect infection probability.
3. Declines in some aspects of immunity may induce **compensatory increases** in others.

**KEY STUDY — Cohen et al. (1991):**
- Assessed psychological stress levels in 394 healthy participants via questionnaires.
- Participants randomly received saline nasal drops with a **respiratory virus** or only saline → all quarantined.
- Result: **Higher proportion of those with high stress scores developed colds.**

**Three Types of Evidence Supporting Stress → Susceptibility to Infectious Disease:**
1. Correlational studies in humans (e.g., Glaser et al., 1987: more respiratory infections during final exams).
2. Controlled experiments with laboratory animals.
3. Partially controlled studies with humans (rare for ethical reasons).

### Early Experience of Stress

- Early exposure to severe stress → variety of **adverse effects** on subsequent development.
- Children subjected to maltreatment/severe stress display brain and endocrine system abnormalities (Evans & Kim, 2007; Teicher et al., 2003).
- Some psychiatric disorders may result from interaction between **inherited susceptibility** and **early exposure to severe stress** (discussed further in Chapter 18).
- Early stress exposure often **increases intensity** of subsequent stress responses (increases glucocorticoid release in response to later stressors).

**Prenatal Stress:**
- Adverse effects of stress begin **before birth**.
- Many experiments demonstrate adverse effects of **prenatal stress** in laboratory animals (Maccari et al., 2003; Weinstock, 2008).

**Rat Pup Handling Studies (Sapolsky, 1997):**
- Handling rat pups for **a few minutes per day** during first few weeks of life has **salutary (health-promoting)** effects.
- Most effects result from **decrease in the magnitude of stress responses**.
- As adults, handled pups displayed **smaller increases in circulating glucocorticoids** in response to stressors.

## 17.5 Brain Mechanisms of Human Emotion

### Cognitive Neuroscience of Emotion

- Modern cognitive neuroscience of emotion uses neuroimaging and lesion studies to identify neural substrates of human emotional experience.
- Phineas Gage (1848): frontal lobe damage affected both medial prefrontal lobes, which are now known to be involved in **planning and emotion** (Machado & Bachevalier, 2006; Vogt, 2005).

### Amygdala and Human Emotion

- The amygdala plays a central role in human emotional processing, particularly for **fear-related stimuli**.
- The amygdala is specifically implicated in the recognition of **fearful facial expressions**.

### The Case of S.P.: The Woman Who Couldn't Perceive Fear

- S.P. had **bilateral amygdala damage**.
- She was **unable to recognize fearful facial expressions** despite being able to recognize other emotions.
- This case provides strong evidence for the amygdala's specific role in processing **fear-related information** in humans.
- Supports the broader finding from animal research on fear conditioning regarding the amygdala's central role.

### Medial Prefrontal Lobes

- **Medial prefrontal lobes** are involved in **planning and emotion** (Machado & Bachevalier, 2006; Vogt, 2005).
- Connection to the Phineas Gage case (1848): the tamping iron destroyed both medial prefrontal lobes.
- The medial prefrontal cortex projects to the **lateral nucleus of the amygdala** and is thought to **suppress conditioned fear**.
- Damage → profound changes in personality and emotional regulation (consistent with what we see in Gage and similar cases).

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Principle of antithesis** | Darwin's principle that opposite messages are often signaled by opposite movements and postures. |
| **James-Lange theory** | Theory (James & Lange, 1884) that ANS/somatic responses to emotion-inducing stimuli produce the feeling of emotion. |
| **Cannon-Bard theory** | Theory (Cannon, ~1915; Bard) that emotional experience and emotional expression are parallel, independent processes with no direct causal relation. |
| **Sham rage** | Exaggerated, poorly directed aggressive responses in decorticate animals (Bard, 1929); hypothalamus required; cortex inhibits/directs aggression. |
| **Limbic system** | Circuit of interconnected nuclei and tracts ringing the thalamus, proposed by Papez (1937); includes amygdala, hippocampus, cingulate gyrus, mammillary body, fornix, septum, olfactory bulb, hypothalamus. |
| **Kluver-Bucy syndrome** | Syndrome (1939) following bilateral anterior temporal lobectomy; characterized by hyperphagia, hypersexuality, hypermetamorphosis, oral tendencies, and lack of fear. Primarily due to amygdala damage. |
| **Polygraphy** | Method of interrogation using ANS indexes of emotion; detects emotions, not lies. |
| **Control-question technique** | Polygraph method comparing physiological responses to target questions vs. control questions; assumes lying = greater sympathetic activation. ~80% accuracy in mock-crime studies. |
| **Guilty-knowledge technique** | Polygraph method using information known only to the guilty; assesses differential reactions to actual vs. contrived crime details (Lykken, 1959). 88% accurate; no false positives in the classic study. |
| **Facial feedback hypothesis** | Hypothesis that facial expressions influence emotional experience (Rutledge & Hupka, 1985; Adelmann & Zajonc, 1989). |
| **Duchenne smile** | Genuine smile of enjoyment involving both orbicularis oculi (involuntary) and zygomaticus major (voluntary); first described by Duchenne (1862); named by Ekman. |
| **Microexpressions** | Brief (~0.05 second) involuntary facial expressions of real emotion that break through false expressions (Porter & ten Brinke, 2008). |
| **Orbicularis oculi** | Facial muscle encircling the eye; contracts during genuine smiles; normally cannot be controlled voluntarily; its absence unmasks a false smile. |
| **Zygomaticus major** | Facial muscle pulling lip corners up; contracts during smiles; can be controlled voluntarily. |
| **Emotional valence** | The general positive or negative character of an emotion. |
| **Defensive behaviors** | Behaviors whose primary function is to protect the organism from threat or harm. |
| **Aggressive behaviors** | Behaviors whose primary function is to threaten or harm. |
| **Predatory aggression** | Stalking and killing members of other species for the purpose of eating; rats kill by biting the back of the neck. |
| **Social aggression** | Unprovoked aggression toward a conspecific to establish, alter, or maintain a social hierarchy; primarily among males; related to testosterone in many species. |
| **Target-site concept** | The idea that aggressive and defensive behaviors are designed to attack specific body sites on another animal while protecting specific sites on one's own body. |
| **Colony-intruder model** | Blanchard and Blanchard's model for studying rat aggression and defense using alpha-male/intruder interactions. |
| **Fear conditioning** | Establishment of fear in response to a previously neutral stimulus (CS) by pairing it with an aversive stimulus (US). |
| **Conditional stimulus (CS)** | Previously neutral stimulus (e.g., tone) that comes to elicit a fear response after pairing with an aversive stimulus. |
| **Unconditional stimulus (US)** | Aversive stimulus (e.g., electric shock) that unconditionally elicits a fear response. |
| **Contextual fear conditioning** | Process by which contexts (environments) come to elicit fear through association with fear-inducing stimuli. |
| **Amygdala complex** | The cluster of many nuclei (a dozen or so major nuclei with subnuclei) that makes up the amygdala; NOT a single structure. |
| **Lateral nucleus of the amygdala** | Specific nucleus critically involved in acquisition, storage, and expression of conditioned fear; receives projections from prefrontal cortex (suppresses fear) and hippocampus (contextual learning). |
| **Medial geniculate nucleus** | Auditory relay nucleus of the thalamus; critical for auditory fear conditioning; projects to amygdala via direct and indirect (cortical) pathways. |
| **Periaqueductal gray (PAG)** | Midbrain structure receiving amygdala input; mediates appropriate defensive behavioral responses in fear conditioning. |
| **Stress response / stress** | Cluster of physiological changes produced when the body is exposed to harm or threat; first described by Selye (1950s). |
| **Stressor** | Experience that induces the stress response. |
| **ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)** | Released from anterior pituitary in response to stressors; triggers release of glucocorticoids from adrenal cortex. |
| **Glucocorticoids** | Hormones released from adrenal cortex in response to ACTH; produce many components of the stress response; most commonly used physiological measure of stress. |
| **Cytokines** | Peptide hormones released by many cells; participate in physiological and immunological responses, causing inflammation and fever; classified as major stress hormones (discovered relevant to stress in 1990s). |
| **Subordination stress** | Stress experienced when conspecific threat becomes an enduring feature of daily life; studied in species with dominance hierarchies. |
| **Psychosomatic disorders** | Medical disorders that have psychological causes. |
| **Gastric ulcers** | Painful lesions to the lining of the stomach and duodenum; caused by H. pylori AND stress as co-factors. |
| **H. pylori (Helicobacter pylori)** | Bacteria found in 75% of healthy individuals; associated with gastric ulcers, but stress is also required as a co-factor for ulcer development. |
| **Psychoneuroimmunology** | Study of interactions among psychological factors, the nervous system, and the immune system; emerged early 1980s. |
| **Antigens** | Protein molecules on the surface of a cell that identify it as native or foreign. |
| **Innate immune system** | First line of defense; acts near body entry points; attacks general classes of pathogen molecules; no memory. |
| **Adaptive immune system** | Targeted defense; binds to specific antigens; has immunological memory. |
| **Phagocytes** | Cells (macrophages, microglia) that destroy and ingest pathogens via phagocytosis. |
| **Phagocytosis** | The destruction and ingestion of foreign matter by phagocytes. |
| **Toll-like receptors** | Receptors on phagocytes that bind to pathogens, triggering phagocytosis and cytokine release; structurally similar to the "toll" receptor in fruit flies. |
| **Lymphocytes** | Specialized white blood cells of the adaptive immune system; produced in bone marrow and thymus; two types: T cells and B cells. |
| **T lymphocytes (T cells)** | Direct cell-mediated immunity; destroy microorganisms or body cells invaded by them. |
| **B lymphocytes (B cells)** | Direct antibody-mediated immunity; produce antibodies against specific foreign antigens. |
| **Cell-mediated immunity** | Adaptive immune response directed by T cells; kills microorganisms and infected body cells. |
| **Antibody-mediated immunity** | Adaptive immune response directed by B cells; involves antibody production that binds to and destroys foreign antigens. |
| **Antibodies** | Lethal forms of B cell receptor molecules released into intracellular fluid; bind to and destroy/deactivate foreign antigens. |
| **Memory B cells** | Long-lived B cells produced during antibody-mediated immune reaction; accelerate future immune responses to the same antigen. |
| **T-reg cells (regulatory T cells)** | T cells that combat autoimmune diseases by identifying and destroying T cells that attack the body's own tissue (Fehervari & Sakaguchi, 2006). |
| **Autoimmune diseases** | Diseases caused by T cells mistakenly attacking the body's own tissue (e.g., multiple sclerosis). |
| **Immunization** | The process of creating immunity through vaccination. |
| **Eustress** | Stress that improves health or other aspects of functioning (acute stressors). |
| **Distress** | Stress that disrupts health or other aspects of functioning (chronic stressors). |
| **Salutary effects** | Health-promoting effects (as in the rat pup handling studies). |
| **Mock-crime procedure** | Experimental method in polygraphy research: volunteer subjects participate in a mock crime; then given a polygraph test by an examiner unaware of guilt/innocence. |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **James-Lange theory (1884):** Body responses (ANS/somatic) PRODUCE the feeling of emotion — proposed independently by James AND Lange; the first physiological theory of emotion.

2. **Cannon-Bard theory (~1915):** Emotional experience and emotional expression are parallel, independent processes. All emotions produce the SAME general pattern of sympathetic activation. The feeling of emotion does NOT depend on ANS feedback.

3. **Sham rage (Bard, 1929):** Exaggerated, poorly directed aggression in decorticate cats. Requires the hypothalamus (cannot occur without it). Demonstrates: hypothalamus is critical for aggressive expression; cortex inhibits and directs aggression.

4. **Kluver-Bucy syndrome (1939):** Bilateral anterior temporal lobectomy. Five key features: hyperphagia, hypersexuality (inappropriate objects), hypermetamorphosis, oral tendencies, LACK OF FEAR. Most symptoms attributable to AMYGDALA damage.

5. **Limbic system structures (Papez, 1937; MacLean, 1952):** Amygdala, hippocampus, mammillary body, fornix, cingulate gyrus, septum, olfactory bulb, hypothalamus.

6. **Duchenne smile:** Involves orbicularis oculi (involuntary; lateral portion; contracts only in genuine pleasure) AND zygomaticus major (voluntary). False smiles lack the lateral orbicularis oculi component. Duchenne (1862) first described; Ekman named it.

7. **Six primary facial expressions (Ekman & Friesen):** Surprise, anger, sadness, disgust, fear, happiness. Only HAPPINESS has positive emotional valence. All other expressions are mixtures of these primaries.

8. **Guilty-knowledge technique (Lykken, 1959):** 88% of mock criminals correctly identified; NO innocent subjects judged guilty. Uses only electrodermal activity. Compares reactions to real vs. contrived crime details. Avoids the problem that affects the control-question technique (all suspects react to target questions).

9. **Auditory fear conditioning neural circuit (LeDoux):** Medial geniculate nucleus (thalamic auditory relay) → direct pathway to amygdala (mediates simple tones) OR → auditory cortex → amygdala (mediates complex sounds). Amygdala → PAG (defensive behaviors) + lateral hypothalamus (sympathetic responses). LATERAL NUCLEUS of amygdala is the critical site for acquisition, storage, and expression of conditioned fear.

10. **Contextual fear conditioning:** Requires the HIPPOCAMPUS (memory for spatial location). Bilateral hippocampal lesions block fear of context but NOT fear of the explicit CS (tone). Bilateral amygdala lesions block both.

11. **Stress response (Selye, 1950s):** Dual system — (1) anterior pituitary → ACTH → adrenal cortex → glucocorticoids; (2) sympathetic NS → adrenal medulla → epinephrine/norepinephrine. Cytokines added as major stress hormones in the 1990s. Circulating glucocorticoid level = most common physiological measure of stress.

12. **Acute vs. chronic stress and immunity (Segerstrom & Miller, 2004):** ACUTE stressors (<100 min) → IMPROVE innate immune function (eustress). CHRONIC stressors → IMPAIR adaptive immune system (distress). Cytokines: short-term = help fight infection; long-term = adverse health consequences.

13. **H. pylori and gastric ulcers:** H. pylori alone is insufficient — 75% of healthy people have it. Stress is a necessary co-factor increasing susceptibility. Psychological treatments help ulcers WITHOUT reducing H. pylori. Both antibiotic AND psychological treatments are effective.

14. **Testosterone and aggression:** Blood testosterone = NOT the best measure (brain levels matter more). Aggressive activity INCREASES testosterone (bidirectional). In humans, most violent outbursts are DEFENSIVE ATTACKS (not social aggression), which are not testosterone-related.

15. **Psychoneuroimmunology:** Emerged early 1980s after 1970s discovery that stress can increase susceptibility to infectious disease. Cohen et al. (1991): higher stress scores → higher proportion developing colds when exposed to respiratory viruses. Stress may not directly increase infectious disease incidence due to redundancy, short duration of changes, and compensatory immune increases.
