# Chapter 1: Biopsychology as a Neuroscience

**Overview:** This chapter introduces biopsychology as a scientific discipline within neuroscience, defines its scope and relation to other neuroscientific fields, characterizes its research approaches (human/nonhuman, experimental/nonexperimental, pure/applied), describes its six major divisions, and illustrates how converging operations and scientific inference are used. Two critical-thinking case studies (Delgado's bull; Moniz's lobotomy) anchor the chapter's emphasis on evaluating biopsychological claims.

---

## 1. What Is Biopsychology?

- **Biopsychology** is the scientific study of the **biology of behavior** (Dewsbury, 1991).
- Alternative names: **psychobiology**, **behavioral biology**, **behavioral neuroscience**; Pinel prefers "biopsychology" because it signals a *biological approach to psychology* rather than a psychological approach to biology.
- **Psychology** (as used here): the scientific study of behavior — all overt activities of the organism *plus* the internal processes presumed to underlie them (learning, memory, motivation, perception, emotion).
- **Neuroscience**: the scientific study of the nervous system; biopsychology is one discipline within it.
- The **human brain**: approximately **1.3 kilograms**, contains an estimated **100 billion neurons** (Azevedo et al., 2009) and an estimated **100 trillion connections** among them.
- **Neurons**: cells that receive and transmit electrochemical signals.

### Historical Origin

- Biopsychology did not become a major neuroscientific discipline until the **20th century**.
- Key founding text: **"The Organization of Behavior" (1949)** by **D.O. Hebb**.
  - Hebb developed the **first comprehensive theory** of how complex psychological phenomena (perceptions, emotions, thoughts, memories) might be produced by brain activity.
  - His work discredited the view that psychological functioning is too complex to have roots in brain physiology/chemistry.
  - Used an **eclectic approach**: human experiments, animal experiments, clinical case studies, and logical argument from observation — this has become a **hallmark of biopsychological inquiry**.
- Described as an "infant" science compared to physics, chemistry, and biology.

### Case of Jimmie G. (Oliver Sacks)

- Jimmie G.: 49-year-old male on a neurological ward, tested by neurologist **Oliver Sacks**.
- Believed he was 19; had no memory of events since his early 20s.
- Could not form **new lasting memories** — forgot everything said or shown to him within a few seconds.
- Condition: **Korsakoff's syndrome** (fully discussed in section 6).
- Source: *The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat* (Oliver Sacks, 1986).

## 2. Four Major Themes of This Book

Each theme is marked with a distinct tab throughout the text.

### 2.1 Thinking Creatively about Biopsychology

- **Thinking creatively**: thinking in productive, unconventional ways.
- Conventional wisdom and preconceptions shared by researchers often **impede scientific progress**.
- Most important advances come from researchers who overcome restrictive conventional thinking.
- Described as the "cornerstone of any science."

### 2.2 The Evolutionary Perspective

- **Evolutionary perspective**: understanding biological phenomena by considering the environmental pressures that led to evolution of brains and behavior.
- Key component: **comparative approach** — understanding biological phenomena by comparing them in different species.
- Humans have learned much about themselves by studying evolutionarily related species.
- Evolutionary approach is one of the "cornerstones of modern biopsychological inquiry."
- Related topic: **genetics of behavior** (Chapter 2).

### 2.3 Neuroplasticity

- Until early 1990s: brain was conceptualized as a static 3D array of neural elements wired in a massive network.
- **Neuroplasticity**: the brain is a **plastic (changeable) organ** that continuously grows and changes in response to genes and experiences.
- Discovery of neuroplasticity is "arguably the **single most influential discovery in modern neuroscience**."

### 2.4 Clinical Implications

- **Clinical**: pertaining to illness or treatment.
- Two aspects:
  1. Much of what is learned about the **normal brain** comes from studying the diseased or damaged brain.
  2. Much biopsychological research has relevance for the **treatment of brain disorders**.

## 3. What Is the Relation between Biopsychology and the Other Disciplines of Neuroscience?

- Biopsychologists bring **knowledge of behavior and methods of behavioral research** to neuroscience — this behavioral orientation is their unique contribution.
- The ultimate purpose of the nervous system is to **produce and control behavior** (Grillner & Dickinson, 2002).
- Biopsychology is an **integrative discipline**: draws together knowledge from other neuroscientific disciplines.

## 4. What Types of Research Characterize the Biopsychological Approach?

Three major dimensions along which biopsychological research approaches vary:
1. **Human vs. nonhuman subjects**
2. **Experiments vs. nonexperiments**
3. **Pure vs. applied research**

### 4.1 Human and Nonhuman Subjects

**Advantages of human subjects:**
- Can follow instructions.
- Can report subjective experiences.
- Often cheaper (high animal-care standards make animal labs expensive).
- Have human brains — directly relevant to human brain function.

**Why use nonhuman subjects? (Evolutionary Continuity)**
- Human brains differ from other mammals primarily in **overall size** and **extent of cortical development** — differences are **more quantitative than qualitative**.
- Many principles of human brain function can be clarified by studying nonhumans (Nakahara et al., 2002; Passingham, 2009; Platt & Spelke, 2009).

**Advantages of nonhuman subjects:**
1. Simpler brains and behavior — more likely to reveal **fundamental brain-behavior interactions**.
2. **Comparative approach** yields insights (comparing species with vs. without cerebral cortex illuminates cortical function).
3. Permits research **ethically impossible in humans** (though nonhuman research is governed by strict ethics too).

**Ethics:**
- All biopsychological research regulated by **independent committees** and **strict ethical guidelines**.
- Ulrich (1991): "if the animals we observe are reasonable models of our own most intricate actions, then they must be respected as we would respect our own sensibilities."
- Most common nonhuman subjects: **rats**; also mice, cats, dogs, nonhuman primates.

### 4.2 Experiments and Nonexperiments

#### The Experiment

- The **experiment** is the method used to study **causation** (what causes what).
- **Between-subjects design**: different groups tested under each condition.
- **Within-subjects design**: same group tested under each condition.
- **Independent variable (IV)**: the one relevant difference between conditions; manipulated by the experimenter.
- **Dependent variable (DV)**: the variable measured to assess the effect of the IV.
- **Confounded variable**: an unintended variable that differs between conditions and could explain the DV; must be eliminated.

> **KEY STUDY: Lester & Gorzalka (1988) — Coolidge Effect in Female Hamsters**
>
> - **Background**: The **Coolidge effect** is the phenomenon whereby a copulating male who becomes incapable of continuing with one partner can resume with a new one. Named after **President Calvin Coolidge** (anecdote about roosters).
> - **Problem**: Coolidge effect had not been demonstrated in females. Lester & Gorzalka argued this was due to a **confounded variable**: males fatigue faster, so increased female receptivity to a new male could reflect the new male's greater vigor, not a genuine Coolidge effect.
> - **Design**: Female copulated with male 1; simultaneously the "unfamiliar" male (male 2) copulated with a different female (equalizing fatigue). Both males rested. Female then tested with either familiar or unfamiliar male.
> - **DV**: Duration of **lordosis** (arched-back, rump-up, tail-diverted posture = female rodent sexual receptivity).
> - **Result**: Females showed significantly greater lordosis duration to the **unfamiliar male** on the third test, despite equal fatigue in both males.
> - **Conclusion**: Females DO show the Coolidge effect; demonstrates importance of controlling confounded variables; males and females are more similar than commonly assumed.

#### Quasiexperimental Studies

- **Quasiexperimental studies**: studies of groups exposed to conditions of interest in the real world; resemble experiments but are **not true experiments** because subjects are not randomly assigned, and potential confounded variables are not controlled.
- Example: **Acker et al. (1984)** — 100 detoxified male alcoholics vs. 50 male nondrinkers; alcoholics performed worse on perceptual, motor, and cognitive tests and showed brain damage on scans. But causation cannot be inferred — groups also differed in education, head injury history, other drug use, diet quality, etc.

#### Case Studies

- **Case studies**: focus on a single case or subject.
- Advantages: in-depth picture; excellent source of testable hypotheses.
- Key limitation: **generalizability** — results may not apply to other cases due to individual variability in brain and behavior.

### 4.3 Pure and Applied Research

- **Pure research**: motivated by curiosity; done solely to acquire knowledge; no immediate application required.
- **Applied research**: intended to bring about direct benefit to humankind.
- Many scientists hold that pure research ultimately proves more practically beneficial because applications flow from understanding basic principles.
- Pure research is more **vulnerable to political regulation**.

## 5. What Are the Divisions of Biopsychology?

Six major divisions; significant overlap exists and many biopsychologists work across more than one.

### 5.1 Physiological Psychology

- **Focus**: Neural mechanisms of behavior through **direct manipulation of the brain** in controlled experiments.
- Methods: **surgical and electrical** brain manipulation.
- Subjects: almost always **laboratory animals**.
- Tradition of **pure research**; emphasis on theory of neural control of behavior.
- Memory example: surgical removal of **hippocampus** in rats and assessment of memory task performance.

### 5.2 Psychopharmacology

- **Focus**: Manipulation of neural activity and behavior with **drugs**.
- Emerged partly from physiological psychology.
- Substantial portion is **applied** (developing therapeutic drugs; reducing drug abuse).
- Subjects: laboratory animals and humans (when ethics permits).
- Memory example: drugs increasing **acetylcholine** levels in Alzheimer's patients.

### 5.3 Neuropsychology

- **Focus**: **Psychological effects of brain damage in human patients**.
- Methods: almost exclusively **case studies** and **quasiexperimental studies**.
- Most **applied** of all biopsychological divisions.
- Focus on **cerebral cortex** (most commonly damaged by accident or surgery).
- Neuropsychological tests: facilitate diagnosis, guide treatment, inform patient care and counseling.

> **Case of Mr. R. (Kolb & Whishaw, 1990):**
> - 21-year-old left-handed male; head injury in car accident; declined from honor student to mediocre.
> - Language functions in **right hemisphere** (occurs in ~1/3 of left-handers).
> - Verbal memory and reading speed low-average despite superior IQ — indicated right temporal lobe damage.
> - Outcome: recommended vocations not requiring superior verbal memory; switched to architecture.

### 5.4 Psychophysiology

- **Focus**: Relation between **physiological activity** and **psychological processes** in humans.
- Recording: **noninvasive** (from body surface).
- Primary brain measure: **scalp EEG**.
- Other measures: muscle tension, eye movement, ANS indicators (heart rate, blood pressure, pupil dilation, skin electrical conductance).
- **Autonomic nervous system (ANS)**: division of the nervous system regulating the body's inner environment.
- Clinical example: schizophrenics show impaired **smooth pursuit eye tracking** (pendulum; Chen et al., 2008; Iacono & Koenig, 1983).
- Memory example: familiar faces elicit normal ANS changes even when brain-damaged patients report not recognizing a face.

### 5.5 Cognitive Neuroscience

- **Focus**: **Neural bases of cognition** (thought, memory, attention, complex perception).
- **Youngest** and currently one of the most active divisions.
- Subjects: mostly **humans**; methods: **noninvasive**.
- Major method: **functional brain imaging** — recording images of living brain activity during cognitive tasks.
- Typically involves **interdisciplinary collaboration**: biopsychologists, cognitive psychologists, social psychologists, economists, mathematicians, various neuroscientists.
- Memory example: brain-imaging shows changes in various regions during memory tasks.

### 5.6 Comparative Psychology

- **Focus**: **Biology of behavior** broadly — evolution, genetics, and **adaptiveness of behavior**.
- Method: **comparative approach** — comparing behavior of different species.
- Subfields:
  - **Ethological research**: behavior in its **natural environment**.
  - **Evolutionary psychology**: understanding behavior via likely evolutionary origins (Caporael, 2001; Duchaine, Cosmides, & Tooby, 2001).
  - **Behavioral genetics**: genetic influences on behavior (Carson & Rothstein, 1999; Plomin et al., 2002).
- Memory example: **seed-caching bird species** tend to have **larger hippocampi** — confirming hippocampus involvement in spatial/location memory.

### Summary Table: Six Divisions

| Division | Focus | Subjects | Methods | Pure/Applied |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Physiological Psychology | Neural mechanisms of behavior | Nonhuman animals | Direct brain manipulation | Mostly pure |
| Psychopharmacology | Drug effects on brain and behavior | Animals + humans | Drug administration | Both |
| Neuropsychology | Effects of brain damage in humans | Human patients | Case studies, quasiexperiments | Mostly applied |
| Psychophysiology | Physiology-psychology relations in humans | Humans | Noninvasive physiological recording | Both |
| Cognitive Neuroscience | Neural bases of human cognition | Humans | Functional brain imaging | Both |
| Comparative Psychology | Evolution, genetics, adaptiveness of behavior | Various species | Comparative method, ethology | Both |

## 6. Converging Operations: How Do Biopsychologists Work Together?

- **Converging operations**: combined use of different research approaches focused on a single problem such that strengths of one compensate for weaknesses of others (Thompson, 2005).
- Major issues rarely resolved by a single experiment or single line of experiments.

### Neuropsychology vs. Physiological Psychology (Complementary Strengths)

| Approach | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| Neuropsychology | Directly involves human patients | Cannot conduct experiments (ethical) |
| Physiological Psychology | Can use experimental method and neuroscientific technology | Relevance of animal research to human deficits always questionable |

### Case Study: Korsakoff's Syndrome and Converging Operations

- **Korsakoff's syndrome**: first described by **S.S. Korsakoff** (Russian physician, late 19th century).
- **Primary symptom**: severe memory loss; sufferers are often otherwise cognitively capable.
- Initially believed to be a **direct toxic effect of alcohol** on the brain (quasiexperimental inference — later shown wrong).

**How converging operations revealed the true cause:**

1. **Neuropsychological case studies**: Korsakoff's found in malnourished non-alcoholics — alcohol alone could not be the cause.
2. **Quasiexperiments with humans**: confirmed memory deficits and brain damage in alcoholics vs. nondrinkers.
3. **Controlled animal experiments**: **thiamine (vitamin B1)-deficient rats** showed memory deficits and brain damage patterns similar to human alcoholics (Mumby, Cameli, & Glenn, 1999).

- **True cause**: **thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency** — not direct alcohol toxicity.
- Alcoholics develop Korsakoff's because: (a) alcohol provides calories without vitamins, and (b) alcohol **interferes with thiamine metabolism**.
- Alcohol may also have a direct toxic effect on thiamine-deficient brains (Zimitat et al., 1990).
- **Treatment today**: stop drinking + **massive doses of thiamine** — limits further damage; may yield slight improvement; existing damage is largely **permanent**.

## 7. Scientific Inference: How Do Biopsychologists Study the Unobservable Workings of the Brain?

- **Scientific inference**: empirical method used to study **unobservable processes** — scientists measure observable events and logically infer the nature of unobservable events from those measures.
- Analogous to a detective recreating an unwitnessed crime from clues.
- Neural mechanisms cannot be directly observed; must be studied via scientific inference.

### Eye-Movement Demonstration (Four Conditions)

| Condition | Eye | Object | Retinal Image | Perception |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Stationary | Stationary | Stationary | No movement |
| 2 | **Actively** rotates up | Stationary | Moves up | **No movement perceived** (active movement subtracted) |
| 3 | **Passively** rotated up (finger) | Stationary | Moves up | **Object seen to move down** (passive movement NOT subtracted) |
| 4 | Stationary | Moves down | Moves up | Object seen to move down |

**Conclusion (by inference):** The brain subtracts only **actively commanded** eye movements from total retinal image motion to perceive object motion; it does NOT subtract passive eye movements.

> **Hammond, Merton, & Sutton (1956) Experiment:**
> - Merton injected **curare** (paralytic substance; active ingredient used by South American natives on blow darts) into his own eye muscles.
> - Tried to move eyes; stationary world appeared to **move in the same direction as attempted movements**.
> - Interpretation: Brain sent active motor commands, assumed movement occurred, attributed stationary retinal image to object movement.
> - Confirms the brain uses a **corollary discharge / efference copy** mechanism, not actual eye position feedback, to compute perceived motion.

## 8. Critical Thinking about Biopsychological Claims

- **Critical thinking**: identifying weaknesses in existing ideas and the evidence on which they are based.
- First step in evaluating a claim: determine if it was **published in a reputable peer-reviewed scientific journal** (Rensberger, 2000).
  - Peer review: typically **3-4 expert reviewers**; best journals publish only a small proportion of submitted manuscripts.
  - Peer review is NOT a guarantee of freedom from unrecognized flaws.

### Case 1: José and the Bull (Delgado)

**The demonstration:**
- José Delgado entered a Spanish bullring with a radio transmitter; sent a weak electrical current through an electrode implanted in the **caudate nucleus** of a bull.
- Bull stopped charging; stood tamely.
- Delgado's claim: discovered a caudate **"taming center"**; caudate stimulation eliminates aggression; suggested as treatment for human psychopaths.
- Disseminated through **news media, not peer-reviewed publication**.

**Critical analysis:**
- Multiple simpler/more probable explanations exist for the same observation:
  - Bull became confused, dizzy, nauseous, sleepy, temporarily blind.
  - Stimulation was painful.
  - Stimulation activated a **motor pathway** (forced the bull to turn — Valenstein, 1973).
- **Morgan's Canon**: when multiple interpretations exist, give precedence to the **simplest one**.
- Valenstein (1973): film evidence suggests stimulation activated a neural pathway controlling movement; "he capitalizes on every individual effect his electrodes happen to produce."
- Lesson: observations with multiple equally plausible explanations provide little support for any single interpretation.

### Case 2: Becky, Moniz, and Prefrontal Lobotomy

**The claim:**
- 1935: Egas Moniz heard at a scientific meeting that **Becky** (a chimpanzee) that became upset when making errors in a food-rewarded task stopped doing so after a **bilateral lesion of her prefrontal lobes**.
- Moniz persuaded neurosurgeon **Almeida Lima** to operate on psychiatric patients using a **leucotome** (cutting out six cores of prefrontal tissue).
- **Prefrontal lobotomy**: surgery severing connections between the **prefrontal lobes** and the rest of the brain as treatment for mental illness.
- **Prefrontal lobes**: large areas at the very front of the brain (left and right).
- Moniz received the **Nobel Prize in Physiology and Medicine in 1949**.

**Transorbital lobotomy:**
- Variation developed in Italy, popularized in the US by **Walter Freeman** in the late 1940s.
- Procedure: ice-pick-like device inserted under the eyelid, driven through the **orbit (eye socket)** with a mallet taps, pushed into frontal lobes, waved back and forth to sever prefrontal connections.
- Performed in the surgeon's office.
- **Psychosurgery**: any brain surgery performed for the treatment of a psychological problem.

**Critical analysis:**
- Entire program based on **observation of a single chimpanzee in a single situation** — complete disregard for diversity of brain and behavior within and between species.
- No program of psychosurgery should begin without thorough assessment on large samples from various nonhuman mammalian species.
- Early reports of success based on impressions of the **least objective observers** (the prescribing physicians and colleagues).
- "Improvement" defined as greater **manageability**; no careful evaluation of psychological adjustment or adverse effects.

**Eventual findings:**
- Little therapeutic benefit.
- Wide range of undesirable side effects:
  - Amorality
  - Lack of foresight
  - Emotional unresponsiveness
  - Epilepsy
  - Urinary incontinence
- Over **40,000 patients** lobotomized in the US alone before abandonment.
- Still performed in some countries.

**Howard Dully case (Dully & Fleming, 2007):**
- Lobotomized at age **12** by Walter Freeman; arranged by his stepmother; agreed to by his father; performed in 10 minutes.
- Spent much of his life in asylums, jails, and halfway houses.
- Investigation found he was a **normal child** whose stepmother was obsessed by hatred for him.

**Lesson:** Sound scientific methods are not unnecessary obstacles; the unforeseen consequences of lobotomy caution against abandoning science for expediency. Only by observing the rules of science can scientists protect the public from bogus claims.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Biopsychology's founding text**: *The Organization of Behavior* (1949) by **D.O. Hebb** — first comprehensive theory of brain-behavior relationships; established biopsychology as a discipline; used an eclectic approach.

2. **Definition distinction**: Biopsychology = "scientific study of the **biology of behavior**." Synonyms: psychobiology, behavioral biology, behavioral neuroscience. Pinel prefers "biopsychology" because psychology commands center stage.

3. **Neuroplasticity**: "arguably the **single most influential discovery in modern neuroscience**" — shifts view from static wired network to continuously changing, experience-responsive organ.

4. **Coolidge effect** is named after **President Calvin Coolidge** (not a researcher). Lester & Gorzalka (1988) demonstrated it in **female hamsters**; DV = **lordosis duration**; controlled for male fatigue via simultaneous mating design.

5. **Korsakoff's syndrome** is caused primarily by **thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency**, NOT directly by alcohol toxicity. Converging evidence from: case studies (non-alcoholic malnourished patients), quasiexperiments (alcoholics vs. nondrinkers), and controlled animal experiments (thiamine-deficient rats).

6. **Six divisions**: Physiological psychology, Psychopharmacology, Neuropsychology, Psychophysiology, Cognitive neuroscience, Comparative psychology. Neuropsychology is the **most applied**; Cognitive neuroscience is the **youngest**.

7. **Morgan's Canon**: when multiple interpretations exist for a behavioral observation, give precedence to the **simplest one**. Key principle in critiquing Delgado's bull demonstration.

8. **Prefrontal lobotomy** (Moniz; 1949 Nobel Prize): based on **one chimpanzee (Becky)** in a single situation. Over **40,000 patients** lobotomized in the US. Side effects include amorality, lack of foresight, emotional unresponsiveness, epilepsy, urinary incontinence. Instrument used by Lima: **leucotome**.

9. **Transorbital lobotomy**: ice-pick through the **orbit (eye socket)**; popularized by **Walter Freeman** in the US in the late 1940s; performed in the surgeon's office.

10. **Psychophysiology** uses **noninvasive** recording from body surface; primary brain measure = **EEG**; classic finding = schizophrenics show **impaired smooth-pursuit eye tracking** of a pendulum (Iacono & Koenig, 1983; Chen et al., 2008).

11. **Comparative psychology** includes three subfields: **ethological research** (behavior in natural environment), **evolutionary psychology** (behavior via evolutionary origins), and **behavioral genetics** (genetic influences on behavior).

12. **Hammond, Merton, & Sutton (1956)** — used **curare** to paralyze eye muscles; subject Merton saw stationary world moving when he tried to move his paralyzed eyes. Demonstrates the brain uses **efference copy (corollary discharge)** of active motor commands, not actual eye movement feedback, to compute perceived motion.

13. **Quasiexperiments vs. true experiments**: quasiexperiments cannot establish causation because subjects self-select into conditions — potential confounds remain (e.g., Acker et al. 1984 showed alcoholics have more brain damage but could not prove alcohol caused it).

14. **Human brain statistics**: ~1.3 kg; ~100 billion neurons (Azevedo et al., 2009); ~100 trillion connections.

15. **Neuropsychology and the cerebral cortex**: cerebral cortex is most commonly damaged by accident or surgery — hence the focus. About **1/3 of left-handers** have language represented in the **right hemisphere** (Case of Mr. R.).

- The **six divisions table** is heavily tested — especially which uses invasive vs. noninvasive methods, which uses humans vs. animals, and which is most applied.
