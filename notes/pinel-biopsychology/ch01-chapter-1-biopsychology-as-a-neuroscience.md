# Chapter 1: Biopsychology as a Neuroscience

**Overview:** Introduces biopsychology as a discipline within neuroscience: its scope, relation to other neuroscientific fields, research approaches (human/nonhuman, experimental/nonexperimental, pure/applied), six divisions, and the use of converging operations and scientific inference. Two case studies (Delgado's bull; Moniz's lobotomy) anchor the emphasis on evaluating claims critically.

---

## 1. What Is Biopsychology?

- **Biopsychology** = the scientific study of the **biology of behavior** (Dewsbury, 1991).
- Synonyms: **psychobiology**, **behavioral biology**, **behavioral neuroscience**; Pinel prefers "biopsychology" (signals a *biological approach to psychology*).
- **Psychology**: scientific study of behavior — all overt activities plus the internal processes presumed to underlie them (learning, memory, motivation, perception, emotion).
- **Neuroscience**: scientific study of the nervous system; biopsychology is one discipline within it.
- **Human brain**: ~**1.3 kg**, ~**100 billion neurons** (Azevedo et al., 2009), ~**100 trillion connections**.
- **Neurons**: cells that receive and transmit electrochemical signals.

### Historical Origin

- Biopsychology became a major discipline only in the **20th century**.
- Founding text: **"The Organization of Behavior" (1949)** by **D.O. Hebb**.
  - First **comprehensive theory** of how complex psychological phenomena (perceptions, emotions, thoughts, memories) arise from brain activity.
  - Discredited the view that psychological functioning is too complex to root in brain physiology/chemistry.
  - Used an **eclectic approach** (human + animal experiments, clinical case studies, logical argument) — now a **hallmark of biopsychology**.

### Case of Jimmie G. (Oliver Sacks)

- 49-year-old male; believed he was 19; no memory since his early 20s.
- Could not form **new lasting memories** (forgot things within seconds).
- Condition: **Korsakoff's syndrome**. Source: *The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat* (Sacks, 1986).

## 2. Four Major Themes of This Book

### 2.1 Thinking Creatively about Biopsychology

- **Thinking creatively**: thinking in productive, unconventional ways.
- Conventional wisdom and preconceptions often **impede progress**; major advances come from overcoming them. The "cornerstone of any science."

### 2.2 The Evolutionary Perspective

- **Evolutionary perspective**: understanding biological phenomena via the environmental pressures that drove evolution of brains and behavior.
- Key component: **comparative approach** — comparing phenomena across species.
- A cornerstone of modern biopsychology. Related: **genetics of behavior** (Ch. 2).

### 2.3 Neuroplasticity

- Pre-1990s view: brain as a static, fixed-wired network.
- **Neuroplasticity**: the brain is a **plastic (changeable) organ** that continuously grows and changes in response to genes and experience.
- "Arguably the **single most influential discovery in modern neuroscience**."

### 2.4 Clinical Implications

- **Clinical**: pertaining to illness or treatment. Two aspects:
  1. Much about the **normal brain** is learned from the diseased/damaged brain.
  2. Much research has relevance for **treating brain disorders**.

- Biopsychologists' unique contribution: **knowledge of behavior and behavioral research methods**.

- Biopsychology is an **integrative discipline**, drawing on other neuroscientific fields.

## 4. Types of Research in the Biopsychological Approach

Three dimensions: (1) **human vs. nonhuman subjects**, (2) **experiments vs. nonexperiments**, (3) **pure vs. applied research**.

### 4.1 Human and Nonhuman Subjects

**Advantages of human subjects:** follow instructions; report subjective experience; often cheaper; have human brains.

**Why use nonhumans (evolutionary continuity):** human brains differ from other mammals mainly in **size** and **cortical development** — differences **more quantitative than qualitative** (Nakahara et al., 2002; Passingham, 2009; Platt & Spelke, 2009).

**Advantages of nonhuman subjects:**
1. Simpler brains/behavior — reveal **fundamental brain-behavior interactions**.
2. **Comparative approach** yields insights (e.g., species with vs. without cortex).
3. Permits research **ethically impossible in humans**.

**Ethics:** all research is regulated by **independent committees** and **strict guidelines**. Ulrich (1991): research-model animals "must be respected as we would respect our own sensibilities." Most common subject: **rats** (also mice, cats, dogs, nonhuman primates).

### 4.2 Experiments and Nonexperiments

#### The Experiment

- The method used to study **causation**.
- **Between-subjects design**: different groups per condition. **Within-subjects design**: same group across conditions.
- **Independent variable (IV)**: the one relevant difference between conditions; manipulated by experimenter.
- **Dependent variable (DV)**: measured to assess the IV's effect.
- **Confounded variable**: an unintended variable differing between conditions that could explain the DV; must be eliminated.

> **KEY STUDY: Lester & Gorzalka (1988) — Coolidge Effect in Female Hamsters**
>
> - **Coolidge effect**: a copulating male unable to continue with one partner can resume with a new one. Named after **President Calvin Coolidge** (rooster anecdote).
> - **Problem**: not shown in females; Lester & Gorzalka argued this reflected a **confound** — males fatigue faster, so apparent female receptivity to a new male might just reflect the new male's vigor.
> - **Design**: female copulated with male 1 while male 2 simultaneously copulated with another female (equalizing fatigue); both males rested; female then tested with the familiar or unfamiliar male.
> - **DV**: duration of **lordosis** (arched-back, rump-up, tail-diverted receptivity posture).
> - **Result**: greater lordosis to the **unfamiliar male** despite equal male fatigue.
> - **Conclusion**: females DO show the Coolidge effect; importance of controlling confounds.

#### Quasiexperimental Studies

- **Quasiexperimental studies**: study real-world groups exposed to conditions of interest; resemble experiments but **lack random assignment** and control of confounds, so causation cannot be inferred.
- Example: **Acker et al. (1984)** — 100 detoxified male alcoholics vs. 50 nondrinkers; alcoholics performed worse on perceptual/motor/cognitive tests with brain damage on scans, but groups also differed in education, head injury, drug use, diet.

#### Case Studies

- **Case studies**: focus on a single case/subject. Give in-depth detail and good testable hypotheses; key limitation = **generalizability**.

### 4.3 Pure and Applied Research

- **Pure research**: motivated by curiosity, done to acquire knowledge.
- **Applied research**: intended for direct benefit to humankind.
- Pure research often proves most practically beneficial long-term, but is more **vulnerable to political regulation**.

## 5. Divisions of Biopsychology

Six divisions; significant overlap exists.

### 5.1 Physiological Psychology

- **Focus**: neural mechanisms of behavior via **direct brain manipulation** (surgical/electrical) in controlled experiments.
- Subjects: almost always **lab animals**; tradition of **pure research**.
- Memory example: surgical removal of **hippocampus** in rats, then memory testing.

### 5.2 Psychopharmacology

- **Focus**: manipulating neural activity and behavior with **drugs**.
- Substantial **applied** component (therapeutic drugs; reducing abuse). Subjects: animals and humans.
- Memory example: drugs raising **acetylcholine** in Alzheimer's patients.

### 5.3 Neuropsychology

- **Focus**: **psychological effects of brain damage in human patients**.
- Methods: mainly **case studies** and **quasiexperiments**. The **most applied** division.
- Focus on **cerebral cortex** (most commonly damaged). Tests aid diagnosis, treatment, counseling.

> **Case of Mr. R. (Kolb & Whishaw, 1990):** 21-year-old left-handed male; car-accident head injury; declined from honor student. Language in **right hemisphere** (~1/3 of left-handers). Low-average verbal memory/reading despite superior IQ indicated right temporal lobe damage; switched to architecture.

### 5.4 Psychophysiology

- **Focus**: relation between **physiological activity** and **psychological processes** in humans.
- Recording: **noninvasive** (body surface); primary brain measure = **scalp EEG**.
- Other measures: muscle tension, eye movement, ANS indicators (heart rate, blood pressure, pupil dilation, skin conductance).
- **Autonomic nervous system (ANS)**: division regulating the body's inner environment.
- Clinical example: schizophrenics show impaired **smooth pursuit eye tracking** (Chen et al., 2008; Iacono & Koenig, 1983).

### 5.5 Cognitive Neuroscience

- **Focus**: **neural bases of cognition** (thought, memory, attention, complex perception).
- **Youngest** and most active division; subjects mostly **humans**; **noninvasive** methods.
- Major method: **functional brain imaging**. Typically **interdisciplinary**.

### 5.6 Comparative Psychology

- **Focus**: **biology of behavior** broadly — evolution, genetics, **adaptiveness of behavior**; uses the **comparative approach**.
- Subfields:
  - **Ethological research**: behavior in its **natural environment**.
  - **Evolutionary psychology**: behavior via its likely evolutionary origins (Caporael, 2001; Duchaine, Cosmides, & Tooby, 2001).
  - **Behavioral genetics**: genetic influences on behavior (Plomin et al., 2002).
- Memory example: **seed-caching birds** have **larger hippocampi** — supports hippocampal role in spatial memory.

### Summary Table: Six Divisions

| Division | Focus | Subjects | Methods | Pure/Applied |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Physiological Psychology | Neural mechanisms of behavior | Nonhuman animals | Direct brain manipulation | Mostly pure |
| Psychopharmacology | Drug effects on brain and behavior | Animals + humans | Drug administration | Both |
| Neuropsychology | Effects of brain damage in humans | Human patients | Case studies, quasiexperiments | Mostly applied |
| Psychophysiology | Physiology-psychology relations in humans | Humans | Noninvasive physiological recording | Both |
| Cognitive Neuroscience | Neural bases of human cognition | Humans | Functional brain imaging | Both |
| Comparative Psychology | Evolution, genetics, adaptiveness of behavior | Various species | Comparative method, ethology | Both |

## 6. Converging Operations

- **Converging operations**: combined use of different research approaches on a single problem so each approach's strengths offset others' weaknesses (Thompson, 2005). Major issues are rarely resolved by a single experiment.

### Neuropsychology vs. Physiological Psychology

| Approach | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| Neuropsychology | Directly involves human patients | Cannot run experiments (ethical) |
| Physiological Psychology | Uses experimental method + neuroscience tech | Relevance of animal research to humans always questionable |

### Case Study: Korsakoff's Syndrome

- **Korsakoff's syndrome**: first described by **S.S. Korsakoff** (Russian physician, late 19th c.). **Primary symptom**: severe memory loss (otherwise often cognitively capable).
- Initially blamed on **direct alcohol toxicity** (later shown wrong).

**Converging evidence for the true cause:**
1. **Case studies**: Korsakoff's found in malnourished non-alcoholics — alcohol alone insufficient.
2. **Quasiexperiments**: confirmed memory deficits + brain damage in alcoholics vs. nondrinkers.
3. **Animal experiments**: **thiamine (B1)-deficient rats** showed similar deficits/damage (Mumby, Cameli, & Glenn, 1999).

- **True cause**: **thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency**. Alcoholics develop it because alcohol gives calories without vitamins and **interferes with thiamine metabolism**; alcohol may also be directly toxic to thiamine-deficient brains (Zimitat et al., 1990).
- **Treatment**: stop drinking + **massive thiamine doses**; limits further damage but existing damage is largely **permanent**.

## 7. Scientific Inference

- **Scientific inference**: measuring observable events and logically inferring the nature of unobservable processes (e.g., neural mechanisms). Like a detective recreating an unwitnessed crime.

### Eye-Movement Demonstration

| Condition | Eye | Object | Retinal Image | Perception |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Stationary | Stationary | Stationary | No movement |
| 2 | **Actively** rotates up | Stationary | Moves up | **No movement** (active movement subtracted) |
| 3 | **Passively** rotated (finger) | Stationary | Moves up | **Object seen to move down** (passive NOT subtracted) |
| 4 | Stationary | Moves down | Moves up | Object seen to move down |

**Conclusion (inferred):** the brain subtracts only **actively commanded** eye movements from retinal image motion; it does NOT subtract passive movements.

> **Hammond, Merton, & Sutton (1956):** Merton injected **curare** (paralytic; used on blow darts) into his own eye muscles. When he tried to move his eyes, the stationary world appeared to **move in the direction of the attempted movement**. Confirms the brain uses **corollary discharge / efference copy** (active motor commands), not actual eye-position feedback, to compute perceived motion.

## 8. Critical Thinking about Biopsychological Claims

- **Critical thinking**: identifying weaknesses in ideas and their supporting evidence.
- First step in evaluating a claim: check if it was **published in a reputable peer-reviewed journal** (Rensberger, 2000). Peer review = ~3-4 expert reviewers, but is NOT a guarantee against flaws.

### Case 1: José and the Bull (Delgado)

- Delgado entered a bullring and sent a weak current via an electrode in the bull's **caudate nucleus**; the bull stopped charging. He claimed a caudate **"taming center"** that could treat human psychopaths — disseminated via **news media, not peer review**.
- **Critical analysis:** simpler explanations exist (confusion, dizziness, nausea, pain, or activation of a **motor pathway** forcing the bull to turn — Valenstein, 1973).
- **Morgan's Canon**: when multiple interpretations exist, prefer the **simplest one**. Observations with multiple plausible explanations support none strongly.

### Case 2: Becky, Moniz, and Prefrontal Lobotomy

- 1935: Moniz heard that **Becky** (a chimpanzee) stopped getting upset over task errors after a **bilateral prefrontal lobe lesion**. He had neurosurgeon **Almeida Lima** operate on psychiatric patients with a **leucotome** (cutting six cores of prefrontal tissue).
- **Prefrontal lobotomy**: severing connections between the **prefrontal lobes** (large areas at the front of the brain) and the rest of the brain to treat mental illness.
- Moniz received the **Nobel Prize 1949**.
- **Transorbital lobotomy**: popularized in the US by **Walter Freeman** (late 1940s); ice-pick device through the **orbit (eye socket)**, waved to sever connections, done in the office. **Psychosurgery** = any brain surgery to treat a psychological problem.

**Critical analysis:**
- Based on **one chimpanzee in one situation**, ignoring brain/behavior diversity.
- Early "success" came from the **least objective observers** (prescribing physicians); "improvement" meant greater **manageability**, not real adjustment.

**Eventual findings:** little benefit; side effects = **amorality, lack of foresight, emotional unresponsiveness, epilepsy, urinary incontinence**. Over **40,000 patients** lobotomized in the US before abandonment.

**Howard Dully case (Dully & Fleming, 2007):** a **normal child** lobotomized at age **12** by Freeman (arranged by his stepmother); spent much of his life in asylums, jails, and halfway houses.

**Lesson:** scientific method protects the public from bogus claims; lobotomy's harms warn against abandoning science for expediency.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Founding text**: *The Organization of Behavior* (1949) by **D.O. Hebb** — first comprehensive brain-behavior theory; eclectic approach.

2. **Definition**: Biopsychology = "scientific study of the **biology of behavior**." Synonyms: psychobiology, behavioral biology, behavioral neuroscience.

3. **Neuroplasticity**: "single most influential discovery in modern neuroscience" — from static network to continuously changing organ.

4. **Coolidge effect** named after **President Calvin Coolidge** (not a researcher). Lester & Gorzalka (1988): female hamsters; DV = **lordosis duration**; controlled male fatigue via simultaneous mating.

5. **Korsakoff's syndrome** caused primarily by **thiamine (B1) deficiency**, NOT direct alcohol toxicity. Converging evidence: case studies, quasiexperiments, animal experiments (thiamine-deficient rats).

6. **Six divisions**: Physiological psychology, Psychopharmacology, Neuropsychology, Psychophysiology, Cognitive neuroscience, Comparative psychology. Neuropsychology = **most applied**; Cognitive neuroscience = **youngest**.

7. **Morgan's Canon**: prefer the **simplest** interpretation. Key to critiquing Delgado's bull.

8. **Prefrontal lobotomy** (Moniz; 1949 Nobel): based on **one chimpanzee (Becky)**. Over **40,000 patients** lobotomized in the US. Side effects: amorality, lack of foresight, emotional unresponsiveness, epilepsy, urinary incontinence. Lima's instrument: **leucotome**.

9. **Transorbital lobotomy**: ice-pick through the **orbit (eye socket)**; popularized by **Walter Freeman** (late 1940s); done in the office.

10. **Psychophysiology**: **noninvasive** recording; primary brain measure = **EEG**; classic finding = schizophrenics show impaired **smooth-pursuit eye tracking** (Iacono & Koenig, 1983).

11. **Comparative psychology** subfields: **ethological research**, **evolutionary psychology**, **behavioral genetics**.

12. **Hammond, Merton, & Sutton (1956)**: **curare** to paralyze eye muscles; demonstrates **efference copy (corollary discharge)**, not eye-movement feedback, for perceived motion.

13. **Quasiexperiments vs. experiments**: quasiexperiments cannot establish causation (no random assignment; e.g., Acker et al., 1984).

14. **Human brain stats**: ~1.3 kg; ~100 billion neurons; ~100 trillion connections.

15. **Neuropsychology & cortex**: cortex most commonly damaged. ~**1/3 of left-handers** have language in the **right hemisphere** (Mr. R.).
