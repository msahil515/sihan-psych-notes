# Chapter 8: The Sensorimotor System

**Overview:** Neural control of voluntary movement from association cortex down through secondary/primary motor cortex, cerebellum, basal ganglia, descending pathways, and spinal circuits to muscles. Three principles: hierarchical organization; motor output guided by sensory feedback; learning changes the nature and locus of control.

---

## 8.1 Three Principles of Sensorimotor Function

### Principle 1: Hierarchically Organized
- A **parallel, hierarchically organized system**; commands cascade *downward* from highest (**association cortex** — sets general goals) to lowest (**muscles** — execute).
- **Parallel hierarchical**: signals flow over multiple paths simultaneously (e.g., association cortex can directly inhibit an eye-blink reflex).
- **Functional segregation**: each level has different units performing different functions.
- Advantage: frees higher levels for complex functions. Note: in sensory systems info flows *up*; in the sensorimotor system, *down*.

### Principle 2: Motor Output Guided by Sensory Input
- The system monitors its own activity via **sensory feedback** (eyes, vestibular system, skin/muscle/joint receptors) to fine-tune movement.
- Exception: **Ballistic movements** — brief, all-or-none, high-speed (e.g., swatting a fly) — are not normally influenced by feedback.
- Many feedback adjustments are made **unconsciously** by lower levels.

> **KEY CASE: G.O. (Rothwell et al., 1982):** infection **selectively destroyed the somatosensory nerves of his arms**. He struggled with intricate tasks even under visual guidance, couldn't compensate for unexpected disturbances, and (greatest problem) **couldn't maintain constant muscle contraction**. Shows somatosensory feedback is critical for normal motor output.

### Principle 3: Learning Changes Nature and Locus of Control
- Early learning: each response under **conscious control**. After practice: responses become **integrated sequences (motor programs)** run smoothly with feedback but **without conscious regulation**, transferred to **lower levels** (e.g., typing, swimming, piano) (Ashe et al., 2006).

## 8.2 Sensorimotor Association Cortex

- **Association cortex** sits at the TOP. Two major areas: **posterior parietal** and **dorsolateral prefrontal** association cortex (each several functionally distinct areas).

### Posterior Parietal Association Cortex
- **Location:** parietal neocortex *posterior* to primary somatosensory cortex.
- **Functions:** integrate (1) original **body-part positions** and (2) **external object positions**; (3) provide **spatial information**; (4) direct **attention** (Britten, 2008).
- **Inputs:** visual, auditory, somatosensory (hence "association"; Andersen & Buneo, 2003).
- **Outputs:** dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, secondary motor cortex, **frontal eye field** (controls eye movements).
- **Organization:** a **mosaic of small areas**, each guiding specific eye/head/arm/hand movements (Culham & Valyear, 2006).

> **KEY STUDY: Desmurget et al. (2009):** stimulating inferior posterior parietal cortex in conscious patients produced an *intention* to act (low current) or a *feeling* of having acted (high current) — but no actual movement. Implicates this region in the **sense of intention/agency**.

**Damage consequences:** deficits in spatial perception/memory, reaching/grasping, eye movement, and attention. Two striking forms:

#### Apraxia
- **Apraxia**: disorder of voluntary movement not due to paralysis, weakness, or comprehension/motivation deficits (Heilman, Watson, & Rothi, 1997). Difficulty making **requested** movements (esp. out of context) though natural performance is preserved (e.g., a carpenter hammers fine at work but can't demonstrate it). **Bilateral** symptoms; often from **left posterior parietal** damage (Jax, Buxbaum, & Moll, 2006).

#### Contralateral Neglect
- **Contralateral neglect**: failure to respond to stimuli **contralateral** to a lesion, without simple sensory/motor deficits (Driver et al., 2004). Patients act as if the **left side of the world doesn't exist** and often lack insight (Berti et al., 2005); usually from large **right posterior parietal** lesions (Mort et al., 2003).

> **KEY CASE: Mrs. S. (Oliver Sacks):** right-hemisphere stroke → left neglect. She applied makeup and ate only on the right; she turned a full circle rightward to "find" the rest of her food. (*The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat*.)

**Types:**
1. **Egocentric left neglect**: failure to respond to the left of one's **own body** (gravitational coordinates; head tilt doesn't tilt the neglected field).
2. **Object-based neglect**: failure to respond to the **left sides of objects** regardless of position (Kleinman et al., 2007). Both field types exist in primate parietal cortex (Olson, 2003).

**Unconscious perception:** (1) repeated neglected-spot objects drew future looks despite no awareness (Geng & Behrmann, 2002); (2) complete drawings shown in the neglected left field helped identify fragmented right-field drawings (Vuilleumier et al., 2002) — **unconscious** processing.

### Dorsolateral Prefrontal Association Cortex
- **Inputs**: from posterior parietal cortex. **Outputs**: secondary/primary motor cortex, frontal eye field; reciprocal projections back to parietal cortex.
- **Function**: **evaluate external stimuli** and **initiate voluntary reactions** (Matsumoto & Tanaka, 2004). Monkey neurons (Rao, Rainer, & Miller, 1997) encode object characteristics, locations, both, and the response itself (firing before and through it).
- Among all cortical motor areas, these neurons fire **FIRST** in anticipation. **Decisions to initiate movement** may be made here (Tanji & Hoshi, 2001), depending on interaction with posterior parietal cortex.

## 8.3 Secondary Motor Cortex

- Receives input from association cortex; outputs to **primary motor cortex**; located in the frontal lobe just *anterior* to primary motor cortex.
- Originally two areas: **supplementary motor area (SMA)** (over the top onto the medial surface) and **premotor cortex** (strip from SMA to the lateral fissure).
- **Current understanding — ≥8 areas/hemisphere:** 3 supplementary (SMA, preSMA, supplementary eye field), 2 premotor (dorsal, ventral), 3 cingulate motor areas.
- **Function:** stimulation elicits **complex, often bilateral movements**; neurons activate just before movement; **program specific movement patterns** from dorsolateral prefrontal instructions (Hoshi & Tanji, 2007); active when **imagining/planning** movements (Kosslyn et al., 2001).
- **Sensory inputs:** SMA mainly from **somatosensory** system; premotor cortex mainly from **visual** system.

### Premotor Cortex
- **Location:** the **lateral** strip of secondary motor cortex, running from the SMA down to the **lateral fissure**; sits just *anterior* to the primary motor cortex. Two divisions per hemisphere: **dorsal premotor (PMd)** and **ventral premotor (PMv)**.
- **Main input:** the **visual** system (contrast: SMA is fed mainly by the **somatosensory** system). This is the key exam distinction — **premotor = visually guided; SMA = somatosensory/internally guided**.
- **Function:** preparing and selecting movements that are **guided by external sensory cues** (e.g., reaching for a seen object); converts the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex's "what to do" into the **specific motor program**; fires *just before* movement onset and is active when merely **planning or imagining** a movement (Kosslyn et al., 2001).
- **Mirror neurons live here:** Rizzolatti found them in macaque **ventral premotor cortex** (early 1990s) — see below.
- **vs. SMA at a glance:** premotor = lateral surface, visual input, externally cued movement. SMA = medial surface (over the top), somatosensory input, internally generated / sequenced movement.

### Supplementary Motor Area (SMA)
- **Location:** **medial** surface of the frontal lobe (folds "over the top" of the hemisphere), anterior to primary motor cortex. The supplementary family includes **SMA**, **preSMA**, and the **supplementary eye field**.
- **Main input:** the **somatosensory** system.
- **Function:** programming **internally generated** and **well-learned, sequenced** movements; like premotor, active during the planning/imagining of movement.

### Mirror Neurons
- **Mirror neurons**: fire both when an individual **performs** a goal-directed hand movement AND when **observing** the same movement (Fadiga, Craighero, & Olivier, 2005).
- Discovered **early 1990s** by **Giacomo Rizzolatti** in macaque **ventral premotor cortex** (some neurons fired when the experimenter, not the monkey, picked up an object).
- **Properties:** respond to the **understanding of an action**, not surface features (e.g., respond to the sound of cracking a peanut; ~half fire even when the action is hidden). Also in the **inferior posterior parietal lobe**, where some respond to an action's **purpose** (grasping to eat, not to place in a bowl).
- **Significance:** possible mechanism for **social cognition**, understanding, cooperation, imitation (Iacoboni, 2005).
- **Humans:** not yet *directly* confirmed (Turella et al., 2007); fMRI shows motor cortex active when performing/watching/imagining an action (Rizzolatti & Fabbri-Destro, 2008); possible role in disorders (Ramachandran & Oberman, 2006).

## 8.4 Primary Motor Cortex

- **Location:** **precentral gyrus**. Major point of **convergence** of cortical sensorimotor signals and major **departure** point for cortical motor output.

### Conventional View

> **KEY STUDY: Penfield & Boldrey (1937) / Penfield & Rasmussen (1950) — Motor Homunculus:** brief low-intensity stimulation of primary motor cortex in conscious patients moved a particular **contralateral** muscle (a simple movement). Organized **somatotopically** = the **motor homunculus**; most cortex for **hands and mouth**.

- Each site receives feedback from the **muscles/joints** it influences. In monkeys, at least two hand areas exist; one gets **skin receptor** input (facilitating **stereognosis**).
- **Stereognosis**: identifying objects by touch (interplay of motor responses and somatosensory stimulation; Johansson & Flanagan, 2009).
- **Directional encoding view:** each neuron fires maximally for its **preferred direction** of movement.

### Current View

> **KEY RESEARCH: Graziano (2006) — Long-Burst Stimulation:** longer current bursts (~0.5 s) elicited **complex natural sequences** (e.g., a full feeding response: reach, grasp, hand to mouth, open mouth). Revealed only **crude** somatotopy; sites for different body parts **overlap greatly** → small hand-area lesions never selectively disrupt one finger (Scheiber, 1999).

- **Endpoint encoding:** in freely moving monkeys, many neurons' firing related to a movement's **endpoint**, not its direction; target-location neurons activate regardless of movement direction. Stimulating a site always produced the **same final position** regardless of starting position (e.g., elbow to 90°).
- **Implications:** (1) signals from each site **diverge greatly**; (2) the system is **inherently plastic** — a site can reach a target from any start; (3) the route is determined by **somatosensory feedback** (Davidson et al., 2007).

> **KEY CASE: Belle (Nicolelis & Chapin, 2002):** owl monkey moved a joystick toward lights for juice; **100 microelectrodes** in her primary motor cortex controlled a robotic arm at **MIT** in the same direction as her arm — foundational for **brain-machine interfaces (BMIs)**/neuroprosthetics (Lebedev & Nicolelis, 2006).

### Effects of Lesions
- Extensive damage has **less effect than expected** (parallel pathways from secondary areas bypass it). Large lesions may disrupt **independent** body-part movement, cause **astereognosia**, and reduce speed/accuracy/force — but do **NOT** eliminate voluntary movement.

## 8.5 Cerebellum and Basal Ganglia

- Neither is part of the descending pathway; both **interact with multiple levels** to coordinate/modulate (Glickstein, 2000).

### Cerebellum
- **10% of brain mass** but **more than half of all brain neurons** (Azevedo et al., 2009); systematic lobes/columns/layers.
- **Inputs:** (1) primary/secondary motor cortex; (2) descending motor signals from brain stem; (3) sensorimotor feedback (somatosensory, vestibular).
- **Function:** **compares** inputs and **corrects deviating movements** (Bastian, 2006); major role in **motor learning**, especially **timing** of sequences (D'Angelo & De Zeeuw, 2008).
- **Diffuse damage:** loss of precise **direction/force/velocity/amplitude** control; can't adapt to changing conditions; **tremor**; disturbed **balance, gait, speech, eye movement**; difficulty learning new sequences.
- **Expanded view:** active in **non-motor cognitive tasks** (Strick, Dum, & Fiez, 2009); connected to prefrontal cortex; may fine-tune/learn cognitive responses like motor ones (Doya, 2000).

### Basal Ganglia
- A **heterogeneous collection of interconnected nuclei**; fewer neurons than cerebellum.
- **Function:** **modulatory**; few descending fibers; part of **neural loops** returning to cortex via the **thalamus** (McHaffie et al., 2005); many loops involve **motor** cortex.
- **Current view:** also serve **cognitive functions** (Graybiel, 2005). Participate in gradual, trial-by-trial **response learning** (reward/punishment; Joshua, Adler, & Bergman, 2009).

## 8.6 Descending Motor Pathways

Four pathways from primary motor cortex to spinal motor neurons: **two dorsolateral**, **two ventromedial**.

### Dorsolateral Corticospinal Tract (Direct)
- Descends through the **medullary pyramids**, **decussates**, continues contralaterally. Most notable neurons: **Betz cells** (huge pyramidal neurons). Synapse on interneurons → motor neurons of **distal muscles** (wrist, hands, fingers, toes). In digit-dexterous mammals, some synapse **directly** on digit motor neurons (Porter & Lemon, 1993).

### Dorsolateral Corticorubrospinal Tract (Indirect)
- Synapses in the **red nucleus** (midbrain); axons **decussate**, descend, some to **cranial nerve motor neurons** (face), rest to **distal arm/leg muscles** (*rubro* = red nucleus).

### Ventromedial Corticospinal Tract (Direct)
- Descends **ipsilaterally**; each axon **branches diffusely** to interneurons on **both sides** across several spinal segments.

### Ventromedial Cortico-brainstem-spinal Tract (Indirect)
- Feeds a **brain stem network** descending **bilaterally**; each side carries signals from **both hemispheres**; synapses across segments to **proximal muscles** of trunk/limbs.
- **Four brain stem structures:** (1) **Tectum** (auditory/visual spatial location); (2) **Vestibular nucleus** (balance from semicircular canals); (3) **Reticular formation** (motor programs for walking/swimming/jumping); (4) **Motor nuclei of cranial nerves** (face).

| Feature | Dorsolateral | Ventromedial |
|---|---|---|
| **Diffuseness** | Contralateral half of one segment; sometimes direct on motor neuron | Diffuse; both sides, several segments |
| **Muscle targets** | Distal (fingers) | Proximal (trunk/limbs) |
| **Movements** | Discrete limb, independent digits | Posture, whole-body |
| **Crossing** | Decussate; contralateral | Bilateral; signals from both hemispheres |

> **KEY EXPERIMENTS: Lawrence & Kuypers (1968) — Monkey Transections**
>
> **Exp 1 (1968a):** cut bilateral **dorsolateral corticospinal tracts** at the pyramids. Still stood/walked/climbed; reaching recovered. **Permanent deficits:** could never (1) move fingers **independently** (used all as a unit) or (2) **release grasp voluntarily** ("like a pig rooting for truffles") — though still released cage bars when climbing (same response, different context/control).
>
> **Exp 2 (1968b):**
> - **+Dorsolateral corticorubrospinal cut:** still stood/walked/climbed; limbs hung limp; reaching thrown from the shoulder ("rubber-handled rake").
> - **+Both ventromedial cut:** severe **postural** abnormalities; trouble walking/sitting; falls from slight disturbance; lost **shoulder** control.
>
> **Conclusions:** ventromedial tracts = **posture/whole-body**; dorsolateral tracts = **limb movements**; only the **corticospinal** dorsolateral tract mediates **independent digit movement**.

## 8.7 Sensorimotor Spinal Circuits

- Spinal motor circuits show **complexity and independent functioning** without brain input (Grillner & Jessell, 2009).

### Muscles
- **Motor unit**: smallest unit of motor activity — one motor neuron + all fibers it innervates (all contract together). Fewest fibers (fingers, face) = **finest control**.
- Muscle fibers bound in a membrane, attached to bone by a **tendon**. **Neuromuscular junction**: motor neurons release **acetylcholine (ACh)** onto the **motor end-plate** → contraction. Muscles generate force in **one direction** (contraction only). **Motor pool**: all motor neurons innervating one muscle.

| Feature | Fast Fibers | Slow Fibers |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Quick | Slower |
| Force | Great | Weaker |
| Fatigue | Quickly | Sustained |
| Vascularization | Poor | Rich |
| Color | Pale | Redder |
| Function | Quick movements (jumping) | Gradual (walking) |

- Each muscle has **both** fiber types.
- **Flexors** bend a joint; **extensors** straighten it (**biceps** flexor, **triceps** extensor of elbow). **Synergistic** = same movement; **antagonistic** = opposition.
- **Isometric contraction**: tension without shortening (no movement). **Dynamic contraction**: shortens to move bones.
- Tension increased by: (1) more firing neurons in the pool; (2) higher firing rates; (3) both (most common).

### Receptor Organs of Tendons and Muscles
- **Golgi tendon organs**: in **tendons**; respond to **muscle tension** (not length); when tension is dangerously high, excite **inhibitory interneurons** → muscle relaxes (protective).
- **Muscle spindles**: in **muscle tissue**; respond to **length** (not tension); contain an **intrafusal muscle** (with its **intrafusal motor neuron**); regular fibers = **extrafusal muscles**. The intrafusal motor neuron shortens the intrafusal muscle when the extrafusal muscle shortens, keeping the spindle taut and responsive.

### Stretch Reflex
- **Patellar (knee-jerk) reflex**: a **stretch reflex** — reflex from a sudden external stretch.
- **Mechanism (monosynaptic):** tendon tap stretches the muscle → activates **muscle-spindle stretch receptors** → **spindle afferent neurons** (dorsal root) → **motor neurons** in the **ventral horn** → contraction of the same muscle (leg extension).
- **Monosynaptic** (afferent directly on motor neuron). Function: keep external forces from changing body position; lower spinal circuits handle it without higher levels.

### Withdrawal Reflex
- Reflex withdrawal from a painful stimulus; **polysynaptic** (not monosynaptic). First flexor responses ~**1.6 ms** after the stimulus — time to cross **two synapses** → at least **one interneuron**; additional multisynaptic pathways (some cortical).
- **Reciprocal innervation**: flexors contract while extensors are inhibited for smooth withdrawal (coordinated agonist/antagonist relationship).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Three principles**: hierarchical; motor output guided by sensory feedback; learning changes nature and locus of control.

2. **Ballistic movements** = the ONLY type NOT guided by sensory feedback (brief, all-or-none, high-speed).

3. **Apraxia vs. contralateral neglect**: both posterior parietal. Apraxia = movements fine naturally but impaired on request (often LEFT). Neglect = ignores contralateral stimuli (often RIGHT, left-side neglect).

4. **Motor homunculus**: Penfield & Boldrey (1937), conscious-patient stimulation; most cortex for **hands and mouth**.

5. **Mirror neurons**: Rizzolatti, **ventral premotor cortex**, early 1990s; fire performing AND observing; mechanism for social cognition.

5a. **Premotor vs. SMA** (high-yield): **premotor** = lateral, **visual** input, externally/visually cued movement, holds mirror neurons. **SMA** = medial ("over the top"), **somatosensory** input, internally generated/sequenced movement. Both are secondary motor cortex and both fire before movement and during planning.

6. **Cerebellum**: **10% of brain mass** but **>half** of all neurons; compares three inputs and corrects deviations; key for motor learning, especially **timing**.

7. **Basal ganglia**: neural loops back to cortex via the **thalamus**; gradual reward-based **response learning** plus cognition.

8. **Dorsolateral** pathways = **distal** muscles (fingers); **ventromedial** = **proximal**/posture; only the **dorsolateral corticospinal tract** mediates **independent digit movement**.

9. **Lawrence & Kuypers (1968)**: cutting dorsolateral corticospinal tract → lost independent fingers and voluntary release; cutting both ventromedial tracts → severe postural instability, lost shoulder control.

10. **Betz cells** = huge pyramidal neurons of the dorsolateral corticospinal tract.

11. **Golgi tendon organs** = muscle **tension** (protective inhibition); **muscle spindles** = muscle **length** (intrafusal system prevents going slack).

12. **Stretch reflex** = **monosynaptic**; **withdrawal reflex** = **polysynaptic** (≥1 interneuron; ~1.6 ms).

13. **Graziano (2006)**: primary motor cortex encodes **endpoints/targets**, not directions; same final position regardless of start — **plasticity**.

14. **Belle (Nicolelis & Chapin, 2002)**: 100 microelectrodes drove a robotic arm at MIT — foundational BMI demonstration.

15. **Dorsolateral prefrontal** neurons fire **FIRST** among cortical motor areas in anticipation — may initiate the decision to move (with posterior parietal interaction).
