# Chapter 8: The Sensorimotor System

**Chapter Overview:** This chapter traces the neural control of voluntary movement from the highest cortical levels (association cortex) downward through secondary and primary motor cortex, cerebellum, basal ganglia, descending pathways, and spinal circuits to the muscles themselves. Three foundational principles structure the entire chapter: the sensorimotor system is hierarchically organized, motor output is guided by sensory feedback, and learning changes both the nature and the locus of sensorimotor control.

---

## 8.1 Three Principles of Sensorimotor Function

### Principle 1: The Sensorimotor System Is Hierarchically Organized

- The sensorimotor system operates as a **parallel, hierarchically organized system** in which commands cascade *downward* from higher to lower levels.
- Highest levels: **association cortex** (issues general goals, not specific movement plans).
- Lowest levels: **muscles** (execute the specific movement).
- Analogy used in the text: Association cortex = company president; muscles = workers. The president issues broad goals; lower personnel handle implementation details.
- Key features of the hierarchy:
  - **Parallel hierarchical system**: signals flow between levels over *multiple paths* simultaneously (not a single chain of command). This enables higher levels to exert control in more than one way (e.g., association cortex can directly inhibit an eye-blink reflex to allow contact lens insertion).
  - **Functional segregation**: each level is composed of different units (neural structures) each performing a different function.
- Main advantage of hierarchical organization: higher levels are freed to perform more complex functions.
- **Sensory vs. Sensorimotor direction of information flow**: In sensory systems, information flows primarily *upward* through the hierarchy; in the sensorimotor system, information flows primarily *downward*.

### Principle 2: Motor Output Is Guided by Sensory Input

- The sensorimotor system continuously monitors the effects of its own activity and uses that information to fine-tune ongoing movements.
- **Sensory feedback** sources: eyes, organs of balance (vestibular system), receptors in skin, muscles, and joints.
- In most instances, sensory feedback plays an important role in directing the *continuation* of the responses that produced it.
- Exception: **Ballistic movements** — brief, all-or-none, high-speed movements (e.g., swatting a fly) that are *not* normally influenced by sensory feedback.
- Many adjustments in motor output in response to sensory feedback are controlled **unconsciously** by lower levels of the hierarchy without involvement of higher levels.

> **KEY CASE: G.O., the Man with Too Little Feedback (Rothwell et al., 1982)**
> - An infection **selectively destroyed the somatosensory nerves of G.O.'s arms**.
> - Deficits:
>   - Difficulty performing intricate responses (buttoning, picking up coins) even under visual guidance.
>   - Could not compensate for unexpected external disturbances (e.g., spilling coffee when bumped).
>   - **Greatest problem**: inability to maintain a constant level of muscle contraction (could not grip consistently).
>   - Had to visually monitor even simple tasks (e.g., glancing at a suitcase to confirm he had not dropped it).
>   - Visual feedback was insufficient for tasks requiring constant force output (grasping a pen, holding a cup) because visual feedback cannot correct micro-level force errors fast enough.
> - **Significance**: Demonstrates that somatosensory feedback from the arms is critically required for normal motor output maintenance.
> - Citation: Rothwell et al., 1982, p. 539.

### Principle 3: Learning Changes the Nature and Locus of Sensorimotor Control

- Early in motor learning: each individual response is performed under **conscious control** (higher cortical involvement).
- After extensive practice: individual responses become organized into continuous **integrated sequences of action** (motor programs) that flow smoothly and are adjusted by sensory feedback *without conscious regulation*.
- Control is transferred to **lower levels** of the nervous system.
- Examples of acquired sensorimotor skills: typing, swimming, knitting, basketball, dancing, piano playing.
- Researchers: Ashe et al. (2006); Kübler, Dixon, & Garavan (2006).

### A General Model of Sensorimotor System Function (Figure 8.1)

- Illustrates the full hierarchy from association cortex down to muscle.
- Key features depicted:
  - Hierarchical structure
  - Functional segregation of levels (e.g., secondary motor cortex)
  - Parallel connections between levels
  - Numerous **feedback pathways** (ascending sensory feedback and descending motor commands)
- The chapter traces voluntary motor signals *downward* from association cortex through the hierarchy to skeletal muscles.

## 8.2 Sensorimotor Association Cortex

- **Association cortex** sits at the TOP of the sensorimotor hierarchy.
- Two major areas of sensorimotor association cortex:
  1. **Posterior parietal association cortex**
  2. **Dorsolateral prefrontal association cortex**
- Each is composed of several functionally distinct areas.
- No general consensus on how best to divide these areas or how comparable subdivisions are across humans, monkeys, and rats (Calton & Taube, 2009; Husain & Nachev, 2006).

---

### Posterior Parietal Association Cortex

**Location:** Portion of parietal neocortex *posterior* to the primary somatosensory cortex.

**Functions:**
1. Integrates information about the **original positions of body parts** to be moved.
2. Integrates information about the **positions of external objects** with which the body will interact.
3. Directs behavior by providing **spatial information**.
4. Directs **attention** (Britten, 2008; Husain & Nachev, 2006).

**Inputs (three sensory systems):**
- Visual system
- Auditory system
- Somatosensory system

Classified as association cortex because it receives input from *more than one sensory system* (Andersen & Buneo, 2003; Macaluso, Driver, & Frith, 2003).

**Outputs (to motor areas of frontal cortex):**
- Dorsolateral prefrontal association cortex
- Various areas of secondary motor cortex
- **Frontal eye field** (a small area of prefrontal cortex that controls eye movements)

**Organization:** fMRI and TMS studies show the posterior parietal cortex comprises a **mosaic of small areas**, each specialized for guiding particular movements of eyes, head, arms, or hands (Culham & Valyear, 2006; Fogassi & Luppino, 2005).

> **KEY STUDY: Desmurget et al. (2009)**
> - **Method:** Electrical stimulation applied to the inferior portions of the posterior parietal cortex of *conscious neurosurgical patients*.
> - **Findings:**
>   - At **low current levels**: patients experienced an *intention* to perform a particular action.
>   - At **high current levels**: patients *felt* they had actually performed the action.
>   - In **neither case** did the action actually occur.
> - **Significance:** The posterior parietal cortex appears to be involved in generating the *sense of intention and agency*.

**Damage Consequences:**
- Deficits in perception and memory of spatial relationships
- Deficits in accurate reaching and grasping
- Deficits in control of eye movement
- Deficits in attention (Freund, 2003)
- **Two most striking consequences:**

#### Apraxia
- **Apraxia**: A disorder of voluntary movement that is *not attributable* to a simple motor deficit (not paralysis or weakness) *nor* to any deficit in comprehension or motivation (Heilman, Watson, & Rothi, 1997).
- Hallmark: Difficulty making specific movements when *requested* to do so (especially out of context); yet the patient can readily perform the very same movements under *natural conditions* without thinking about it.
- Classic example: An apraxic carpenter hammers nails flawlessly at work but cannot demonstrate hammering movements when asked, particularly without a hammer.
- Symptoms are **bilateral** (affect both sides of the body).
- Cause: Often **unilateral damage to the LEFT posterior parietal lobe** or its connections (Jax, Buxbaum, & Moll, 2006).

#### Contralateral Neglect
- **Contralateral neglect**: A disturbance of a patient's ability to respond to stimuli on the side of the body *opposite (contralateral)* to the side of a brain lesion, in the *absence* of simple sensory or motor deficits (Driver, Vuilleumier, & Husain, 2004; Luauté et al., 2006; Pisella & Mattingley, 2004).
- Most patients behave as if the *left side of their world does not exist*.
- Patients often fail to appreciate that they have a problem (anosognosia-like lack of insight) (Berti et al., 2005).
- Usually associated with large lesions of the **RIGHT posterior parietal lobe** (Mort et al., 2003; Van Vleet & Robertson, 2006).

> **KEY CASE: Mrs. S., the Woman Who Turned in Circles (Oliver Sacks)**
> - Mrs. S. suffered from **contralateral neglect** after a massive stroke to the posterior portions of her *right hemisphere*.
> - She could not recognize or respond to things on her *left* — including external objects and parts of her own body.
> - She applied makeup only to the *right* side of her face.
> - She could see and eat only food on the *right half* of her plate.
> - **Adaptive strategy:** Requested a wheelchair capable of turning in place; when still hungry, turned rightward in a full circle to "discover" the remaining food, ate the right half, then turned again.
> - Source: Oliver Sacks, *The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat*.

**Types of Contralateral Neglect:**
1. **Egocentric left neglect**: Failure to respond to stimuli to the left of one's *own body*. The neglected field is defined by gravitational coordinates — tilting the head does NOT tilt the field of neglect.
2. **Object-based contralateral neglect**: Failure to respond to the *left sides of objects*, regardless of where objects are in the visual field (Kleinman et al., 2007).
   - Both egocentric and object-based receptive fields have been found in primate parietal cortex (Olson, 2003; Pouget & Driver, 2000).

**Evidence for Unconscious Perception in Contralateral Neglect:**
- Evidence 1: Objects repeatedly presented in the neglected spot caused patients to look to that spot on future trials, despite no conscious awareness (Geng & Behrmann, 2002).
- Evidence 2: Patients more readily identified fragmented drawings in their right field if complete versions had been shown in the neglected left field (Vuilleumier et al., 2002) — demonstrating *unconscious* processing of neglected stimuli.

---

### Dorsolateral Prefrontal Association Cortex

**Connections:**
- **Inputs:** From posterior parietal cortex
- **Outputs:** To areas of secondary motor cortex, primary motor cortex, and frontal eye field
- Major *reciprocal* projections back to posterior parietal cortex (not shown in the textbook figure).

**Functions:**
- Plays a role in the **evaluation of external stimuli** and the **initiation of voluntary reactions** to them (Matsumoto & Tanaka, 2004; Ohbayashi, Ohki, & Miyashita, 2003).
- Evidence from monkey neurophysiology (Rao, Rainer, & Miller, 1997): Neurons show activity related to:
  - Characteristics of objects
  - Locations of objects
  - Combination of both object characteristics and location
  - The *response itself* — these neurons begin firing *before* the response and continue firing until it is complete.
- Neurons in *all* cortical motor areas fire in anticipation of motor activity, but those in the dorsolateral prefrontal association cortex fire **FIRST**.
- **Decisions to initiate voluntary movements** may be made in this area (Rowe et al., 2000; Tanji & Hoshi, 2001), but depend on critical interactions with posterior parietal cortex (Brass et al., 2005; Connolly, Andersen, & Goodale, 2003; de Lange, Hagoort, & Toni, 2005).

## 8.3 Secondary Motor Cortex

**Definition and Location:**
- Areas that receive much of their input from association cortex (posterior parietal and dorsolateral prefrontal) and send much of their output to *primary motor cortex*.
- Located in the frontal lobe, just *anterior* to the primary motor cortex.

**Originally known two areas:**
1. **Supplementary motor area (SMA)**: wraps over the top of the frontal lobe and extends down its medial surface into the longitudinal fissure.
2. **Premotor cortex**: runs in a strip from the supplementary motor area to the lateral fissure.

### Identifying the Areas of Secondary Motor Cortex

**Current understanding — at least 8 areas per hemisphere:**
- **3 supplementary motor areas**: SMA, preSMA, supplementary eye field
- **2 premotor areas**: dorsal premotor, ventral premotor
- **3 cingulate motor areas**: in the cortex of the cingulate gyrus

Criteria to qualify as secondary motor cortex: must be appropriately connected with association and secondary motor areas.

**Functional characteristics:**
- Electrical stimulation elicits **complex movements**, often involving *both sides* of the body.
- Neurons become more active *just prior* to initiation of a voluntary movement and continue active throughout it.
- General role: **programming of specific patterns of movements** after receiving general instructions from dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (Hoshi & Tanji, 2007).
- Active when subjects *imagine* or *plan* a series of movements, not just when executing them (Kosslyn, Ganis, & Thompson, 2001; Sirigu & Duhamel, 2001).

**Sensory inputs to secondary motor areas:**
- **Supplementary motor area**: receives most direct sensory input from the **somatosensory system**.
- **Premotor cortex**: receives most direct sensory input from the **visual system**.

---

### Mirror Neurons

**Definition:** **Mirror neurons** are neurons that fire (1) when an individual performs a particular goal-directed hand movement AND (2) when the same movement is *observed* being performed by another (Fadiga, Craighero, & Olivier, 2005).

**Discovery:**
- Discovered in the **early 1990s** by **Giacomo Rizzolatti** and colleagues.
- Location: **ventral premotor cortex** of macaque monkeys.
- Original context: Rizzolatti was studying macaque ventral premotor neurons encoding particular goal objects (fired when monkey reached for one object, not another).
- Serendipitous observation: Some neurons fired equally when the *experimenter* (not the monkey) picked up the same object — named **mirror neurons**.

**Properties:**
- Fire in response to the *understanding of an action*, not its superficial aspects.
- Example: Mirror neurons responding to the sight of a sound-making action (e.g., cracking a peanut) also responded robustly to the *sound alone*.
- Fire even when the key action is hidden behind a screen — approximately half of mirror neurons fire even without direct perception of the action (monkey constructs a mental representation).
- Also found in the **inferior portion of the posterior parietal lobe**.
  - Some posterior parietal mirror neurons respond to the *purpose* of an action rather than the action itself.
  - Example: Fired when a monkey grasped food to *eat* it, but NOT when the same grasping motion was followed by placing food in a bowl.

**Significance:**
- Provide a possible mechanism for **social cognition** (knowledge of perceptions, ideas, and intentions of others).
- Facilitate **social understanding, cooperation, and imitation** (Iacoboni, 2005; Jackson & Decety, 2004; Knoblick & Sebanz, 2006).

**Mirror Neurons in Humans:**
- Not yet *directly confirmed* in humans (few opportunities to record individual neurons during required behavioral tests; Turella et al., 2007).
- Indirect evidence: fMRI studies show areas of human motor cortex active when a person *performs, watches, or imagines* a particular action (Rizzolatti & Fabbri-Destro, 2008; Rodriguez et al., 2008).
- Researchers examining whether pathology of mirror neurons may play a role in human neuropsychological disorders (Ramachandran & Oberman, 2006).

## 8.4 Primary Motor Cortex

**Location:** **Precentral gyrus** of the frontal lobe.

**Role:**
- Major point of **convergence** of cortical sensorimotor signals.
- Major (but not only) point of **departure** of sensorimotor signals from the cerebral cortex.

### Conventional View of Primary Motor Cortex Function

> **KEY STUDY: Penfield & Boldrey (1937) / Penfield & Rasmussen (1950) — Motor Homunculus**
> - **Method:** Brief, low-intensity electrical stimulations applied to various primary motor cortex points of *conscious human patients* during neurosurgery; noted which body part moved.
> - **Finding:** Each cortical site activated a particular *contralateral* muscle, producing a *simple movement*.
> - Primary motor cortex is organized **somatotopically** — according to a map of the body.
> - This somatotopic layout = the **motor homunculus**.
> - Most cortex dedicated to body parts capable of *intricate movements* (hands, mouth).

**Somatosensory feedback to primary motor cortex:**
- Each site receives sensory feedback from receptors in the **muscles and joints** that site influences.
- Exception in monkeys: At least *two hand areas* in primary motor cortex; one receives input from **skin receptors** rather than muscle/joint receptors — presumably facilitating **stereognosis** (identifying objects by touch).

**Stereognosis:** The process of identifying objects by touch; depends on complex interplay between motor responses and the somatosensory stimulation they produce (Johansson & Flanagan, 2009).

**Conventional directional encoding view:**
- Each primary motor cortex neuron encodes the *direction of movement*.
- Each neuron fires maximally when the arm reaches in a particular direction (the neuron's "preferred direction").

### Current View of Primary Motor Cortex Function

> **KEY RESEARCH: Graziano (2006) — Long-Burst Stimulation**
> - **Method:** Used *longer bursts of current* (e.g., 0.5 second duration, similar to duration of a motor response) at slightly higher intensities instead of brief threshold pulses.
> - **Findings:**
>   - Elicited **complex, natural-looking response sequences** (e.g., a complete "feeding response": arm reaches forward, hand closes as if grasping food, hand comes to mouth, mouth opens).
>   - Revealed a *crude* somatotopic organization (face area stimulation elicited face movements, etc.).
>   - Elicited responses were complex species-typical movements involving several body parts.
>   - Sites that moved one body part **overlapped greatly** with sites that moved other body parts.
>   - Therefore: small lesions in the hand area of primary motor cortex NEVER selectively disrupt the activity of a single finger (Scheiber, 1999; Scheiber & Poliakov, 1998).

**Challenge to directional encoding — endpoint encoding:**
- Graziano (2006) recorded from primary motor cortex neurons as monkeys moved *freely* (not in constrained tasks).
- Many neurons' firing was most closely related to the **endpoint of a movement**, not its direction.
- If a monkey reached toward a particular location, neurons sensitive to that *target location* became active *regardless of the direction* of movement needed to reach it.
- Stimulation evidence: Stimulating a given cortical site always produced the *same final position* of the body part, regardless of starting position (e.g., always bends elbow to 90°, regardless of whether it starts at 45° or 180°).

**Implications of endpoint encoding:**
1. Signals from each site in primary motor cortex **diverge greatly** — each site can bring a body part to a target location regardless of starting position.
2. The sensorimotor system is **inherently plastic**: each primary motor cortex location can produce innumerable muscle contraction patterns to get a body part from any starting position to a target.
3. The route neural signals follow from a given area of primary motor cortex is extremely plastic and is determined at any given time by **somatosensory feedback** (Davidson et al., 2007).

> **KEY CASE: Belle — The Monkey That Controlled a Robot with Her Mind (Nicolelis & Chapin, 2002)**
> - **Setup:** Owl monkey Belle watched lights on a control panel; learned that moving a joystick in the direction of a light earned fruit juice.
> - **Method:** **100 microelectrodes** recorded extracellular unit activity from Belle's *primary motor cortex*. Computer analyzed signals and transmitted them to a robotic arm at **MIT** (hundreds of kilometers away).
> - **Finding:** Belle's neural signals directed the robotic arm in the same direction as her own arm simultaneously.
> - **Significance:** Demonstrated that primary motor cortex activity can directly control external devices — foundational work for **brain-machine interfaces (BMIs)** and neuroprosthetics.
> - **Clinical implication:** Injured people may one day control wheelchairs, prosthetic limbs, or paralyzed limbs through neural signals (Lebedev & Nicolelis, 2006; Scherberger, 2009).

### Effects of Primary Motor Cortex Lesions

- Extensive damage has *less* effect than expected (parallel pathways descend directly from secondary motor areas to subcortical circuits, bypassing primary motor cortex).
- Large lesions may:
  - Disrupt ability to move one body part (e.g., one finger) **independently** of others.
  - Produce **astereognosia** (deficits in stereognosis).
  - Reduce speed, accuracy, and force of movements.
- Large lesions do **NOT** eliminate voluntary movement.

## 8.5 Cerebellum and Basal Ganglia

- Neither is a major part of the *descending pathway* of the sensorimotor hierarchy.
- Both **interact with different levels** of the hierarchy to coordinate and modulate its activities.
- Their interconnections explain why damage to cortical connections between visual cortex and frontal motor areas does not abolish visually guided responses (Glickstein, 2000).

---

### Cerebellum

**Structure:**
- Constitutes only **10% of brain mass** but contains **more than half of the brain's neurons** (Azevedo et al., 2009).
- Organized systematically in lobes, columns, and layers.

**Inputs (three sources):**
1. Information from **primary and secondary motor cortex**.
2. Information about **descending motor signals** from brain stem motor nuclei.
3. **Sensorimotor feedback** via somatosensory and vestibular systems.

**Function:**
- **Compares** three sources of input and **corrects ongoing movements** that deviate from their intended course (Bastian, 2006; Bell, Han, & Sawtell, 2008).
- Major role in **motor learning**, particularly learning sequences of movements in which **timing** is a critical factor (D'Angelo & De Zeeuw, 2008; Jacobson, Rokni, & Yarom, 2008).

**Effects of diffuse cerebellar damage:**
- Loss of ability to control precisely the **direction, force, velocity, and amplitude** of movements.
- Inability to adapt motor output patterns to changing conditions.
- Difficulty maintaining steady postures; attempts lead to **tremor**.
- Severe disturbances in **balance, gait, speech, and eye movement control**.
- Particular difficulty learning **new motor sequences** (Shin & Ivry, 2003; Thach & Bastian, 2004).

**Expanded view of cerebellar function:**
- Traditional view: limited to fine-tuning and learning of motor responses.
- Current evidence: functional brain imaging shows cerebellar activity during *non-motor cognitive tasks* (Strick, Dum, & Fiez, 2009).
- Cognitive deficits in patients with cerebellar damage (Fabbro et al., 2004; Hoppenbrouwers et al., 2008).
- Connected to cognitive areas including prefrontal cortex (Ramnani, 2006).
- Most parsimonious current theory: cerebellum functions in **fine-tuning and learning of cognitive responses** in the same way it functions for motor responses (Doya, 2000).

---

### Basal Ganglia

**Structure:**
- A **complex heterogeneous collection of interconnected nuclei** (unlike the systematic architecture of the cerebellum).
- Do not contain as many neurons as the cerebellum.

**Function:**
- Perform a **modulatory function**.
- Contribute *few fibers* to descending motor pathways.
- Part of **neural loops**: receive cortical input from various cortical areas and transmit it back to the cortex via the **thalamus** (McHaffie et al., 2005; Smith et al., 2004).
- Many loops carry signals to and from **motor areas** of the cortex (Nambu, 2008).

**Evolution of theories:**
- Traditional view: like cerebellum, involved in modulation of motor output.
- Current view: involved in a variety of **cognitive functions** in addition to motor modulation (Graybiel, 2005; Graybiel & Saka, 2004; Strick, 2004). Consistent with projections to prefrontal cortex.

**Learning role:**
- Basal ganglia participate in **response learning** — learning to respond correctly to obtain reward and avoid punishment, acquired gradually, trial by trial (Joshua, Adler, & Bergman, 2009; Surmeier, Plotkin, & Shen, 2009).
- Cognitive functions not limited to this form of response learning (Ravizza & Ivry, 2001).

## 8.6 Descending Motor Pathways

Neural signals are conducted from primary motor cortex to spinal cord motor neurons over **four different pathways**:
- **Two** pathways descend in the **dorsolateral** region of the spinal cord.
- **Two** pathways descend in the **ventromedial** region of the spinal cord.

---

### Dorsolateral Corticospinal Tract (Direct — Dorsolateral)

- Axons descend from primary motor cortex through the **medullary pyramids** (two bulges on the ventral surface of the medulla).
- Then **decussate** (cross over) and continue descending in the contralateral dorsolateral spinal white matter.
- Most notable neurons: **Betz cells** — extremely large pyramidal neurons of the primary motor cortex.
- Most axons synapse on **small interneurons** of the spinal gray matter, which then synapse on motor neurons of **distal muscles** of the wrist, hands, fingers, and toes.
- Primates and a few other mammals (e.g., hamsters, raccoons) capable of moving digits independently have dorsolateral corticospinal tract neurons that synapse *directly* on digit motor neurons (Porter & Lemon, 1993).

### Dorsolateral Corticorubrospinal Tract (Indirect — Dorsolateral)

- Axons from primary motor cortex synapse in the **red nucleus** of the midbrain.
- Axons of red nucleus neurons **decussate** and descend through the medulla.
- Some terminate in nuclei of **cranial nerve motor neurons** controlling facial muscles.
- The rest descend in the **dorsolateral portion of the spinal cord**.
- Synapse on interneurons that in turn synapse on motor neurons projecting to **distal muscles of the arms and legs**.
- Name: *rubro* = red nucleus.

---

### Ventromedial Corticospinal Tract (Direct — Ventromedial)

- Long axons descend **ipsilaterally** from primary motor cortex into the ventromedial areas of spinal white matter.
- As each axon descends, it **branches diffusely** and innervates interneuron circuits in **several different spinal segments on BOTH sides** of the spinal gray matter.

### Ventromedial Cortico-brainstem-spinal Tract (Indirect — Ventromedial)

- Motor cortex axons feed into a **complex network of brain stem structures**.
- Brain stem network neurons descend **bilaterally** in the ventromedial portion of the spinal cord.
- Each side carries signals from **both hemispheres**.
- Each neuron synapses on interneurons of **several different spinal cord segments** controlling **proximal muscles** of trunk and limbs.

**Four major brain stem structures in this pathway:**
1. **Tectum**: receives auditory and visual information about spatial location.
2. **Vestibular nucleus**: receives balance information from semicircular canals of the inner ear.
3. **Reticular formation**: contains motor programs for complex species-typical movements (walking, swimming, jumping).
4. **Motor nuclei of cranial nerves**: control muscles of the face.

---

### Comparison of Dorsolateral vs. Ventromedial Motor Pathways

| Feature | Dorsolateral Tracts | Ventromedial Tracts |
|---|---|---|
| **Diffuseness** | Terminate in contralateral half of one spinal cord segment; sometimes directly on a motor neuron | Much more diffuse; innervate interneurons on both sides of spinal gray matter in several segments |
| **Muscle targets** | Distal muscles (e.g., finger muscles) | Proximal muscles of trunk and limbs (e.g., shoulder muscles) |
| **Type of movement** | Discrete limb movements; independent digit movement | Posture and whole-body movements (walking, climbing) |
| **Crossing** | Decussate; control contralateral limbs | Bilateral; each side carries signals from both hemispheres |

> **KEY EXPERIMENTS: Lawrence & Kuypers (1968) — Transection Studies in Monkeys**
>
> **Experiment 1 (Lawrence & Kuypers, 1968a):** Transected left and right *dorsolateral corticospinal tracts* at the medullary pyramids.
> - Could still stand, walk, and climb normally.
> - Reaching movements initially weak and poorly directed.
> - Recovered reaching ability over weeks.
> - **Permanent deficits:**
>   1. Could never again move fingers *independently* — used all fingers as a unit when picking up food.
>   2. Could never release objects from grasp voluntarily — had to root for food "like a pig rooting for truffles."
>   - Notable exception: still released cage bars when climbing (same response, different context, different CNS control).
>
> **Experiment 2 (Lawrence & Kuypers, 1968b):** Additional transections in the same monkeys.
>
> - **Group A — Dorsolateral corticorubrospinal tract also transected:**
>   - Could still stand, walk, climb.
>   - Limbs hung limply by sides when sitting.
>   - When an arm was used for reaching, it was thrown from the shoulder and used to drag objects back ("rubber-handled rake").
>
> - **Group B — Both ventromedial tracts also transected:**
>   - Severe **postural abnormalities**.
>   - Great difficulty walking or sitting.
>   - Slightest disturbance (e.g., a loud noise) caused falling.
>   - Lost ability to control *shoulders*.
>   - Fed using only elbow and whole-hand movements; upper arms hung limply.
>
> **Conclusions from both experiments:**
> - Two ventromedial tracts: control **posture and whole-body movements** (walking, climbing); also control limb movements involved in such activities.
> - Both dorsolateral tracts: control **movements of the limbs**.
> - Only the *corticospinal* division of the dorsolateral system can mediate **independent movements of the digits**.

## 8.7 Sensorimotor Spinal Circuits

- Spinal cord motor circuits are NOT mere cables.
- Show considerable **complexity and independent functioning** without signals from the brain (Grillner & Jessell, 2009).

---

### Muscles

**Motor Unit:**
- **Motor unit**: The *smallest unit of motor activity* — one motor neuron plus all the skeletal muscle fibers it innervates.
- When the motor neuron fires, *all* muscle fibers of its unit contract together.
- Units with the *fewest fibers* (fingers, face) permit the **highest degree of selective motor control**.

**Skeletal Muscle Structure:**
- Hundreds of thousands of threadlike **muscle fibers** bound together in a tough membrane; attached to bone by a **tendon**.
- **Neuromuscular junction**: Motor neurons release **acetylcholine (ACh)** activating the **motor end-plate** on each muscle fiber, causing the fiber to contract.
- Muscles can generate force in **only one direction** (contraction only).
- **Motor pool**: All motor neurons that innervate the fibers of a single muscle.

**Types of Skeletal Muscle Fibers:**

| Feature | Fast Muscle Fibers | Slow Muscle Fibers |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Contract and relax quickly | Slower |
| Force | Great force | Weaker |
| Fatigue | Fatigue quickly | More sustained contraction |
| Vascularization | Poorly vascularized | Richly vascularized |
| Color | Pale | Much redder |
| Function | Quick movements (e.g., jumping) | Gradual movements (e.g., walking) |

Each muscle contains **both** fast and slow fibers.

**Muscle types by action:**
- **Flexors**: Act to *bend/flex* a joint.
- **Extensors**: Act to *straighten/extend* a joint.
- Classic example: **Biceps** = flexor of elbow; **Triceps** = extensor of elbow.
- **Synergistic muscles**: Produce the *same* movement.
- **Antagonistic muscles**: Act in *opposition* (e.g., biceps and triceps).

**Types of Muscle Contraction:**
- **Isometric contraction**: Activation increases tension WITHOUT shortening the muscle (no joint movement).
- **Dynamic contraction**: Muscle shortens and pulls bones together (produces movement).

Muscle tension increased by:
1. Increasing the *number of neurons* in the motor pool that are firing.
2. Increasing the *firing rates* of neurons already firing.
3. A combination of both (most common).

### Receptor Organs of Tendons and Muscles

#### Golgi Tendon Organs
- **Location**: Embedded in the **tendons**.
- **Respond to**: Increases in **muscle tension** (the pull of muscle on tendon).
- **Do NOT respond to**: Changes in muscle length.
- **Primary function**: Provide CNS with information about muscle tension.
- **Protective function**: When contraction is dangerously extreme, Golgi tendon organs excite **inhibitory interneurons** in the spinal cord causing the muscle to **relax** (prevents tearing/damage).

#### Muscle Spindles
- **Location**: Embedded in the **muscle tissue** itself.
- **Respond to**: Changes in **muscle length**.
- **Do NOT respond to**: Changes in muscle tension.
- Each spindle has its own **intrafusal muscle** (a threadlike muscle fiber within the spindle), innervated by its own **intrafusal motor neuron**.
- The regular skeletal muscle fibers are called **extrafusal muscles**.

**Why the intrafusal system is necessary:**
- Without intrafusal motor input, a muscle spindle would go **slack** every time the extrafusal muscle contracted — the spindle would be unresponsive to further length changes.
- The **intrafusal motor neuron** shortens the intrafusal muscle *each time* the extrafusal muscle shortens, maintaining appropriate tension on the stretch-sensitive portion of the spindle so it remains responsive to slight length changes.

---

### Stretch Reflex

- **Patellar tendon reflex** (knee-jerk reflex): a classic example of a **stretch reflex** — a reflex elicited by a sudden external stretching force on a muscle.
- **Mechanism (monosynaptic):**
  1. Tap to patellar tendon stretches the extensor (thigh) muscle.
  2. Stretch activates **muscle-spindle stretch receptors**.
  3. Stretch receptors initiate action potentials carried into the spinal cord by **spindle afferent neurons** via the *dorsal root*.
  4. Action potentials excite **motor neurons** in the **ventral horn** of the spinal cord.
  5. Motor neurons send impulses back to the same stretched muscle — compensatory contraction — sudden leg extension.
- **Monosynaptic**: Spindle afferent neuron synapses directly on the motor neuron (no interneuron required).
- **Real-life function**: Keep external forces from altering the intended position of the body. An unexpected force stretching a muscle triggers immediate compensatory contraction via the spindle feedback circuit.
- Illustrates two core principles: the critical role of sensory feedback in motor regulation; and the ability of lower spinal circuits to handle "business details" without higher-level involvement.

---

### Withdrawal Reflex

- **Definition**: Reflex withdrawal from a painful stimulus (e.g., pulling hand away from a hot pot).
- **Polysynaptic** — NOT monosynaptic (unlike the stretch reflex).
- First responses in arm flexor motor neurons approximately **1.6 milliseconds** after the painful stimulus — the time for a neural signal to cross **two synapses** — meaning the shortest pathway involves **one interneuron**.
- Additional responses triggered over **multisynaptic pathways**, some involving the cortex.
- **Reciprocal Innervation**: During the withdrawal reflex, flexor muscles contract AND extensor muscles are simultaneously *inhibited* — allowing smooth, effective withdrawal. This coordinated reciprocal relationship between agonist and antagonist muscles is termed **reciprocal innervation**.

|---|---|

| **Functional segregation** | Each level of the sensorimotor hierarchy is composed of different units each performing different functions |
| **Ballistic movements** | Brief, all-or-none, high-speed movements (e.g., swatting a fly) not normally influenced by sensory feedback |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Three principles of sensorimotor function**: (1) hierarchically organized, (2) motor output guided by sensory feedback, (3) learning changes nature and locus of control. All introduced through Rhonda the cashier.

2. **Ballistic movements** are the ONLY type of movement NOT guided by sensory feedback — brief, all-or-none, high-speed (e.g., swatting a fly).

3. **Apraxia vs. Contralateral neglect**: Both from posterior parietal damage. Apraxia = can perform movements naturally but not on request (often LEFT parietal). Contralateral neglect = fails to respond to contralateral stimuli (often RIGHT parietal, left-side neglect).

4. **Motor homunculus** was mapped by **Penfield & Boldrey (1937)** using electrical stimulation in conscious patients during neurosurgery. Most primary motor cortex dedicated to **hands and mouth**.

5. **Mirror neurons** discovered by **Rizzolatti** in **ventral premotor cortex** of macaque monkeys (early 1990s). Fire both during performance AND observation of the same goal-directed movement. Proposed mechanism for social cognition.

6. **Cerebellum**: Only **10% of brain mass** but contains **MORE THAN HALF** of all brain neurons. Compares three inputs (motor cortex signals, brainstem motor signals, sensorimotor feedback) and corrects deviations. Critical for motor learning especially **timing of movement sequences**.

7. **Basal ganglia**: Part of neural loops from cortex back to motor cortex via the **thalamus**. Involved in gradual, reward-based **response learning** and cognitive functions in addition to motor modulation.

8. **Dorsolateral pathways** (both tracts) control **distal muscles** (fingers, wrist, toes). **Ventromedial pathways** control **proximal muscles** (trunk, shoulders) and posture/whole-body movements. **ONLY the dorsolateral corticospinal tract** can mediate **independent digit movements**.

9. **Lawrence & Kuypers (1968)** key findings: Cutting dorsolateral corticospinal tract = walking preserved but independent finger movements and voluntary release of grasp permanently lost. Cutting both ventromedial tracts = severe postural instability; could not control shoulders.

10. **Betz cells** = extremely large pyramidal neurons of the primary motor cortex; most notable neurons of the dorsolateral corticospinal tract.

11. **Golgi tendon organs** respond to **muscle tension** (not length); protective function via inhibitory spinal interneurons. **Muscle spindles** respond to **muscle length** (not tension); have intrafusal muscle + intrafusal motor neuron to prevent going slack during contraction.

12. **Stretch reflex** is **monosynaptic** (spindle afferent neuron directly on ventral horn motor neuron). **Withdrawal reflex** is **polysynaptic** (minimum one interneuron; ~1.6 ms latency).

13. **Graziano (2006)** long-burst stimulation showed primary motor cortex encodes **movement endpoints/targets** (not individual muscle contractions or directions). Each cortical site produces same final position regardless of starting position — demonstrates inherent **plasticity** of sensorimotor system.

14. **Belle experiment (Nicolelis & Chapin, 2002)**: 100 microelectrodes in owl monkey's primary motor cortex directed a robotic arm at MIT — foundational demonstration for brain-machine interfaces and neuroprosthetics.

15. **Dorsolateral prefrontal association cortex** neurons fire **FIRST** among all cortical motor areas in anticipation of a voluntary movement — suggesting this area initiates the decision to move; decision requires interaction with posterior parietal cortex.
