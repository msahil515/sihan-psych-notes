# Chapter 13: Hormones and Sex

**Overview:** Hormones influence sex two ways: (1) directing development of sexual characteristics conception to maturity (**developmental/organizational** effects), and (2) activating reproduction-related behavior in adults (**activational** effects). Challenges the "men-are-men-and-women-are-women" assumption throughout.

---

## Developmental and Activational Effects
- **Developmental (organizational) effects**: shape sexual characteristics from conception to maturity.
- **Activational effects**: activate reproduction-related behavior in adults.
- The distinction is imperfect: the brain develops into the late teens, so adolescent hormone surges can have **both** effects (Cohen-Bendahan, van de Beek, & Berenbaum, 2005; Phoenix, 2008).

## 13.1 Neuroendocrine System

### MAMAWAWA
- **MAMAWAWA (men-are-men-and-women-are-women assumption)**: treating maleness and femaleness as discrete, mutually exclusive opposites — **seductive but fundamentally flawed**.

### Glands
- **Exocrine glands**: release into ducts (e.g., sweat glands). **Endocrine glands (ductless)**: release **hormones** directly into the circulation.
- Other organs (stomach, liver, intestine, **body fat**) also release hormones. Major endocrine glands: pineal, hypothalamus, pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, thymus, adrenal, pancreas, ovary, testis.

### Gonads
- **Gonads**: male **testes** and female **ovaries**; produce **sperm** and **ova**. Fertilized ovum = **zygote**.
- All cells (except gametes) have **23 pairs of chromosomes**; gametes have **23** (half); fertilization restores 23 pairs.
- **Sex chromosomes**: females **XX**, males **XY**. Every ovum carries X; sperm carry X or Y (Y-sperm → male). Y encodes **27 proteins**; X encodes ~**1,500** (Arnold, 2004). (All chromosomes are X-shaped after duplication; the Y is just smaller.)

### Classes of Hormones
1. **Amino acid derivative hormones**: from an amino acid (e.g., **epinephrine** from tyrosine).
2. **Peptide hormones** (short chains) and **protein hormones** (long chains).
3. **Steroid hormones**: from **cholesterol**.

**Steroid properties (MCQ-critical):** **all sex hormones are steroids**; small and **fat-soluble**, so they (1) bind cell-membrane receptors AND (2) enter cells to bind cytoplasmic/nuclear receptors and **directly influence gene expression** → most diverse, long-lasting effects (Brown, 1994).

### Sex Steroids
- Testes and ovaries release the **same** hormones (anti-MAMAWAWA).
- **Androgens** (most common = **testosterone**); **estrogens** (most common = **estradiol**). Ovaries release more estrogens; testes more androgens. Calling them "male/female hormones" is misleading.
- **Progestins** (most common = **progesterone**): in women, prepares uterus/breasts for pregnancy.
- **Adrenal cortex**: regulates glucose/salt; also releases **small amounts of all sex steroids**.

### Pituitary Hormones
- **Pituitary** = the **"master gland"**; most of its hormones are **tropic hormones** (stimulate other glands). **Gonadotropin** = tropic hormone stimulating gonadal hormone release. The pituitary is two fused glands:
- **Posterior pituitary**: from **hypothalamic tissue**; releases **vasopressin (ADH)** (renal water reabsorption) and **oxytocin** (uterine contractions; milk ejection) — peptide hormones made in the **paraventricular** and **supraoptic nuclei**, transported down axons. **Neurosecretory cells** release hormones into circulation. **Receives neural input**.
- **Anterior pituitary**: from roof-of-mouth tissue; releases **tropic hormones**; receives **NO neural input**.

### Cyclic vs. Steady Hormone Levels
- Women: gonadal/gonadotropic hormones cycle ~**28 days** (menstrual cycle). Men: steady.

> **KEY STUDY — Geoffrey Harris (1950s):** transplanting a female pituitary into a male made it steady-state; a male pituitary into a female made it cycle → pituitaries are **not** inherently cyclic/steady; the **hypothalamus** controls the pattern.

### Neural Control of the Pituitary
- Light-dark cycles control breeding hormones → **visual input** controls tropic hormone release. The **hypothalamus** regulates the anterior pituitary, despite no neural input — via hormones.

### Hypothalamic Control Mechanisms
- **Posterior pituitary**: vasopressin/oxytocin made in paraventricular/supraoptic nuclei, transported down axons, released on action potentials.
- **Anterior pituitary** (Harris, 1955): regulated by hypothalamic hormones via the **hypothalamopituitary portal system** (a **portal vein** connects two capillary networks); cutting the portal veins disrupts anterior pituitary release.

### Releasing Hormones
- **Releasing hormones** stimulate, **release-inhibiting factors** inhibit, specific anterior pituitary hormones.

> **KEY DISCOVERY — Guillemin and Schally (late 1960s):** isolated **thyrotropin-releasing hormone** (sheep/pig hypothalamus) → triggers thyrotropin → thyroid hormone. **Nobel Prize 1977**. Schally's group then isolated **gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH)** (Schally, Kastin, & Arimura, **1971**), which stimulates **both FSH and LH**. All releasing/tropic hormones are **peptides**.

- Two anterior pituitary gonadotropins: **FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone)** and **LH (luteinizing hormone)**.

### Regulation of Hormone Levels
1. **Neural signals**: all endocrine glands except the anterior pituitary are directly regulated by the nervous system; hormone release can be regulated by **experience** (so hormonal and experiential explanations are not exclusive).
2. **Hormonal feedback**: circulating hormones feed back to maintain stable levels (high gonadal hormones → **decrease** release).
3. **Nonhormonal chemicals**: glucose, calcium, sodium (e.g., high glucose → insulin).

### Pulsatile Release
- Hormones released in **pulses**, several surges/day, each **a few minutes** (Khadra & Li, 2006). "Steady" male levels = no systematic day-to-day change.

### Gonadal Regulation Cascade (Figure 13.5)
Brain → hypothalamus (**GnRH** into portal system) → anterior pituitary (gonadotropin) → gonads (estrogens, androgens, progestins) → feedback to pituitary/hypothalamus and action on the brain.

## 13.2 Hormones and Sexual Development of the Body

- Humans are **dimorphic**. **Key principle**: all are programmed to develop **female** bodies; male bodies arise only when the female program is **overridden** (anti-MAMAWAWA).

### Gonads (Figure 13.6)
- At **6 weeks**: identical **primordial gonads** with a **cortex** (→ ovary) and **medulla** (→ testis).
- At 6 weeks, the **Sry gene** (Y chromosome) makes **Sry protein** → medulla becomes a **testis**. Without Sry protein, the cortex becomes **ovaries** (default; no female counterpart to Sry). Injecting Sry protein into a female fetus → testes; blocking it in a male → ovaries.

### Internal Reproductive Ducts (Figure 13.7)
- At 6 weeks all fetuses have two duct systems:

| System | Develops into | Structures |
|---|---|---|
| **Wolffian (male)** | Male ducts | Seminal vesicles; vas deferens |
| **Müllerian (female)** | Female ducts | Uterus; upper vagina; fallopian tubes |

- **Third month of male development**: testes secrete **testosterone** (→ Wolffian development) and **Müllerian-inhibiting substance** (→ Müllerian degeneration + testicular descent into the **scrotum**). It is **testosterone** (not chromosomes) that triggers Wolffian development.
- **Female ducts** develop in any fetus **not exposed to testicular hormones** (ovaries are nearly inactive in fetal life); confirmed in normal, ovariectomized, and orchidectomized fetuses (Jost, 1972).
- **Ovariectomy** = remove ovaries; **orchidectomy** = remove testes; **gonadectomy (castration)** = remove gonads.

### External Reproductive Organs (Figure 13.8)
- Both sexes develop from the **same bipotential precursor** (~2 months):

| Precursor | Male | Female |
|---|---|---|
| **Glans** | Penis head | Clitoris |
| **Urethral folds** | Urethra | Labia minora |
| **Lateral bodies** | Penis shaft | Clitoral hood |
| **Labioscrotal swellings** | Scrotum | Labia majora |

- **Testosterone present** → male; **absent** → female (default).

### Puberty: Secondary Sex Characteristics
- **Puberty**: fertility achieved; growth spurt; **secondary sex characteristics** develop.
- Anterior pituitary release increases (Grumbach, 2002): (1) **growth hormone** (only one without a gland target; acts on bone/muscle → growth spurt); (2) **gonadotropic hormone** + **ACTH** → gonads/adrenal cortex raise sex hormones.
- Males: **androgens > estrogens** → masculinization; females: estrogens predominate → feminization. Pre-pubertal castration prevents maturation without replacement.
- **Androstenedione**: adrenal androgen driving **female pubic/axillary hair** (against "male hormone" labeling).
- **Timing**: now girls ~**10.5**, boys ~**11.5** years; 150 years ago **15-16** (improved conditions).

## 13.3 Hormones and Sexual Development of Brain and Behavior

### Sex Differences in the Brain
- Male brains average ~**15% larger**. **Sexual dimorphisms**: structural male-female brain differences (nuclei volumes, cell numbers, synapses). We don't yet understand how these affect behavior (Cahill, 2006).

### Pfeiffer's Experiments (1936)
> **KEY STUDY — Pfeiffer (1936):** neonatal rats gonadectomized/transplanted. (1) Gonadectomy of either sex → adult **female cyclic** gonadotropin release; (2) testes transplant → **steady male** pattern; (3) ovary transplant → no effect. Pfeiffer (wrongly) credited the **pituitary**; corrected (Harris & Levine, 1965): perinatal androgens override the **hypothalamus**.
- **Perinatal** = around birth. The female cyclic pattern develops unless overridden by perinatal testosterone.

### Aromatization Hypothesis
- **Aromatase**: enzyme converting testosterone → estradiol. **Aromatization**: that conversion (Balthazart & Ball, 1998).
- **Aromatization hypothesis**: perinatal testosterone masculinizes the brain via **estradiol aromatized from it** (the "female hormone" masculinizes the male brain). Evidence in rodents: (1) early estradiol masculinizes; (2) testosterone + aromatization blockers does NOT; (3) non-aromatizable androgens (e.g., **dihydrotestosterone**) do NOT.
- **Alpha fetoprotein**: perinatal blood protein that **deactivates circulating estradiol**, protecting the female brain. The male brain is still masculinized because **testosterone** is immune to it and crosses to brain cells, where aromatase makes estradiol; alpha fetoprotein doesn't cross the **blood-brain barrier**.

### Modern Perspectives
1. **Region-specific**: aromatase is in only a few areas (e.g., hypothalamus).
2. **Species differences**: aromatization is less prominent in **primates** (Zuloaga et al., 2008).
3. **Timing varies**: many sex differences develop at **puberty**, not perinatally (Sisk & Zehr, 2005).
4. **Sex chromosomes act independently of hormones**: different brain gene expression exists **before** gonads function (Dewing et al., 2003; Arnold, 2009).
5. **Estradiol actively shapes female brain development** (not passive default; Bakker & Baum, 2007).

The "female default" model fits reproductive organs well but falters for the brain.

### Perinatal Hormones and Behavior

> **KEY STUDY — Phoenix, Goy, Gerall, & Young (1959):** testosterone to pregnant guinea pigs → female offspring (ovariectomized) showed more **mounting** and less **lordosis** as adults → perinatal testosterone **masculinizes and defeminizes**.

> **KEY STUDY — Grady, Phoenix, & Young (1965):** neonatally castrated male rats showed deficient male copulation but more **lordosis** as adults → lack of early testosterone **feminizes and demasculinizes**.

- **Lordosis** (receptivity posture), **intromission** (penile insertion), **ejaculation**, **proceptive behaviors** (hopping, darting, ear wiggling), **mounting**.
- Masculinization window in rats: first **11 days** after birth. Aromatization is important in **rodents** (Goy & McEwen, 1980) but not **monkeys** (Wallen, 2005).
- Other perinatal testosterone effects in animals: disrupts proceptive/maternal behavior, increases aggression and rough play in females.
- **Human (correlational)**: prenatal androgen exposure contributes to differences in **interests, spatial ability, aggressiveness** between sexes; no convincing within-sex effect (Cohen-Bendahan et al., 2005).
- **Four separate dimensions**: masculinization ≠ defeminization; feminization ≠ demasculinization; male and female behaviors can co-exist. **Medial preoptic area** lesions abolish male (not female) reproductive behavior in both sexes (Singer, 1968).

## 13.4 Three Cases of Exceptional Human Sexual Development

Each subject was male in some respects, female in others — each a test of theory.

### Case 1: Anne S. — Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (CAIS)
- 26-year-old woman; no menstruation, painful intercourse (Jones & Park, 1971); sparse pubic/axillary hair, normal external genitals, vagina only **4 cm**, underdeveloped uterus.
- **Genetic sex was male (XY)**: XY oral cells, internalized **testes**, no ovaries, male hormone levels.
- **CAIS**: mutation in the **androgen receptor gene** → androgen receptors unresponsive (~**5 in 100,000 male births**). Testes made androgens but the body couldn't respond → female-line development; testes made **Müllerian-inhibiting substance** → short vagina, underdeveloped uterus; at puberty, estrogens feminized the body.
- **Behavior**: typically female gender identity, orientation, interests, cognition.

### Case 2: Elaine — Adrenogenital Syndrome
- Ambiguous external genitals; raised as a girl; masculinized at puberty; treated surgically + hormonally (Money & Ehrhardt, 1972).
- **Adrenogenital syndrome**: **most common disorder of sexual development** (~**1 in 10,000**); caused by **congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH)** — cortisol deficiency → compensatory adrenal hyperactivity → **excess adrenal androgens**.
- **Genetic females**: enlarged clitoris, partially fused labia; gonads/internal ducts usually normal (androgens act too late for Wolffian system). **Males**: mainly accelerated puberty.
- **Treatment**: surgical correction + **cortisol**. **Behavior**: more tomboyishness, strength, aggression; prefer boys' clothes/toys; usually within normal female range. Most heterosexual; some increased bisexuality/homosexuality (Gooren, 2006). Before cortisol (pre-1950): unpredictable, often devastating outcomes.

### Case 3: David Reimer — Ablatio Penis
- Male identical twin whose penis was destroyed during circumcision at **7 months**. **John Money** recommended castration, artificial vagina, female rearing, pubertal estrogen.
- **Money (1975)**: claimed at 12 the patient ("Joan") was a normal female — support for **social-learning theory**.

> **KEY CASE — Diamond & Sigmundson (1997):** despite female genitalia/rearing, **developed along male lines** — preferred boys' activities, tried to urinate standing, suspected he was a boy from second grade, **rejected estrogen** at 12. At **14** decided to live as male ("John"); requested androgens, **mastectomy**, **phalloplasty**; married at 25; strictly heterosexual; reproduction permanently lost. Real name **David Reimer** (*As Nature Made Him*, Colapinto, 2000); **suicide May 4, 2004**. **Reiner (1997)**: the **brain** (not genitals) determines psychosocial sexual development.

- **Clinical implication**: surgically modifying sex at birth should be **curtailed**; irrevocable treatments should await early puberty and emergence of sexual identity.

## 13.5 Effects of Gonadal Hormones on Adults

### Male Behavior and Testosterone
> **KEY STUDY — Bremer (1959):** review of **157 orchidectomized Norwegians** (many sex offenders). Orchidectomy reduced sexual interest/behavior **variably** — ~half became asexual within weeks; others retained some interest. Physical changes: reduced hair, fat on hips/chest, softer skin, less strength. Of 102 sex offenders, only **3 reconvicted**. Recommended castration as a last-resort treatment.

- Some men stay sexually active for months post-orchidectomy despite cleared testicular hormones — possibly maintained by **adrenal androgens** (hypothesis only).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **MAMAWAWA** = the flawed view of male/female as exclusive opposites; both gonads make androgens AND estrogens.

2. **Default development is female**: without Sry protein (then androgens), all embryos develop female gonads, ducts, genitals. Male development requires hormonal override.

3. **Sry gene/protein** (Y chromosome) triggers testis development at **6 weeks**; no female counterpart.

4. **Third month (male)**: testes secrete **testosterone** (Wolffian) and **Müllerian-inhibiting substance** (Müllerian degeneration + descent).

5. **External genitals** from a single **bipotential precursor** (unlike internal ducts).

6. **Harris pituitary transplants (1950s)**: cyclicity vs. steady-state controlled by the **hypothalamus**.

7. **Guillemin & Schally** (Nobel 1977) isolated thyrotropin-releasing hormone; Schally isolated **GnRH (1971)**, which stimulates **both FSH and LH**.

8. **Aromatization hypothesis**: perinatal testosterone masculinizes via **estradiol**; **alpha fetoprotein** protects female brains (doesn't cross the BBB).

9. **Phoenix et al. (1959)** masculinizes + defeminizes females; **Grady et al. (1965)** feminizes + demasculinizes males; these are **separate dimensions**.

10. **CAIS**: androgen receptor mutation; XY → female phenotype, internal testes, female identity; ~**5/100,000 male births**.

11. **Adrenogenital syndrome (CAH)**: **most common** disorder (~1/10,000); excess adrenal androgens virilize genetic females; more tomboyish behavior.

12. **David Reimer (ablatio penis)**: developed male despite female rearing; **brain determines** psychosocial sex (Reiner, 1997); suicide May 4, 2004.

13. **Androstenedione** (adrenal androgen) drives **female** pubic/axillary hair — against "male hormone" labeling.

14. **Pulsatile release**: surges of a few minutes; "steady" male levels = no systematic day-to-day change.

15. **Bremer (1959)**: orchidectomy variably reduces sexual behavior; of 102 sex offenders, **3 reconvicted**; adrenal androgens hypothesized for residual activity.
