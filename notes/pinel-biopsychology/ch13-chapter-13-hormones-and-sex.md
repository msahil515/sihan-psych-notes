# Chapter 13: Hormones and Sex

**Chapter Overview:** This chapter examines how hormones influence sex in two fundamental ways: (1) directing the development of anatomical, physiological, and behavioral sexual characteristics from conception to maturity (developmental/organizational effects), and (2) activating reproduction-related behavior in sexually mature adults (activational effects). It challenges the "men-are-men-and-women-are-women" assumption throughout, demonstrating that sexual differentiation is far more complex and overlapping than popular intuition suggests.

---

## Developmental and Activational Effects of Sex Hormones

- **Developmental (organizational) effects**: Hormones influence development from conception to sexual maturity, shaping anatomical, physiological, and behavioral characteristics distinguishing female from male.
- **Activational effects**: Hormones activate reproduction-related behavior in sexually mature adults.
- The distinction between developmental and activational effects is useful but not always perfectly clear.
  - Reason: The brain continues to develop into the late teens; therefore, adolescent hormone surges can have **both** developmental and activational effects simultaneously.
  - Source: Cohen-Bendahan, van de Beek, & Berenbaum (2005).
- Reference: Phoenix (2008) — framework for distinguishing the two types of hormonal influence.

## 13.1 Neuroendocrine System

### Men-Are-Men-and-Women-Are-Women Assumption (MAMAWAWA)

- **MAMAWAWA (men-are-men-and-women-are-women assumption)**: The tendency to think about femaleness and maleness as discrete, mutually exclusive, opposite categories.
- Applied to hormones: leads to the assumption that females have "female hormones" producing female bodies/behaviors and males have "male hormones" producing opposite male bodies/behaviors.
- This assumption is described as **seductive** (seems intuitively right) but **fundamentally flawed**.
- The chapter consistently challenges MAMAWAWA throughout.

### Glands

- **Exocrine glands**: Release chemicals into ducts, which carry them to targets (mostly on the body surface). Example: sweat glands.
- **Endocrine glands (ductless glands)**: Release chemicals called **hormones** directly into the circulatory system.
  - Hormones travel via the circulatory system to target tissues (other endocrine glands or nervous system sites).
- By convention, only organs whose **primary** function is hormone release are called endocrine glands.
- Other organs also release hormones into general circulation: stomach, liver, intestine, and **body fat** — technically also part of the endocrine system.
- Major endocrine glands (Figure 13.1): Pineal, Hypothalamus, Pituitary, Thyroid, Parathyroid, Thymus, Adrenal, Pancreas, Ovary, Testis.

### Gonads

- **Gonads**: The male **testes** (TEST-eez) and female **ovaries** — the primary sex glands.
- Primary function: production of **sperm cells** (testes) and **ova** (ovaries).
- After **copulation** (sexual intercourse), a sperm cell may fertilize an ovum to form a **zygote** — contains all genetic information for normal adult development.
- Every cell (except ova and sperm) has **23 pairs of chromosomes**.
- Ova and sperm cells contain **half** the number: one member of each of the 23 pairs (23 chromosomes total).
- Fertilization restores the full complement of 23 pairs (one from each parent).

#### Sex Chromosomes

- **Sex chromosomes**: The pair of chromosomes containing genetic programs directing sexual development.
- Females: two large **X chromosomes** (XX).
- Males: one **X chromosome** and one smaller **Y chromosome** (XY).
- Every ovum carries an X chromosome.
- Half of sperm cells carry X chromosomes; half carry Y chromosomes.
- Sex determined by which sperm fertilizes the ovum:
  - X-bearing sperm + ovum = female (XX)
  - Y-bearing sperm + ovum = male (XY)
- **Common misconception**: X and Y chromosomes are NOT actually X- or Y-shaped. All chromosomes become X-shaped after duplication (two duplicates joined at one point). The Y chromosome is simply smaller — early investigators missed one small arm and perceived a Y shape.
- Y chromosome encodes only **27 proteins**; X chromosome encodes approximately **1,500 proteins** (Arnold, 2004).

### Classes of Hormones

Three classes of vertebrate hormones:

1. **Amino acid derivative hormones**: Synthesized in a few simple steps from an amino acid molecule.
   - Example: **Epinephrine** — released from adrenal medulla; synthesized from tyrosine.
2. **Peptide hormones** and **protein hormones**: Chains of amino acids.
   - Peptide hormones = short chains.
   - Protein hormones = long chains.
3. **Steroid hormones**: Synthesized from **cholesterol** (a fat molecule).

#### Properties of Steroid Hormones (critical for MCQs)

- **Sex hormones** are ALL steroid hormones.
- Most hormones bind to receptors in **cell membranes**.
- Steroid hormones are **small and fat-soluble** — can penetrate cell membranes.
- Can influence cells in TWO ways:
  1. Bind to receptors in cell membranes (like other hormones).
  2. Enter the cell, bind to receptors in the **cytoplasm or nucleus**, and **directly influence gene expression**.
- Amino acid derivative and peptide hormones affect gene expression less commonly and by less direct mechanisms.
- Consequence: steroid hormones tend to have the **most diverse and long-lasting effects** on cellular function (Brown, 1994).

### Sex Steroids

- Testes and ovaries both release the **same** hormones — a key anti-MAMAWAWA point.
- Two main classes of gonadal hormones:
  1. **Androgens**: Most common = **testosterone**.
  2. **Estrogens**: Most common = **estradiol**.
- Adult ovaries release **more estrogens** than androgens; adult testes release **more androgens** than estrogens.
- Calling androgens "male sex hormones" and estrogens "female sex hormones" is **misleading** — should be avoided due to MAMAWAWA implications.
- Third class: **Progestins**.
  - Most common: **Progesterone**.
  - In women: prepares uterus and breasts for pregnancy. Function in men: unclear.
- **Adrenal cortex**: Primary function = glucose and salt regulation; also releases **small amounts of all sex steroids**.

### Hormones of the Pituitary

- **Pituitary gland**: Called the **"master gland"** because most of its hormones are **tropic hormones**.
- **Tropic hormones**: Hormones whose primary function is influencing hormone release from other glands ("tropic" = able to stimulate or change something).
- **Gonadotropin**: Pituitary tropic hormone that stimulates gonadal hormone release.
- The pituitary is really **two glands** fused during embryological development:

#### Posterior Pituitary
- Develops from an outgrowth of **hypothalamic tissue** — dangles from the hypothalamus on the pituitary stalk.
- Two major hormones:
  1. **Vasopressin (antidiuretic hormone, ADH)**: Facilitates renal water reabsorption.
  2. **Oxytocin**: Stimulates uterine contractions during labor; stimulates milk ejection during suckling.
- Both are **peptide hormones** synthesized in neuron cell bodies in the **paraventricular nuclei** and **supraoptic nuclei** of the hypothalamus.
- Transported along axons to posterior pituitary terminals; released into bloodstream when action potentials arrive.
- **Neurosecretory cells**: Neurons that release hormones into general circulation.
- **Receives neural input** from the hypothalamus.

#### Anterior Pituitary
- Begins as part of the same embryonic tissue as the **roof of the mouth**; pinches off and migrates upward during development.
- Releases **tropic hormones** — this part qualifies as the true "master gland."
- Receives **NO neural input** whatsoever from the hypothalamus or any other neural structure.

### Female Gonadal Hormone Levels Are Cyclic; Male Gonadal Hormone Levels Are Steady

- In women: gonadal and gonadotropic hormones cycle every **~28 days** (controls menstrual cycle).
- In men: gonadal and gonadotropic hormones change little from day to day — **steady state**.

#### Harris's Pituitary Transplant Studies (1950s)

> **KEY STUDY — Geoffrey Harris (1950s)**
> - **Method**: Transplanted pituitaries between male and female rats.
>   - Female cycling pituitary transplanted into male → became steady-state.
>   - Male steady-state pituitary transplanted into female → began cycling.
> - **Finding**: Anterior pituitaries are NOT inherently cyclical (female) or steady-state (male).
> - **Conclusion**: Pattern of hormone release is controlled by some other part of the body — the hypothalamus.
> - Reference: Raisman (1997).

### Neural Control of the Pituitary

- Seasonal light-dark cycle triggers breeding-related hormone changes in seasonally breeding animals.
- Reversing lighting conditions reversed breeding seasons → **visual input** to the nervous system controls tropic hormone release.
- The **hypothalamus** is the regulator of the anterior pituitary (confirmed by stimulation and lesion experiments).
- Puzzle: The anterior pituitary receives NO neural input from the hypothalamus — how does the hypothalamus control it?

### Control of the Anterior and Posterior Pituitary by the Hypothalamus

Two different mechanisms:

#### Posterior Pituitary Control
1. Vasopressin and oxytocin synthesized in cell bodies in paraventricular and supraoptic nuclei of hypothalamus.
2. Transported along axons to terminals in posterior pituitary.
3. Released into bloodstream when action potentials arrive.

#### Anterior Pituitary Control
- Harris (1955) hypothesis: Anterior pituitary hormones are regulated by hypothalamic hormones.
- Two supporting findings:
  1. **Hypothalamopituitary portal system**: Hypothalamic capillaries feed portal veins down the pituitary stalk into anterior pituitary capillaries.
     - **Portal vein**: A vein connecting one capillary network with another.
  2. Cutting portal veins disrupts anterior pituitary hormone release until veins regenerate (Harris, 1955).

### Discovery of Hypothalamic Releasing Hormones

- **Releasing hormones**: Hypothalamic hormones that stimulate release of specific anterior pituitary hormones.
- **Release-inhibiting factors**: Hypothalamic hormones that inhibit specific anterior pituitary hormone release.

> **KEY DISCOVERY — Guillemin and Schally (late 1960s)**
> - Guillemin and colleagues: Isolated **thyrotropin-releasing hormone** from sheep hypothalamus.
> - Schally and colleagues: Isolated the same hormone from pig hypothalamus.
> - **Thyrotropin-releasing hormone**: Triggers thyrotropin release from anterior pituitary → stimulates thyroid hormone release.
> - **Nobel Prize 1977**: Guillemin and Schally.
> - Schally's group subsequently isolated **gonadotropin-releasing hormone** (Schally, Kastin, & Arimura, **1971**).
>   - Stimulates release of BOTH anterior pituitary gonadotropins: **FSH** and **LH**.
> - All hypothalamic releasing hormones and tropic hormones are **peptides**.

#### Two Anterior Pituitary Gonadotropins:
1. **FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone)**
2. **LH (luteinizing hormone)**

### Regulation of Hormone Levels

Three types of regulatory signals:

#### 1. Regulation by Neural Signals
- All endocrine glands EXCEPT the anterior pituitary are directly regulated by signals from the nervous system.
- Brain glands: regulated by cerebral neurons. Extra-CNS glands: innervated by both sympathetic and parasympathetic branches of the ANS (often with opposite effects).
- Key principle: **Hormone release can be regulated by experience** (e.g., increasing daylight triggers sex hormone release in spring breeders).
- Implication: Hormonal and experiential explanations of behavior are NOT mutually exclusive — they may be different aspects of the same mechanism.

#### 2. Regulation by Hormonal Signals (Feedback)
- Circulating hormones feed back to hypothalamus, pituitary, and other brain sites.
- Function: maintenance of **stable blood hormone levels**.
- High gonadal hormones → **decrease** subsequent gonadal hormone release (negative feedback).
- Low levels → **increase** release.

#### 3. Regulation by Nonhormonal Chemicals
- Glucose, calcium, and sodium blood levels influence specific hormones.
- Example: High blood glucose → increased insulin release from pancreas → insulin lowers blood glucose.

### Pulsatile Hormone Release

- Hormones are released in **pulses** (Armstrong et al., 2009; Khadra & Li, 2006).
- Discharged several times per day in large surges, each lasting **no more than a few minutes**.
- Hormone levels regulated by changes in **frequency and duration** of pulses.
- Consequence: Large minute-to-minute fluctuations in circulating levels (Koolhaas, Schuurman, & Wierpkema, 1980).
- "Steady" male hormone levels = no major systematic day-to-day changes, not literally constant.

### Summary Model of Gonadal Endocrine Regulation (Figure 13.5)

Regulatory cascade (in order):
1. **Brain** (neural signals)
2. **Hypothalamus** releases gonadotropin-releasing hormone into hypothalamopituitary portal system
3. **Anterior pituitary** releases gonadotropin into general circulation
4. **Gonads** release estrogens, androgens, and progestins
5. Gonadal hormones **feed back** (positive or negative) to pituitary and hypothalamus
6. Gonadal hormones act on the **brain** to influence behavior

## 13.2 Hormones and Sexual Development of the Body

- Humans are **dimorphic** — two standard models: female and male.
- **Key principle**: We are all genetically programmed to develop female bodies. Genetic males develop male bodies ONLY because the female program is overridden.
  - Directly challenges MAMAWAWA (no two parallel but opposite programs; only one program with an override mechanism).

### Fetal Hormones and Development of Reproductive Organs

#### Gonads (Figure 13.6)

- At **6 weeks after fertilization**: Every fetus (regardless of genetic sex) has identical **primordial gonads**.
  - **Primordial gonads**: Gonadal structures present at the beginning of development ("primordial" = existing at the beginning).
  - Each has:
    - **Cortex** (outer covering): Potential to develop into an **ovary**.
    - **Medulla** (internal core): Potential to develop into a **testis**.

- At **6 weeks after conception**:
  - **Sry gene** on the Y chromosome triggers synthesis of **Sry protein** (Arnold, 2004; Wu et al., 2009).
  - Sry protein causes the **medulla** to develop into a **testis**.
  - In the **absence of Sry protein**: Cortical cells automatically develop into **ovaries** (default program; no female counterpart to Sry protein).

- Experimental demonstrations:
  - Inject Sry protein into genetic female fetus at 6 weeks → genetic female with **testes**.
  - Inject Sry-blocking drugs into male fetus → genetic male with **ovaries**.

#### Internal Reproductive Ducts (Figure 13.7)

- At **6 weeks after fertilization**: ALL fetuses have TWO complete sets of reproductive ducts:

| System | Potential development | Structures |
|---|---|---|
| **Wolffian system** (male) | Male reproductive ducts | Seminal vesicles; Vas deferens |
| **Müllerian system** (female) | Female reproductive ducts | Uterus; Upper vagina; Fallopian tubes |

- In the **third month of male fetal development**:
  - Testes secrete **testosterone** → stimulates **Wolffian system** development.
  - Testes secrete **Müllerian-inhibiting substance** → causes **Müllerian system** to degenerate + testes to **descend into the scrotum**.
  - **Scrotum**: Sac holding testes outside the body cavity.
  - It is **testosterone** (NOT sex chromosomes) that triggers Wolffian development.
  - Proof: Genetic females injected with testosterone during the appropriate fetal period develop male reproductive ducts along with their female ones.

- **Differentiation of female internal ducts**:
  - NOT under control of ovarian hormones (ovaries are almost completely **inactive** during fetal development).
  - Müllerian system develops in ANY fetus **not exposed to testicular hormones** during the critical period.
  - Normal female fetuses, ovariectomized female fetuses, AND orchidectomized male fetuses all develop female reproductive ducts (Jost, 1972).

#### Terminology

- **Ovariectomy**: Surgical removal of the ovaries.
- **Orchidectomy**: Surgical removal of the testes (Greek: orchis = testicle).
- **Gonadectomy (castration)**: Surgical removal of gonads (either ovaries or testes).

#### External Reproductive Organs (Figure 13.8)

- Key difference: Both male and female external genitals develop from the **same bipotential precursor** (unlike internal organs which have two separate precursors).

**Bipotential precursor** at ~2 months of pregnancy — four parts:

| Precursor Part | Male Development | Female Development |
|---|---|---|
| **Glans** | Head of the penis | Clitoris |
| **Urethral folds** | Fuse to form urethra | Labia minora |
| **Lateral bodies** | Shaft of the penis | Hood of the clitoris |
| **Labioscrotal swellings** | Scrotum | Labia majora |

- Control: **Testosterone present** → male external genitals. **Testosterone absent** → female external genitals (default).

### Puberty: Hormones and Development of Secondary Sex Characteristics

- During childhood: low gonadal hormone levels; immature reproductive organs; little sex difference in general appearance.
- **Puberty**: Transitional period between childhood and adulthood:
  - Fertility achieved.
  - Adolescent growth spurt occurs.
  - **Secondary sex characteristics** develop (features other than reproductive organs distinguishing sexually mature men and women).

#### Hormonal Changes at Puberty

- Increase in anterior pituitary hormone release (Grumbach, 2002):
  1. **Growth hormone** (only anterior pituitary hormone without a gland as primary target): Acts directly on bone and muscle → **pubertal growth spurt**.
  2. Increased **gonadotropic hormone** + **ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)** → gonads and adrenal cortex increase sex hormone release → maturation of genitals and secondary sex characteristics.

- General principle:
  - Pubertal males: **androgens > estrogens** → **masculinization**.
  - Pubertal females: **estrogens predominate** → **feminization**.
  - Individuals castrated prior to puberty do not sexually mature without replacement injections.

#### Challenge to MAMAWAWA at Puberty

- **Androstenedione**: An androgen released primarily by the **adrenal cortex** — responsible for **pubic and axillary hair growth in females**.
  - Male pubic hair pattern: pyramid.
  - Female pubic hair pattern: inverted pyramid.
  - Calling androgens "male hormones" is untenable when one drives female pubic hair growth.

#### Puberty Timing
- Current North America/Europe: Girls begin at ~**10.5 years**; boys at ~**11.5 years**.
- 150 years ago: Onset was at **15-16 years**.
- Acceleration attributed to improved dietary, medical, and socioeconomic conditions.

## 13.3 Hormones and Sexual Development of Brain and Behavior

### Sex Differences in the Brain

- Male brains average about **15% larger** than female brains.
- **Sexual dimorphisms**: Male-female structural differences in the brain.
- Documented in: volumes of nuclei and fiber tracts; numbers/types of neural and glial cells; numbers/types of synapses.
- Studied primarily in nonhuman mammals but many documented in humans (Arnold, 2003; Cahill, 2005, 2006; de Vries & Södersten, 2009).
- Caveat: We still do NOT understand how any of these anatomical differences influence behavior.

### First Discovery of a Sex Difference in Mammalian Brain Function

#### Pfeiffer's Experiments (1936)

> **KEY STUDY — Pfeiffer (1936)**
> - **Method**: Neonatal rats (both sexes) — gonadectomized and/or received gonad transplants.
> - **Findings**:
>   1. Gonadectomizing neonatal rats of EITHER sex → adults developed the **female cyclic pattern** of gonadotropin release.
>   2. Transplanting testes into gonadectomized or intact female neonatal rats → adults developed the **steady male pattern**.
>   3. Transplanting ovaries → no effect on the pattern of hormone release.
> - **Pfeiffer's (incorrect) conclusion**: Presence/absence of testicular hormones influenced the development of the **pituitary**.
> - **Correct interpretation** (1960s): These experiments demonstrated that perinatal androgens override the preprogrammed cyclic female pattern from the **hypothalamus** (not the pituitary — Pfeiffer didn't know about hypothalamic control of the anterior pituitary).
> - Reference: Harris & Levine (1965).

- **Perinatal**: Around the time of birth.
- Key principle: The female cyclic pattern of gonadotropin release develops UNLESS overridden by perinatal testosterone.

### Neuroplasticity and the Aromatization Hypothesis

#### Aromatization

- **Aromatase**: Enzyme that converts testosterone to estradiol.
- **Aromatization**: Process of converting testosterone to estradiol (Balthazart & Ball, 1998).

#### The Aromatization Hypothesis

- **Aromatization hypothesis**: Perinatal testosterone does NOT directly masculinize the brain; the brain is masculinized by **estradiol aromatized from perinatal testosterone**.
- Counterintuitive: The alleged "female hormone" estradiol masculinizes the male brain.
- Evidence (rats and mice):
  1. Early **estradiol injections** produce masculinizing effects on the brain.
  2. Testosterone administered WITH **aromatization-blocking agents** does NOT masculinize the brain.
  3. Androgens that **cannot be aromatized** (e.g., **dihydrotestosterone**) do NOT masculinize the brain.

#### Alpha Fetoprotein — The Protective Mechanism

- Problem: If estradiol masculinizes the brain, why aren't female fetuses masculinized by maternal estradiol in the blood?
- Answer: **Alpha fetoprotein** — present in rat blood during the **perinatal period**.
  - Deactivates circulating estradiol by binding to it (Bakker et al., 2006; Bakker & Baum, 2007; De Mees et al., 2006).
  - Protects the female brain.
- How does estradiol still masculinize the MALE brain?
  - **Testosterone** is immune to alpha fetoprotein → travels from testes to brain cells unaffected.
  - In brain cells, testosterone is converted to estradiol by aromatase.
  - Alpha fetoprotein does NOT readily penetrate the **blood-brain barrier** → estradiol is not broken down in the brain.

### Modern Perspectives on Sexual Differentiation of Mammalian Brains

Current view: **No single mechanism** accounts for all sexual dimorphisms in mammalian brains. Key findings:

1. **Region-specificity**: Aromatase is found in only a few rat brain areas (e.g., hypothalamus); aromatization is critical only in those areas (Ball & Balthazart, 2006; Balthazart & Ball, 2006).

2. **Species differences**: Aromatization plays a less prominent role in **primates** than in rats and mice (Zuloaga et al., 2008).

3. **Developmental timing varies**: Many sex differences develop at **puberty**, not perinatally — ignored by early theories (Ahmed et al., 2008; Sisk & Zehr, 2005; Bakker & Baum, 2007).

4. **Sex chromosomes act independently of hormones**: Different gene expression patterns exist in male vs. female mouse brains **before** gonads become functional (Dewing et al., 2003; Arnold, 2009; Jazon & Cahill, 2010).

5. **Estradiol plays an active role in female brain development**: The female program is NOT simply passive default. Knockout mice without the estradiol receptor gene do NOT display a normal female pattern of brain development (Bakker & Baum, 2007).

**Summary**: The "female default" model explains differentiation of reproductive organs well but falters for brain differentiation.

### Perinatal Hormones and Behavioral Development

Most research based on the female default model, assessing perinatal testosterone effects on reproductive behaviors in lab animals.

#### Phoenix et al. (1959) — Masculinization and Defeminization

> **KEY STUDY — Phoenix, Goy, Gerall, and Young (1959)**
> - **Method**: Injected pregnant guinea pigs with testosterone. Female offspring were ovariectomized at birth. At maturity, injected with testosterone and assessed copulatory behavior.
> - **Findings**:
>   1. Females with perinatal testosterone exposure displayed more **male-like mounting behavior** in adulthood.
>   2. When given progesterone and estradiol and mounted by males, they displayed less **lordosis**.
> - **Conclusion**: Perinatal testosterone both **masculinizes** and **defeminizes** adult copulatory behavior in genetic females.

#### Grady, Phoenix, and Young (1965) — Feminization and Demasculinization

> **KEY STUDY — Grady, Phoenix, and Young (1965)**
> - **Method**: Male rats castrated shortly after birth; tested as adults after testosterone or estrogen+progesterone treatment.
> - **Findings**:
>   1. Neonatally castrated males failed to display the normal male copulatory pattern (mounting, intromission, ejaculation) when given testosterone as adults.
>   2. When given estrogen and progesterone as adults, they exhibited more **lordosis** than uncastrated controls.
> - **Conclusion**: Lack of early testosterone both **feminizes** and **demasculinizes** male copulatory behavior.

#### Key Behavioral Terms

- **Lordosis**: Intromission-facilitating arched-back posture signaling female rodent receptivity.
- **Intromission**: Penile insertion.
- **Ejaculation**: Ejection of sperm.
- **Proceptive behaviors**: Female solicitation behaviors (hopping, darting, ear wiggling in rats).
- **Mounting behavior**: Male-typical copulatory behavior involving mounting a partner.

#### Timing of Perinatal Testosterone Effects

- Ability of single testosterone injections to masculinize and defeminize the rat brain is restricted to the **first 11 days after birth**.
- Aromatization is important for both defeminization and masculinization in **rodents** (Goy & McEwen, 1980; Shapiro, Levine, & Adler, 1980).
- In **monkeys**: aromatization does NOT appear critical (Wallen, 2005).

#### Additional Behavioral Effects of Perinatal Testosterone in Animals

- Disrupts **proceptive behaviors** (hopping, darting, ear wiggling) in female rats.
- Increases **aggressiveness** in female mice.
- Disrupts **maternal behavior** in female rats.
- Increases **rough social play** in female monkeys and rats.

#### Human Evidence (Correlational Studies)

- Ethical considerations prohibit experimental studies in humans.
- Cohen-Bendahan, van de Beek, and Berenbaum (2005) review:
  - Weight of evidence: prenatal androgen exposure contributes to differences in **interests, spatial ability, and aggressiveness** between men and women.
  - No convincing evidence that differences in prenatal androgen exposure account for behavioral differences **within** the same sex.

#### Critical Conceptual Point — Four Separate Dimensions

- **Masculinization** does NOT equal defeminization.
- **Feminization** does NOT equal demasculinization.
- Male and female behaviors can **co-exist** in the same individual.
- Evidence:
  - Male behaviors (e.g., mounting) observed in females of many mammalian species.
  - Female behaviors (e.g., lordosis) observed in males.
  - Medial preoptic area lesions abolish male reproductive behaviors in both male and female rats WITHOUT affecting female behaviors (Singer, 1968).

## 13.4 Three Cases of Exceptional Human Sexual Development

Purpose: "The exception proves the rule" — in the Latin sense of *probare* (to test). Each case provides a test of existing theories of hormonal sexual development.

> Each of the three subjects was male in some respects and female in others — each a challenge to MAMAWAWA.

### Case 1: The Case of Anne S. — Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (CAIS)

- **Patient**: Anne S., 26-year-old female, sought treatment for lack of menstruation and pain during sexual intercourse (Jones & Park, 1971).
- **Physical examination**:
  - Sparse, fine pubic and axillary hair.
  - Normal external genitals.
  - Vagina only **4 centimeters long**.
  - Underdeveloped uterus.
- **Diagnosis**: Genetic sex was **male** (XY).
  - Evidence 1: Oral cells were **XY type**.
  - Evidence 2: Internal exam revealed **internalized testes** and **no ovaries**.
  - Evidence 3: Hormone levels were those of a **male**.

#### Mechanism of CAIS

- **Complete androgen insensitivity syndrome (CAIS)**: Mutation to the **androgen receptor gene** rendering androgen receptors totally unresponsive (Fink et al., 1999; Goldstein, 2000).
- Prevalence: ~**5 in 100,000 male births**.
- Development:
  - Testes released normal male androgens → body could NOT respond (no functional androgen receptors).
  - Development proceeded as if **no androgens were released** → female program unopposed.
  - External genitals, brain, and behavior developed along **female lines**.
  - Testes could not descend (no scrotum).
  - Testes still released **Müllerian-inhibiting substance** → short vagina, underdeveloped uterus (no normal female internal ducts).
  - At puberty: Testes released estrogens → **feminized the body** (unopposed by androgens).
  - Adrenal androstenedione could NOT stimulate pubic and axillary hair growth (no androgen receptors).

#### Behavioral Profile

- Gender identity, sexual orientation, interests, and cognitive abilities: **typically female** (Cohen-Bendahan et al., 2005).

#### Medical Ethics Issue

- Anne's doctor did NOT disclose her genetic sex. Vagina surgically enlarged; counseled to adopt.
- Women with **partial** androgenic insensitivity recommend **full disclosure**.

### Case 2: The Case of the Little Girl Who Grew into a Boy — Adrenogenital Syndrome

- **Patient**: "Elaine" — born with ambiguous external genitals; raised as a girl; at puberty developed male secondary sex characteristics.
- **Treatment**: Surgical (increase vaginal size, decrease clitoris size) + hormonal (suppress androgens). Outcome: Developed into an attractive woman (narrow hips, husky voice being residual signs); married 15 years later (Money & Ehrhardt, 1972).
- **Diagnosis**: **Adrenogenital syndrome**.

#### Adrenogenital Syndrome

- **Most common disorder of sexual development**: ~**1 in 10,000**.
- Cause: **Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH)**:
  - Congenital deficiency in **cortisol** release from adrenal cortex.
  - Compensatory adrenal hyperactivity → **excessive release of adrenal androgens**.
- Effect on males: Little except **accelerating puberty**.
- Effect on genetic females:
  - Born with **enlarged clitoris** and **partially fused labia**.
  - Gonads and internal ducts usually **normal** (adrenal androgens released too late to stimulate Wolffian system).

#### Management

- Diagnosed at birth → surgical correction of external genitals + **cortisol administration**.
- Following early treatment: physically normal females; menstruation onset may be **later than normal**.

#### Behavioral Profile of Treated Adrenogenital Females (Adolescence)

- More **tomboyishness**, greater **strength**, more **aggression**.
- Prefer **boys' clothes and toys**; play mainly with boys; daydream about careers rather than motherhood.
- (Collaer et al., 2008; Hines, 2003; Matthews et al., 2009.)
- Behavior usually within the **range of normal female behavior**.

#### Romantic/Sexual Preferences as Adults

- Most are **heterosexual**.
- Some studies: increased tendency toward **bisexuality or homosexuality**; less involvement in heterosexual relationships (Gooren, 2006).
- Complicating factor: Differences may reflect experiential influences from ambiguous genitalia rather than direct hormonal effects.

#### Pre-Cortisol Era (before 1950)

- Untreated females raised as either boys or girls; pubertal development was unpredictable.
- Some raised as boys were feminized at puberty; some raised as girls were masculinized — with devastating emotional consequences.

### Case 3: The Case of the Twin Who Lost His Penis — Ablatio Penis

- **Patient**: Male identical twin whose penis was accidentally destroyed during circumcision at **7 months of age**.
- **John Money's recommendation**:
  - Castrate the child.
  - Surgically create an artificial vagina.
  - Raise the child as a girl.
  - Administer estrogen at puberty.
- Parents followed this advice.

#### Money's 1975 Report

- **Money (1975)**: Reported at age 12 that the patient ("Joan") had developed as a **normal female**.
- Seen as strong support for **social-learning theory of sexual identity**.
- Argued that being raised as a girl overrode the effects of male genes and prenatal androgens.
- Genetically identical twin brother served as control.

#### Long-Term Follow-Up: Diamond & Sigmundson (1997)

> **KEY CASE — Diamond & Sigmundson (1997) — John/Joan (David Reimer)**
> - Despite female genitalia and female rearing, **John/Joan developed along male lines**.
> - From early age: preferred **boys' activities**; little interest in conventionally female activities.
> - Age 4: Put shaving cream on face when watching father shave; rejected lipstick.
> - "Suspected I was a boy **from the second grade**."
> - Often tried to urinate while **standing**; sometimes went to the boys' lavatory.
> - Teased by other girls; retaliated violently (expelled from school).
> - **Rejected estrogen regimen** at age 12; hated developing breasts; refused to wear a bra.
> - At **age 14**: Decided to live as a **male** ("John").
> - Father revealed entire history: "All of a sudden everything clicked. For the first time I understood who and what I was."
> - Requested androgen treatment, **mastectomy**, and **phalloplasty** (surgical creation of a penis).
> - Married at **age 25**; adopted wife's children; strictly heterosexual.
> - Ejaculation and orgasm returned after androgen treatment.
> - Early castration permanently eliminated **reproductive capacity**.
> - Cooperated in writing ***As Nature Made Him*** (Colapinto, 2000).
> - Real name: **David Reimer**.
> - **May 4, 2004**: David Reimer committed **suicide**.
> - **Reiner (1997) conclusion**: The **brain** (not the genitals) determines the course of psychosocial development.

#### Clinical Implication

- Surgically modifying a person's sex at birth should be **curtailed**.
- Irrevocable treatments should await **early puberty** and emergence of the patient's **sexual identity and sexual attraction**.

## 13.5 Effects of Gonadal Hormones on Adults

### Male Reproduction-Related Behavior and Testosterone

- Orchidectomy demonstrates the role of gonadal hormones in activating male sexual behavior.

#### Bremer's Study (1959)

> **KEY STUDY — Bremer (1959)**
> - **Method**: Reviewed cases of **157 orchidectomized Norwegians** (many were sex offenders who agreed to castration to reduce prison sentences).
> - **Findings**:
>   1. Orchidectomy leads to **reduced sexual interest and behavior**.
>   2. Rate and degree of loss are **variable**:
>      - ~Half became completely **asexual** within a few weeks.
>      - Others lost erectile ability but retained some sexual interest and pleasure.
>      - A few continued to copulate somewhat less enthusiastically throughout the study.
>   3. Physical changes: Reduced trunk/extremity/facial hair; fat deposition on hips and chest; softened skin; reduced strength.
>   4. Of 102 sex offenders: only **3 reconvicted** of sex offenses.
> - **Conclusion**: Bremer recommended castration as an effective **treatment of last resort** for male sex offenders.

#### Persistence of Sexual Activity Post-Orchidectomy

- Some men remain sexually active for months despite testicular hormones being cleared within days.
- Possible explanation: **Adrenal androgens** may maintain sexual activity in some castrated men (hypothesis only — no direct evidence).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **MAMAWAWA** is the "men-are-men-and-women-are-women assumption" — the fundamentally flawed tendency to view male/female as mutually exclusive opposite categories. Both testes and ovaries produce androgens AND estrogens; calling them "male/female hormones" is misleading.

2. **Default sex development is female**: In the absence of Sry protein (and subsequently in the absence of androgens), all embryos develop female gonads, internal ducts, and external genitals. Male development requires active hormonal override at multiple stages.

3. **Sry gene/protein** on the Y chromosome triggers testis development from the gonadal medulla at **6 weeks post-conception**. Without it, the cortex develops into ovaries. No female counterpart to Sry protein exists.

4. **Third month of male fetal development**: Testes secrete BOTH **testosterone** (Wolffian system development) AND **Müllerian-inhibiting substance** (Müllerian degeneration + testicular descent into scrotum).

5. **External genitals** develop from a **single bipotential precursor** (unlike internal organs, which have two separate precursors). The same four structures form different organs depending on testosterone presence.

6. **Harris's pituitary transplant studies (1950s)**: Showed anterior pituitaries are not inherently male or female — cyclicity vs. steady-state is controlled by the **hypothalamus**, not the pituitary.

7. **Guillemin and Schally** received the **Nobel Prize in 1977** for isolating thyrotropin-releasing hormone. Schally's group isolated **GnRH in 1971** — GnRH stimulates BOTH FSH AND LH from the anterior pituitary.

8. **Aromatization hypothesis**: Perinatal testosterone masculinizes the rat brain by being converted to **estradiol** (not testosterone itself) by aromatase. **Alpha fetoprotein** protects female brains by binding circulating estradiol; it does NOT cross the blood-brain barrier.

9. **Phoenix et al. (1959)**: Perinatal testosterone masculinizes AND defeminizes female guinea pig behavior. **Grady et al. (1965)**: Neonatal castration feminizes AND demasculinizes male rat behavior. Masculinization and defeminization are **separate, independent dimensions**.

10. **Complete androgen insensitivity syndrome (CAIS)**: Mutation in androgen receptor gene; XY individuals develop fully female external phenotype; testes present internally; typically female gender identity and behavior. Prevalence: **~5 in 100,000 male births**.

11. **Adrenogenital syndrome (congenital adrenal hyperplasia)**: **Most common disorder of sexual development** (~1 in 10,000); cortisol deficiency → excess adrenal androgens → virilization of genetic females (enlarged clitoris, partially fused labia). Adrenogenital females show more tomboyish behavior, greater spatial ability, and more aggression.

12. **David Reimer (ablatio penis case)**: Despite female rearing and surgical feminization, developed along male lines. Reiner's (1997) conclusion: The **brain** (not genitals) determines psychosocial sexual development. Suicide: **May 4, 2004**.

13. **Androstenedione** (adrenal androgen) is responsible for pubic and axillary hair growth in **females** — a key example against calling androgens "male hormones."

14. **Pulsatile hormone release**: Hormones discharged several times daily in surges lasting **no more than a few minutes**. "Steady" male hormone levels = no systematic day-to-day change, not literally constant minute-to-minute.

15. **Bremer (1959)**: Orchidectomy reduces sexual interest/behavior variably; ~half became completely asexual within weeks; of 102 sex offenders, only **3 were reconvicted**. Adrenal androgens hypothesized (but not proven) to explain residual sexual activity post-orchidectomy.
