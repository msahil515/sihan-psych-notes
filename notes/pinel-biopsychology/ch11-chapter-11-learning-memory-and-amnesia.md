# Chapter 11: Learning, Memory, and Amnesia

This chapter examines the neural bases of learning and memory through the lens of human amnesic patients and animal models. Core themes include the distinction between explicit and implicit memory, the role of specific brain structures (hippocampus, rhinal cortex, medial diencephalon, basal forebrain) in different memory types, and the process of memory consolidation. The chapter moves from landmark clinical cases (H.M., K.C., R.B., N.A.) through experimental animal models to theories of hippocampal function.

---

## 11.1 Amnesic Effects of Bilateral Medial Temporal Lobectomy

### Background: The Case of H.M.

- **H.M.** (Henry Molaison, identity revealed posthumously) underwent **bilateral medial temporal lobectomy** in **1953** to relieve intractable epilepsy performed by surgeon **William Beecher Scoville**.
- Surgery removed large portions of both **medial temporal lobes**, including much of the **hippocampus**, **amygdala**, and adjacent cortex.
- Post-surgery: intellectual abilities remained largely intact; mild **retrograde amnesia** for events ~2 years before surgery; remote memories (childhood) reasonably normal.
- **Short-term anterograde memory** remained normal: **digit span** = 6 digits (Wickelgren, 1968).
- Severe and almost total inability to form new **long-term memories**: once attention shifted from a new experience, it was lost forever.
- H.M. lived in a nursing home for many years, did crossword puzzles (progress preserved in writing), and died in **2008**.

> Key quote (Milner, 1965): "Forgetting occurred the instant the patient's focus of attention shifted."

> Key quote (Milner, Corkin, & Teuber, 1968): "Every day is alone in itself, whatever enjoyment I've had, and whatever sorrow I've had."

---

### Formal Assessment of H.M.'s Anterograde Amnesia: Seven Tests

#### Test 1: Digit Span + 1 Test

- **Purpose:** Classic test of verbal **long-term memory**.
- **Procedure:** Subject repeats a digit sequence; each successful repetition adds 1 digit to the end.
- **H.M.'s result:** After 25 trials, could not repeat an 8-digit sequence.
- **Normal performance:** ~15 digits after 25 trials (Drachman & Arbit, 1966).
- **Conclusion:** Severe deficit in verbal long-term memory.

#### Test 2: Block-Tapping Memory-Span Test

- **Researcher:** Milner (1971).
- **Procedure:** Neuropsychologist touches a sequence of 9 blocks on a board; patient repeats the sequence. +1 version adds one block each time.
- **H.M.'s result:** Block-tapping span of 5 (normal range); could not learn a 6-block sequence even after 12 repetitions.
- **Conclusion:** **Global amnesia** — amnesia not restricted to verbal material; extends to all sensory modalities.

#### Test 3: Mirror-Drawing Test

- **Researcher:** Milner (1965).
- **Procedure:** Draw a line within a star-shaped target while watching the hand in a mirror; 10 trials/day for 3 consecutive days; errors (going outside boundaries) recorded.
- **H.M.'s result:** Performance **improved** across 3 days (indicating retention), but H.M. could not recall ever having seen the task before.
- **Significance:** **First indication** that H.M.'s anterograde amnesia did not extend to all long-term memories — demonstrated **implicit memory** without conscious awareness.

> **KEY STUDY — Mirror Drawing (Milner, 1965):** Method: star-tracing with mirror reflection. Finding: Skill improved across days without conscious recollection. Implication: Long-term implicit memory preserved in amnesia.

#### Test 4: Rotary-Pursuit Test

- **Researcher:** Corkin (1968).
- **Procedure:** Subject keeps tip of a stylus in contact with a rotating target on a revolving turntable; time-on-target recorded. 9 daily practice sessions.
- **H.M.'s result:** Performance improved significantly; retained over a 7-day interval; claimed each day to have never seen the task.
- **Conclusion:** Confirmed implicit sensorimotor learning intact.

#### Test 5: Incomplete-Pictures Test

- **Origin:** Gollin (1960).
- **Procedure:** Five sets of fragmented drawings of 20 objects. Set 1 = most fragmented; Set 5 = complete. Subject tries to identify objects starting from Set 1 (sketchiest); unrecognized items presented in Set 2, etc.
- **H.M.'s result:** Showed improved performance on retest 1 hour later (recognized more fragmented versions); no conscious awareness of prior exposure.
- **Significance:** Extended implicit memory beyond sensorimotor tasks — demonstrated implicit memory for **nonsensorimotor** material.

#### Test 6: Pavlovian Conditioning (Eye-Blink)

- **Researcher:** Woodruff-Pak (1993).
- **Procedure:** Tone (CS) followed by puff of air to eye (UCS) until tone alone elicited eye-blink (CR).
- **H.M.'s result:** Learned conditioning (albeit at retarded rate); retained conditioned response almost perfectly **2 years** later; had no conscious recollection of training.
- **Conclusion:** Classical conditioning = implicit; survives medial temporal lobe damage.

#### Test 7: Repetition Priming Tests (later development)

- **Procedure:** Participants examine a list of words (not asked to memorize). Later shown word fragments (e.g., _ O B _ _ E R) and asked to complete them.
- **Normal controls:** Perform well due to prior exposure.
- **Amnesic patients:** Often perform equally well despite no explicit memory of seeing the list. (Answer in example: "LOBSTER.")
- **Conclusion:** **Repetition priming** reflects intact implicit memory; became a standard tool for assessing implicit long-term memory.

### Three Major Scientific Contributions of H.M.'s Case

#### Contribution 1: Medial Temporal Lobes and Memory Localization

- Challenged the then-prevailing view (associated with Karl Lashley) that memory functions are **diffusely and equivalently distributed** throughout the brain.
- Showed medial temporal lobes play a **specific, critical role** in memory.
- Spawned massive research effort on **mnemonic** (memory-related) functions of the hippocampus.

#### Contribution 2: Dissociation of Short-term, Long-term, and Remote Memory

- Bilateral medial temporal lobectomy abolished ability to form **certain kinds of long-term memories** while:
  - Leaving **short-term memory** intact (digit span normal).
  - Leaving **remote memory** (distant past) largely intact.
- Supported the theory of **different modes of storage** for STM, LTM, and remote memory.
- H.M.'s specific problem: **memory consolidation** — the translation of short-term memories into long-term memories (Nee et al., 2008; Squire & Bayley, 2007).

#### Contribution 3: Discovery of Implicit Memory

- First case to reveal an amnesic patient can demonstrate memory through improved performance while claiming no recollection.
- Created two distinct long-term memory categories:
  - **Explicit memories:** Conscious long-term memories.
  - **Implicit memories:** Long-term memories demonstrated by improved performance without conscious awareness.
- This distinction proved **generally relevant**: many amnesic patients lose explicit memory while retaining implicit memory.

---

### Medial Temporal Lobe Amnesia

- **Definition:** Neuropsychological condition with a profile of mnemonic deficits similar to H.M., including preserved intellectual functioning and evidence of medial temporal lobe damage.
- Core symptom: difficulty forming **explicit long-term memories** while retaining ability to form **implicit long-term memories** of the same experiences (Eichenbaum, 1999).
- Assessment of implicit memory now plays a major role in study of human memory (Schacter, Dobbins, & Schnyer, 2004).

#### Clinical Implication: Why Two Memory Systems?

- Implicit system likely evolved first (simpler, does not involve consciousness).
- Advantage of having an explicit system: **flexibility**.
- Evidence: In two studies (Reber, Knowlton, & Squire, 1996 — amnesic humans; Buckley & Gaffan, 1998 — amnesic monkeys with medial temporal lobe lesions), amnesic subjects learned implicit tasks as well as controls, but failed when asked to use implicit knowledge in a **different context or way**.
- Conclusion: Explicit memory systems evolved to provide **flexible use of information**.

---

### Semantic and Episodic Memories

- Explicit long-term memories fall into two varieties:
  - **Semantic memories:** Explicit memories for general facts or information (e.g., knowing what a dog is, historical facts, language).
  - **Episodic memories:** Explicit memories for particular events (episodes) of one's own life — also called **autobiographical memory** (Hampton & Schwartz, 2004; Rubin, 2006; Patterson, Nestor, & Rogers, 2007).
- People with medial temporal lobe amnesia have **particular difficulty with episodic memories**.
- They can remember what friends are like, a language learned, world events (semantic) but cannot remember having lunch, attending a specific lecture (episodic).
- Episodic memory has been likened to **mentally traveling back in time** (Tulving).

#### Endel Tulving

- Major force in research on the **semantic-episodic dichotomy** (Tulving, 2002).
- Proposed that episodic memory involves **mental time travel**.

---

### The Case of K.C. — The Man Who Can't Time Travel

- Motorcycle accident in **1981**; suffered **diffuse brain damage**, including medial temporal lobes.
- Patient of **Endel Tulving**.
- **Preserved:** General intelligence, language use, concentration, organ/chess/card games, reasoning, mathematics, history, science, geography, facts about his early life (birth date, schools attended, car models and colors owned).
- **Impaired:** Cannot recall **a single personal event** for more than a minute or two, covering his entire life — pure **episodic amnesia**.
- Effective in conversations using semantic memory; memory problems less obvious to others.
- Understands time but **cannot time travel** into past or future.
- Cannot imagine what he will be doing for the rest of the day, week, or his life — future episodic imagination also absent.
- Demonstrates: Semantic memory can function normally even with severe episodic amnesia.

---

### Evolutionary Perspective: Early-Life Medial Temporal Lobe Damage

- **Vargha-Khadem et al. (1997):** Followed three patients with **bilateral medial temporal lobe damage** from early in life.
- Despite poor episodic memory (could remember few daily experiences), all three progressed through **mainstream schools** and acquired reasonable language ability and factual knowledge (semantic memory).
- Episodic memory did **not improve** with development despite academic success (de Haan et al., 2006).
- Implication: Semantic and episodic memory systems are partially dissociable developmentally.

---

### The Case of the Clever Neuropsychologist: Spotting Episodic Memory Deficits

- Episodic memory deficits are difficult to spot because:
  1. Neuropsychologists typically have no way of knowing the true events of a patient's life.
  2. Patients become very effective at providing **semantic answers to episodic questions**.
- Example exchange (paraphrased): Patient who was a history teacher could discuss general topics (semantic) but could not describe **a specific lecture** that stood out (episodic). When told a hockey score and asked to remember it, patient forgot within an hour but later spontaneously produced the score as if it were a piece of general knowledge.
- Key diagnostic challenge: Patients confabulate seamlessly from semantic knowledge.

---

### Effects of Cerebral Ischemia on the Hippocampus and Memory

- **Cerebral ischemia:** Interruption of blood supply to the brain.
- Often results in medial temporal lobe amnesia.

#### The Case of R.B. — Product of a Bungled Operation

- **Case source:** Zola-Morgan, Squire, & Amaral (1986).
- At age **52**, R.B. underwent **cardiac bypass surgery**; the blood-circulating pump broke down for several minutes, causing **ischemic brain damage**.
- Amnesia comparable to H.M.'s in many aspects, though not as severe.
- R.B. died in **1983** of a heart attack; detailed **postmortem examination** performed with family permission.
- **Neuropathology:** Obvious brain damage restricted largely to the **pyramidal cell layer of the CA1 subfield** of the hippocampus.
- **Implication:** R.B.'s case suggested **hippocampal damage by itself** can produce medial temporal lobe amnesia. (This conclusion was later challenged.)

#### Hippocampal Structure (Figure 11.5)

| Subfield | Notes |
|---|---|
| CA1 | Pyramidal cell layer; most vulnerable to ischemia |
| CA2 | Part of hippocampus proper |
| CA3 | Part of hippocampus proper |
| CA4 | Part of hippocampus proper |
| Dentate gyrus | Input region of hippocampus |

- **CA** stands for **cornu ammonis** (another name for hippocampus, meaning "horn of Ammon").

## 11.2 Amnesia of Korsakoff's Syndrome

- **Korsakoff's syndrome:** Disorder of memory common in people who have consumed large amounts of alcohol.
- **Cause:** Largely attributable to **thiamine (Vitamin B1) deficiency** from heavy alcohol consumption causing brain damage.
- **Advanced stage features:** Sensory and motor problems, extreme confusion, personality changes, risk of death from liver, gastrointestinal, or heart disorders.
- **Postmortem neuropathology:** Lesions to **medial diencephalon** (medial thalamus + medial hypothalamus) plus diffuse damage to neocortex, hippocampus, and cerebellum (Sullivan & Marsh, 2003).

### Memory Profile of Korsakoff's Syndrome

- **Early stages:** Anterograde amnesia for explicit **episodic memories** is the most prominent symptom.
- **Progressive course:** Severe **retrograde amnesia** develops, extending back to childhood.
- **Complication:** Gradual, insidious onset makes it difficult to distinguish whether amnesia for recent events reflects:
  - Retrograde disruption of existing memories, OR
  - Gradually increasing anterograde blockage of forming new ones.

### Neural Basis: Which Diencephalic Structure?

- Initial hypothesis: Damage to **mammillary bodies** of hypothalamus.
  - Based on several small postmortem studies.
  - However, cases of Korsakoff amnesia **without** mammillary body damage were found.
- In all exceptional cases: Damage to **mediodorsal nuclei of the thalamus**.
- Current view: Memory deficits not attributable to any single diencephalic structure (Tsivilis et al., 2008; Vann & Aggleton, 2004).

### Medial Diencephalic Amnesia

- **Definition:** Amnesia associated with damage to the medial diencephalon (as in Korsakoff's syndrome).
- N.A. is a particularly well-known patient with this type.

---

### The Up-Your-Nose Case of N.A.

- Joined U.S. Air Force after junior college; served as **radar technician** until accident in **December 1960**.
- **Mechanism of injury:** While assembling a model airplane, his roommate was making thrusts with a **miniature fencing foil**; N.A. turned suddenly and was **stabbed through the right nostril**.
- The foil penetrated the **cribriform plate** (thin bone around base of frontal lobes), taking an upward course to the left into the forebrain (Squire, 1987).
- **Initial retrograde amnesia:** ~2 years before accident; appeared to shrink over time; by 2.5 years post-accident, only ~2 weeks before injury.
- **Anterograde:** During first 6-8 months, recall of day-to-day events extremely poor; has not been able to return to gainful employment.
- **MRI (late 1980s, Squire et al., 1989):** Revealed extensive **medial diencephalic damage**, including mediodorsal nuclei and mammillary bodies.
- N.A.'s case confirms mediodorsal nucleus involvement in amnesia.

## 11.3 Amnesia of Alzheimer's Disease

- **First sign:** Mild deterioration of memory.
- **Progressive disease:** Dementia develops and becomes so severe that patient is incapable of simple activities (eating, speaking, recognizing a spouse, bladder control).
- **Terminal.**
- **Research focus:** Predementia Alzheimer's patients (those who have not yet developed dementia).

### Memory Profile in Alzheimer's Disease

- More **general** than medial temporal lobe, medial diencephalic, or Korsakoff deficits.
- Deficits in:
  - Anterograde explicit memory.
  - Retrograde explicit memory.
  - **Short-term memory** (unlike pure medial temporal lobe amnesia where STM is intact).
  - **Implicit memory for verbal and perceptual material** (impaired).
  - Implicit memory for **sensorimotor learning** is **NOT impaired** (preserved; Gabrieli et al., 1993; Postle, Corkin, & Growdon, 1996).

### Neural Basis of Alzheimer's Amnesia

- **Acetylcholine** level greatly reduced in Alzheimer's brains.
- Reduction results from degeneration of the **basal forebrain** (midline area located just above the hypothalamus) — the brain's main source of acetylcholine.
- Strokes in the basal forebrain area can also cause amnesia (Morris et al., 1992).
- Initial view: Acetylcholine depletion causes Alzheimer's amnesia.
- **Current understanding:** Acetylcholine depletion is **not the only factor**:
  - Brain damage is extremely diffuse (see Fig. 10.13).
  - Involves medial temporal lobe and prefrontal cortex.
  - Some basal forebrain damage produces **attentional deficits** that mimic memory problems (Baxter & Chiba, 1999; Everitt & Robbins, 1997).

### Comparison of Amnesia Types

| Feature | Medial Temporal Lobe | Korsakoff's | Alzheimer's |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anterograde explicit | Severe | Severe | Severe |
| Retrograde explicit | Mild (recent) | Severe (extends to childhood) | Severe |
| Short-term memory | Intact | Variable | Impaired |
| Implicit sensorimotor | Intact | Intact | Intact |
| Implicit verbal/perceptual | Intact | Intact | Impaired |
| Primary structure damaged | Hippocampus/rhinal cortex | Medial diencephalon | Basal forebrain + diffuse |
| Primary neurochemical | N/A | N/A | Acetylcholine |

## 11.4 Amnesia After Concussion: Evidence for Consolidation

### Key Definitions

- **Concussion:** Temporary disturbance of consciousness produced by a **nonpenetrating head injury**.
- **Posttraumatic amnesia (PTA):** Amnesia following a nonpenetrating blow to the head. Most common cause of amnesia (Levin, 1989).
- **Coma:** Pathological state of unconsciousness; typically lasts seconds to minutes after severe blow; can last weeks in severe cases.

### Posttraumatic Amnesia Profile

- After regaining consciousness, patient experiences a period of **confusion**.
- Testing after confusion reveals:
  - **Permanent retrograde amnesia** for events just before the blow.
  - **Permanent anterograde amnesia** for events during the subsequent period of confusion.
- Short-term memory appears normal during confusion (patient seems lucid), but later no recollection of conversations held during this period.
- **Severity scales with blow severity:** More severe blow → longer coma, longer confusion period, longer amnesia.
- **Islands of memory:** Surviving memories for isolated events within periods otherwise wiped out.
- **Timeline (Figure 11.6):** Duration of confusion/anterograde amnesia > coma > retrograde amnesia.

---

### Gradients of Retrograde Amnesia and Memory Consolidation

- **Temporally graded retrograde amnesia:** Concussions preferentially disrupt **recent** memories, suggesting older memories have been strengthened (**consolidated**).

#### Hebb's Consolidation Theory

- **Donald Hebb** proposed that memories are first stored in the short term by **reverberating neural activity** — neural activity circulating in closed circuits.
- Reverberating activity is susceptible to disruption (e.g., blow to head, ECS).
- Eventually, reverberating activity induces **structural changes in synapses**, providing stable long-term storage.
- This structural change = **consolidation**.

#### Electroconvulsive Shock (ECS) Studies

- **ECS (Electroconvulsive Shock):** Intense, brief, diffuse, seizure-inducing current administered to brain through large scalp electrodes.
- Rationale: ECS disrupts neural activity, erasing only memories not yet converted to structural changes.
- Length of retrograde amnesia produced by ECS estimates consolidation time.

> **KEY STUDY — ECS and Consolidation in Rats (Pinel, 1969):**
> - Method: Thirsty rats placed in box with niche for 5 habituation days. Day 6 (learning trial): water spout in niche, each rat allowed to drink 15 seconds. ECS administered at 10 sec, 1 min, 10 min, 1 hour, or 3 hours after learning trial. Next day: how many times rats explored niche (no water present) measured.
> - Controls (no ECS): Explored niche ~10 times (remembered water).
> - ECS at 1 hour or 3 hours after: Also ~10 explorations (remembered).
> - ECS at 10 sec, 1 min, or 10 min after: Significantly fewer explorations (retrograde amnesia).
> - **Conclusion:** Consolidation took between 10 minutes and 1 hour.

> **KEY STUDY — Long ECS Gradients in Humans (Squire, Slater, & Chace, 1975):**
> - Method: ECS-treated patients (treated for depression) tested on memory for one-season TV shows from different years. Tested before and after 5 electroconvulsive shocks. Difference = memory loss estimate.
> - Finding: 5 ECS disrupted retention of TV shows that had played in the **3 years prior**, but NOT those that had played earlier.
> - **Conclusion:** Long gradients of retrograde amnesia; consolidation can continue for years.

#### Challenge to Hebb's Theory

- **Long gradients of retrograde amnesia** (covering years) are incompatible with Hebb's reverberatory theory.
- Neural activity cannot realistically reverberate for days, weeks, or years.
- Long gradients indicate consolidation can continue for a very long time after learning, possibly indefinitely (Squire & Spanis, 1984).

---

### Hippocampus and Consolidation

- Scoville and Milner (1957): H.M.'s temporally graded retrograde amnesia suggests hippocampus and related structures play a role in consolidation.

#### Standard Consolidation Theory

- Memories temporarily stored in the hippocampus until transferred to a more stable **cortical storage system** (Moscovitch et al., 2006).
- Supported by demonstrations that medial temporal lobe lesions produce temporally graded retrograde amnesia in animals (Haist, Bowden, & Mao, 2001; Hanson, Bunsey, & Riccio, 2002; Squire, Clark, & Knowlton, 2001).

#### Multiple-Trace Theory (Nadel & Moscovitch, 1997)

- Compatible with finding that retrograde amnesia gradients are often very long.
- **Proposal:** Hippocampus and other involved structures store memories **as long as they exist** — not just during the period immediately after learning.
- When a conscious experience occurs, it is **rapidly and sparsely encoded** in a distributed fashion throughout the hippocampus and other structures.
- Each time a similar experience occurs or the original memory is **recalled**, a new **engram** is established and linked to the original.
- Result: Memory becomes progressively more resistant to disruption; the original engram becomes harder to disrupt.
- **Engram:** A change in the brain that stores a memory.

---

### Reconsolidation

- **Reconsolidation:** Hypothesis that each time a memory is retrieved from long-term storage, it is temporarily held in **labile (changeable/unstable) short-term memory**, where it is again susceptible to disruption until **reconsolidated**.
- Interest originated in 1960s, faded, then rekindled by a key study.

> **KEY STUDY — Reconsolidation (Nader, Schafe, & LeDoux, 2000):**
> - Method: Rats trained on fear conditioning. Later, when rats were required to **recall** the fear conditioning trial, **anisomycin** (protein-synthesis inhibitor) was infused into the **amygdalae**.
> - Finding: Infusion produced **retrograde amnesia** for the fear conditioning, even though the original conditioning trial had occurred days before.
> - **Implication:** Recalled memories must be reconsolidated (require protein synthesis); each retrieval makes a memory temporarily labile.
> - Note: Not all memory types are susceptible to reconsolidation (Biedenkapp & Rudy, 2004).

## 11.5 Neuroanatomy of Object-Recognition Memory

### Why Animal Models Are Necessary

- Human amnesic patients cannot be subjected to controlled experiments (precise lesions, controlled learning/testing conditions).
- Major effort was made to develop animal models of human brain-damage-produced amnesia.
- Initial failure in 1950s–60s: Medial temporal lobe lesions did not produce severe anterograde amnesia in rats/monkeys.

#### Two Reasons for Initial Failure

1. H.M.'s anterograde amnesia was **specific to explicit memories**; early animal tests used **implicit memory tasks** (Pavlovian/operant conditioning).
2. Incorrectly assumed that amnesia was **entirely attributable to hippocampal damage**; most efforts focused on hippocampal lesions only.

---

### Monkey Model: Delayed Nonmatching-to-Sample Test

- **Breakthrough in mid-1970s**, over two decades after H.M.'s initial reports.
- Independently developed by:
  - **Gaffan (1974)**
  - **Mishkin and Delacour (1975)**

> **KEY STUDY — Delayed Nonmatching-to-Sample (DNMTS) Test:**
> - **Method:** Monkey is shown a distinctive **sample object** under which it finds food. After a delay, it is shown the sample object and a novel object; it must select the **unfamiliar/novel object** to get food (nonmatching = choose the novel one).
> - **Intact monkeys:** ~90% correct at delays of a few minutes or less.
> - **Monkeys with bilateral medial temporal lobe lesions:** Major object-recognition deficits. Normal at delays of a few seconds; near chance at delays of several minutes; extremely susceptible to distraction (Squire & Zola-Morgan, 1985).
> - **Humans with medial temporal lobe amnesia** were also tested on DNMTS (with coins as reward) and mirrored the monkeys' performance.
> - **Implication:** Valid animal model of medial temporal lobe amnesia.

---

### Rat Version: The Mumby Box

- Developed by **David Mumby**.
- Rat DNMTS closely resembles monkey version.
- Key finding: Rats perform almost as well as monkeys at delays up to **1 minute** (Mumby, Pinel, & Wood, 1989).
- **Combined bilateral lesions** of hippocampus, amygdala, and rhinal cortex in rats produce major retention deficits at all but shortest intervals (Mumby, Wood, & Pinel, 1992).

#### Why the Rat Model Was Important for Hippocampal Lesion Research

- In **monkeys**, hippocampal aspiration (suction) lesions also destroy **large portions of rhinal cortex** (due to hippocampus location on inferior surface of brain).
- In **rats**, hippocampal aspiration via the dorsal surface destroys only small amounts of parietal neocortex; hippocampus can also be lesioned with **electrolytic lesions** or **intracerebral neurotoxin injections** with little extraneous damage.
- The rat model therefore allows much cleaner assessment of pure hippocampal contributions.

---

### Neuroanatomical Basis: What Structure Is Critical?

#### Three Major Medial Temporal Lobe Structures (in monkey brain)

1. **Hippocampus**
2. **Amygdala**
3. **Adjacent rhinal cortex**
   - **Entorhinal cortex:** Cortex within the rhinal fissure.
   - **Perirhinal cortex:** Cortex around the rhinal fissure.

#### Findings from Selective Lesion Studies (1990s onward)

| Structure Lesioned | Effect on DNMTS |
|---|---|
| Rhinal cortex (bilateral) | **Severe and permanent deficits** |
| Hippocampus (bilateral) | Moderate deficits or none at all |
| Amygdala (bilateral) | **No effect** |

- Multiple reviews reached same conclusion: **Rhinal cortex** is the critical structure for object-recognition memory (Brown & Aggleton, 2001; Bussey & Saksida, 2005; Duva, Kornecook, & Pinel, 2000; Mumby, 2001; Murray, 1996; Murray & Richmond, 2001).
- Specifically, **perirhinal cortex** is the critical region (Bussey & Saksida, 2005; Murray, Bussey, & Saksida, 2007).
- Function of perirhinal cortex: Constructs and maintains **complex representations of objects** used in a variety of processes (perception, categorization, memory) — not only memory (Eacott, Machin, & Gaffan, 2001; Hampton & Murray, 2002).

#### The CA1/Ischemia Paradox and Mumby's Resolution

- Problem: Ischemia-produced lesions to **one small part** of the hippocampus (CA1) produce **severe** DNMTS deficits, yet **total removal** of the hippocampus produces only slight deficits. How?

> **KEY STUDY — Mumby et al. (1996): Resolving the Paradox:**
> - Hypothesis: Ischemia-produced hyperactivity of CA1 pyramidal cells damages neurons **outside the hippocampus** (possibly via excessive release of excitatory amino acid neurotransmitters); this extrahippocampal damage is not readily detectable by conventional histology; this extrahippocampal damage causes the object-recognition deficits.
> - Method: Cerebral ischemia produced in rats (carotid arteries temporarily ligated). Then one group received bilateral hippocampectomy 1 hour later; second group 1 week later; third group no hippocampectomy.
> - Finding: Groups with hippocampectomy at 1 week and no hippocampectomy showed **severe** object-recognition deficits. Group with hippocampectomy at **1 hour** did NOT.
> - Conclusion: Early hippocampectomy **blocked** the extrahippocampal damage that causes object-recognition deficits, supporting the extrahippocampal damage hypothesis.

- Supporting evidence: Allen et al. (2006) found patients with greatly reduced hippocampal volume and anterograde amnesia also had **extensive neocortical damage**.
- **Conclusion:** Ischemia-produced deficits do not constitute strong evidence for hippocampal involvement in object-recognition memory; rhinal/perirhinal cortex is the more likely critical substrate.

## 11.6 Hippocampus and Memory for Spatial Location

- Discovery that perirhinal cortex (not hippocampus) underlies object recognition led to re-evaluation of hippocampal contributions.
- Substantial evidence implicates hippocampus specifically in **memory for spatial location**.

### Spatial Tasks Disrupted by Hippocampal Lesions

- Bilateral hippocampal lesions in lab animals often have **little or no effect** on non-spatial memory tests.
- **Exception:** Hippocampal lesions **consistently disrupt** performance on tasks involving memory for spatial location.

#### Morris Water Maze Test

- **Procedure:** Rats placed at various locations in a circular pool of murky water; must swim to a **stationary platform hidden just below the surface**.
- **Intact rats:** Rapidly learn to find the platform.
- **Rats with hippocampal lesions:** Learn with great difficulty.
- Tests: Allocentric spatial memory.

#### Radial Arm Maze Test

- **Procedure:** Several arms (e.g., 8) radiate from central starting chamber; **same few arms baited with food each day**.
- **Reference memory:** Memory for general principles/skills needed to perform a task — measured by ability to visit only baited arms.
- **Working memory:** Temporary memory necessary for successful performance on a current task — measured by ability to not revisit an arm more than once per day.
- **Rats with hippocampal lesions:** Major deficits on **both** reference memory and working memory measures.

---

### Hippocampal Place Cells

- **Place cells:** Hippocampal neurons that respond only when a subject is in specific locations — the **place fields** of those neurons (Ahmed & Mehta, 2009; Fenton, 2007; Leutgeb et al., 2007).
- When first placed in an unfamiliar environment: No hippocampal neurons have place fields there.
- As rat familiarizes itself: Many hippocampal neurons **acquire place fields** — each fires only when the rat is in a particular part of the environment.
- **Each place cell has a place field in a different part of the environment.**
- Key finding: Firing of place cells indicates **where the rat thinks it is**, not necessarily where it actually is (Kubie et al., 2007; O'Keefe & Speakman, 1987; Wilson & McNaughton, 1993).

---

### Hippocampal and Entorhinal Grid Cells

- **Grid cells:** Entorhinal cortex neurons that each have an extensive array of **evenly spaced place fields**, producing a pattern reminiscent of **graph paper** (Jeffrey, 2007; Moser, Kropff, & Moser, 2008).
- Distance between evenly spaced place fields is **flexible**: In smaller environments, fields are closer together (Barry et al., 2007).
- Even spacing may enable spatial computations in hippocampal place cells.

#### Other Spatial Neuron Types in Entorhinal Cortex

| Cell Type | Function |
|---|---|
| Grid cells | Evenly spaced place fields (graph paper pattern) |
| Head-direction cells | Tuned to direction of head orientation |
| Border cells | Fire when subject is near borders of immediate environment (Solstad et al., 2008) |

#### Relation Between Grid Cells and Place Cells

- Entorhinal grid cells respond relatively **reflexively to location**.
- Hippocampal place cells respond to place **in combination with other features** of the test environment (Buzsáki, 2005; Leutgeb et al., 2004; Poucet & Save, 2005; Wills et al., 2005).

---

### Comparative Studies: Food-Caching Birds

> **KEY STUDY — Sherry & Vaccarino (1989):**
> - Finding: Food-caching bird species have **larger hippocampuses** than related non-food-caching species.
> - Interpretation: Spatial memory demands drive hippocampal size.

> **KEY STUDY — Clayton (2001):**
> - Finding: In **mountain chickadees**, caching and retrieving (not just one or the other) are required to **trigger hippocampal growth** and maintain its size.

- Evidence of hippocampal involvement in spatial memory across many species; primate evidence is **less consistent** (possible reason: primates tested stationary on computer screens rather than navigating through environments).

---

### Cognitive Map Theory of Hippocampal Function

- **O'Keefe and Nadel (1978).**
- **Proposal:** Multiple specialized memory systems exist in the brain; hippocampus specializes in **memory for spatial location**.
- The hippocampus constructs and maintains **allocentric maps** of the external world from sensory input.
  - **Allocentric:** Representations of space based on relations among external objects and landmarks (independent of one's own position).
  - **Egocentric:** Representations of space based on relations of objects/landmarks to one's own position.
- Hippocampus plays a role in **episodic memory** because spatial context is critical for acquiring and recalling any episode.

#### Three Criticisms of Cognitive Map Theory

1. Place cell firing depends on more than spatial location — also depends on **recent or pending behavior** in the place field.
2. Hippocampal damage sometimes impairs tasks with **no obvious spatial component**.
3. Hippocampus is a large, complex structure — unlikely to act as a unit with a **single function**; each part's function needs to be addressed separately (Bannerman et al., 2004; Bird & Burgess, 2008; Shapiro, Kennedy, & Ferbinteanu, 2006).

## 11.7 Where Are Memories Stored?

- **Important distinction:** Medial temporal lobe structures are critical for memory processes, but memories are not necessarily **stored** in these structures.
- Most straightforward method: Assess whether lesions produce **retrograde amnesia**.
  - If a structure were the storage site for memories of a particular type, its destruction should eliminate ALL prior memories of that type.
  - No brain structure shows this result.
  - Lesions either produce **no retrograde amnesia** or retrograde amnesia only for events in the **days/weeks just before surgery**.

### Two Major Conclusions About Memory Storage

1. **Memories are stored diffusely** throughout the brain and can survive destruction of any single structure.
2. **Memories become more resistant to disruption over time** (consolidation).

### Each Memory Stored in Circuits Used During Original Experience

- Each memory appears to be stored in brain structures that **participated in the original experience** (Martin, 2006; Pasternak & Greenlee, 2005; Weinberger, 2004).
- Substantial sensory and motor loss often accompanies lesion-produced retrograde amnesia, complicating assessment.
- Evidence for specific storage sites relies more on **recording and stimulation studies** than lesion studies.

### Four Structures Implicated in Memory Storage (Summary from Chapter)

| Structure | Memory Type Implicated | Clinical Association |
|---|---|---|
| Hippocampus | Spatial memory | Medial temporal lobe amnesia |
| Perirhinal cortex | Object recognition | Medial temporal lobe amnesia |
| Mediodorsal nucleus (thalamus) | Explicit episodic memory | Korsakoff's syndrome |
| Basal forebrain | General/diffuse | Alzheimer's disease |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **H.M.'s surgery (1953):** Bilateral medial temporal lobectomy; caused severe anterograde amnesia with intact STM (digit span = 6) and largely intact remote memory; died 2008.

2. **Explicit vs. Implicit memory distinction** originated from H.M.'s case: mirror-drawing, rotary pursuit, incomplete pictures, Pavlovian conditioning, and repetition priming tests all demonstrate intact implicit memory with absent explicit memory in amnesia.

3. **Three contributions of H.M.'s case:** (1) Medial temporal lobes critical for memory — challenged diffuse distribution theory. (2) Dissociation of STM, LTM, and remote memory — proposed consolidation deficit. (3) Discovery of explicit/implicit memory distinction.

4. **Semantic vs. Episodic memory (Tulving):** Semantic = general facts; Episodic = autobiographical events. Medial temporal lobe amnesia disproportionately impairs episodic memory. K.C. is the paradigm case: good semantic, absent episodic, cannot imagine future.

5. **Korsakoff's syndrome:** Caused by thiamine deficiency from alcohol; primarily medial diencephalic damage (mammillary bodies + mediodorsal nuclei of thalamus); anterograde then retrograde amnesia.

6. **Alzheimer's disease memory profile:** Unlike pure medial temporal lobe amnesia, STM is also impaired; implicit verbal/perceptual memory impaired (but sensorimotor implicit memory preserved); reduced acetylcholine from basal forebrain degeneration.

7. **Memory consolidation:** Hebb's reverberatory theory (short-term) → structural synaptic changes (long-term). ECS studies show consolidation window of 10 min to 1 hour in rats (Pinel, 1969) but gradients can extend 3+ years in humans (Squire et al., 1975) — incompatible with Hebb's reverberatory mechanism.

8. **Standard consolidation theory (Scoville & Milner, 1957):** Hippocampus temporarily holds memories → transfers to cortex. Multiple-trace theory (Nadel & Moscovitch, 1997): Hippocampus stores memories permanently; each recall creates a new linked engram.

9. **Reconsolidation (Nader, Schafe, & LeDoux, 2000):** Retrieved memories become labile; anisomycin into amygdala after recall → retrograde amnesia. Implication: Memories must be reconsolidated each time retrieved.

10. **Delayed Nonmatching-to-Sample (DNMTS) test:** Monkey/rat paradigm for object-recognition memory; choose the NOVEL object. Developed by Gaffan (1974) and Mishkin & Delacour (1975). Rat version = Mumby box.

11. **Critical structure for object-recognition memory = Perirhinal cortex, NOT hippocampus:** Rhinal cortex lesions → severe DNMTS deficits; hippocampus lesions → slight or no effect; amygdala lesions → no effect.

12. **CA1 ischemia paradox (Mumby et al., 1996):** Ischemia damages CA1 AND causes extrahippocampal damage via excitatory amino acid release; early hippocampectomy (1 hour post-ischemia) blocks the extrahippocampal damage and prevents DNMTS deficits — showing the deficit was NOT purely hippocampal.

13. **Hippocampus and spatial memory:** Place cells respond at specific locations; grid cells (entorhinal cortex) have evenly spaced place fields (graph paper pattern). Hippocampal lesions disrupt Morris water maze and radial arm maze (both reference and working memory). Cognitive map theory (O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978): hippocampus constructs allocentric spatial maps.

14. **Food-caching birds:** Food-caching species have larger hippocampuses (Sherry & Vaccarino, 1989); caching + retrieving (not just one) triggers hippocampal growth (Clayton, 2001, mountain chickadees) — comparative evidence for hippocampal spatial specialization.

15. **R.B. (Zola-Morgan et al., 1986):** Ischemic amnesia from bungled cardiac bypass surgery; CA1 pyramidal cell damage; suggested (but was later challenged) that hippocampal damage alone can cause medial temporal lobe amnesia.
