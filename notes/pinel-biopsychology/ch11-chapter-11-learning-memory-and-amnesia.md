# Chapter 11: Learning, Memory, and Amnesia

Neural bases of learning and memory via amnesic patients and animal models: explicit vs. implicit memory; roles of the hippocampus, rhinal cortex, medial diencephalon, and basal forebrain; and memory consolidation. Moves from landmark cases (H.M., K.C., R.B., N.A.) through animal models to theories of hippocampal function.

---

## 11.1 Amnesic Effects of Bilateral Medial Temporal Lobectomy

### The Case of H.M.
- **H.M.** (Henry Molaison) had a **bilateral medial temporal lobectomy** in **1953** (surgeon **William Beecher Scoville**) for intractable epilepsy, removing much of both **medial temporal lobes** (hippocampus, amygdala, adjacent cortex).
- Intellect intact; mild **retrograde amnesia** for ~2 years pre-surgery; remote memories normal. **Short-term memory** intact (**digit span = 6**; Wickelgren, 1968). Severe inability to form new **long-term memories**. Died **2008**.

> Milner (1965): "Forgetting occurred the instant the patient's focus of attention shifted."

### H.M.'s Seven Tests

| Test | Researcher | Type | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Digit Span +1** | Drachman & Arbit (1966) | Explicit (verbal LTM) | Severely impaired (couldn't reach 8 digits in 25 trials; normal ~15) |
| **Block-Tapping +1** | Milner (1971) | Explicit (nonverbal LTM) | Severely impaired (span 5; couldn't learn 6) → **global amnesia** |
| **Mirror Drawing** | Milner (1965) | Implicit (sensorimotor) | Improved across days; no conscious recall — **first sign of implicit memory** |
| **Rotary Pursuit** | Corkin (1968) | Implicit (sensorimotor) | Improved; retained 7 days; no recall |
| **Incomplete Pictures** | Gollin (1960) | Implicit (nonsensorimotor) | Improved on retest; no recall |
| **Pavlovian (eye-blink)** | Woodruff-Pak (1993) | Implicit (classical) | Learned (slowly); retained ~2 years; no recall |
| **Repetition priming** | — | Implicit (verbal/perceptual) | Intact despite no explicit memory of the word list |

### Three Contributions of H.M.'s Case
1. **Localization**: medial temporal lobes play a **specific, critical** memory role — challenged Lashley's diffuse-distribution view; spawned hippocampal **mnemonic** research.
2. **STM/LTM/remote dissociation**: abolished forming some long-term memories while STM and remote memory stayed intact → a **memory consolidation** deficit (Squire & Bayley, 2007).
3. **Implicit memory discovery**: **explicit memories** (conscious) vs. **implicit memories** (shown by improved performance without awareness); many amnesics lose explicit but retain implicit.

### Medial Temporal Lobe Amnesia
- Mnemonic deficit profile like H.M. with preserved intellect and MTL damage; core symptom = difficulty forming **explicit** long-term memories while retaining **implicit** ones (Eichenbaum, 1999).
- **Why two systems?** Implicit likely evolved first; explicit adds **flexibility**. Reber, Knowlton, & Squire (1996) and Buckley & Gaffan (1998): amnesics learn implicit tasks normally but fail to use the knowledge in a **different context** → explicit memory enables flexible use.

### Semantic and Episodic Memories
- **Semantic memories**: explicit memories for general facts. **Episodic memories**: explicit memories for personal events (autobiographical). MTL amnesia disproportionately impairs **episodic** memory. **Endel Tulving** likened episodic memory to **mental time travel** (Tulving, 2002).

### The Case of K.C. — Can't Time Travel
- Motorcycle accident **1981**; diffuse damage incl. MTL; patient of **Tulving**.
- **Preserved:** intelligence, language, games, math, facts about his own early life. **Impaired:** cannot recall **a single personal event** (pure **episodic amnesia**); cannot imagine his future. Shows semantic memory can function with severe episodic amnesia.

### Early-Life MTL Damage
- **Vargha-Khadem et al. (1997):** three patients with early bilateral MTL damage progressed through **mainstream schools** with reasonable language/factual (semantic) knowledge despite poor episodic memory, which never improved (de Haan et al., 2006).

### Spotting Episodic Deficits
- Hard to spot because patients give **semantic answers to episodic questions** and confabulate seamlessly (e.g., a history teacher discussing topics generally but unable to describe a specific lecture).

### Cerebral Ischemia, Hippocampus, and Memory
- **Cerebral ischemia**: interrupted blood supply; often causes MTL amnesia.

> **Case of R.B.** (Zola-Morgan, Squire, & Amaral, 1986): at age 52, cardiac bypass pump failure caused **ischemic damage**; amnesia like H.M.'s but milder. Postmortem: damage largely restricted to the **CA1 pyramidal cell layer** of the hippocampus — suggesting **hippocampal damage alone** can cause MTL amnesia (later challenged).

| Hippocampal subfield | Notes |
|---|---|
| CA1 | Pyramidal cell layer; most vulnerable to ischemia |
| CA2/CA3/CA4 | Hippocampus proper |
| Dentate gyrus | Input region |

- **CA** = **cornu ammonis** ("horn of Ammon").

## 11.2 Amnesia of Korsakoff's Syndrome

- **Korsakoff's syndrome**: memory disorder common in heavy alcohol users; caused largely by **thiamine (Vitamin B1) deficiency** → brain damage. Advanced: sensory/motor problems, confusion, personality change, death risk.
- **Postmortem:** lesions to the **medial diencephalon** (medial thalamus + hypothalamus) plus diffuse neocortex, hippocampus, cerebellum damage (Sullivan & Marsh, 2003).

**Profile:** early = anterograde amnesia for **episodic memories**; progressive = severe **retrograde amnesia** back to childhood; insidious onset blurs anterograde vs. retrograde.

**Neural basis:** initially blamed on **mammillary bodies**, but Korsakoff cases occur without their damage — all such cases had **mediodorsal thalamic nucleus** damage. Current view: no single structure (Vann & Aggleton, 2004).

- **Medial diencephalic amnesia**: amnesia from medial diencephalon damage (as in Korsakoff's).

> **Case of N.A.**: radar technician; **December 1960** accident — a **miniature fencing foil** stabbed through his right nostril, penetrating the **cribriform plate** into the forebrain (Squire, 1987). Initial retrograde amnesia ~2 years (shrank to ~2 weeks); poor anterograde recall. MRI (Squire et al., 1989): **medial diencephalic damage** (mediodorsal nuclei + mammillary bodies) — confirms mediodorsal nucleus involvement.

## 11.3 Amnesia of Alzheimer's Disease

- First sign: mild memory decline; progresses to severe **dementia**; terminal. Research focuses on **predementia** patients.
- **Profile** (more general than other amnesias): deficits in anterograde explicit, retrograde explicit, **short-term memory** (unlike pure MTL amnesia), and **implicit verbal/perceptual** memory; **implicit sensorimotor** learning is **preserved** (Gabrieli et al., 1993).
- **Neural basis**: greatly reduced **acetylcholine** from degeneration of the **basal forebrain** (above hypothalamus; main ACh source). Current view: ACh depletion is **not the only factor** — damage is diffuse (incl. MTL, prefrontal cortex) and some basal forebrain damage causes attentional deficits mimicking memory loss (Baxter & Chiba, 1999).

### Comparison of Amnesia Types

| Feature | Medial Temporal Lobe | Korsakoff's | Alzheimer's |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anterograde explicit | Severe | Severe | Severe |
| Retrograde explicit | Mild (recent) | Severe (to childhood) | Severe |
| Short-term memory | Intact | Variable | Impaired |
| Implicit sensorimotor | Intact | Intact | Intact |
| Implicit verbal/perceptual | Intact | Intact | Impaired |
| Primary structure | Hippocampus/rhinal cortex | Medial diencephalon | Basal forebrain + diffuse |
| Primary neurochemical | — | — | Acetylcholine |

## 11.4 Amnesia After Concussion: Evidence for Consolidation

- **Concussion**: temporary loss of consciousness from a **nonpenetrating** head injury. **Posttraumatic amnesia (PTA)**: amnesia after such a blow (most common amnesia cause). **Coma**: pathological unconsciousness.
- **Profile**: after consciousness returns, a period of **confusion**; then **permanent retrograde amnesia** for events just before the blow and **permanent anterograde amnesia** for the confusion period. STM appears normal during confusion. Severity scales with blow severity. **Islands of memory**: surviving isolated memories.

### Gradients of Retrograde Amnesia and Consolidation
- **Temporally graded retrograde amnesia**: recent memories preferentially disrupted → older ones are **consolidated**.
- **Hebb's consolidation theory**: memories first held by **reverberating neural activity** (disruptable), then induce **structural synaptic changes** (stable) = **consolidation**.
- **ECS (Electroconvulsive Shock)**: brief diffuse seizure-inducing current; disrupts not-yet-consolidated memories; amnesia length estimates consolidation time.

> **KEY STUDY — Pinel (1969):** thirsty rats learned a niche held water; ECS at 10 s/1 min/10 min after → retrograde amnesia; ECS at 1 h/3 h → no amnesia. **Consolidation took 10 min to 1 hour.**

> **KEY STUDY — Squire, Slater, & Chace (1975):** in ECS-treated depression patients, 5 shocks disrupted memory for TV shows from the **prior 3 years** but not earlier — **long retrograde gradients**; consolidation can continue for years.

- **Challenge to Hebb:** long (multi-year) gradients are incompatible with reverberatory storage; consolidation can continue indefinitely (Squire & Spanis, 1984).

### Hippocampus and Consolidation
- Scoville & Milner (1957): H.M.'s graded retrograde amnesia implicates the hippocampus in consolidation.
- **Standard consolidation theory**: memories held temporarily in the hippocampus, then transferred to stable **cortical storage** (Moscovitch et al., 2006).
- **Multiple-trace theory (Nadel & Moscovitch, 1997)**: hippocampus stores memories **as long as they exist**; each recall creates a new **engram** linked to the original, making memory more resistant. **Engram** = a brain change storing a memory.

### Reconsolidation
- **Reconsolidation**: each retrieval returns a memory to **labile** short-term state, susceptible to disruption until reconsolidated.

> **KEY STUDY — Nader, Schafe, & LeDoux (2000):** rats fear-conditioned; **anisomycin** (protein-synthesis inhibitor) into the **amygdalae** during recall produced **retrograde amnesia** for the fear memory — recalled memories require reconsolidation. (Not all memory types are susceptible; Biedenkapp & Rudy, 2004.)

## 11.5 Neuroanatomy of Object-Recognition Memory

### Why Animal Models
- Patients can't undergo controlled lesions/testing. Early failures (1950s-60s): MTL lesions didn't produce severe amnesia in animals because (1) early tests used **implicit** tasks (H.M.'s deficit was **explicit**), and (2) efforts focused only on the **hippocampus** (wrongly assuming amnesia was purely hippocampal).

### Delayed Nonmatching-to-Sample (DNMTS) Test
- Breakthrough mid-1970s; developed by **Gaffan (1974)** and **Mishkin & Delacour (1975)**.

> **KEY STUDY — DNMTS:** monkey shown a **sample object** (food beneath it); after a delay, shown the sample + a novel object and must choose the **novel** one (nonmatching). Intact monkeys ~**90%** correct at short delays. **Bilateral MTL lesions** → near-chance at longer delays, very distractible (Squire & Zola-Morgan, 1985). Humans with MTL amnesia mirror this → valid model.

- **Rat version — Mumby box** (David Mumby): rats nearly match monkeys at delays up to **1 minute** (Mumby, Pinel, & Wood, 1989); combined hippocampus + amygdala + rhinal cortex lesions cause major deficits (Mumby, Wood, & Pinel, 1992).
- The rat model allows **cleaner** hippocampal lesions (in monkeys, hippocampal aspiration also destroys rhinal cortex).

### Critical Structure

| Lesion (bilateral) | Effect on DNMTS |
|---|---|
| **Rhinal cortex** | **Severe, permanent deficits** |
| Hippocampus | Moderate or none |
| Amygdala | None |

- **Rhinal cortex** (specifically **perirhinal cortex**) is critical (Bussey & Saksida, 2005; Murray, Bussey, & Saksida, 2007); it builds complex **object representations** used in perception, categorization, and memory.
- Rhinal cortex = **entorhinal** (within the rhinal fissure) + **perirhinal** (around it).

**CA1/Ischemia paradox:** small CA1 ischemic lesions cause **severe** DNMTS deficits, yet **total** hippocampectomy causes only slight ones.

> **KEY STUDY — Mumby et al. (1996):** ischemia in rats, then hippocampectomy at 1 hour, 1 week, or never. Severe deficits with hippocampectomy at 1 week or never; **none** at 1 hour. Early removal **blocked the extrahippocampal damage** (from CA1 hyperactivity releasing excitatory amino acids) that actually causes the deficits.

- Allen et al. (2006): amnesic patients with reduced hippocampal volume also had extensive **neocortical** damage. Conclusion: ischemic deficits aren't strong evidence for hippocampal object-recognition; rhinal cortex is the likely substrate.

## 11.6 Hippocampus and Memory for Spatial Location

- Substantial evidence implicates the hippocampus in **spatial location** memory. Hippocampal lesions often spare non-spatial memory but **consistently disrupt** spatial tasks.

### Spatial Tasks
- **Morris water maze**: rats must swim to a **hidden submerged platform**; intact rats learn fast, hippocampal-lesioned rats with great difficulty.
- **Radial arm maze**: arms radiate from a center; same arms baited daily. Tests **reference memory** (general task principles — visit only baited arms) and **working memory** (don't revisit an arm in a day). Hippocampal lesions impair **both**.

### Place Cells, Grid Cells
- **Place cells**: hippocampal neurons firing only at specific locations (their **place fields**); acquired as a rat familiarizes with a new environment; each has a different field; firing reflects **where the rat thinks it is** (O'Keefe & Speakman, 1987).
- **Grid cells**: entorhinal neurons with evenly spaced place fields in a **graph-paper** pattern (Moser, Kropff, & Moser, 2008); spacing is flexible (closer in smaller spaces).

| Entorhinal cell type | Function |
|---|---|
| Grid cells | Evenly spaced fields (graph paper) |
| Head-direction cells | Tuned to head orientation |
| Border cells | Fire near environment borders (Solstad et al., 2008) |

- Grid cells respond reflexively to location; place cells respond to place **combined with other features** (Leutgeb et al., 2004).

### Comparative Studies

> **Sherry & Vaccarino (1989):** food-caching bird species have **larger hippocampuses**.

> **Clayton (2001):** in mountain chickadees, **caching + retrieving** (together) trigger hippocampal growth.

- Primate evidence is **less consistent** (possibly because primates are tested stationary on screens).

### Cognitive Map Theory (O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978)
- Hippocampus specializes in **spatial location** memory, building **allocentric maps** (relations among external landmarks, independent of self) vs. **egocentric** (relative to self). Role in **episodic memory** because spatial context is critical to episodes.
- **Three criticisms:** (1) place-cell firing depends on recent/pending behavior too; (2) hippocampal damage sometimes impairs non-spatial tasks; (3) the hippocampus is too large/complex for a single function (Bird & Burgess, 2008).

## 11.7 Where Are Memories Stored?

- MTL structures are critical for memory **processes**, but memories aren't necessarily **stored** there. If a structure stored a memory type, its destruction should erase all such prior memories — but no structure does this; lesions produce either no retrograde amnesia or only recent-events retrograde amnesia.

**Two conclusions:** (1) memories are **stored diffusely** and survive loss of any single structure; (2) memories become **more resistant** over time (consolidation).

- Each memory is stored in structures that **participated in the original experience** (Martin, 2006); evidence relies more on recording/stimulation than lesions.

| Structure | Memory Type | Clinical Association |
|---|---|---|
| Hippocampus | Spatial memory | Medial temporal lobe amnesia |
| Perirhinal cortex | Object recognition | Medial temporal lobe amnesia |
| Mediodorsal nucleus (thalamus) | Explicit episodic | Korsakoff's syndrome |
| Basal forebrain | General/diffuse | Alzheimer's disease |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **H.M. (1953)**: bilateral MTL lobectomy; severe anterograde amnesia with intact STM (digit span 6) and remote memory; died 2008.

2. **Explicit vs. implicit** from H.M.: mirror-drawing, rotary pursuit, incomplete pictures, Pavlovian conditioning, repetition priming all show intact implicit memory.

3. **Three contributions of H.M.**: MTL localization; STM/LTM/remote dissociation (consolidation deficit); explicit/implicit distinction.

4. **Semantic vs. episodic (Tulving)**: MTL amnesia disproportionately impairs episodic; K.C. = good semantic, absent episodic, can't imagine future.

5. **Korsakoff's**: thiamine deficiency; medial diencephalon (mammillary bodies + mediodorsal nuclei); anterograde then retrograde amnesia.

6. **Alzheimer's**: STM also impaired (unlike pure MTL amnesia); implicit verbal/perceptual impaired, sensorimotor preserved; reduced ACh from basal forebrain.

7. **Consolidation**: Hebb's reverberation → structural change; ECS window 10 min-1 hour in rats (Pinel, 1969); 3-year gradients in humans (Squire et al., 1975) defy Hebb.

8. **Standard consolidation (Scoville & Milner, 1957)** vs. **multiple-trace theory (Nadel & Moscovitch, 1997)** (permanent hippocampal storage; new engram per recall).

9. **Reconsolidation (Nader et al., 2000)**: retrieved memories are labile; anisomycin into amygdala after recall → retrograde amnesia.

10. **DNMTS**: choose the **novel** object; Gaffan (1974) and Mishkin & Delacour (1975); rat version = Mumby box.

11. **Object-recognition critical structure = perirhinal cortex, NOT hippocampus**: rhinal lesions severe; hippocampal slight/none; amygdala none.

12. **CA1 ischemia paradox (Mumby et al., 1996)**: ischemia causes extrahippocampal damage; hippocampectomy 1 hour post-ischemia blocks it and prevents deficits → deficit not purely hippocampal.

13. **Hippocampus & spatial memory**: place cells (locations), grid cells (entorhinal, graph-paper); lesions disrupt Morris water maze and radial arm maze; cognitive map theory (O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978; allocentric maps).

14. **Food-caching birds**: larger hippocampuses (Sherry & Vaccarino, 1989); caching + retrieving triggers growth (Clayton, 2001).

15. **R.B. (Zola-Morgan et al., 1986)**: ischemic amnesia; CA1 damage; suggested (later challenged) hippocampal damage alone causes MTL amnesia.
