# Chapter 3: Anatomy of the Nervous System

## Chapter Overview
Surveys nervous system structure from gross divisions (CNS vs. PNS) to the cellular level (neurons, glia), plus neuroanatomical techniques, directional terms, and a tour of the spinal cord and the five brain divisions.

---

## 3.1 General Layout of the Nervous System

### Major Divisions

- The nervous system is organized as **a system of twos**.
- **Central nervous system (CNS)**: brain + spinal cord.
- **Peripheral nervous system (PNS)**: all nervous tissue outside the CNS.

### PNS Subdivisions

- **Somatic nervous system (SNS)**: interacts with the external environment.
  - **Afferent nerves**: carry sensory signals *toward* the CNS.
  - **Efferent nerves**: carry motor signals *away from* the CNS.
- **Autonomic nervous system (ANS)**: regulates the body's *internal* environment (viscera, glands, vessels); has afferent + efferent divisions.
  - Efferent ANS has two branches: **sympathetic** and **parasympathetic**.

### Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic

| Feature | Sympathetic | Parasympathetic |
|---|---|---|
| Spinal cord origin | Thoracic and lumbar | Cranial nerves and sacral cord |
| Synapse (2nd-stage neuron) | Far from target organ | Near/at target organ |
| 2nd-stage neuron length | Long | Very short |
| Function | Mobilize energy (fight-or-flight) | Conserve energy (rest-and-digest) |
| Psychological state | Arousal | Relaxation |

**Three conventional principles** (with exceptions, per Guyenet, 2006):
1. Sympathetic mobilizes energy in threat; parasympathetic conserves.
2. Each target organ gets *opposing* sympathetic/parasympathetic input.
3. Sympathetic = arousal; parasympathetic = relaxation.

### Cranial Nerves

- **12 pairs of cranial nerves** project directly from the brain; numbered I–XII (anterior to posterior).
- **Only two purely sensory**: **olfactory (I)** and **optic (II)**; most are mixed.
- **Longest**: **vagus nerves (X)** — motor + sensory to/from the gut.
- All autonomic motor fibers in cranial nerves are **parasympathetic**.
- **Clinical**: cranial nerve disruptions localize tumors/pathology; routinely assessed in diagnosis.

## 3.2 Meninges, Ventricles, and Cerebrospinal Fluid

### Meninges

The CNS is encased in bone and three membranes, the **meninges** (sing. meninx), outer to inner:
1. **Dura mater** ("tough mother"): thick, tough, outermost.
2. **Arachnoid membrane**: fine middle layer; beneath it the **subarachnoid space** holds large vessels and CSF.
3. **Pia mater** ("pious mother"): delicate innermost; adheres to the CNS surface.

### Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

- **CSF** fills the subarachnoid space, the spinal **central canal**, and the **cerebral ventricles** (4: left + right lateral, third, fourth). All interconnected as one reservoir.
- **Function**: supports/cushions the brain (CSF drainage causes severe headache).
- **Production**: continuously by the **choroid plexuses** (capillary networks protruding into ventricles).
- **Absorption**: from subarachnoid space into **dural sinuses** → jugular veins.

### Hydrocephalus

- **Hydrocephalus** ("water head"): CSF flow blocked, usually at the **cerebral aqueduct** (connects 3rd and 4th ventricles) → ventricular fluid buildup → brain expands. Treatment: drain fluid; remove obstruction.

---

## 3.3 Blood-Brain Barrier

- **Blood-brain barrier (BBB)** (Banerjee & Bhat, 2007): impedes passage of many toxins from blood to brain.
- **Basis**: cerebral vessel wall cells are *tightly packed* (vs. loosely packed elsewhere), blocking proteins/large molecules (Abbott, Ronnback, & Hannson, 2005).
- **Drugs**: brain effects depend on BBB penetration (Loscher & Potschka, 2005).
- **Exceptions**: critical molecules (e.g., **glucose**) are *actively transported*; some areas allow large molecules through.
- **Astrocytes** help cover cerebral vessels and contribute to BBB function.

- **Neurons**: cells specialized for reception, conduction, and transmission of electrochemical signals; great variety of shapes/sizes (Nelson, Sugino, & Hempel, 2006).

| **Buttons (terminal buttons)** | Axon-ending knobs; release chemicals into synapses |
| **Synapses** | Gaps across which chemical signals are transmitted |

### Internal Anatomy

| Structure | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Nucleus** | Spherical DNA-containing structure |
| **Cytoplasm** | Clear internal fluid |

## 3.5 Glial Cells

- **Glial cells**: non-neuronal cells. Often said to outnumber neurons 10:1, but Azevedo et al. (2009) found numbers are approximately **equal**.

### Four Types

#### 1. Oligodendrocytes (CNS)
- Wrap axons in **myelin** → **myelin sheaths** → faster, more efficient conduction.
- One cell provides **several myelin segments** (often on multiple axons).
- Do **NOT** guide axonal regeneration.

#### 2. Schwann Cells (PNS)
- Same myelination role, but in PNS; each constitutes **one myelin segment**.
- **Only Schwann cells guide axonal regeneration** → mammalian regeneration is restricted to the PNS.

#### 3. Microglia
- **Smallest** glia; respond to injury/disease — multiply, engulf debris, trigger **inflammation** (Nimmerjahn, Kirchhoff, & Helmchen, 2005).

#### 4. Astrocytes
- **Largest**, **star-shaped** glia; cover cerebral vessels and contact cell bodies; role in the **BBB**.
- Old view: structural support only ("glia" = glue). **Modern view** (Kettenmann & Verkhratsky, 2008): astrocytes send/receive signals; control synapse formation/maintenance (Jourdain et al., 2007); modulate neural activity (Rouach et al., 2008); maintain axon function (Edgar & Nave, 2009); participate in glial circuits (Giaume et al., 2010).

### Oligodendrocytes vs. Schwann Cells

| Feature | Oligodendrocytes | Schwann Cells |
|---|---|---|
| Location | CNS | PNS |
| Myelin segments/cell | Several (multiple axons) | One |
| Regeneration guidance | No | Yes |

## 3.6 Neuroanatomical Techniques

Neurons are tightly packed; different prep methods reveal different aspects.

### Golgi Stain
- Discovered **accidentally** by **Camillo Golgi** (early **1870s**; Rapport, 2005) using **potassium dichromate + silver nitrate**; stains a few neurons entirely **black**.
- Best for **overall shape** (silhouette only; few neurons stain).

### Nissl Stain
- Developed by **Franz Nissl** (**1880s**); dye = **cresyl violet**; binds to **neuron cell bodies**.
- Best for **counting cell bodies** and revealing gross structure.

### Electron Microscopy
- Reveals **fine details** beyond light microscopy (~1,500x limit) → **electron micrograph**.
- **Scanning electron microscope**: 3D images, less magnification. Limitation: hard to see general structure.

### Tracing Techniques

| Type | Direction | Method | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anterograde tracing** | Forward (cell body → terminals) | Chemical taken up by cell bodies, transported forward | Axons projecting AWAY from an area |
| **Retrograde tracing** | Backward (terminals → cell bodies) | Chemical taken up by terminals, transported back | Axons projecting INTO an area |

## 3.8 Spinal Cord

- **Gray matter**: inner H-shaped core; cell bodies + unmyelinated interneurons.
- **White matter**: surrounding myelinated axons.
- **Dorsal horns** / **Ventral horns**: dorsal/ventral arms of gray matter.
- **31 pairs** of spinal nerves; each joins the cord via two roots:

**Dorsal Root** — **sensory (afferent) unipolar** neurons; cell bodies in **dorsal root ganglia** outside the cord; terminals in dorsal horns.

**Ventral Root** — **motor (efferent) multipolar** neurons; cell bodies in **ventral horns**; project to skeletal muscle (somatic) or to ganglia → organs (autonomic).

## 3.9 Five Major Divisions of the Brain

- CNS begins as a fluid-filled tube with three swellings (**forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain**); before birth these become **five**.

| Division | Adult Structure | Common Name |
|---|---|---|
| **Telencephalon** | Cerebral hemispheres | (largest, on top) |
| **Diencephalon** | Thalamus + hypothalamus | |
| **Mesencephalon** | Midbrain | Midbrain |
| **Metencephalon** | Pons + cerebellum | |
| **Myelencephalon** | Medulla | Medulla |

- The four divisions below the telencephalon = the **brain stem**.

## 3.10 Major Structures of the Brain

### Myelencephalon (Medulla)
- Most **posterior**; mostly **tracts**.
- **Reticular formation**: ~**100 nuclei** in the brain stem core (reticulum = little net); aka **reticular activating system (RAS)** (misleading). Functions: sleep, attention, movement, muscle tone, cardiac/circulatory/respiratory reflexes.

### Metencephalon
Ascending/descending tracts, part of reticular formation, plus:
- **Pons**: bulge on the brain stem's **ventral surface**.
- **Cerebellum** ("little brain"): convoluted, on the **dorsal surface**; primary role **sensorimotor control** (damage = loss of precise movement); also some cognition (decision making, language).

### Mesencephalon (Midbrain)
**Tectum** ("roof", **dorsal**): composed of **colliculi** —
- **Inferior colliculi** (posterior): **auditory**.
- **Superior colliculi** (anterior): **visual** (entirely visual in lower vertebrates = optic tectum).

**Tegmentum** (**ventral**): reticular formation plus —

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| **Periaqueductal gray (PAG)** | Around cerebral aqueduct; mediates analgesia of **opiate drugs** |
| **Substantia nigra** ("black substance") | Sensorimotor; dopaminergic projection to striatum |
| **Red nucleus** | Sensorimotor |

### Diencephalon
**Thalamus**: two-lobed structure at the top of the brain stem; lobes flank the third ventricle, joined by the **massa intermedia**; most nuclei project to **cortex**. **Sensory relay nuclei** process and relay sensory signals to cortex (and receive cortical feedback):

| Relay Nucleus | System |
|---|---|
| **Lateral geniculate nuclei** | Visual |
| **Medial geniculate nuclei** | Auditory |
| **Ventral posterior nuclei** | Somatosensory |

**Hypothalamus**: below anterior thalamus; regulates **motivated behaviors** (eating, sleep, sex); controls hormone release from the **pituitary gland**. Three inferior-surface structures:
1. **Optic chiasm**: where optic nerves meet; X-shaped because some axons **decussate** (cross). **Contralateral** = decussating; **ipsilateral** = not.
2. **Pituitary gland**: hangs from hypothalamus.
3. **Mammillary bodies**: spherical nuclei behind the pituitary; part of hypothalamus and limbic system.

### Telencephalon
**Largest** division; mediates voluntary movement, sensory interpretation, complex cognition.

#### Cerebral Cortex
- **Cerebral cortex** ("cerebral bark"): covers the hemispheres; mostly **unmyelinated** neurons (gray matter); beneath it **myelinated axons** (white matter).
- Deeply **convoluted** in humans (more surface area, same volume). Most mammals are **lissencephalic** (smooth); convolution relates to **body size**, not intellect.
- **Fissures** (large furrows), **sulci** (small furrows), **gyri** (ridges).
- **Longitudinal fissure** (largest; nearly separates hemispheres); **corpus callosum** (largest **cerebral commissure**); **central fissure** and **lateral fissure** (major landmarks).

**Four Lobes:**

| Lobe | Location | Key Structures | Functions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Frontal** | Anterior to central fissure | Precentral gyrus | Motor control (precentral gyrus); complex cognition (planning, evaluating, social judgment) in anterior frontal cortex |
| **Parietal** | Posterior to central fissure | Postcentral gyrus | Body sensation (postcentral gyrus); object/body location; directing attention |
| **Temporal** | Below lateral fissure | Superior temporal gyrus; inferior + medial temporal cortex | Hearing/language (superior temporal gyrus); complex visual ID (inferior temporal); memory (medial temporal) |
| **Occipital** | Most posterior | | Visual analysis |

- **Lobes are NOT functional units** — the cortex is a folded sheet.

#### Neocortex
- **Neocortex**: ~**90%** of human cortex; **six layers** (I–VI from surface); evolutionarily recent (Rakic, 2009).

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Pyramidal cells** | Large multipolar; pyramid-shaped body; large **apical dendrite**; long axon (Spruston, 2008) |
| **Stellate cells** | Small star-shaped interneurons; short or no axon |

**Four characteristics (Molyneaux et al., 2007):**
1. Neurons are pyramidal or stellate.
2. Six layers differ in cell size, density, pyramidal/stellate ratio.
3. Vertical axons/dendrites → **columnar organization** (vertical mini-circuits; Laughlin & Sejnowski, 2003).
4. Layer thickness varies: **Layer IV** (stellate) thick in **sensory cortex** (thalamic input); **Layer V** (pyramidal, projecting to brain stem/cord) thick in **motor cortex**.

#### Hippocampus
- **NOT neocortex** — only **three layers** (Forster, Ahao, & Frotscher, 2006); medial temporal lobe; sea-horse shaped; major role in **memory, especially spatial location**.

## 3.11 The Limbic System and the Basal Ganglia

### Limbic System
- **Limbic system**: midline circuit ringing the thalamus (limbic = ring); regulates motivated behaviors — the **four Fs**: **Fleeing, Feeding, Fighting, Sexual behavior**.

| Structure | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Amygdala** | Anterior temporal lobe; almond-shaped | Emotion, particularly **fear** (Swanson & Petrovich, 1998) |
| **Hippocampus** | Medial temporal lobe | Memory, especially spatial |
| **Cingulate cortex** | Cingulate gyrus, medial surface, above corpus callosum | Encircles dorsal thalamus |
| **Fornix** | Arc from hippocampus to septum/mammillary bodies | Major limbic tract |
| **Septum** | Midline nucleus at anterior tip of cingulate cortex | Linked to amygdala, hippocampus |
| **Mammillary bodies** | Inferior hypothalamus, behind pituitary | |

**Circuit**: Amygdala → Hippocampus → Cingulate cortex + Fornix → Septum + Mammillary bodies → back, completing the ring.

### Basal Ganglia

| Structure | Description |
|---|---|
| **Caudate** | Tail-like; sweeps from amygdala in nearly a full circle |
| **Putamen** | Connected to caudate via fiber bridges |
| **Striatum** | **Caudate + putamen** (striped) |
| **Globus pallidus** | Pale circular structure; medial to putamen |
| **Nucleus accumbens** | Medial ventral striatum; **rewarding effects of addictive drugs** and reinforcers |

- Role: **voluntary motor responses**. Key pathway: **substantia nigra → striatum** (dopaminergic).
- **Parkinson's disease**: deterioration of this pathway → rigidity, tremors, poverty of voluntary movement.

Notes: the amygdala is part of **both** the limbic system and basal ganglia; the globus pallidus originates from diencephalic tissue that migrates prenatally.

---

## 3.12 Evolutionary Perspective

- Cortical convolution relates to **body size**, not intellect; every large mammal has a highly convoluted cortex; most mammals are **lissencephalic**.
- In humans, the **telencephalon** shows the greatest developmental growth.

---

## 3.13 Clinical Implications Summary

1. **Cranial nerve assessment**: disruptions localize tumors/lesions.
2. **Hydrocephalus**: cerebral aqueduct blockage → ventricular/brain expansion.
3. **Parkinson's disease**: substantia nigra → striatum dopaminergic pathway deterioration → rigidity, tremors, poverty of movement.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Meninges (outer → inner)**: Dura mater → Arachnoid → Pia mater. Subarachnoid space (between arachnoid and pia) holds CSF and vessels.

2. **CSF**: produced by **choroid plexuses**; absorbed into **dural sinuses** → **jugular veins**.

3. **Hydrocephalus**: **cerebral aqueduct** blockage (3rd↔4th ventricle) → CSF buildup → brain expansion.

4. **BBB**: from **tightly packed** cerebral vessel cells; glucose **actively transported** through; drugs must penetrate it.

5. **Golgi vs. Nissl**: Golgi (1870s; dichromate + silver nitrate) stains **few neurons black** → shape; Nissl (1880s; cresyl violet) binds **cell bodies** → counting.

6. **Oligodendrocytes vs. Schwann**: oligodendrocytes (CNS, multiple segments, no regeneration) vs. Schwann (PNS, one segment, **can** regenerate) → mammalian regeneration restricted to the **PNS**.

7. **Dorsal vs. ventral root**: dorsal = **sensory (afferent)** unipolar, **dorsal root ganglia**, dorsal horns; ventral = **motor (efferent)** multipolar, ventral horns.

8. **Five divisions (anterior → posterior)**: Telencephalon → Diencephalon → Mesencephalon → Metencephalon → Myelencephalon.

9. **Colliculi**: superior = **visual**, inferior = **auditory**; both in the **tectum** (midbrain).

10. **Parkinson's**: **substantia nigra → striatum** dopaminergic pathway deterioration → rigidity, tremors, poverty of movement.

11. **Limbic "four Fs"**: Fleeing, Feeding, Fighting, Sexual. Key: amygdala (fear), hippocampus (spatial memory), hypothalamus, cingulate cortex, fornix, septum, mammillary bodies.

12. **Neocortex**: six layers; **Layer IV** (stellate) thickest in **sensory** cortex; **Layer V** (pyramidal) thickest in **motor** cortex; ~**90%** of human cortex.

13. **Nucleus accumbens**: medial ventral striatum; **reward** for addictive drugs and reinforcers.

14. **Hippocampus**: only **three layers**; sea-horse shaped; medial temporal lobe; **spatial location memory**.

15. **Astrocytes (modern view)**: not just glue — signaling, synapse formation (Jourdain et al., 2007), neural modulation (Rouach et al., 2008), axon maintenance (Edgar & Nave, 2009), glial circuits (Giaume et al., 2010), BBB role.
