# Chapter 3: Anatomy of the Nervous System

## Chapter Overview
This chapter surveys the structural organization of the nervous system from its gross divisions (CNS vs. PNS) down to the cellular level (neurons and glia), then covers major neuroanatomical techniques, directional terminology, and a region-by-region tour of the spinal cord and all five brain divisions. It lays the anatomical foundation for understanding brain function in later chapters.

---

## 3.1 General Layout of the Nervous System

### Major Divisions

- The nervous system is organized as **a system of twos** at every level.
- **Central nervous system (CNS)**: composed of the brain and the spinal cord.
- **Peripheral nervous system (PNS)**: all nervous tissue outside the brain and spinal cord.

### PNS Subdivisions

- **Somatic nervous system (SNS)**: interacts with the external environment.
  - **Afferent nerves**: carry sensory signals *toward* the CNS.
  - **Efferent nerves**: carry motor signals *away from* the CNS.
- **Autonomic nervous system (ANS)**: regulates the body's *internal* environment (visceral organs, glands, blood vessels). Also has afferent and efferent divisions.
  - Efferent ANS has two branches: **sympathetic** and **parasympathetic**.

### Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic Nervous System

| Feature | Sympathetic | Parasympathetic |
|---|---|---|
| Spinal cord origin | Thoracic and lumbar regions | Cranial nerves and sacral spinal cord |
| Synapse location (2nd-stage neuron) | Far from target organ | Near (or at) target organ |
| 2nd-stage neuron length | Long | Very short |
| Primary function | Stimulate, organize, mobilize energy (fight-or-flight) | Conserve energy (rest-and-digest) |
| Psychological state | Arousal | Relaxation |

**Three conventional principles** of autonomic function (with qualifications, per Guyenet, 2006):
1. Sympathetic = mobilizes energy in threatening situations; parasympathetic = conserves energy.
2. Each target organ receives *opposing* sympathetic and parasympathetic input; activity controlled by relative levels.
3. Sympathetic changes indicate psychological *arousal*; parasympathetic changes indicate psychological *relaxation*.
- Note: significant exceptions and qualifications exist to all three principles.

### Cranial Nerves

- Most PNS nerves project from the spinal cord, but **12 pairs of cranial nerves** project directly from the brain.
- Numbered sequentially (I through XII) from front to back (anterior to posterior).
- **Only two purely sensory cranial nerves**: **olfactory nerves (I)** and **optic nerves (II)**.
- Most cranial nerves contain both sensory and motor fibers.
- **Longest cranial nerves**: **vagus nerves (X)** — contain motor and sensory fibers to/from the gut.
- All autonomic motor fibers in cranial nerves are **parasympathetic**.
- **Clinical importance**: disruptions of specific cranial nerve functions provide clues about the location and extent of tumors and other brain pathology. Neurologists routinely assess cranial nerve functions for diagnosis.

## 3.2 Meninges, Ventricles, and Cerebrospinal Fluid

### Meninges

The CNS is the most protected organ system in the body: encased in bone and covered by three protective membranes called the **meninges** (singular: **meninx**).

From outermost to innermost:
1. **Dura mater** ("tough mother"): thick, tough outermost meninx.
2. **Arachnoid membrane** ("spider-weblike membrane"): fine middle meninx.
   - Below it lies the **subarachnoid space**: contains large blood vessels and CSF.
3. **Pia mater** ("pious mother"): delicate innermost meninx; adheres directly to the CNS surface.

### Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

- **Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)**: fills the subarachnoid space, the central canal of the spinal cord, and the cerebral ventricles.
- **Central canal**: small central channel running the entire length of the spinal cord.
- **Cerebral ventricles**: four large internal chambers of the brain:
  1. Left lateral ventricle
  2. Right lateral ventricle
  3. Third ventricle
  4. Fourth ventricle
- Subarachnoid space, central canal, and cerebral ventricles are **interconnected** via openings — forming a single reservoir.
- **Functions of CSF**: supports and cushions the brain. Clinical evidence: patients who have CSF drained suffer severe headaches and stabbing pain on head movement.
- **Production**: continuously produced by the **choroid plexuses** — networks of capillaries protruding into the ventricles from the pia mater.
- **Absorption**: continuously absorbed from the subarachnoid space into **dural sinuses** (large blood-filled spaces running through the dura mater), which drain into the jugular veins.

### Hydrocephalus

- **Hydrocephalus** ("water head"): CSF flow blocked — usually near the **cerebral aqueduct** (connects the 3rd and 4th ventricles) or another narrow channel.
- Result: fluid buildup in ventricles causes the entire brain to expand.
- Treatment: drain excess fluid; try to remove the obstruction.

## 3.3 Blood-Brain Barrier

- **Blood-brain barrier (BBB)** (Banerjee & Bhat, 2007): mechanism impeding passage of many toxic substances from blood into the brain.
- **Structural basis**: cerebral blood vessel wall cells are *tightly packed* (unlike loosely packed cells in the rest of the body), creating a barrier — particularly to proteins and other large molecules (Abbott, Ronnback, & Hannson, 2005).
- **Drug implications**: therapeutic or recreational drug influence on brain activity depends on ease of BBB penetration (Loscher & Potschka, 2005).
- **Exceptions**:
  - Molecules critical for normal brain function (e.g., **glucose**) are *actively transported* through.
  - Some brain areas allow certain large molecules to pass through unimpeded.
- **Astrocytes** also play a role in BBB function (covering outer surfaces of cerebral blood vessels).

## 3.4 Anatomy of Neurons

### Definition
- **Neurons**: cells specialized for the reception, conduction, and transmission of electrochemical signals. Incredible variety of shapes and sizes (Nelson, Sugino, & Hempel, 2006).

### External Anatomy of a Typical Neuron

| Structure | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Cell membrane** | Semipermeable membrane enclosing the neuron |
| **Dendrites** | Short processes from cell body; receive most synaptic contacts from other neurons |
| **Cell body (soma)** | Metabolic center of the neuron |
| **Axon hillock** | Cone-shaped region at the junction between axon and cell body |
| **Axon** | Long, narrow process projecting from cell body |
| **Myelin** | Fatty insulation around many axons |
| **Nodes of Ranvier** | Gaps between sections of myelin (pronounced RAHN-vee-yay) |
| **Buttons (terminal buttons)** | Buttonlike axon branch endings; release chemicals into synapses |
| **Synapses** | Gaps between adjacent neurons across which chemical signals are transmitted |

### Internal Anatomy of a Typical Neuron

| Structure | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Nucleus** | Spherical DNA-containing structure of the cell body |
| **Cytoplasm** | Clear internal fluid of the cell |
| **Endoplasmic reticulum** | Folded membrane system; rough portions (ribosomes) = protein synthesis; smooth portions = fat synthesis |
| **Ribosomes** | Structures on which proteins are synthesized; located on endoplasmic reticulum |
| **Mitochondria** | Sites of aerobic (oxygen-consuming) energy release |
| **Golgi complex** | Connected membrane system packaging molecules in vesicles |
| **Microtubules** | Tubules for rapid transport of material throughout neurons |
| **Synaptic vesicles** | Spherical membrane packages storing neurotransmitters near synapses |
| **Neurotransmitters** | Molecules released from active neurons that influence other cells |

### Neuron Cell Membrane
- Composed of a **lipid bilayer** (Piomelli, Astarita, & Rapaka, 2007): two layers of fat molecules.
- Embedded proteins:
  - **Channel proteins**: specific molecules can pass through.
  - **Signal proteins**: transfer signals into the neuron when molecules bind externally.

### Classes of Neurons (by number of processes)

| Class | Definition | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Multipolar neuron** | More than two processes from cell body | Most neurons are multipolar |
| **Unipolar neuron** | One process from cell body | |
| **Bipolar neuron** | Two processes from cell body | |
| **Interneurons** | Short axon or no axon | Integrate activity within a single brain structure only |

### Neurons and Neuroanatomical Structures

| Location | Cell Bodies = | Axon Bundles = |
|---|---|---|
| CNS | **Nuclei** (singular: nucleus) | **Tracts** |
| PNS | **Ganglia** (singular: ganglion) | **Nerves** |

- "Nucleus" has two meanings: (1) DNA-containing structure within a cell; (2) cluster of cell bodies in the CNS.

## 3.5 Glial Cells: The Forgotten Cells

- **Glial cells**: non-neuronal cells throughout the nervous system.
- Widely reported as outnumbering neurons 10:1, but Azevedo et al. (2009) showed overall numbers are approximately **equal**; glia predominate only in some structures.

### Four Types of Glial Cells

#### 1. Oligodendrocytes
- Location: **CNS**.
- Extensions wrap around axons; rich in **myelin** → form **myelin sheaths** → increase speed and efficiency of axonal conduction.
- One oligodendrocyte provides **several myelin segments**, often on more than one axon.
- Do **NOT** guide axonal regeneration after damage.

#### 2. Schwann Cells
- Location: **PNS**.
- Same myelination function as oligodendrocytes, but for PNS.
- Each Schwann cell constitutes **one myelin segment**.
- Key unique feature: **only Schwann cells can guide axonal regeneration** after damage.
- This is why effective axonal regeneration in mammals is restricted to the PNS.

#### 3. Microglia
- **Smaller** than other glia (hence the name).
- Respond to injury/disease: multiply, engulf cellular debris, trigger **inflammatory responses** (Nimmerjahn, Kirchhoff, & Helmchen, 2005).

#### 4. Astrocytes
- **Largest** glial cells.
- **Star-shaped** (astron = star).
- Extensions cover outer surfaces of cerebral blood vessels and contact neuron cell bodies.
- Role in **BBB**: allow passage of some chemicals from blood into CNS neurons; block others (Abbott, Ronnback, & Hannson, 2006).
- Old view: structural support only — nutrition, waste clearance, physical matrix ("glia" = glue).
- **Modern view** (Kettenmann & Verkhratsky, 2008): astrocytes:
  - Send and receive signals from neurons and other glial cells.
  - Control establishment and maintenance of synapses (Jourdain et al., 2007).
  - Modulate neural activity (Rouach et al., 2008).
  - Maintain axon function (Edgar & Nave, 2009).
  - Participate in glial circuits (Giaume et al., 2010).

### Oligodendrocytes vs. Schwann Cells Summary

| Feature | Oligodendrocytes | Schwann Cells |
|---|---|---|
| Location | CNS | PNS |
| Myelin segments per cell | Several (multiple axons) | One |
| Axonal regeneration guidance | No | Yes |

## 3.6 Neuroanatomical Techniques

### The Problem
Neurons are tightly packed and their processes intricately intertwined. Different preparation methods reveal different aspects of neuronal structure; combining them builds a complete picture.

### Golgi Stain
- Discovered **accidentally** by **Camillo Golgi** (Italian physician) in the early **1870s** (Rapport, 2005).
- While attempting to stain meninges using **potassium dichromate + silver nitrate**, silver chromate invaded a few neurons per slice and stained them entirely **black**.
- Significance: first method to see individual neurons (in silhouette).
- Best use: when **overall shape** of neurons is of interest.
- Limitation: only a few neurons stain (silhouette only; no internal details; only part of neuron captured per slice).

### Nissl Stain
- Developed by **Franz Nissl** (German psychiatrist) in the **1880s**.
- Most common dye: **cresyl violet**.
- Binds effectively only to structures in **neuron cell bodies** (penetrates all cells but selective binding).
- Best use: **counting neuron cell bodies** in an area; revealing gross brain structure (layers of cell bodies) under low magnification.

### Electron Microscopy
- Reveals **fine anatomical details** beyond the ~1,500x limit of light microscopy.
- Method: coat thin neural slices with electron-absorbing substance; pass electron beam through tissue onto photographic film → **electron micrograph**.
- **Scanning electron microscope**: three-dimensional images, but less magnification.
- Limitation: extreme detail makes it hard to visualize general neuroanatomical structure.

### Neuroanatomical Tracing Techniques

| Type | Direction | Method | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anterograde tracing** | Forward (from cell body to terminals) | Inject chemical taken up by cell bodies; transported forward along axons | Trace paths of axons projecting AWAY from a particular area |
| **Retrograde tracing** | Backward (from terminals to cell bodies) | Inject chemical taken up by terminal buttons; transported backward to cell bodies | Trace paths of axons projecting INTO a particular area |

### Three Primary Axes

| Term | Synonym | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Anterior** | Rostral | Toward the nose end |
| **Posterior** | Caudal | Toward the tail end |
| **Dorsal** | | Toward the back surface or top of the head |
| **Ventral** | | Toward the chest surface or bottom of the head |

### Human Upright Posture Complication
- In humans, cerebral hemisphere directions are rotated 90 degrees relative to spinal cord and brain stem.
- Top of head AND back of body are both called **dorsal**; bottom of head AND front of body are both called **ventral** (even though they point in different directions).
- To avoid this confusion: **superior** (top of primate head) and **inferior** (bottom of primate head) are used.

## 3.8 Spinal Cord

### Cross-Sectional Structure
- **Gray matter**: inner H-shaped core; cell bodies and unmyelinated interneurons.
- **White matter**: surrounding area; myelinated axons (myelin gives white sheen).
- **Dorsal horns**: dorsal arms of spinal gray matter.
- **Ventral horns**: ventral arms of spinal gray matter.

### Spinal Nerves and Roots
- **31 pairs** of spinal nerves (one left, one right at each of 31 levels).
- Each spinal nerve divides near the cord and joins via two roots:

#### Dorsal Root
- Contains **sensory (afferent) unipolar neurons**.
- Cell bodies grouped just outside the cord: **dorsal root ganglia**.
- Synaptic terminals in the **dorsal horns**.

#### Ventral Root
- Contains **motor (efferent) multipolar neurons**.
- Cell bodies in the **ventral horns**.
- Somatic NS: projects to skeletal muscles.
- Autonomic NS: projects to ganglia, which project to internal organs.

## 3.9 Five Major Divisions of the Brain

### Embryological Basis
- CNS begins as a fluid-filled tube.
- Three initial swellings: **forebrain**, **midbrain**, **hindbrain**.
- Before birth, these become **five** (forebrain splits into two; hindbrain splits into two).

### The Five Divisions (Anterior to Posterior)

| Division | Adult Structure | Common Name |
|---|---|---|
| **Telencephalon** | Cerebral hemispheres | (on top; largest) |
| **Diencephalon** | Thalamus + hypothalamus | |
| **Mesencephalon** | Midbrain structures | Midbrain |
| **Metencephalon** | Pons + cerebellum | |
| **Myelencephalon** | Medulla | Medulla |

- The four divisions below the telencephalon = collectively the **brain stem**.
- Memory mnemonic (Pinel): Telencephalon is on top; the others follow below in near-alphabetical order.

## 3.10 Major Structures of the Brain

### Myelencephalon (Medulla)
- Most **posterior** division.
- Largely composed of **tracts** carrying signals between brain and body.
- Key structure: **Reticular formation**
  - ~**100 tiny nuclei** in the central core of the brain stem (from posterior myelencephalon to anterior midbrain boundary).
  - Named for netlike appearance (**reticulum** = little net).
  - Also called **reticular activating system (RAS)** — but this is misleading; it is involved in many functions, not just arousal.
  - Functions: sleep, attention, movement, muscle tone maintenance, cardiac/circulatory/respiratory reflexes.

### Metencephalon
Contains ascending/descending tracts, part of the reticular formation, plus two major structures:

#### Pons
- Bulge on the brain stem's **ventral surface** created by tracts and reticular formation.

#### Cerebellum ("little brain")
- Large, convoluted structure on the brain stem's **dorsal surface**.
- Primary function: **sensorimotor control** (damage = loss of precise movement control and adaptation).
- Also involved in cognitive functions (decision making, language use), so functions extend beyond sensorimotor.

### Mesencephalon (Midbrain)
Two divisions:

#### Tectum ("roof")
- **Dorsal** surface of midbrain.
- Composed of **colliculi** (little hills) — two pairs:
  - **Inferior colliculi** (posterior pair): **auditory** function.
  - **Superior colliculi** (anterior pair): **visual** function.
- In lower vertebrates: entirely visual (= **optic tectum**).

#### Tegmentum
- **Ventral** to tectum.
- Contains reticular formation + three notable structures:

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| **Periaqueductal gray (PAG)** | Gray matter around cerebral aqueduct; mediates analgesic (pain-reducing) effects of **opiate drugs** |
| **Substantia nigra** ("black substance") | Sensorimotor system; source of dopaminergic projection to striatum |
| **Red nucleus** | Sensorimotor system |

### Diencephalon
Two structures:

#### Thalamus
- Large, two-lobed structure at the **top of the brain stem**.
- One lobe on each side of the third ventricle; joined by the **massa intermedia**.
- Surface has white **lamina** (layers of myelinated axons).
- Most nuclei project to the **cortex**.
- **Sensory relay nuclei**: receive sensory signals, process them, relay to appropriate sensory cortex.
  - All receive **feedback** from the cortex areas they project to.

| Sensory Relay Nucleus | System |
|---|---|
| **Lateral geniculate nuclei** | Visual |
| **Medial geniculate nuclei** | Auditory |
| **Ventral posterior nuclei** | Somatosensory |

#### Hypothalamus
- Just **below the anterior thalamus** (hypo = below).
- Regulates several **motivated behaviors**: eating, sleep, sexual behavior.
- Controls hormone release from the **pituitary gland** (dangling from hypothalamus on ventral brain surface).
  - Etymology: "snot gland" (pituitary) — discovered in a gelatinous state behind the nose of an unembalmed cadaver; incorrectly assumed to be the main source of nasal mucus.

Three structures on the inferior surface of the hypothalamus:
1. **Optic chiasm**: point where optic nerves come together; X-shaped because some axons **decussate** (cross over).
   - **Contralateral** fibers = decussating.
   - **Ipsilateral** fibers = nondecussating.
2. **Pituitary gland**: hangs from the hypothalamus.
3. **Mammillary bodies**: pair of spherical nuclei just behind the pituitary; part of the hypothalamus and limbic system.

### Telencephalon
**Largest** brain division; mediates:
- Voluntary movement
- Sensory interpretation
- Complex cognitive processes (learning, speaking, problem solving)

#### Cerebral Cortex
- **Cerebral cortex** ("cerebral bark"): tissue layer covering the cerebral hemispheres.
- Mainly small, **unmyelinated neurons** → gray ("gray matter").
- Beneath the cortex: large **myelinated axons** → white ("white matter").
- Deeply **convoluted** in humans — increases surface area without increasing brain volume.
- Most mammals are **lissencephalic** (smooth-brained); cortical convolutions relate to **body size**, not intellectual capacity.

**Cortical terminology:**
- **Fissures**: large furrows.
- **Sulci** (singular: sulcus): small furrows.
- **Gyri** (singular: gyrus): ridges between fissures/sulci.

**Key fissures:**
- **Longitudinal fissure**: largest; almost completely separates the two hemispheres.
- **Corpus callosum**: largest **cerebral commissure** (tract connecting the hemispheres).
- **Central fissure**: major lateral landmark.
- **Lateral fissure**: major lateral landmark.

**Four Lobes and Functions:**

| Lobe | Location | Key Structures | Functions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Frontal** | Anterior to central fissure | Precentral gyrus | Motor control (precentral gyrus + adjacent cortex); complex cognition (planning, evaluating outcomes, assessing others' behavior) in anterior frontal cortex |
| **Parietal** | Posterior to central fissure | Postcentral gyrus | Body sensations (postcentral gyrus); perceiving object/body location; directing attention |
| **Temporal** | Below lateral fissure | Superior temporal gyrus; inferior temporal cortex; medial temporal cortex | Hearing and language (superior temporal gyrus); complex visual pattern identification (inferior temporal cortex); certain memory (medial temporal cortex) |
| **Occipital** | Most posterior | | Visual analysis |

- **Cerebral lobes are NOT functional units.** The cortex is a flat sheet folded during development.

#### Neocortex
- **Neocortex**: ~**90%** of human cerebral cortex; six-layered; evolutionarily recent (Douglas & Martin, 2004; Rakic, 2009).
- Layers numbered **I through VI** from the surface.

**Two main neocortical neuron types:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Pyramidal cells** | Large multipolar; pyramid-shaped cell body; large **apical dendrite** from apex to cortex surface; very long axon (Spruston, 2008) |
| **Stellate cells** | Small star-shaped interneurons; short or no axon |

**Four key characteristics of neocortical anatomy (Molyneaux et al., 2007):**
1. Neurons = pyramidal or stellate.
2. Six layers differ in cell body size, density, and pyramidal/stellate proportion.
3. Long axons and dendrites course **vertically** through neocortex → **columnar organization**: vertical columns form mini-circuits performing single functions (Laughlin & Sejnowski, 2003).
4. Layer thickness varies across areas:
   - **Layer IV** (stellate cells): extremely thick in **sensory cortex** (receives thalamic sensory input).
   - **Layer V** (pyramidal cells projecting to brain stem/spinal cord): extremely thick in **motor cortex**.

#### Hippocampus
- **NOT neocortex** — only **three major layers** (Forster, Ahao, & Frotscher, 2006).
- Located at medial edge of cerebral cortex; folds back on itself in the **medial temporal lobe**.
- Cross-section resembles a **sea horse** (hippocampus = sea horse).
- Function: major role in certain kinds of **memory**, particularly **spatial location memory**.

## 3.11 The Limbic System and the Basal Ganglia

### Limbic System
- **Limbic system**: circuit of midline structures circling the thalamus (limbic = ring).
- Involved in regulation of **motivated behaviors** — the "**four Fs of motivation**":
  - **Fleeing, Feeding, Fighting, and Sexual behavior.**

**Major structures:**

| Structure | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Amygdala** | Anterior temporal lobe; almond-shaped (amygdala = almond; pronounced a-MIG-dah-lah) | Emotion, particularly **fear** (Swanson & Petrovich, 1998) |
| **Hippocampus** | Medial temporal lobe, beneath thalamus | Memory, especially spatial |
| **Cingulate cortex** | Cingulate gyrus on medial surface of hemispheres; just superior to corpus callosum | Encircles dorsal thalamus (cingulate = encircling) |
| **Fornix** | Major tract of the limbic system; arc from dorsal hippocampus to septum and mammillary bodies (fornix = arc) | Major connecting tract |
| **Septum** | Midline nucleus at anterior tip of cingulate cortex | Connected to amygdala and hippocampus via tracts |
| **Mammillary bodies** | Inferior surface of hypothalamus, behind pituitary | |

**Limbic circuit**: Amygdala → Hippocampus → Cingulate cortex + Fornix → Septum + Mammillary bodies → (back via tracts) → completing the ring.

### Basal Ganglia

**Structures:**

| Structure | Description |
|---|---|
| **Caudate** | Tail-like structure (caudate = tail-like) sweeping from amygdala, forming almost a complete circle |
| **Putamen** (pew-TAY-men) | Connected to caudate via fiber bridges; both have striped appearance |
| **Striatum** | **Caudate + putamen** (striped structure) |
| **Globus pallidus** ("pale globe") | Pale circular structure; medial to putamen, between putamen and thalamus |
| **Nucleus accumbens** | Medial ventral striatum; role in **rewarding effects of addictive drugs** and other reinforcers |

- Basal ganglia role: **voluntary motor responses**.
- Key pathway: **substantia nigra (midbrain)** → **striatum** (dopaminergic).
- **Parkinson's disease**: deterioration of this substantia nigra-to-striatum pathway → rigidity, tremors, poverty of voluntary movement.

**Note**: amygdala is part of **both** the limbic system and the basal ganglia.
**Note**: globus pallidus originates from diencephalic tissue that migrates into its telencephalic location during prenatal development.

## 3.12 Evolutionary Perspective

- Cortical convolutions appear related more to **body size** than to intellectual capacity.
- Every large mammal has an extremely convoluted cortex.
- Most mammals are **lissencephalic** (smooth-brained).
- In humans, the **telencephalon** undergoes the greatest growth during development compared to other species.

---

## 3.13 Clinical Implications Summary

1. **Cranial nerve assessment** in neurological diagnosis: location and extent of tumors/lesions inferred from which cranial nerves are disrupted.
2. **Hydrocephalus**: blockage of cerebral aqueduct (or other CSF channels) by tumor → ventricular expansion → brain enlargement.
3. **Parkinson's disease**: deterioration of the dopaminergic pathway from substantia nigra to striatum → rigidity, tremors, poverty of voluntary movement.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Meninges order (outer to inner)**: Dura mater → Arachnoid membrane → Pia mater. The innermost is **pia mater**; subarachnoid space is between arachnoid and pia mater (contains CSF and blood vessels).

2. **CSF production vs. absorption**: Produced by **choroid plexuses** (capillary networks in ventricles); absorbed into **dural sinuses**; drains into **jugular veins**.

3. **Hydrocephalus mechanism**: Blockage of the **cerebral aqueduct** (connecting 3rd and 4th ventricles) → CSF buildup → ventricular expansion → brain expansion.

4. **Blood-brain barrier**: Created by **tightly packed** cerebral blood vessel wall cells (unlike the rest of the body). Glucose is **actively transported** through (exception). Drugs that affect the brain must penetrate the BBB.

5. **Golgi vs. Nissl stain distinction (high MCQ target)**:
   - Golgi (Camillo Golgi, 1870s): potassium dichromate + silver nitrate; stains **few neurons entirely black**; reveals silhouette/shape; for studying overall shape.
   - Nissl (Franz Nissl, 1880s): cresyl violet; binds to **all neuron cell bodies**; used to **count neurons**.

6. **Oligodendrocytes vs. Schwann cells (high MCQ target)**:
   - Oligodendrocytes: CNS; multiple myelin segments per cell; **cannot** guide axonal regeneration.
   - Schwann cells: PNS; one myelin segment per cell; **can** guide axonal regeneration.
   - Axonal regeneration in mammals is therefore restricted to the **PNS**.

7. **Dorsal root vs. ventral root**:
   - Dorsal root = **sensory (afferent)** = unipolar neurons = **dorsal root ganglia** outside cord = synapses in dorsal horns.
   - Ventral root = **motor (efferent)** = multipolar neurons = cell bodies in **ventral horns**.

8. **Five brain divisions anterior to posterior**: Telencephalon → Diencephalon → Mesencephalon → Metencephalon → Myelencephalon.

9. **Colliculi functions**: Superior colliculi = **visual**; Inferior colliculi = **auditory**. Both in the **tectum** of the mesencephalon (midbrain).

10. **Parkinson's disease**: Deterioration of the dopaminergic pathway from **substantia nigra** (midbrain) to **striatum** (caudate + putamen). Symptoms: rigidity, tremors, poverty of voluntary movement.

11. **Limbic system "four Fs"**: **Fleeing, Feeding, Fighting, Sexual** behavior. Key structures: amygdala (emotion/fear), hippocampus (spatial memory), hypothalamus (motivated behaviors), cingulate cortex, fornix (major tract), septum, mammillary bodies.

12. **Neocortex layers and specialization**: Six layers (I-VI). **Layer IV** (stellate) = thickest in sensory cortex. **Layer V** (pyramidal) = thickest in motor cortex. ~**90%** of human cerebral cortex is neocortex.

13. **Nucleus accumbens**: Medial ventral striatum; role in **rewarding effects of addictive drugs** and other reinforcers.

14. **Hippocampus**: Only **three layers** (not neocortex); sea-horse shaped; medial temporal lobe; primarily involved in **spatial location memory**.

15. **Astrocytes — modern view**: Not just structural support (glia = glue). Now known to: send/receive signals from neurons and glia; control synapse formation (Jourdain et al., 2007); modulate neural activity (Rouach et al., 2008); maintain axon function (Edgar & Nave, 2009); participate in glial circuits (Giaume et al., 2010). Also play a role in the BBB.
