# Chapter 6: The Visual System

The visual system does not copy the world; it constructs a 3D perception from tiny, distorted 2D retinal images. Understanding vision combines neurophysiology with psychophysics. This chapter traces the pathway from photon to primary visual cortex and how neurons encode edges and color.

---

## The Case of Mrs. Richards: Fortification Illusions

- Reported by **Whitman Richards (1971)** (his wife). She had **migraines**; ~**20% of migraine sufferers** get **fortification illusions** before attacks.
- **Features:** begin as a **gray area of blindness** near visual-field center; over ~20 min expand into a **horseshoe shape** with a **zigzag pattern of flickering lines** at the edge (like a fortification plan); lines thicken and expansion **accelerates** toward the periphery, where the headache begins.
- Sir **George Biddell Airy** (astronomer) published nearly identical drawings in **1870**, confirming the illusions are a consistent product of the visual system. Their features are explained by primary visual cortex organization (revisited later).

## 6.1 Light Enters the Eye and Reaches the Retina

### Nature of Light
- **No animal sees in complete darkness** (vision needs reflected light).
- **Photons**: discrete energy particles at ~300,000 km/s; light also behaves as **waves** (wave-particle duality).
- **Visible light**: electromagnetic energy, **380-760 nm**.
- **Wavelength** → perceived **color (hue)**; **intensity** → perceived **brightness** (physical vs. perceptual).
- Species-specific: rattlesnakes detect **infrared**; some animals detect **ultraviolet**.

### The Pupil and the Lens

| Structure | Description | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Iris** | Contractile band; gives eye color | Regulates light entering |
| **Pupil** | Hole in the iris | Light entry |
| **Lens** | Behind the pupil | Focuses light on retina |
| **Ciliary muscles** | Adjust lens tension | Accommodation |

- **Pupil size** balances **sensitivity** (detect dim objects) vs. **acuity** (fine detail).
- **Constricted** (bright): sharper, greater **depth of focus**, higher acuity. **Dilated** (dim): more light, less acuity.
- **Accommodation**: **ciliary muscles** adjust lens curvature. Near objects → lens **rounder** (more refraction); far objects → **flattened**.

### Eye Position and Binocular Disparity
- **Side-facing eyes** (most vertebrates/prey): see nearly all directions. **Front-facing eyes** (predators, primates): same scene → **depth perception**.
- **Convergence**: eyes turn inward to project a point onto corresponding retinal areas; greatest for close objects.
- **Binocular disparity**: difference in the same image's position on the two retinas; greater for close objects; the basis for constructing **one 3D perception from two 2D images**.

> **Check It Out:** touching fingertips at arm's length while focusing on a distant wall creates a phantom "cocktail sausage" (binocular disparity); closing one eye removes it.

## 6.2 The Retina and Translation of Light into Neural Signals

### Five Layers of Retinal Neurons (inner → outer relative to light)
1. **Retinal ganglion cells** (innermost; axons exit the eye)
2. **Amacrine cells** (lateral communication)
3. **Bipolar cells**
4. **Horizontal cells** (lateral communication)
5. **Receptors** (cones and rods; at the back)

- Over **50 types** of retinal neurons (Dacey, 2004; Masland, 2001; Wässle, 2004); communicate via **chemical synapses AND electrical gap junctions** (Bloomfield & Völgyi, 2009).

### Inside-Out Arrangement
- Light passes through four layers before reaching receptors → two problems: (1) light is **distorted**; (2) ganglion axons exit, creating a **gap** in the receptor layer.

### Fovea
- **Fovea**: central indentation (~0.33 cm) for **high-acuity vision**; thinned ganglion layer **reduces distortion**; **no rods, only cones**.

### Blind Spot
- **Blind spot**: gap where ganglion axons exit as the **optic nerve**; no perception from this area.

### Completion / Filling In
- The visual system uses surrounding receptors to **fill in gaps** (**completion**); it extracts **edges** and constructs full perception in cortex.
- **Surface interpolation**: inferring large-surface appearance (color/brightness of unpatterned surfaces are filled in, not directly perceived).

> KEY PRINCIPLE: the visual system extracts edges and constructs a perception, not a photograph.

### Cone and Rod Vision

| Feature | Cones | Rods |
|---------|-------|------|
| Location | Concentrated in fovea | Absent from fovea; predominate in periphery |
| Vision | **Photopic** (daylight) | **Scotopic** (dim) |
| Acuity | High | Low |
| Color | Yes | No |
| Sensitivity | Lower | Higher |
| Convergence | Low (few cones → 1 ganglion cell) | High (hundreds of rods → 1 ganglion cell) |
| Peak density | At fovea | **20° from fovea center** |

- Observation (1866): diurnal species have **cone-only** retinas; nocturnal species **rod-only**.

**Duplexity theory:** cones and rods mediate different vision.
- **Photopic** (cone): good light; color, high acuity.
- **Scotopic** (rod): dim light; high sensitivity, low acuity, no color.

**Convergence consequences:** rod system summates dim light from many rods → high **sensitivity** but low **acuity** (brain can't localize which rod); cone system has little convergence → lower sensitivity but high acuity.

**Distribution:** fovea = cones only; rods peak at **20°** from center. **Nasal hemiretina** (next to nose) has more rods than **temporal hemiretina** (next to temples).

### Spectral Sensitivity
- **Spectral sensitivity curve**: relative brightness of equal-intensity lights across wavelengths. Humans have two:

| Condition | Peak | System |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Photopic | ~**560 nm** | Cones |
| Scotopic | ~**500 nm** | Rods |

- **Purkinje effect** (Jan Purkinje, **1825**): brightness shift from photopic to scotopic. Daylight: yellow/red brighter (560 nm peak). Dim: blue appears brighter gray (500 nm peak); scene appears in **shades of gray** (no scotopic color).

### Eye Movement
- **Visual field**: area visible at a moment. **Temporal integration** explains why blinking doesn't blank the world.
- **Fixational eye movements** (involuntary): **tremor** (tiny high-frequency oscillations), **drifts** (slow wandering), **saccades** (rapid jerks).
- If a retinal image is **artificially stabilized**, it **fades and disappears** (visual neurons respond to change); fixational movements keep images moving.

### Visual Transduction
- **Transduction**: energy conversion. **Visual transduction**: light → neural signals by receptors.
- **Rhodopsin**: red rod **photopigment** (extracted **1876**); **bleached** by intense light, regained in darkness; absorption is the **first step in rod vision**; a **G-protein coupled receptor** to light. Its absorption spectrum matches the **scotopic sensitivity curve**.

| Condition | Na+ Channels | Rod | Glutamate |
|-----------|----------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Darkness** | Partially open (cyclic GMP) | Slightly depolarized | Continuous release |
| **Light** | Closed (rhodopsin bleached → cascade) | Hyperpolarized | Reduced |

- Principle: **signals are often transmitted by inhibition** (light → hyperpolarization → less glutamate).

## 6.3 From Retina to Primary Visual Cortex

### Retina-Geniculate-Striate Pathways
- **Retina-geniculate-striate pathways** (primary visual pathways): retina → LGN (thalamus) → primary visual cortex; carry ~**90% of ganglion axons**.

**Visual field laterality:** **left visual field → right primary visual cortex**, via the **temporal hemiretina of the right eye** (ipsilateral) and the **nasal hemiretina of the left eye** (contralateral, crossing at the optic chiasm). Reverse for the right field.

- **Optic chiasm**: nasal hemiretina fibers **decussate**; temporal fibers stay ipsilateral.
- **Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)**: in the **thalamus**; **6 layers** (3 from each eye); receives only **contralateral visual field** input; most projections end in **lower layer IV** of cortex.
- **Striate cortex (V1, primary visual cortex)**: named for the **stripe** where LGN axons terminate.

### Retinotopic Organization
- **Retinotopic organization**: each level is a **map of the retina**; adjacent retinal areas → adjacent neurons.
- **Cortical magnification**: ~**25%** of V1 is devoted to the small **fovea**.

> **KEY STUDY — Dobelle, Mladejovsky, & Girvin (1974):** cortical electrodes arranged in a cross made blind patients see a **glowing cross** — confirms retinotopic organization.

### The M and P Channels

| Feature | **P (Parvocellular)** | **M (Magnocellular)** |
|---------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| LGN layers | Top **4** | Bottom **2** |
| Cell size | **Small** | **Large** |
| Input | **Cones** | **Rods** |
| Responds to | Color, fine detail, slow/stationary | **Movement** |

- P and M neurons project to different sites in lower layer IV, then to different parts of visual cortex.

## 6.4 Seeing Edges

- **Edges** (where two areas of an image meet) are the most informative feature — a contrast, not a thing. Focus here: **brightness contrast**.

### Lateral Inhibition and Contrast Enhancement
- **Mach bands**: illusory bright/dark stripes at edges (named after Ernst Mach); make the bright side look brighter and dark side darker → **enhance contrast**; created by the visual system, not the stimulus.

> **Check It Out:** isolating each stripe (covering neighbors) shows each is homogeneous — Mach bands vanish, then reappear.

- Classic studies: **horseshoe crab** (Ratliff, 1972), whose lateral eye has large receptors (**ommatidia**), each with its own axon.
- **Lateral inhibition**: a firing receptor inhibits its neighbors; strongest from intensely lit receptors onto immediate neighbors.

**Mechanism (bright-dark edge):** receptor **D** (bright side, by the edge) gets full stimulation but **less lateral inhibition** (dim neighbors) → fires **more** (bright band). Receptor **E** (dark side, by the edge) gets low stimulation but **more lateral inhibition** (bright neighbors) → fires **less** (dark band). Result: Mach bands.

> KEY PRINCIPLE: perception of edges is **better than physical reality** — a nervous-system operation.

### Receptive Fields of Visual Neurons
- **David Hubel** and **Torsten Wiesel** (Nobel Prize 1981): recorded single neurons in **cats and monkeys** — positioned a microelectrode, paralyzed eye muscles, identified each neuron's **receptive field**, and characterized the stimulus that maximally drove it, working from receptors up.
- **Receptive field**: area of the visual field within which a stimulus can influence a neuron's firing.

### Receptive Fields: Retina-Geniculate-Striate System
Recorded from ganglion cells → LGN → lower layer IV (Hubel & Wiesel, 1979).

**Four commonalities at all three levels:**
1. **Foveal receptive fields smaller** than peripheral.
2. **Circular** receptive fields.
3. **Monocular** (one eye only).
4. Often an **excitatory** and an **inhibitory** area with a circular boundary.

| Cell Type | Center (light on) | Surround (light on) |
|-----------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **On-center** | ON firing | Inhibition + OFF firing when off |
| **Off-center** | Inhibition + OFF firing when off | ON firing |

- Both respond best to **center-surround contrast**; **diffuse illumination** has little effect → these neurons signal **brightness contrast**.

> KEY NOTE: most visual neurons are **spontaneously active**; Arieli et al. (1996): cortical activity level at stimulus onset shapes the response — a route by which **cognition influences perception**.

### Simple Cortical Cells
- In striate cortex but NOT lower layer IV. **Simple cells**: receptive fields divisible into antagonistic **straight-line** on/off regions.
- Respond best to **bars of light** (or dark bars, or single straight edges); **position-specific**, **orientation-specific**, **rectangular** fields, all **monocular**; unresponsive to diffuse light.

### Complex Cortical Cells
- **Complex cells** are more numerous; share rectangular fields, orientation preference, and diffuse-light unresponsiveness with simple cells.

| Feature | Simple Cells | Complex Cells |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Field size | Smaller | **Larger** |
| On/off subregions | Divisible | **Not divisible** |
| Ocular input | All **monocular** | Many **binocular** (>50% in monkeys) |

- **Position invariance**: respond to a preferred-orientation edge **regardless of position** in the field; a moving bar drives them continuously. Many prefer **movement in a specific direction**.
- **Binocular cells**: same location/orientation preference through each eye; both eyes together fire more strongly. **Ocular dominance**: most respond more to **one eye**. Some respond best to **retinal disparity** → **depth perception**.

### Columnar Organization of V1
**Two conclusions:**
1. Receptive fields arise from **simpler → more complex** flow: on/off-center cells → **simple cells** → **complex cells**.
2. Neurons are grouped in **vertical functional columns**.

| Vertical track | Horizontal track |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Same receptive-field location | Successive cells: slightly different location |
| Same orientation preference | Successive cells: slightly different orientation |
| Same ocular dominance | Alternates through **left/right ocular dominance columns** |

- **Ocular dominance columns**: alternating left-eye/right-eye dominant regions.
- **Hubel and Wiesel cluster model:** all columns for one retinal area cluster together; half right-eye dominant, half left-eye; spanning all orientations.

### Mrs. Richards, Revisited
- Closing one eye then the other **did not change** the illusion → the disturbance is **cortical** (V1 is the first site with binocular neurons; a monocular subcortical lesion would change with eye closure).
- **Acceleration**: a wave expands from foveal V1 at a **constant ~3 mm/min**; less cortex serves the periphery, so the same expansion covers more visual field peripherally.
- **Straight lines**: V1 codes **straight-line stimuli** (simple/complex cells) → zigzag fortification percepts.

### Plasticity of Receptive Fields
- Old assumptions (simple stimuli reveal mechanisms; fields are static) are challenged by **natural-scene** studies (Felsen & Dan, 2006). Responses depend on **larger scene context** (Bair, 2005); **plasticity** is fundamental; simple-stimulus research is incomplete.

## 6.5 Seeing Color

- **Hues** = colors. **Black** = no light; **white** = intense broad-wavelength mix (equal proportions); **gray** = same mix, lower intensity.
- Perceived color depends largely on **reflected wavelengths**, but not entirely.

### Component and Opponent Processing

**Component (Trichromatic) Theory** — **Thomas Young (1802)**, refined by **Hermann von Helmholtz (1852)** (Young-Helmholtz theory):
- Three cone types with different spectral sensitivities; color = **ratio of activity** across them.
- Any color can be matched by mixing **three** wavelengths. Confirmed (Wald, 1964):
  - **S cones**: ~420 nm (blue); **M cones**: ~530 nm (green); **L cones**: ~560 nm (red-yellow).

**Opponent-Process Theory** — **Ewald Hering (1878)**: three cell classes signal opponent pairs —
1. **Red-Green**, 2. **Blue-Yellow**, 3. **Black-White** (brightness).
- **Complementary colors** (red-green, blue-yellow) produce white/gray when combined equally.
- Behavioral support: (1) no "bluish yellow"/"reddish green" (Billock & Tsou, 2010); (2) **complementary afterimages** (red → green afterimage; yellow → blue).

**Resolution (DeValois et al., 2000):** both operate — at the **cone level**, **component (trichromatic)** coding; at all later levels (retina-geniculate-striate), **opponent processing** (Chatterjee & Callaway, 2003).

| Group | Photopigments | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| Most primates | **3** (trichromats) | Human color vision |
| Most other mammals | **2** (dichromats) | Poor red-end discrimination |
| Some birds, fish, reptiles | **4** (tetrachromats) | Detect ultraviolet |
| Transgenic mice (Jacobs et al., 2007) | **3** | Acquired trichromacy |

> **KEY STUDY — Jacobs et al. (2007):** introduced the long-wavelength photopigment gene into dichromat mice; behavioral tests confirmed acquired color discrimination — adding a photopigment can create functional new color vision.

### Color Constancy and Retinex Theory
- **Color constancy**: perceived color stays stable across illumination conditions (e.g., same object under incandescent, fluorescent, daylight) despite different reflected wavelengths. Component/opponent processing alone can't explain it.
- **Retinex theory** (Land, 1977; retina + cortex): the visual system computes a surface's color by **comparing its reflected wavelengths with surrounding surfaces**, not from absolute wavelengths.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Visible light** = 380-760 nm; wavelength → color, intensity → brightness.

2. **Duplexity theory**: cones = photopic (color, high acuity, good light); rods = scotopic (achromatic, high sensitivity, dim).

3. **Purkinje effect (1825)**: in dim light blue looks brighter relative to red/yellow (scotopic peak ~500 nm vs. photopic ~560 nm).

4. **Rhodopsin**: rod photopigment; G-protein coupled; light → channels close → rod hyperpolarizes → less glutamate. Absorption matches scotopic curve.

5. **Five retinal layers**: receptors, horizontal, bipolar, amacrine, ganglion cells. Retina is "inside-out."

6. **Retina-geniculate-striate**: left field → right cortex (nasal fibers cross at chiasm; temporal ipsilateral). LGN has **6 layers** (3 per eye).

7. **P layers** (top 4): parvocellular, small, cones, color/detail. **M layers** (bottom 2): magnocellular, large, rods, movement.

8. **Fovea**: cones only; ~25% of V1 (cortical magnification); rods peak at **20°** from center.

9. **Hubel & Wiesel** (Nobel): cats/monkeys; on/off-center, simple cells (orientation-specific, monocular, rectangular, position-specific), complex cells (position-invariant, often binocular, direction-selective), columnar organization.

10. **Simple vs. complex**: simple = static on/off subregions, monocular; complex = no static subregions, position-invariant, >50% binocular (monkeys).

11. **Lateral inhibition** (Ratliff, 1972, horseshoe crab): produces Mach bands/contrast enhancement; edge perception better than reality.

12. **Trichromatic (Young 1802, Helmholtz 1852)**: three cone types; color = activity ratio. **Opponent (Hering 1878)**: red-green, blue-yellow, black-white. Both operate (cones trichromatic; later levels opponent).

13. **Color constancy**: stable despite illumination changes; **Retinex theory** (Land) — cortex compares surfaces.

14. **Fortification illusions**: migraine; expand center → periphery at ~3 mm/min cortical rate; monocular closure doesn't change them (cortical/binocular origin); straight lines from orientation-selective neurons.

15. **Completion/surface interpolation**: visual system extracts edges and fills in surfaces; unpatterned surface color/brightness is inferred.
