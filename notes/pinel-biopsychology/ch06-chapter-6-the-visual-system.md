# Chapter 6: The Visual System

The visual system does not produce an accurate internal copy of the external world; it creates a three-dimensional perception from tiny, distorted, two-dimensional retinal images that is in some respects better than external reality. Understanding vision requires integrating neuroanatomical/neurophysiological research with psychophysical research on what we see. The chapter traces the pathway from photon to primary visual cortex and describes how neurons encode edges and color.

---

## The Case of Mrs. Richards: Fortification Illusions

- Reported by **Whitman Richards (1971)**; his subject was his wife.
- Mrs. Richards suffered from **migraine headaches**.
- About **20% of migraine sufferers** experience visual displays called **fortification illusions** prior to attacks.

### Features of Fortification Illusions
- Begin as a **gray area of blindness** near the center of the visual field.
- Expand over ~20 minutes into a **horseshoe shape** with a **zigzag pattern of flickering lines** at the advancing edge (resembling plans of a military fortification, hence the name).
- Lines become **thicker** and expansion **accelerates** as the illusion spreads toward the periphery.
- At the periphery, the headache begins.

### Thinking Creatively / Clinical Implications
- Mrs. Richards could trace the illusion onto paper because it expanded slowly.
- Sir **George Biddell Airy** (British astronomer) published virtually identical drawings in **1870** — confirming the illusions are a consistent product of the human visual system, not imagination.
- The illusion's features (straight lines, peripheral acceleration) are explained by the organization of primary visual cortex (revisited at end of chapter).

## 6.1 Light Enters the Eye and Reaches the Retina

### Nature of Light
- **No animal can see in complete darkness** — vision requires reflected light.
- **Photons**: discrete particles of energy traveling at ~300,000 km/second.
- Light also behaves as **waves of energy** (wave-particle duality).
- **Visible light**: electromagnetic energy with wavelengths between **380 and 760 nanometers (nm)** (billionths of a meter).
- **Wavelength** determines perceived **color (hue)**.
- **Intensity** determines perceived **brightness**.
- Key distinction: wavelength and color are not the same thing; color is a perception, wavelength is a physical property. Same for intensity vs. brightness.

### Evolutionary Perspective on "Light"
- Definition of light is species-specific: rattlesnakes can detect **infrared waves** (too long for humans) to see warm-blooded prey in darkness.
- Some animals detect **ultraviolet** wavelengths invisible to humans.

---

### The Pupil and the Lens

| Structure | Description | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Iris** | Donut-shaped band of contractile tissue; gives eyes their color | Regulates amount of light entering |
| **Pupil** | Hole in the iris | Entry point for light |
| **Lens** | Behind the pupil | Focuses light onto retina |
| **Ciliary muscles** | Adjust lens tension | Control lens shape for accommodation |

- **Pupil size** represents a compromise between:
  - **Sensitivity**: ability to detect dimly lit objects
  - **Acuity**: ability to see fine details

- **Constricted pupil** (bright light): sharper image, greater **depth of focus** (range of depths simultaneously in focus), higher acuity.
- **Dilated pupil** (dim light): more light admitted, sacrifices acuity and depth of focus.

- **Accommodation**: process by which the **ciliary muscles** adjust lens curvature to focus images on the retina.
  - Near objects: lens assumes its **natural cylindrical (rounder) shape** — increases ability to refract (bend) light.
  - Far objects: lens is **flattened**.

---

### Eye Position and Binocular Disparity

#### Evolutionary Perspective: Why Two Eyes?
- **Side-facing eyes** (most vertebrates): see almost every direction without moving the head — favored in **prey animals**.
- **Front-facing eyes** (predators, primates, owls): both eyes view the same scene simultaneously — enables **depth perception** through binocularity.

#### Convergence
- Eyes **converge** (turn slightly inward) to project the same point onto corresponding retinal areas.
- Convergence is greatest when viewing close objects.

#### Binocular Disparity
- **Binocular disparity**: the difference in the position of the same image on the two retinas (because the eyes view from slightly different positions).
- Greater for **close objects**, lesser for **distant objects**.
- The visual system uses the degree of binocular disparity to construct **one 3D perception from two 2D retinal images**.

> **Check It Out — Cocktail Sausage Demonstration**: Touching fingertips held at arm's length while focusing on a distant wall creates a phantom "cocktail sausage" — a product of binocular disparity. Closing one eye makes it disappear.

## 6.2 The Retina and Translation of Light into Neural Signals

- The retina converts light to neural signals, conducts them to the CNS, and participates in processing.

### Five Layers of Retinal Neurons (inner to outer relative to incoming light)
1. **Retinal ganglion cells** (innermost; axons exit the eye)
2. **Amacrine cells** (lateral communication)
3. **Bipolar cells**
4. **Horizontal cells** (lateral communication)
5. **Receptors** (cones and rods; outermost, at back of eyeball)

- Over **50 different kinds** of retinal neurons have been identified (Dacey, 2004; Masland, 2001; Wässle, 2004).
- Retinal neurons communicate via **chemical synapses** AND **electrical gap junctions** (Bloomfield & Völgyi, 2009).

### Inside-Out Arrangement of the Retina
- The retina is "inside-out": light must pass through four retinal layers before reaching the receptor layer.
- Creates two problems:
  1. Incoming light is **distorted** by retinal tissue it passes through.
  2. Retinal ganglion axons must bundle and exit, creating a **gap in the receptor layer**.

### Fovea
- **Fovea**: indentation (~0.33 cm diameter) at the center of the retina.
- Specialized for **high-acuity (fine detail) vision**.
- The thinning of the retinal ganglion cell layer at the fovea **reduces distortion** of incoming light (minimizes problem 1).
- **No rods in the fovea** — only cones.

### Blind Spot
- **Blind spot**: gap in the receptor layer where retinal ganglion cell axons bundle and exit the eye as the **optic nerve**.
- There is NO visual perception from this area.

### Completion / Filling In
- Visual system uses information from receptors around the blind spot to **fill in gaps** in retinal images.
- **Completion** (filling in): a fundamental visual system function — not just a response to blind spots.
- The visual system extracts **key edge information** and conducts it to cortex where **full perception is constructed** from partial information.
- **Surface interpolation**: the process by which we perceive surfaces — the visual system extracts edge information and infers the appearance of large surfaces (color, brightness of unpatterned surfaces are filled in, not directly perceived).

> KEY PRINCIPLE: The visual system does not transmit a photograph — it extracts edges and constructs a perception.

---

### Cone and Rod Vision

| Feature | Cones | Rods |
|---------|-------|------|
| Shape | Cone-shaped | Rod-shaped |
| Location | Concentrated in fovea; some in periphery | Absent from fovea; predominate in periphery |
| Vision type | **Photopic** (daylight) | **Scotopic** (dim light) |
| Acuity | High | Low |
| Color | Yes | No |
| Sensitivity | Lower | Higher |
| Convergence | Low (few cones → 1 ganglion cell) | High (hundreds of rods → 1 ganglion cell) |
| Peak density | At fovea | At **20° from center of fovea** |

- Observation that led to the theory (1866): species active only in daytime have **cone-only retinas**; nocturnal species have **rod-only retinas**.

#### Duplexity Theory of Vision
- **Duplexity theory**: cones and rods mediate different kinds of vision.
- **Photopic vision**: cone-mediated; predominates in good lighting; high-acuity, color perception.
- **Scotopic vision**: rod-mediated; predominates in dim light; high sensitivity but low acuity, no color.

#### Convergence and Its Consequences
- **Scotopic (rod) system**: hundreds of rods converge onto a single retinal ganglion cell.
  - Dim light stimulating many rods can **summate** to activate the ganglion cell → high sensitivity.
  - Cost: when ganglion cell fires, brain cannot determine which rod(s) contributed → **low acuity**.
- **Photopic (cone) system**: only a few cones converge onto each retinal ganglion cell.
  - Less summation → requires more intense light → lower sensitivity.
  - Benefit: when ganglion cell fires, location of stimulus is unambiguous → **high acuity**.

#### Distribution on the Retina
- **Fovea**: cones only, no rods.
- At boundaries of fovea: proportion of cones declines, rods increase.
- Rod density peaks at **20° from the center of the fovea**.
- **Nasal hemiretina**: more rods than **temporal hemiretina**.
  - Nasal hemiretina = half of each retina next to the nose.
  - Temporal hemiretina = half next to the temples.

---

### Spectral Sensitivity

- **Spectral sensitivity curve**: graph of relative brightness of lights of same intensity at different wavelengths.
- Humans have **two** spectral sensitivity curves (because they have both cones and rods):

| Condition | Peak Sensitivity Wavelength | System |
|-----------|----------------------------|--------|
| Photopic | ~**560 nm** | Cones |
| Scotopic | ~**500 nm** | Rods |

#### The Purkinje Effect
- **Purkinje effect** (Jan Purkinje, **1825**): the shift in relative brightness of colors during the transition from photopic to scotopic vision.
- In daylight (photopic): yellow and red flowers appear brighter than blue ones (peak at 560 nm).
- In dim light (scotopic): blue flowers appear as brighter grays than yellow/red ones (peak shifts to 500 nm, which is closer to blue).
- Entire scene appears in **shades of gray** at night (scotopic vision has no color).

---

### Eye Movement

- **Visual field**: the entire area visible at a particular moment.
- The visual system integrates information over time through **temporal integration** — explains why the world does not vanish when we blink.
- Eyes continuously scan the visual field even during apparent fixation.

#### Fixational Eye Movements (involuntary; three types)
1. **Tremor**: tiny, high-frequency oscillations.
2. **Drifts**: slow, wandering movements.
3. **Saccades**: small, rapid, jerky movements (flicks); pronounced "sah-KAHDS."

- Critical function: if retinal images were **artificially stabilized** (kept perfectly still on retina), they would **fade and disappear** — because visual neurons respond to **change**.
- Fixational movements keep images moving on the retina, enabling continuous perception during fixation.

---

### Visual Transduction: The Conversion of Light to Neural Signals

- **Transduction**: conversion of one form of energy to another.
- **Visual transduction**: conversion of light to neural signals by visual receptors.

#### Rhodopsin
- **Rhodopsin**: red pigment extracted from rods in **1876**.
- A **photopigment** (any substance that absorbs light).
- When exposed to intense light: **bleached** (loses color), loses ability to absorb light.
- When returned to darkness: regains redness and light-absorbing capacity.
- Rhodopsin's absorption of light is the **first step in rod-mediated vision**.
- Evidence: the **absorption spectrum of rhodopsin** matches the **human scotopic spectral sensitivity curve** — proving dim-light sensitivity is a direct consequence of rhodopsin's absorption properties.
- Rhodopsin is a **G-protein coupled receptor** that responds to light (not neurotransmitter molecules).

#### Mechanism of Rod Transduction

| Condition | Sodium Channels | Rod Polarization | Glutamate Release |
|-----------|----------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **In darkness** | Partially open (cyclic GMP keeps them open) | Slightly depolarized | Continuous, steady release |
| **In light** | Closed (rhodopsin bleached → cascade closes channels) | Hyperpolarized | Reduced |

- Key principle: **signals are often transmitted through neural systems by inhibition** (light → rod hyperpolarization → less glutamate).

## 6.3 From Retina to Primary Visual Cortex

### Retina-Geniculate-Striate Pathways

- **Retina-geniculate-striate pathways**: the major visual pathways conducting signals from each retina to primary visual cortex via lateral geniculate nuclei of the thalamus.
- Also called: **primary visual pathways**.
- **~90% of retinal ganglion cell axons** become part of these pathways.
- No other sensory system has such a dominant pair of cortical pathways.

#### Key Rule: Visual Field Laterality
- All signals from the **left visual field** → reach the **right primary visual cortex**:
  - **Ipsilaterally** from the **temporal hemiretina of the right eye**.
  - **Contralaterally** (via optic chiasm) from the **nasal hemiretina of the left eye**.
- Reverse is true for the right visual field.

#### Optic Chiasm
- The point where nasal hemiretina fibers **cross (decussate)** to the opposite hemisphere.
- Temporal hemiretina fibers do NOT cross — they project ipsilaterally.

#### Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN)
- Located in the **thalamus**.
- Each LGN has **6 layers**.
- Each layer receives input from **all parts of the contralateral visual field** of **one eye**.
- Three layers receive input from one eye; three from the other.
- Each LGN receives visual input only from the **contralateral visual field**.
- Most LGN neurons projecting to primary visual cortex terminate in the **lower part of cortical layer IV**.

#### Striate Cortex (Primary Visual Cortex, V1)
- Named for the characteristic **stripe (striation)** visible in cross-section where LGN axons terminate.
- Also called **V1** or **primary visual cortex**.

---

### Retinotopic Organization

- **Retinotopic organization**: each level of the retina-geniculate-striate system is organized as a **map of the retina**.
- Adjacent retinal areas → adjacent neurons at all levels of the system.
- **Disproportionate representation of the fovea**: although the fovea is a small part of the retina, ~**25%** of primary visual cortex is dedicated to analyzing its input (**cortical magnification**).

> **KEY STUDY — Dobelle, Mladejovsky, & Girvin (1974)**: Implanted electrodes in primary visual cortex of blind patients. Administering current through an electrode array in the shape of a cross caused patients to report seeing a **glowing image of that shape** — dramatic confirmation of retinotopic organization.

---

### The M and P Channels

Two parallel channels run through each LGN:

| Feature | **P Channel (Parvocellular)** | **M Channel (Magnocellular)** |
|---------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Layers in LGN | Top **4 layers** | Bottom **2 layers** |
| Cell body size | **Small** (parvo = small) | **Large** (magno = large) |
| Primary input | **Cones** | **Rods** |
| Responsive to | Color, fine pattern details, stationary/slow-moving objects | **Movement** |
| Function | Detail, color | Motion detection |

- P and M neurons project to **different sites** in lower layer IV of striate cortex.
- M and P portions of layer IV then project to **different parts of visual cortex**.

## 6.4 Seeing Edges

- **Edges** are the most informative features of any visual display — they define extent and position of objects.
- A visual edge is the **place where two different areas of a visual image meet** — it is a contrast, not a thing in itself.
- This section focuses on **brightness contrast** (edges defined by differences in brightness).

---

### Lateral Inhibition and Contrast Enhancement

#### Mach Bands
- **Mach bands**: illusory stripes of brightness and darkness running adjacent to edges.
- Adjacent to each edge: brighter stripe looks **brighter than it really is**; darker stripe looks **darker than it really is**.
- Named after Ernst Mach (though Mach used a different figure).
- Function: **enhance contrast at each edge** → makes edges easier to see.
- Mach bands are created by the visual system, not by the physical stimulus.

> **Check It Out**: Viewing each stripe in isolation (covering adjacent stripes with paper) reveals each is homogeneous — Mach bands disappear. Remove paper → Mach bands reappear.

#### Horseshoe Crab Research
- Classic studies on physiological basis of contrast enhancement conducted on the **horseshoe crab** (Ratliff, 1972).
- Horseshoe crab lateral eyes: ideal for neurophysiology — composed of large receptors called **ommatidia**, each with its own large axon.

#### Lateral Inhibition
- **Lateral inhibition**: when a receptor fires, it inhibits its neighbors via a lateral neural network.
- Inhibition magnitude: greatest from the most intensely illuminated receptors; greatest effect on immediate neighbors.
- Firing rate of a single ommatidium: proportional to light intensity striking it.

#### Mechanism of Contrast Enhancement (Figure 6.15)

At a bright-dark edge, receptors A, B, C, D (bright side) and E, F, G, H (dark side):
- **A, B, C**: all receive same high stimulation + same high lateral inhibition from highly stimulated neighbors → fire at same rate.
- **D** (bright side, adjacent to edge): receives same high stimulation but **less lateral inhibition** (neighbors E-H are dimly lit) → fires **MORE** than A, B, C.
- **F, G, H**: all receive same low stimulation + same low lateral inhibition → fire at same low rate.
- **E** (dark side, adjacent to edge): receives same low stimulation but **more lateral inhibition** (neighbors A-D are brightly lit) → fires **LESS** than F, G, H.
- Result: D fires more than expected (bright band); E fires less than expected (dark band) → **Mach bands**.

> KEY PRINCIPLE: Our perception of edges is **better than the physical reality** — contrast enhancement is a nervous system operation, not a property of the stimulus.

### Receptive Fields of Visual Neurons

#### Hubel and Wiesel's Method (Nobel Prize)
- **David Hubel** and **Torsten Wiesel** (see Hubel & Wiesel, 2004) — Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (1981).
- Subjects: **cats and monkeys**.
- Method:
  1. Position microelectrode tip near a single neuron.
  2. Paralyze eye muscles to block eye movements; focus retinal image with adjustable lens.
  3. Identify the **receptive field** of the neuron.
  4. Record responses to various stimuli within the receptive field.
  5. Characterize the stimulus properties that maximally drive the neuron.
  6. Advance electrode and repeat for next neuron.
- Strategy: start near receptors, work up through progressively higher system levels to understand increasing response complexity.

#### Receptive Field (Definition)
- **Receptive field of a visual neuron**: the area of the visual field within which it is possible for a visual stimulus to influence the firing of that neuron.

---

### Receptive Fields: Neurons of the Retina-Geniculate-Striate System

Hubel and Wiesel (1979) recorded from: retinal ganglion cells → lateral geniculate neurons → striate neurons of lower layer IV.

#### Four Commonalities at All Three Levels
1. **Foveal receptive fields are smaller** than peripheral ones (consistent with high foveal acuity).
2. All neurons have **circular receptive fields**.
3. All neurons are **monocular** (receptive field in one eye only).
4. Many neurons have receptive fields with an **excitatory area and an inhibitory area** separated by a circular boundary.

#### On-Center vs. Off-Center Cells

| Cell Type | Center Response (light on) | Periphery Response (light on) |
|-----------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **On-center cell** | ON firing (burst of activity) | Inhibition + OFF firing when light off |
| **Off-center cell** | Inhibition + OFF firing when light off | ON firing (burst of activity) |

- Both types respond best to **contrast** between center and periphery.
- Most effective stimulus: illuminate **entire center** OR **entire surround** while leaving the other region dark.
- **Diffuse illumination of the entire receptive field**: little effect on firing.
- Conclusion: many neurons in the retina-geniculate-striate system function to respond to the **degree of brightness contrast** between the two areas of their receptive fields.

> KEY NOTE: Most visual neurons are **spontaneously active** even without visual input. Responses to external stimuli consume only a small portion of ongoing brain energy expenditure. Arieli et al. (1996): level of visual cortical activity at time of stimulus presentation influences how cells respond — one mechanism by which **cognition influences perception**.

---

### Receptive Fields: Simple Cortical Cells

- Located in striate cortex (primary visual cortex), but NOT in lower layer IV.
- **Simple cells**: primary visual cortex neurons whose receptive fields can be divided into antagonistic on and off regions.

#### Properties of Simple Cells
- Receptive fields divisible into **straight-line on and off regions** (not circular, unlike lower layer IV).
- Respond best to:
  - **Bars of light** in a dark field.
  - **Dark bars** in a light field.
  - **Single straight edges** between dark and light areas.
- **Position-specific**: each simple cell responds maximally only when its preferred stimulus is in a **particular position** in the field.
- **Orientation-specific**: each simple cell responds maximally only to straight-edge stimuli in a **particular orientation**.
- Receptive fields are **rectangular** (not circular).
- All simple cells are **monocular**.
- Unresponsive to diffuse light.

---

### Receptive Fields: Complex Cortical Cells

- **Complex cells** are more numerous than simple cells.

#### Properties Shared with Simple Cells
- Rectangular receptive fields.
- Respond best to straight-line stimuli in a specific orientation.
- Unresponsive to diffuse light.

#### Three Key Differences from Simple Cells

| Feature | Simple Cells | Complex Cells |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Receptive field size | Smaller | **Larger** |
| On/off subregions | Static, divisible | **Cannot be divided** into static on/off regions |
| Ocular input | All **monocular** | Many are **binocular** (>50% in monkeys) |

- **Position invariance**: complex cells respond to a particular straight-edge stimulus of a particular orientation **regardless of its position** within the receptive field. A moving bar of the correct orientation drives the cell continuously as it sweeps across the field.
- Many complex cells respond more robustly to **movement in a specific direction**.

#### Binocular Complex Cells and Ocular Dominance
- Binocular cells: receptive field measured through one eye has **same location and orientation preference** as measured through the other eye.
- Stimulating **both eyes simultaneously** produces more robust firing than one eye alone.
- **Ocular dominance**: most binocular cells in primary visual cortex of monkeys respond more robustly to stimulation of **one eye** than the other.
- Some binocular cells respond best to **retinal disparity** (slightly different positions of same stimulus on two retinas) → role in **depth perception**.

---

### Columnar Organization of Primary Visual Cortex

#### Two Important Conclusions from Receptive Field Studies
1. Characteristics of visual cortex neuron receptive fields result from **flow of signals from simpler to more complex fields**:
   - On-center / off-center cells (lower layer IV) → **simple cells** → **complex cells**.
2. Primary visual cortex neurons are grouped in **functional vertical columns** (at right angles to cortical layers).

#### Evidence from Electrode Tracks

| Vertical track | Horizontal track |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Each cell in column: receptive field in **same area** of visual field | Each successive cell: receptive field in **slightly different location** |
| All cells: respond best to straight lines of **same orientation** | Each successive cell: maximally responsive to **slightly different orientation** |
| Monocular/binocular cells with ocular dominance: all dominated by **same eye** | Tip moves alternately through **left-eye and right-eye dominance columns** |

- **Ocular dominance columns**: alternating regions of left-eye and right-eye dominance encountered during horizontal electrode advance.

#### Hubel and Wiesel's Model of a Cortical Cluster (Figure 6.20)
- All functional columns analyzing input from **one retinal area** are clustered together.
- Half the cluster: input dominated by **right eye**.
- Other half: input dominated by **left eye**.
- The cluster includes neurons with preferences for straight-line stimuli of **various orientations**.

### The Case of Mrs. Richards, Revisited

- Test: Mrs. Richards was asked to shut one eye then the other and report what happened to the illusion.
- Result: **Nothing changed** — the illusion was the same with either eye.
- Conclusion: the disturbance is **cortical** — the visual cortex is the first part of the retina-geniculate-striate system containing neurons that receive input from **both eyes**; if the lesion were subcortical (monocular), closing one eye would have altered the illusion.

#### Why the Lines Accelerated
- The illusion represents a wave of disturbance expanding from the foveal area of primary visual cortex outward at a **constant rate of ~3 mm per minute**.
- Acceleration occurs because **proportionally less visual cortex is dedicated to peripheral visual field** — so the same constant expansion covers progressively more visual field per mm of cortex.

#### Why Straight Lines?
- The primary visual cortex is specialized for coding **straight-line stimuli** (simple and complex cells) — so a disturbance in this cortex would naturally produce straight-line percepts (the zigzag fortification pattern).

---

### Plasticity of Receptive Fields in Visual Cortex

- Traditional assumptions: (1) simplified artificial stimuli can reveal visual mechanisms; (2) receptive field properties are static.
- Research using **natural scene video clips** challenges both assumptions (Felsen & Dan, 2006).
- Response of a visual cortex neuron depends not only on stimuli in its receptive field but on the **larger scene context** (Bair, 2005; Kayser, Körding, & König, 2004).
- **Plasticity** — the ability to adapt to change — is a fundamental property of visual cortex function.
- Research based solely on simple stimuli (spots, bars) cannot provide a complete explanation of visual processing.

## 6.5 Seeing Color

- **Hues**: the correct term for what we commonly call colors.
- **Black**: absence of light.
- **White**: intense mixture of a wide range of wavelengths in roughly equal proportions.
- **Gray**: same mixture at lower intensity.
- Perceived color depends largely on **wavelengths of light reflected by an object** — but that is not the entire story.

---

### Component and Opponent Processing

#### Component Theory (Trichromatic Theory)
- Proposed by **Thomas Young (1802)**, refined by **Hermann von Helmholtz (1852)**.
- Also called: **Young-Helmholtz theory** or **trichromatic theory**.
- Postulates: three different kinds of cones, each with a different spectral sensitivity; color is encoded by the **ratio of activity** across the three cone types.
- Basis: **any color** in the visible spectrum can be matched by mixing three different wavelengths in varying proportions (metameric matching). Minimum of three wavelengths needed (any three, as long as one cannot be matched by mixing the other two).
- Confirmed in early 1960s: measurement of absorption spectra of individual cones revealed **three cone types** (Wald, 1964):
  - **Short-wavelength (S) cones**: most sensitive to short wavelengths (~420 nm, blue).
  - **Medium-wavelength (M) cones**: most sensitive to medium wavelengths (~530 nm, green).
  - **Long-wavelength (L) cones**: most sensitive to long wavelengths (~560 nm, red-yellow).

#### Opponent-Process Theory
- Proposed by **Ewald Hering (1878)**.
- Postulates three classes of cells:
  1. **Red-Green cells**: signal red by changing activity in one direction (e.g., hyperpolarization) and signal green in the opposite direction.
  2. **Blue-Yellow cells**: signal blue and yellow in the same opponent fashion.
  3. **Black-White cells**: encode brightness — signal both black and white in opponent fashion.
- **Complementary colors**: pairs (e.g., green + red; blue + yellow) that produce white/gray when combined in equal measure.
- Behavioral basis for opponent theory:
  1. **Complementary colors cannot coexist**: no "bluish yellow" or "reddish green" (Billock & Tsou, 2010).
  2. **Complementary afterimages**: staring at red produces a green afterimage; staring at yellow produces a blue afterimage.

#### Resolution: Both Mechanisms Coexist
- The debate between trichromatic and opponent theories was misguided: **both mechanisms operate** in the visual system (DeValois et al., 2000).
- At the **cone level**: coding operates on a **component (trichromatic) basis**.
- At all subsequent levels (retina-geniculate-striate pathway): evidence of **opponent processing** — cells respond in one direction to one color and in the opposite direction to its complementary color (Chatterjee & Callaway, 2003; Gegenfurtner & Kiper, 2003).

#### Number of Photopigments Across Species

| Group | Photopigment Types | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| Most primates | **3** (trichromats) | Standard human color vision |
| Most other mammals | **2** (dichromats) | Lack long-wavelength pigment; poor red-end discrimination |
| Some birds, fish, reptiles | **4** (tetrachromats) | Can detect ultraviolet light |
| Transgenic mice (Jacobs et al., 2007) | **3** (converted) | Gene for long-wavelength pigment introduced; acquired trichromacy behaviorally |

> **KEY STUDY — Jacobs et al. (2007)**: Introduced the gene for the long-wavelength photopigment into dichromat mice. Behavioral tests confirmed the transgenic mice acquired the ability to perceive long wavelengths and make color discriminations — demonstrating that adding a photopigment genetically can produce functional new color vision.

---

### Color Constancy and the Retinex Theory

- **Color constancy**: the perceived color of an object does NOT simply depend on the wavelengths it reflects; it remains relatively stable across different illumination conditions.
- Example: an object looks the same color under incandescent, fluorescent, and natural daylight — despite these sources containing very different wavelength mixtures.
- Neither component nor opponent processing alone can explain color constancy.
- **Retinex theory** (Land, 1977): the visual system determines the color of surfaces by comparing the wavelengths reflected from each surface with those reflected from surrounding surfaces — the cortex computes color from multiple retinal comparisons, not from absolute wavelengths.
  - "Retinex" = retina + cortex.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Visible light** is electromagnetic energy between **380-760 nm**; wavelength determines color, intensity determines brightness (these are physical vs. perceptual distinctions).

2. **Duplexity theory**: cones = photopic vision (color, high acuity, good light); rods = scotopic vision (achromatic, high sensitivity, dim light). First noticed when species with cone-only retinas are diurnal and rod-only retinas are nocturnal.

3. **Purkinje effect (1825)**: in dim light (scotopic), blue appears brighter relative to red/yellow — because scotopic peak sensitivity is ~500 nm (blue-green), whereas photopic peak is ~560 nm (yellow-green).

4. **Rhodopsin**: photopigment of rods; G-protein coupled receptor; bleached by light → sodium channels close → rod hyperpolarizes → less glutamate released. Absorption spectrum matches scotopic spectral sensitivity curve.

5. **Retinal layers** (5 types): receptors, horizontal cells, bipolar cells, amacrine cells, retinal ganglion cells. Retina is "inside-out" — light passes through 4 layers before reaching receptors.

6. **Retina-geniculate-striate pathway**: left visual field → right visual cortex (nasal hemiretina fibers cross at the optic chiasm; temporal hemiretina fibers are ipsilateral). Each LGN has **6 layers**; 3 from each eye.

7. **P layers** (top 4 LGN layers): parvocellular, small cells, cones, responsive to color/fine detail. **M layers** (bottom 2): magnocellular, large cells, rods, responsive to movement.

8. **Fovea**: only cones (no rods); ~25% of primary visual cortex dedicated to foveal input (cortical magnification); center of high-acuity vision. Rod density peaks at **20° from fovea center**.

9. **Hubel and Wiesel**: Nobel Prize; used microelectrode recordings in cats and monkeys. Discovered on-center/off-center cells, simple cells (orientation-specific, monocular, rectangular, position-specific), complex cells (position-invariant, often binocular, direction-selective, larger fields), and columnar cortical organization.

10. **Simple vs. complex cells**: Simple cells have static on/off subregions and are all monocular. Complex cells cannot be divided into static on/off subregions, are position-invariant, and many are binocular (>50% in monkeys).

11. **Lateral inhibition** (Ratliff, 1972, horseshoe crab): receptor firing inhibits neighbors; produces Mach bands and contrast enhancement; perception of edges is better than the physical stimulus.

12. **Trichromatic (Young-Helmholtz) theory (Young 1802, Helmholtz 1852)**: three cone types (S/M/L); color = ratio of activity. **Opponent-process theory (Hering 1878)**: red-green, blue-yellow, black-white opponent pairs. Both operate simultaneously: cones use trichromatic coding; all subsequent levels use opponent processing.

13. **Color constancy**: perceived color stable despite illumination changes; explained by **Retinex theory** (Land) — cortex compares wavelengths reflected from a surface with surrounding surfaces.

14. **Fortification illusions**: migraine-associated; expand from center to periphery over ~20 min at constant cortical rate (~3 mm/min); monocular closure does NOT alter them (confirming cortical, binocular origin); straight lines predicted by orientation-selective cortical neurons.

15. **Completion / surface interpolation**: fundamental visual system function; the visual system extracts edge information and fills in the appearance of large surfaces — color and brightness of unpatterned surfaces are not directly perceived but are inferred.
