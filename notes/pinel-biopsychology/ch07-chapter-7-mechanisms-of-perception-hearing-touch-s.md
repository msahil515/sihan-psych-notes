# Chapter 7: Mechanisms of Perception: Hearing, Touch, Smell, Taste, and Attention

**Chapter Overview:** This chapter examines the organizational principles of sensory systems (hierarchical, functionally segregated, parallel) and then applies them to the auditory, somatosensory, and chemical sensory systems. It covers structural anatomy from peripheral receptors to cortex, clinical deficits from damage at each level, pain mechanisms including descending analgesia, and the olfactory/gustatory systems. The case of Dr. P. (the man who mistook his wife for a hat) anchors the distinction between sensation and perception.

---

## The Case of the Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat

- Patient: **Dr. P.**, a respected musician and teacher; described by neurologist **Oliver Sacks** (book: *The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat*, 1985).
- Visual **acuity** was intact (could spot a pin on the floor).
- Could not recognize a glove by sight; described it as "a container divided into five compartments."
- When leaving the examination, Dr. P. grasped his wife's head and tried to put it on as a hat.
- Deficit: **visual perception**, not visual sensation — the higher-order processes of integrating, recognizing, and interpreting sensory patterns were impaired.
- **Sensation**: the process of detecting the presence of stimuli.
- **Perception**: the higher-order process of integrating, recognizing, and interpreting complete patterns of sensations.
- Clinical teaching point: damage higher in the sensory hierarchy produces more specific and complex deficits while leaving fundamental sensory abilities intact.

---

## Functional Segregation

- **Old assumption**: primary, secondary, and association cortex at any given level are each **functionally homogeneous** — all areas at a given level perform the same function.
- **Current understanding**: **functional segregation** characterizes sensory system organization — each of the three cortical levels (primary, secondary, association) contains functionally distinct areas that specialize in different kinds of analysis.
- Implication: the visual cortex, auditory cortex, and somatosensory cortex each contain multiple specialized subregions rather than a single uniform processing field.

---

## Parallel Processing

- **Old assumption**: sensory hierarchy levels are connected in a **serial fashion** — information flows through a single pathway like beads on a string.
- **Serial system**: information flows among components over just one pathway.
- **Current understanding**: sensory systems are **parallel systems** — information flows through multiple pathways simultaneously.
- **Parallel processing**: the simultaneous analysis of a signal in different ways by the multiple parallel pathways of a neural network.
- Two fundamental kinds of parallel streams:
  1. Streams that influence behavior **without conscious awareness**.
  2. Streams that influence behavior **through conscious awareness**.

---

## Summary Model of Sensory System Organization

| Feature | Former Model (1960s) | Current Model |
|---|---|---|
| Organization | Hierarchical | Hierarchical |
| Cortical function | Functionally Homogeneous | Functionally Segregated |
| Pathway structure | Serial | Parallel |

- Hierarchical organization is confirmed by comparing damage effects: receptor destruction = complete loss; association/secondary cortex damage = specific, complex deficits.
- **Division of labor**: multiple specialized areas at multiple levels interconnected by multiple parallel pathways.
- **Binding problem**: the challenge of explaining how the brain combines individually perceived sensory attributes (shape, color, movement) into integrated perceptual wholes. No single cortical area receives input from all other areas of a sensory system; perception is a product of combined activity across interconnected cortical areas (Billock & Tsou, 2004; Botly & De Rosa, 2008).
- **Top-down signals**: neurons that descend from higher to lower levels of sensory hierarchies, allowing higher cortex to modulate sensory input (Saalmann, Pigarev, & Vidyasagar, 2007; Yantis, 2008).
- Reference: Tong (2003) for evidence of the current model.

## Auditory System

**Definition of sound**: vibrations of air molecules that stimulate the auditory system. Humans hear vibrations between approximately **20 and 20,000 hertz** (cycles per second).

### Physical and Perceptual Dimensions of Sound

| Physical Dimension | Perceptual Dimension |
|---|---|
| **Amplitude** | Loudness |
| **Frequency** | Pitch |
| **Complexity** | Timbre |

- **Pure tones** (sine wave vibrations) exist only in laboratories/recording studios; real-world sounds are always complex patterns of vibrations.
- **Fourier analysis**: the mathematical procedure for breaking down complex waves into their component sine waves. One theory of audition proposes the auditory system performs a Fourier-like analysis of complex sounds.
- **Fundamental frequency**: the highest frequency of which the various component frequencies of a sound are multiples; most closely related to the perceived pitch of natural complex sounds.
  - Example: a sound mixing 100, 200, and 300 Hz is perceived at a pitch related to 100 Hz.
- **Missing fundamental**: the pitch of a complex sound may not be directly related to the frequency of any of its components. A mixture of 200, 300, and 400 Hz tones is perceived as having the same pitch as a pure 100 Hz tone, because 100 Hz is the fundamental frequency of those three components.

---

### The Ear

Anatomy (outside-in order):

1. **Outer ear** (pinna/auricle) — funnels sound waves
2. **Auditory canal** — carries waves to tympanic membrane
3. **Tympanic membrane** (eardrum) — vibrates in response to sound waves
4. **Ossicles** (three small bones of the middle ear):
   - **Malleus** (the hammer)
   - **Incus** (the anvil)
   - **Stapes** (the stirrup)
5. **Oval window** — elastic membrane that transfers vibrations from stapes to cochlear fluid
6. **Cochlea** (snail-shaped; kokhlos = land snail) — fluid-filled coiled tube
   - Contains the **organ of Corti** (the auditory receptor organ), an internal membrane running almost to the cochlea's tip
   - Composed of two membranes:
     - **Basilar membrane**: auditory hair cells are mounted in it
     - **Tectorial membrane**: rests on top of the hair cells
   - **Hair cells**: the auditory receptor cells; deflection of organ of Corti creates a shearing force on hair cells, stimulating them
   - Hair cells increase firing in axons of the **auditory nerve** (a branch of **cranial nerve VIII**, the auditory-vestibular nerve)
7. **Round window**: elastic membrane in cochlea wall that dissipates cochlear fluid vibrations

- Cochlear sensitivity: humans can detect differences in pure tones that differ in frequency by only **0.2%**.
- **Tonotopic organization**: different frequencies produce maximal stimulation at different points along the basilar membrane.
  - **Higher frequencies** → greater activation closer to the oval/round windows
  - **Lower frequencies** → greater activation at the tip (apex) of the basilar membrane
- Auditory system organization is primarily **tonotopic** (analogous to the **retinotopic** organization of the visual system).
- **Semicircular canals**: receptive organs of the **vestibular system**, which carries information about direction and intensity of head movements for balance maintenance.

---

### From the Ear to the Primary Auditory Cortex

There is no single major pathway comparable to the visual retina-geniculate-striate pathway. Instead, there is a **network of auditory pathways**.

**Auditory pathway (ascending):**

1. Auditory nerve axons → **ipsilateral cochlear nuclei**
2. Cochlear nuclei → **superior olives** (bilaterally — signals from each ear are combined here at a very low level)
3. Superior olive axons → via the **lateral lemniscus** → **inferior colliculi** (midbrain tectum)
4. Inferior colliculi → **medial geniculate nuclei** of the thalamus
5. Medial geniculate nuclei → **primary auditory cortex**

Key point: signals from each ear are combined at the superior olives and transmitted to **both ipsilateral and contralateral** auditory cortex.

---

### Subcortical Mechanisms of Sound Localization

- Sound localization in space is mediated by the **lateral and medial superior olives**.
- **Medial superior olives**: neurons respond to slight differences in **time of arrival** of signals from the two ears (interaural time differences).
- **Lateral superior olives**: neurons respond to slight differences in **amplitude** of sounds from the two ears (interaural intensity differences).
- Superior olives project to the **superior colliculus** and **inferior colliculus**.
- Deep layers of the **superior colliculi** (receiving auditory input) are organized according to a **map of auditory space** (King, Schnupp, & Thompson, 1998).
- Superficial layers of superior colliculi (receiving visual input) are organized **retinotopically**.
- General function of superior colliculi: **locating sources of sensory input in space**.

> **KEY STUDY: Barn Owl Sound Localization (Konishi, 2003; Cohen & Knudsen, 1999)**
> Barn owls can locate sound sources better than any other tested animal. Nocturnal hunters locating field mice by sound alone. Each auditory neuron in the barn owl's superior colliculus is finely tuned to sounds from a particular spatial location.

---

### Auditory Cortex

- **Primary auditory cortex** location: temporal lobe, hidden within the **lateral fissure**.
- Receives majority of input from the **medial geniculate nucleus**.
- Adjacent areas form the **core region** (three areas total).
- Surrounding: **belt** (secondary auditory cortex band).
- Beyond belt: **parabelt areas**.
- Approximately **10 separate areas of secondary auditory cortex** in primates.

**Two principles of primary auditory cortex organization:**
1. **Columnar organization**: neurons in a vertical column respond to the same frequency range (Schreiner, 1992).
2. **Tonotopic organization**: each area organized by frequency (Schreiner, Read, & Sutter, 2000).

**Two Streams of Auditory Cortex:**
- **Anterior auditory pathway**: identifying sounds (**what**)
- **Posterior auditory pathway**: locating sounds (**where**)
- Both ultimately project to **prefrontal cortex** and **posterior parietal cortex**.

**Auditory-Visual Interactions:**
- fMRI studies show sensory interactions occurring at the lowest level of sensory cortex, including in primary sensory cortex (Macaluso & Driver, 2005; Schroeder & Foxe, 2005).
- Posterior parietal cortex neurons in monkeys may have visual, auditory, or both types of receptive fields covering the same spatial location (Mulette-Gillman, Cohen, & Groh, 2005).
- Conclusion: sensory system interaction is an **early and integral** part of sensory processing.

> **KEY STUDY: Bendor & Wang (2005) — Pitch Area**
> Probed monkey auditory cortex with microelectrodes using missing fundamental stimuli. Found a small area **just anterior to primary auditory cortex** whose neurons respond to **pitch** rather than frequency regardless of sound quality. A parallel pitch area identified in humans by fMRI.

---

### Effects of Damage to the Auditory System

**Bilateral auditory cortex lesions:**
- Temporary complete hearing loss, then recovery.
- Permanent: loss of ability to **localize sounds** and impaired **frequency discrimination** (Heffner & Heffner, 2003).

**Unilateral auditory cortex lesions:**
- Disrupts sound localization **contralateral** to the lesion; system is partially contralateral.

**Deafness in Humans:**
- ~250 million people with disabling hearing impairment (Taylor & Forge, 2005).
- Total deafness in only **1%** of hearing-impaired — due to diffuse parallel pathways.
- **Conductive deafness**: ossicle damage.
- **Nerve deafness**: cochlea/auditory nerve damage; major cause is **hair cell loss**.
- **Age-related hearing loss**: first affects high frequencies (s, f, t sounds difficult).
- **Tinnitus**: caused by central auditory changes; cutting the peripheral nerve does NOT relieve it (Eggermont & Roberts, 2004).
- **Cochlear implants**: convert sounds to electrical signals; earlier implantation = better outcomes.

## Somatosensory System: Touch and Pain

The **somatosensory system** comprises three interacting systems:

| System | Function |
|---|---|
| **Exteroceptive** | External skin stimuli (touch, temperature, pain) |
| **Proprioceptive** | Body position (muscles, joints, balance organs) |
| **Interoceptive** | Internal body conditions (temperature, blood pressure) |

---

### Cutaneous Receptors

| Receptor | Adaptation | Responds to |
|---|---|---|
| **Free nerve endings** | — | Temperature change and pain |
| **Pacinian corpuscles** | Rapidly adapting | Sudden skin displacement, NOT constant pressure |
| **Merkel's disks** | Slowly adapting | Gradual skin indentation |
| **Ruffini endings** | Slowly adapting | Skin stretch |

- **Stereognosis**: identification of objects by touch; requires manipulating objects to continually change stimulation pattern.
- All cutaneous receptors: stimuli deform/alter receptor chemistry → membrane ion permeability change → neural signal.

---

### Dermatomes

- **Dermatome**: area of the body innervated by left and right dorsal roots of a given spinal cord segment.
- Adjacent dermatome overlap: single dorsal root destruction usually produces little somatosensory loss.
- Face: innervated by **V1, V2, V3** (three branches of the **trigeminal nerve**).

---

### Two Major Somatosensory Pathways

#### Dorsal-Column Medial-Lemniscus System
- Carries: **touch and proprioception** (primarily).
- Route: dorsal root → ipsilateral dorsal columns → **dorsal column nuclei** (medulla) → **decussate** → **medial lemniscus** → **contralateral ventral posterior nucleus** (thalamus) → SI / SII / posterior parietal cortex.
- Trigeminal nerve branches carry face somatosensation to ventral posterior nucleus.
- Longest neurons in the human body: dorsal column neurons originating in the **toes**.

#### Anterolateral System
- Carries: **pain and temperature** (primarily).
- Three tracts:
  1. **Spinothalamic** → ventral posterior nucleus of thalamus
  2. **Spinoreticular** → reticular formation → parafascicular and intralaminar thalamic nuclei
  3. **Spinotectal** → tectum (colliculi)
- Second-order neurons mostly decussate at spinal cord entry level.

> **KEY CLINICAL STUDY: Mark, Ervin & Yakolev (1962)**
> Thalamic lesions in chronic cancer pain patients:
> - Ventral posterior nuclei lesions: reduced touch, temperature, sharp pain sensitivity; **no effect on deep chronic pain**.
> - Parafascicular/intralaminar nuclei lesions: **reduced deep chronic pain** without disrupting cutaneous sensitivity.
> Implication: spinoreticular/parafascicular-intralaminar pathway processes deep/chronic pain separately.

---

### Cortical Areas of Somatosensation

> **KEY STUDY: Penfield et al. (1937) — Somatosensory Homunculus**
> Electrical stimulation of postcentral gyrus in conscious surgical patients. Mapped SI as somatotopic. Greatest territory devoted to hands, lips, tongue.

- **SI (primary somatosensory cortex)**: postcentral gyrus; somatotopic; mostly contralateral input; composed of four functional strips, each with a separate somatotopic map, each preferring a different tactile quality; anterior-to-posterior hierarchical organization.
- **SII (secondary somatosensory cortex)**: just ventral to SI; extends into lateral fissure; receives bilateral input; output to posterior parietal lobe.
- Two proposed streams from SI (Hsiao, 2008):
  - **Dorsal stream** → posterior parietal cortex (multisensory integration, attention direction)
  - **Ventral stream** → SII (object shape perception)

---

### Effects of Damage to Primary Somatosensory Cortex

- Corkin, Milner & Rasmussen (1970): unilateral SI excision.
  - Contralateral deficits: reduced light touch detection; reduced stereognosis.
  - Bilateral deficits only when lesion encroached on SII.

---

### Somatosensory System and Association Cortex

- Posterior parietal cortex: **bimodal neurons** responding to both somatosensory and visual stimuli; visual receptive field moves with the hand.

> **KEY CASE: W.M. — Reducing Scotoma with His Hand (Schendel & Robertson, 2004)**
> Stroke in right posterior cerebral artery → severe left hemianopsia.
> With left hand in lap: 13.6% detection in left visual field.
> With left hand extended into left visual field: significantly improved detection of nearby stimuli.
> Extended even further with tennis racket in hand.
> Explained by bimodal neurons whose visual receptive fields track the hand position.

---

### Somatosensory Agnosias

| Agnosia | Definition | Lesion Location |
|---|---|---|
| **Astereognosia** | Inability to recognize objects by touch | SI area (pure form is rare) |
| **Asomatognosia** | Failure to recognize parts of one's own body (usually left side) | Right posterior parietal lobe |

- **Anosognosia**: failure to recognize one's own neuropsychological symptoms; commonly accompanies asomatognosia.
- **Contralateral neglect**: tendency not to respond to stimuli contralateral to a right-hemisphere injury; asomatognosia is commonly a component.

> **THE CASE OF AUNT BETTY**: Right hemisphere stroke; left hemiplegia; could not identify her own left hand; showed no awareness of deficit (anosognosia); exemplifies asomatognosia with contralateral neglect.

## Perception of Pain

Pain is paradoxical in three ways:

### Paradox 1: Adaptiveness of Pain

- Pain is essential for survival; warns against harmful stimulation.
- No special pain stimulus: pain responds to any potentially harmful stimulation (Craig, 2003).

> **THE CASE OF MISS C. (Melzack & Wall, 1982)**
> Never felt pain in her life. Bitten off tongue tip; suffered third-degree burns. No response to electric shock, hot water, prolonged ice bath. No cardiovascular/respiratory changes to pain stimuli. No sneezing/coughing; no corneal reflexes. Joint pathology from lack of protective pain signals. Died at **age 29** from massive infections and bone/skin trauma.
> Cox et al. (2006): six cases of congenital insensitivity to pain in a Pakistani family; gene abnormality affecting **sodium ion channel synthesis** — possible target for new analgesics.

### Paradox 2: No Clear Cortical Representation

- Painful stimuli activate many cortical areas, but specific areas vary across studies and individuals (Apkarian, 2008; Tracey, 2005).
- No single cortical area is **necessary** for pain perception.
- Removal of SI and SII: no change in pain threshold.
- **Hemispherectomized patients** still perceive pain from both sides of the body.
- **Anterior cingulate cortex**: most linked to pain; involved in the **emotional reaction** to pain, not pain threshold itself (Panksepp, 2003; Price, 2000).

### Paradox 3: Descending Pain Control

- Pain can be effectively suppressed by cognitive and emotional factors.
- Examples: religious ceremonies, soldiers' battlefield wounds, life-threatening situations.

**Gate-Control Theory (Melzack & Wall, 1965):**
- Signals descending from the brain activate **neural gating circuits in the spinal cord** to block incoming pain signals.

**Descending Analgesia Circuit (Basbaum & Fields, 1978):**

Three key discoveries:
1. **Reynolds (1969)**: PAG stimulation alone produces sufficient analgesia for surgery in rats.
2. PAG and other brain areas contain receptors for **opiate drugs** (e.g., morphine).
3. **Endorphins** — endogenous opiate analgesics isolated from brain.

Circuit: **PAG** (opiate-sensitive; opiates inhibit PAG inhibitory interneurons, thereby increasing PAG output) → **serotonergic raphé nuclei** (core of medulla) → descend via **dorsal columns** of spinal cord → activate **inhibitory interneurons** in **dorsal horn** → block incoming pain signals.

Later addition: some descending circuits can **increase** pain perception (Fields, 2004; Gebhart, 2004; Vanegas & Schaible, 2004).

---

### Neuropathic Pain

- **Neuropathic pain**: severe chronic pain with **no recognizable pain stimulus**.
- Develops after injury; persists after healing; can be triggered by innocuous stimuli (e.g., light touch).
- Caused by **pathological CNS changes** from original injury (Reichling & Levine, 2009).
- Aberrant **glial cell signals** may trigger neural pain pathway hyperactivity (Fields, 2009).
- May be perceived in a limb — even an amputated limb.
- Cutting peripheral nerves often ineffective; standard pain medications often ineffective.

## Chemical Senses: Smell and Taste

- **Olfaction**: response to airborne chemicals drawn over nasal receptors.
- **Gustation**: response to chemicals in solution in the oral cavity.
- **Flavor**: integrated perception combining smell and taste; people with no sense of smell cannot distinguish apple from onion flavor.
- **Pheromones**: chemicals released by one member of a species that influence the physiology and behavior of **conspecifics**.

> **KEY STUDY: Murphy & Schneider (1970) — Hamster Pheromones**
> Normal male hamsters kill unfamiliar males and mate with sexually receptive females. Males without olfactory ability: neither behavior occurs. Swabbing a male intruder with female vaginal secretions → converts it from target of aggression to target of sexual behavior. Demonstrates pheromonal control of both aggressive and sexual behavior.

**Suggestive human pheromone evidence:**
1. Women's olfactory sensitivity peaks during ovulation/pregnancy.
2. Menstrual cycle synchronization among cohabiting women.
3. Humans can identify sex from breath or underarm odor.
4. Men can judge stage of menstrual cycle from vaginal odor.
- But: **no direct evidence** for human sex attractant pheromones; these odors are generally not found attractive.

---

### Olfactory System

- **Olfactory receptor cells**: in the **olfactory mucosa** (upper nose; mucus-covered tissue).
- Axons pass through **cribriform plate** → synapse in **olfactory bulbs** → **olfactory tracts** → brain.
- ~10 million olfactory receptor cells in mice (Imai & Sakano, 2007).

**Receptor diversity:**
- Old assumption: few receptor types encoding smells by activity profiles (like 3 cone types for color).
- Current: rats/mice have ~**1,500 receptor protein types**; humans have ~**1,000** (Keller & Vosshall, 2008).
- Each olfactory receptor cell contains **only one type of receptor protein** (Imai & Sakano, 2007).
- Odors encoded by **component processing** — pattern of activity across receptor types.
- Receptor types scattered throughout mucosa with **no discernible spatial organization**.

**Olfactory glomeruli:**
- All receptors with the same receptor protein project to the same location in the olfactory bulb.
- **Olfactory glomeruli**: discrete neuron clusters near the surface of olfactory bulbs; each glomerulus receives input from cells sharing the **same receptor protein**.
- ~Two glomeruli per olfactory bulb per receptor protein type.
- **Systematic layout in olfactory bulbs** (Soucy et al., 2009):
  - Mirror symmetry between left and right bulbs.
  - Glomeruli arrayed identically across members of the same species.
  - Similar layout in related species.
  - But only a slight tendency for similar-odor glomeruli to be near each other — organizing principle unknown.

**Olfactory receptor cell renewal:**
- Unlike other sensory systems, olfactory receptor cells are **replaced throughout life** (Doty, 2001).
- Each new cell survives only a **few weeks**.
- New cells develop axons that grow to appropriate olfactory bulb sites.

**Olfactory projections:**
- Each olfactory tract → **amygdala**, **piriform cortex**, and other medial temporal structures.
- Via thalamus (**medial dorsal nucleus**) → **orbitofrontal cortex**.
- Diffuse projections to the **limbic system**.
- Key anatomical distinction: olfaction is the **only sensory system** that does NOT primarily relay through the thalamus en route to cortex.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Three principles of current sensory system organization**: hierarchical, **functionally segregated** (not homogeneous), and **parallel** (not serial) — all three changed from the 1960s model.

2. **Fundamental frequency and missing fundamental**: perceived pitch of a complex sound corresponds to the fundamental frequency (highest common factor of component frequencies); the fundamental may be entirely absent from the stimulus yet the pitch is unchanged.

3. **Tonotopic organization**: high frequencies stimulate near the oval window; low frequencies stimulate near the apex of the basilar membrane; applies throughout the auditory system (analogous to retinotopic in vision).

4. **Sound localization — superior olives**: medial superior olives process **interaural time differences**; lateral superior olives process **interaural intensity differences**; deep superior colliculus layers are mapped by auditory space.

5. **Auditory cortex location and streams**: primary auditory cortex is in the **temporal lobe within the lateral fissure**; anterior pathway = what (identification); posterior pathway = where (localization).

6. **Conductive vs. nerve deafness**: conductive = ossicle damage; nerve deafness major cause = hair cell loss; tinnitus = central origin, cutting the peripheral nerve does NOT help.

7. **Four cutaneous receptors**: free nerve endings (pain/temp), Pacinian corpuscles (rapidly adapting, sudden displacement), Merkel's disks (slowly adapting, indentation), Ruffini endings (slowly adapting, stretch).

8. **Two ascending somatosensory pathways**: dorsal-column medial-lemniscus (touch/proprioception; ipsilateral dorsal columns, decussate at medulla) vs. anterolateral (pain/temperature; three tracts; decussate at spinal cord entry level); Mark et al. (1962) showed parafascicular/intralaminar nuclei mediate deep chronic pain.

9. **Somatosensory homunculus (Penfield, 1937)**: postcentral gyrus; somatotopic; greatest territory for hands, lips, tongue; SI is primarily contralateral; SII receives bilateral input.

10. **Asomatognosia vs. astereognosia**: asomatognosia = failure to recognize own body parts (left side, right posterior parietal lesion), often with anosognosia and contralateral neglect; astereognosia = failure to recognize objects by touch.

11. **Gate-control theory (Melzack & Wall, 1965)**: descending brain signals activate spinal gating circuits to block pain; explains cognitive/emotional pain suppression.

12. **Descending analgesia circuit (Basbaum & Fields, 1978)**: PAG → serotonergic raphé nuclei → dorsal horn inhibitory interneurons; opiates/endorphins act at PAG; Reynolds (1969) demonstrated PAG stimulation-only analgesia for surgery in rats.

13. **Anterior cingulate cortex and pain**: involved in the **emotional reaction** to pain, not pain threshold; no single cortical area is necessary for pain perception; even hemispherectomized patients feel pain bilaterally.

14. **Neuropathic pain**: chronic pain without recognizable stimulus; caused by CNS pathological changes (aberrant glial signals); cutting peripheral nerves usually ineffective; may be felt in amputated limb.

15. **Olfactory system unique features**: only sense that does NOT relay through the thalamus primarily; olfactory receptor cells are continuously replaced (lifespan: a few weeks); humans have ~1,000 receptor protein types; each receptor cell expresses only one type; odors encoded by component processing across receptor types; same receptor protein type projects to the same glomerulus in the olfactory bulb.
