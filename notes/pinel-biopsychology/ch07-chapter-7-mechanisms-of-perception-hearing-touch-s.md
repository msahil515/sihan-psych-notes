# Chapter 7: Mechanisms of Perception: Hearing, Touch, Smell, Taste, and Attention

**Overview:** Organizational principles of sensory systems (hierarchical, functionally segregated, parallel) applied to the auditory, somatosensory, and chemical sensory systems: anatomy from receptors to cortex, clinical deficits, pain mechanisms including descending analgesia, and olfaction/gustation. The case of Dr. P. anchors the sensation-perception distinction.

---

## The Case of the Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat

- **Dr. P.**, a musician; described by **Oliver Sacks** (*The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat*, 1985).
- Visual **acuity** intact (spotted a pin), but could not recognize a glove ("a container with five compartments") and tried to put his wife's head on as a hat.
- Deficit was **visual perception**, not sensation.
- **Sensation**: detecting the presence of stimuli. **Perception**: integrating, recognizing, and interpreting patterns of sensations.
- Damage higher in the hierarchy → more specific, complex deficits while basic abilities remain.

---

## Functional Segregation
- **Old assumption**: cortex at each level is **functionally homogeneous**. **Current**: **functional segregation** — primary, secondary, and association cortex each contain functionally distinct areas specializing in different analyses (visual, auditory, somatosensory cortex each have multiple specialized subregions).

## Parallel Processing
- **Old assumption**: levels connected **serially** (one pathway). **Current**: **parallel systems** — info flows through multiple pathways at once.
- **Parallel processing**: simultaneous analysis of a signal in different ways across pathways. Two kinds of streams: (1) influence behavior **without** conscious awareness; (2) influence behavior **through** awareness.

## Summary Model of Sensory System Organization

| Feature | Former Model (1960s) | Current Model |
|---|---|---|
| Organization | Hierarchical | Hierarchical |
| Cortical function | Homogeneous | **Segregated** |
| Pathway structure | Serial | **Parallel** |

- **Binding problem**: how the brain combines separately perceived attributes (shape, color, movement) into wholes; no single area receives all input — perception emerges from combined activity (Billock & Tsou, 2004).
- **Top-down signals**: descend from higher to lower levels, letting higher cortex modulate sensory input (Saalmann, Pigarev, & Vidyasagar, 2007). (See Tong, 2003.)

## Auditory System

**Sound**: vibrations of air molecules; humans hear ~**20-20,000 Hz**.

| Physical | Perceptual |
|---|---|
| **Amplitude** | Loudness |
| **Frequency** | Pitch |
| **Complexity** | Timbre |

- **Pure tones** (sine waves) are lab-only; real sounds are complex.
- **Fourier analysis**: breaks complex waves into component sine waves; the auditory system may perform something similar.
- **Fundamental frequency**: highest frequency of which a sound's components are multiples; most related to perceived pitch (e.g., 100, 200, 300 Hz → pitch near 100 Hz).
- **Missing fundamental**: pitch may correspond to a frequency physically absent (200, 300, 400 Hz heard at the pitch of a 100 Hz tone).

### The Ear (outside-in)
1. **Outer ear (pinna)** — funnels sound
2. **Auditory canal** — to the eardrum
3. **Tympanic membrane (eardrum)** — vibrates
4. **Ossicles** — **malleus** (hammer), **incus** (anvil), **stapes** (stirrup)
5. **Oval window** — transfers vibrations from stapes to cochlear fluid
6. **Cochlea** (snail-shaped, fluid-filled) — houses the **organ of Corti** (auditory receptor organ):
   - **Basilar membrane** (holds hair cells) + **tectorial membrane** (rests on hair cells)
   - **Hair cells** = receptors; deflection shears them → increase firing in the **auditory nerve** (branch of **cranial nerve VIII**)
7. **Round window** — dissipates cochlear fluid vibrations

- Cochlear sensitivity: can detect a **0.2%** frequency difference.
- **Tonotopic organization**: frequencies stimulate different basilar membrane points — **high** frequencies near the oval/round windows; **low** at the apex. (Analogous to **retinotopic** in vision.)
- **Semicircular canals**: receptors of the **vestibular system** (head movement, balance).

### From Ear to Primary Auditory Cortex
No single dominant pathway; a **network**. Ascending:
1. Auditory nerve → **ipsilateral cochlear nuclei**
2. → **superior olives** (bilateral; binaural combination at a low level)
3. → via **lateral lemniscus** → **inferior colliculi** (midbrain tectum)
4. → **medial geniculate nuclei** (thalamus)
5. → **primary auditory cortex**

Signals from each ear combine at the superior olives and reach **both** ipsilateral and contralateral cortex.

### Subcortical Sound Localization
- Mediated by **lateral and medial superior olives**:
  - **Medial superior olives**: **interaural time differences**.
  - **Lateral superior olives**: **interaural intensity differences**.
- Superior olives → superior/inferior colliculi. Deep **superior colliculus** layers carry a **map of auditory space** (King, Schnupp, & Thompson, 1998); superficial layers are **retinotopic**. General function: **localizing sensory input in space**.

> **KEY STUDY: Barn Owl Localization (Konishi, 2003):** barn owls localize sound better than any tested animal; each superior colliculus neuron is tuned to a specific spatial location.

### Auditory Cortex
- **Primary auditory cortex**: temporal lobe, within the **lateral fissure**; main input from the **medial geniculate nucleus**. Adjacent areas = **core**; surrounding = **belt** (secondary); beyond = **parabelt**. ~**10** secondary areas in primates.
- **Two principles**: **columnar** (column = same frequency range; Schreiner, 1992) and **tonotopic** (Schreiner, Read, & Sutter, 2000).
- **Two streams**: **anterior** = identifying sounds (**what**); **posterior** = locating sounds (**where**); both → prefrontal + posterior parietal cortex.
- **Auditory-visual interactions** occur early, even in primary sensory cortex (Macaluso & Driver, 2005); posterior parietal neurons can have visual, auditory, or both fields (Mulette-Gillman, Cohen, & Groh, 2005).

> **KEY STUDY: Bendor & Wang (2005):** a small area **just anterior to primary auditory cortex** responds to **pitch** (not frequency) using missing-fundamental stimuli; a parallel area exists in humans.

### Effects of Damage
- **Bilateral** auditory cortex lesions: temporary total deafness, then recovery; permanent loss of **sound localization** and impaired **frequency discrimination** (Heffner & Heffner, 2003).
- **Unilateral**: disrupts **contralateral** localization (partially contralateral).
- **Deafness:** total deafness in only **1%** of impaired (parallel pathways). **Conductive deafness** = ossicle damage; **nerve deafness** = cochlea/auditory nerve (mainly **hair cell loss**); **age-related** loss hits high frequencies first; **tinnitus** = central origin (cutting the peripheral nerve does NOT help; Eggermont & Roberts, 2004); **cochlear implants** convert sound to electrical signals (earlier = better).

## Somatosensory System: Touch and Pain

| System | Function |
|---|---|
| **Exteroceptive** | External skin stimuli (touch, temperature, pain) |
| **Proprioceptive** | Body position (muscles, joints, balance) |
| **Interoceptive** | Internal body conditions |

### Cutaneous Receptors

| Receptor | Adaptation | Responds to |
|---|---|---|
| **Free nerve endings** | — | Temperature change and pain |
| **Pacinian corpuscles** | Rapidly adapting | Sudden skin displacement |
| **Merkel's disks** | Slowly adapting | Gradual indentation |
| **Ruffini endings** | Slowly adapting | Skin stretch |

- **Stereognosis**: identifying objects by touch (requires manipulation to change stimulation).

### Dermatomes
- **Dermatome**: body area innervated by the dorsal roots of one spinal cord segment; adjacent dermatomes overlap (single dorsal root loss → little deficit). Face: **V1, V2, V3** of the **trigeminal nerve**.

### Two Major Somatosensory Pathways

**Dorsal-Column Medial-Lemniscus System** — **touch and proprioception**:
- dorsal root → ipsilateral dorsal columns → **dorsal column nuclei** (medulla) → **decussate** → **medial lemniscus** → **contralateral ventral posterior nucleus** (thalamus) → SI/SII/posterior parietal cortex.
- Trigeminal branches carry face somatosensation. Longest human neurons: dorsal column neurons from the **toes**.

**Anterolateral System** — **pain and temperature**; three tracts:
1. **Spinothalamic** → ventral posterior nucleus
2. **Spinoreticular** → reticular formation → parafascicular/intralaminar nuclei
3. **Spinotectal** → tectum
- Second-order neurons mostly decussate at the spinal cord entry level.

> **KEY CLINICAL STUDY: Mark, Ervin & Yakolev (1962):** ventral posterior lesions reduced touch/temperature/sharp pain but **not deep chronic pain**; parafascicular/intralaminar lesions **reduced deep chronic pain** without disrupting cutaneous sensitivity → separate deep-pain pathway.

### Cortical Areas of Somatosensation

> **KEY STUDY: Penfield et al. (1937):** stimulating the postcentral gyrus in conscious patients mapped SI as **somatotopic**, with greatest territory for hands, lips, tongue (the **somatosensory homunculus**).

- **SI (primary somatosensory cortex)**: postcentral gyrus; somatotopic; mostly contralateral; four functional strips (each its own map, each a different tactile quality).
- **SII (secondary)**: ventral to SI, into the lateral fissure; **bilateral** input; outputs to posterior parietal lobe.
- Two streams from SI (Hsiao, 2008): **dorsal** → posterior parietal (multisensory integration, attention); **ventral** → SII (object shape).

### Effects of SI Damage
- Corkin, Milner & Rasmussen (1970): unilateral SI excision → contralateral reduced light touch and stereognosis; bilateral deficits only when encroaching on SII.

### Somatosensory Association Cortex
- Posterior parietal cortex has **bimodal neurons** (somatosensory + visual); the visual field moves with the hand.

> **KEY CASE: W.M. (Schendel & Robertson, 2004):** right posterior cerebral artery stroke → left hemianopsia. Hand in lap: 13.6% detection in the left field; hand extended into the left field: improved detection (further still with a tennis racket) — bimodal neurons whose visual fields track the hand.

### Somatosensory Agnosias

| Agnosia | Definition | Lesion |
|---|---|---|
| **Astereognosia** | Cannot recognize objects by touch | SI (pure form rare) |
| **Asomatognosia** | Fails to recognize own body parts (usually left) | Right posterior parietal lobe |

- **Anosognosia**: failure to recognize one's own deficits; often with asomatognosia. **Contralateral neglect**: no response to stimuli contralateral to a right-hemisphere injury.

> **CASE OF AUNT BETTY:** right hemisphere stroke; left hemiplegia; could not identify her own left hand; no awareness of deficit (anosognosia) — asomatognosia with contralateral neglect.

## Perception of Pain

Pain is paradoxical in three ways.

### Paradox 1: Adaptiveness
- Pain is essential and warns of harm; there is **no special pain stimulus** — pain responds to any potentially harmful stimulation (Craig, 2003).

> **CASE OF MISS C. (Melzack & Wall, 1982):** never felt pain; bit off her tongue tip, suffered burns and joint pathology; no cardiovascular/respiratory or corneal-reflex responses. Died at **age 29** of infections/trauma. Cox et al. (2006): six Pakistani-family cases of congenital insensitivity from a **sodium ion channel** gene abnormality — a possible analgesic target.

### Paradox 2: No Clear Cortical Representation
- Many areas activate, varying across studies; **no single area is necessary**. SI/SII removal doesn't change pain threshold; **hemispherectomized** patients feel pain bilaterally.
- **Anterior cingulate cortex**: most linked to pain — to the **emotional reaction**, not threshold (Price, 2000).

### Paradox 3: Descending Pain Control
- Pain is suppressed by cognitive/emotional factors (ceremonies, battlefield wounds).

**Gate-Control Theory (Melzack & Wall, 1965):** descending brain signals activate **spinal gating circuits** to block incoming pain.

**Descending Analgesia Circuit (Basbaum & Fields, 1978):** three discoveries:
1. **Reynolds (1969)**: PAG stimulation alone gives surgical analgesia in rats.
2. PAG and other areas have **opiate receptors** (e.g., morphine).
3. **Endorphins** = endogenous opiate analgesics.

Circuit: **PAG** (opiates inhibit PAG inhibitory interneurons, raising output) → **serotonergic raphé nuclei** (medulla) → descend via **dorsal columns** → activate **inhibitory interneurons** in the **dorsal horn** → block pain. Some descending circuits can **increase** pain (Fields, 2004).

### Neuropathic Pain
- **Neuropathic pain**: severe chronic pain with **no recognizable stimulus**; persists after healing; triggered by innocuous stimuli; from **pathological CNS changes** (Reichling & Levine, 2009) and aberrant **glial signals** (Fields, 2009); may be felt in an amputated limb; cutting peripheral nerves and standard meds often fail.

## Chemical Senses: Smell and Taste

- **Olfaction**: airborne chemicals over nasal receptors. **Gustation**: chemicals in oral solution. **Flavor**: combined smell + taste (no smell → can't tell apple from onion).
- **Pheromones**: chemicals released by one member of a species influencing **conspecifics'** physiology/behavior.

> **KEY STUDY: Murphy & Schneider (1970):** male hamsters kill unfamiliar males and mate with receptive females; without olfaction, neither occurs. Swabbing a male intruder with female vaginal secretions → converts it from aggression target to sexual target — pheromonal control of both behaviors.

**Suggestive human evidence:** (1) women's smell peaks at ovulation/pregnancy; (2) menstrual synchrony among cohabiting women; (3) sex identifiable from breath/underarm odor; (4) men judge cycle stage from vaginal odor. But **no direct evidence** of human sex-attractant pheromones (these odors aren't generally found attractive).

### Olfactory System
- **Olfactory receptor cells** in the **olfactory mucosa** (upper nose); axons pass the **cribriform plate** → **olfactory bulbs** → **olfactory tracts** → brain. ~10 million cells in mice.
- **Receptor diversity:** rats/mice ~**1,500** receptor protein types; humans ~**1,000** (Keller & Vosshall, 2008). Each cell has **only one** receptor protein (Imai & Sakano, 2007). Odors encoded by **component processing** (activity pattern across types); receptors scattered with no clear spatial organization.
- **Olfactory glomeruli**: discrete bulb clusters; each receives input from cells sharing the **same receptor protein** (~2 glomeruli per type per bulb). Layout is **systematic** (Soucy et al., 2009): mirror-symmetric, consistent across a species, similar in related species; organizing principle unknown.
- **Renewal:** olfactory receptor cells are **replaced throughout life** (Doty, 2001), each surviving only a **few weeks**, with axons growing to the correct bulb sites.
- **Projections:** olfactory tract → **amygdala**, **piriform cortex**, medial temporal structures; via thalamus (**medial dorsal nucleus**) → **orbitofrontal cortex**; diffuse **limbic** projections. Olfaction is the **only** sense that does NOT primarily relay through the thalamus.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Three principles** of current sensory organization: hierarchical, **functionally segregated** (not homogeneous), **parallel** (not serial).

2. **Fundamental frequency / missing fundamental**: pitch corresponds to the fundamental (common factor), even if absent from the stimulus.

3. **Tonotopic organization**: high frequencies near the oval window, low near the apex (analogous to retinotopic).

4. **Sound localization**: medial superior olives = **interaural time differences**; lateral = **interaural intensity differences**; deep superior colliculus = auditory-space map.

5. **Auditory cortex**: temporal lobe within the **lateral fissure**; anterior = what, posterior = where.

6. **Conductive vs. nerve deafness**: conductive = ossicles; nerve = hair cell loss; tinnitus = central (cutting nerve doesn't help).

7. **Four cutaneous receptors**: free nerve endings (pain/temp), Pacinian (rapid, displacement), Merkel's disks (slow, indentation), Ruffini (slow, stretch).

8. **Two ascending pathways**: dorsal-column medial-lemniscus (touch/proprioception; decussates at medulla) vs. anterolateral (pain/temp; three tracts; decussates at spinal entry); Mark et al. (1962) — parafascicular/intralaminar = deep chronic pain.

9. **Somatosensory homunculus (Penfield, 1937)**: postcentral gyrus; biggest for hands/lips/tongue; SI mainly contralateral; SII bilateral.

10. **Asomatognosia vs. astereognosia**: asomatognosia = own body parts (left; right posterior parietal; with anosognosia/neglect); astereognosia = objects by touch.

11. **Gate-control theory (Melzack & Wall, 1965)**: descending signals gate spinal pain.

12. **Descending analgesia (Basbaum & Fields, 1978)**: PAG → serotonergic raphé nuclei → dorsal horn; opiates/endorphins at PAG; Reynolds (1969) PAG-only analgesia.

13. **Anterior cingulate cortex**: emotional reaction to pain, not threshold; no single area necessary; hemispherectomized patients feel bilateral pain.

14. **Neuropathic pain**: chronic, no stimulus; CNS pathology/glial signals; may occur in amputated limb; peripheral nerve cuts usually fail.

15. **Olfactory uniqueness**: only sense NOT primarily relaying through the thalamus; receptor cells replaced lifelong (weeks lifespan); ~1,000 receptor types; one type per cell; component processing; same type → same glomerulus.
