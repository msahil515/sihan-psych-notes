# Chapter 14: Sleep, Dreaming, and Circadian Rhythms

Sleep occupies >175,000 hours of a human lifetime. Covers sleep stages, theories of why/when we sleep, sleep-deprivation effects, circadian timing, and the neural substrates of sleep-wake regulation.

---

## 14.1 Stages of Sleep

### Three Standard Measures
- **Electroencephalogram (EEG)**: brain electrical activity; primary staging measure.
- **Electrooculogram (EOG)**: eye movements; **rapid eye movements (REMs)** occur during low-voltage fast EEG (discovered 1950s).
- **Electromyogram (EMG)**: muscle (neck) activity; **Berger and Oswald (1962)** found loss of neck EMG during REMs/fast EEG.

Together (EEG + EOG + EMG) these are the **three standard bases for defining sleep stages**.

- **First-night phenomenon**: disturbed sleep on the first lab night; participants sleep several nights before a study.

### Sleep EEG Stages
- **Alpha waves**: **8-12 Hz** bursts when eyes shut, preparing for sleep.
- **Stage 1**: low-voltage, high-frequency; no EMG/EOG change initially.
- **Stage 2**: higher amplitude, lower frequency; **K complexes** (a large negative then positive wave) and **sleep spindles** (1-2 s bursts of **12-14 Hz**).
- **Stage 3**: occasional **delta waves** (largest, slowest, **1-2 Hz**).
- **Stage 4**: predominance of delta waves; deepest NREM.

| Stage | Voltage | Frequency | Defining Feature |
|-------|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Alert wakefulness | Low | High | Beta waves |
| Pre-sleep | Low-Med | 8-12 Hz | Alpha waves |
| Stage 1 | Low | High-ish | No special waves |
| Stage 2 | Medium | Lower | K complexes + sleep spindles |
| Stage 3 | High | 1-2 Hz | Occasional delta waves |
| Stage 4 | Highest | 1-2 Hz | Predominance of delta waves |

### Sleep Architecture
- Progression: 1 → 2 → 3 → 4, then back up to Stage 1.
- **Initial stage 1 EEG**: first time through; no REMs, no muscle-tone loss. **Emergent stage 1 EEG**: all later returns; with REMs and core-muscle tone loss.
- Each cycle ~**90 minutes**; later in the night, more emergent Stage 1 (REM), less Stage 4.
- **REM sleep** = emergent Stage 1. **NREM sleep** = all other stages. **Slow-wave sleep (SWS)** = Stages 3 + 4.
- **REM correlates**: cerebral activity rises to waking levels; ANS variability increases; extremity twitches; **penile erection** in males (even in babies; unrelated to sexual content); **core muscle tone lost**.

### REM Sleep and Dreaming
- **Nathaniel Kleitman's lab** (1953) linked REM to dreaming.

> **KEY STUDY (Kleitman lab, 1953):** waking sleepers during REM yielded vivid narrative dream recall (10-12 dreams/night).

- **80%** of REM awakenings yield dream recall vs. **7%** of NREM. NREM dreams isolated; REM dreams narrative.

### Testing Beliefs about Dreaming
1. **External stimuli (Dement & Wolpert, 1958):** water sprayed on REM sleepers incorporated into the dream in **14 of 33 (42%)** cases.
2. **Real time (Dement & Kleitman, 1957):** subjects judged 5- vs. 15-min REM duration correctly in **92 of 111** cases — dreams run in real time.
3. **"I don't dream" (Goodenough et al., 1959):** claimed non-dreamers have normal REM and report dreams when woken from REM.
4. **Erections (Karacan et al., 1966):** erections during REM are no more complete during sexual dreams — a REM correlate, not sexual content.
5. **Sleeptalking (somniloquy)**: no special REM association; any stage. **Sleepwalking (somnambulism)**: Stage 3/4, **NEVER during REM** (core muscles relaxed); no proven treatment.

### Interpretation of Dreams
- **Freud**: dreams from repressed (often sexual) wishes. **Manifest dreams** (experienced) disguise **latent dreams** (true) via an **unconscious censor**. **Criticism**: no convincing evidence.
- **Activation-synthesis theory (Hobson, 1989)**: REM dreams are the cortex's effort to make sense of largely **random** brain-stem signals.

## 14.2 Why and When Do We Sleep?

### Recuperation Theories
- Wakefulness disrupts **homeostasis**; sleep restores it. Predict: long wakefulness → disturbances that worsen and are recovered afterward.

### Adaptation Theories (evolutionary/circadian)
- Sleep results from an **internal 24-hour clock**; evolved to protect from nighttime accident/predation. Focus on **when** we sleep; some say sleep plays no physiological role (like reproductive behavior — motivated but not needed for health) (Siegel, 2009).

### Comparative Analysis
- Most **mammals and birds** sleep (high-amplitude slow waves + low-amplitude fast waves); evidence in amphibians/reptiles/fish/insects is unclear.

| Species | Hours/Day | Species | Hours/Day |
|---------|---|---------|---|
| Giant sloth | 20 | Rhesus monkey, chimpanzee, baboon, red fox | 9 |
| Opossum, brown bat | 19 | Human, rabbit, guinea pig, pig | 8 |
| Giant armadillo | 18 | Gray seal, gray hyrax, Brazilian tapir | 6 |
| Owl monkey, nine-banded armadillo | 17 | Tree hyrax, rock hyrax | 5 |
| Arctic ground squirrel | 16 | Cow, goat, elephant, donkey, sheep | 3 |
| Tree shrew | 15 | Roe deer, horse | 2 |
| Cat, golden hamster | 14 | | |
| Mouse, rat, gray wolf | 13 | | |
| Arctic fox, gorilla, raccoon | 12 | | |

**Four conclusions:**
1. **Sleep serves an important function**: at-risk animals still sleep; **dolphins** evolved **unihemispheric sleep** (Rattenborg, Amlaner, & Lima, 2000).
2. **Not a higher-order human function** (improbable given comparative evidence).
3. **Not needed in large quantities** for survival (horses thrive on 2-3 hours).
4. **Energy expenditure doesn't predict sleep duration** (Siegel, 2005); adaptation theory better predicts time via **vulnerability while sleeping** + survival needs (zebras ~2 h; lions sleep 2-3 days after a kill).

## 14.3 Effects of Sleep Deprivation

- **Stress problem**: low sleep usually co-occurs with stress; deprivation studies must be interpreted cautiously.
- **Recuperation predictions**: wakefulness → worsening disturbances → sleep rebound.

### Classic Cases
- **Sleep-deprived students (Kleitman, 1963):** sleepiness came in **waves**, greatest at the same time each night (circadian influence).
- **Randy Gardner (Dement, 1978):** 1965 science-fair record of **264 hours 12 minutes (11 days)** awake; recovered with only **14 hours** sleep; no abnormal behavior — sleep debt not fully recovered.

### Experimental Studies
- **Even moderate deprivation (3-4 h less)** consistently: (1) increased sleepiness; (2) negative affect; (3) vigilance deficits.
- **Cognition**: complex effects less consistent (Drummond et al., 2004); logical deduction/critical thinking spared, but **executive function** (prefrontal-dependent: innovative/lateral/insightful thinking) is susceptible (Nilsson et al., 2005).
- **Physical performance**: surprisingly inconsistent.
- **Physiological**: lower body temperature, higher BP, reduced immune function, hormonal/metabolic changes — little proven health impact.

> **KEY STUDY: Cohen et al. (2009):** 153 volunteers exposed to a cold virus; those sleeping <8 h/night were not less likely to be infected but **more likely to develop cold symptoms** (correlational).

- **Microsleeps**: ~2-3 s sleep episodes after 2-3 days of deprivation; disrupt vigilance (deficits also occur without microsleeps).
- **Puzzles**: sleep-deprivation effects are subtle/variable; **4 hours** of sleep eliminated deficits from **64 hours** of deprivation (Rosa, Bonnett, & Warm, 2007).

### Animal Studies
> **KEY STUDY: Carousel apparatus (Rechtschaffen et al., 1983):** when an experimental rat's EEG showed sleep, a disk rotated, pushing it toward water (yoked control got identical rotations). Experimental rats **died after ~12 days** with swollen adrenals, ulcers, internal bleeding — **stress confound**; sleep deprivation may not be productively studied in nonhumans.

### REM-Sleep Deprivation
- **Two effects**: (1) **REM rebound** (extra REM for 2-3 nights; Brunner et al., 1990); (2) **increasing pressure** for REM (Webb & Agnew, 1967: **17 awakenings** night 1 → **67** night 7).
- **REM and memory contested**: **Vertes and Siegel (2005)** — evidence is unconvincing; antidepressants blocking REM don't impair memory; blocking REM didn't hurt verbal consolidation and **improved motor-task** consolidation (Rasch et al., 2008).
- **Default theory of REM sleep (Horne, 2000)**: when continuous NREM can't be maintained, the brain switches to **wakefulness** (if bodily needs exist) or the **default state (REM)** (if not).

> **KEY STUDY: Nykamp et al. (1998):** substituting **15 min of wakefulness** for each lost REM period → no next-day tiredness and **no REM rebound** (only 5 h sleep). Replicated in rats (Oniani & Lortkipanidze, 2003).

### Sleep Deprivation Increases Sleep Efficiency
- Deprived people become **more efficient sleepers** with more **slow-wave sleep (SWS)** (the restorative function).

**Six lines of evidence:**
1. After deprivation, people regain little total sleep but most **lost Stage 4**.
2. Post-deprivation SWS has an even higher proportion of slow waves.
3. 6-hour sleepers get as much SWS as 8+ hour sleepers.
4. Morning naps (few slow waves) don't reduce that night's sleep.
5. Gradual sleep reduction cuts Stage 1/2 but leaves **SWS about the same**.
6. Waking from REM barely increases next-day sleepiness; waking from SWS has major effects.

- **Methodological implication**: short-term deprivation studies are **virtually useless** for finding true sleep need — only **long-term sleep-reduction** studies (allowing efficiency to maximize) can.

## 14.4 Circadian Sleep Cycles

- **Circadian** ("about a day"): ~24-hour cycles. **Circadian sleep-wake cycle** is the most obvious; nearly all physiological processes show circadian rhythmicity (Gillette & Sejnowski, 2005).
- **Zeitgebers** ("time givers"): cues that entrain circadian rhythms; the most important is the **light-dark cycle** (others: social interaction, eating, exercise).

### Free-Running Cycles
- **Free-running rhythms**: persist without zeitgebers. **Free-running period**: usually **longer than 24 hours**; ~**24.2 hours** in humans under constant moderate light (Czeizler et al., 1999) — the clock runs slightly slow.
- Free-running cycles are innate (even rats raised in constant conditions show them; Richter, 1971), supporting circadian over recuperative factors.
- **Internal desynchronization**: two circadian rhythms (e.g., sleep-wake and body temperature) develop different free-running periods → multiple clocks exist; sleep is not causally tied to body-temperature drops.
- Staying awake longer → **shorter** following sleep (Wever, 1979), another challenge to recuperation theories.

### Jet Lag and Shift Work
- **Jet lag**: from rapidly shifted zeitgebers. **East-bound = phase advances** (harder); **west-bound = phase delays** (easier). ~10 days to fully adjust to a 10.5-hour advance.
- **Countermeasures**: gradual pre-flight shifting; early-morning bright light after east-bound flights.

> **KEY STUDY: Mrosovsky & Salmon (1987):** hamsters forced to run 7 hours before usual activity adapted to an 8-hour phase advance in **1 day** vs. ~10 for controls — exercise speeds phase-advance adaptation.

- **Shift work**: schedule **phase delays** rather than advances (Driscoll, Grunstein, & Rogers, 2007), like the east/west flight asymmetry.

### Circadian Clock in the SCN
- **Richter (1967)**: medial hypothalamic lesions disrupt circadian cycles. Lesions of the **suprachiasmatic nuclei (SCN)** abolish circadian periodicity of sleep but not total sleep time.

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Ralph et al. (1990) — SCN Transplant:** mutant hamsters have **20-hour** cycles (normal = 25 h). Transplanting mutant fetal SCN into SCN-lesioned normal hamsters restored **~20-hour** cycles; the reverse restored **~25-hour** cycles → the rhythm belongs to the **SCN itself**.

- **SCN is not the only clock**: some rhythms survive SCN lesions; food/water entrainment persists; isolated body cells show free-running cycles in culture.

### Neural Mechanisms of Entrainment
- Cutting **optic nerves** (before chiasm) eliminated entrainment; cutting **optic tracts** (after chiasm) did not → critical axons branch off near the chiasm: the **retinohypothalamic tracts** to the SCN.
- Neither **rods** nor **cones** are necessary; entrainment uses specialized **retinal ganglion cells** tuned to slow background illumination, with the photopigment **melanopsin** (Hattar et al., 2002).

### Genetics of Circadian Rhythms
- **Tau gene** (Ralph & Menaker, **1988**): first mammalian circadian gene (mutation → 20-hour hamster cycles); molecular structure characterized **2000**.
- **Clock gene** (mice): first to have its molecular structure characterized (**1997**).
- **Three discoveries**: (1) circadian genes are **evolutionarily conserved** across species; (2) the mechanism involves **gene expression** (protein transcription cycles); (3) similar **cellular clocks** exist in most body cells, entrained by the SCN.

## 14.5 Four Brain Areas Involved in Sleep

### Two Hypothalamic Areas (von Economo)
- **Baron Constantin von Economo** (Viennese neurologist, WWI) studied **encephalitis lethargica** (viral infection; most victims slept continuously, a minority had insomnia).

| Damage | Effect |
|----------------------|--------------|
| Posterior hypothalamus + adjacent midbrain | **Excessive sleepiness** |
| Anterior hypothalamus + adjacent basal forebrain | **Insomnia** |

- **Posterior hypothalamus promotes wakefulness; anterior hypothalamus promotes sleep** (confirmed in animals; Szymusiak & McGinty, 2008).

### Reticular Formation
- **Cerveau isolé (Bremer, 1936)**: midcollicular transection → isolated forebrain shows **almost continuous slow-wave sleep**.
- **Encéphale isolé (Bremer, 1937)**: caudal transection → **normal sleep-wake cycle** preserved.
- **Moruzzi and Magoun (1949)**: combining transection + lesion + stimulation evidence (reticular core lesions disrupt sleep-wake EEG; pontine reticular stimulation awakens cats) → a **wakefulness-producing area in the reticular formation** lies between the transection sites.

## Case Study: The Woman Who Wouldn't Sleep (Meddis, 1977)
- "Miss M": awake ~23 hours/day; **~99 minutes** total sleep/night; never felt sleepy; lab EEG confirmed genuine short sleep. Challenges beliefs about necessary sleep amount.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Three measures**: EEG, EOG, EMG define sleep stages; Berger & Oswald (1962) found the EMG loss.

2. **Stage 2** = K complexes + sleep spindles (12-14 Hz); **Stage 4** = predominant delta (1-2 Hz); **Stage 3** = occasional delta.

3. **REM** = emergent Stage 1 + REMs + core-muscle atonia + high cerebral activity.

4. **80% REM** vs. **7% NREM** awakenings yield dream recall; REM dreams narrative.

5. **Sleepwalking** = Stage 3/4, **never REM**; sleeptalking = any stage.

6. **Freud** (manifest/latent, unconscious censor) vs. **Hobson activation-synthesis (1989)** (random brain-stem signals).

7. **Recuperation** (need ∝ prior wakefulness) vs. **adaptation** (timing from internal clock).

8. **Randy Gardner (1965)**: 264 h 12 min awake; recovered with only 14 h — debt not fully recovered.

9. Sleep deprivation impairs **executive function** (prefrontal); spares logical deduction/critical thinking.

10. **Sleep efficiency increases** with deprivation; mostly **Stage 4** is regained → brief studies underestimate true need.

11. **SCN** = primary clock; lesions abolish periodicity, not total sleep; **Ralph et al. (1990)** transplant proved it.

12. **Free-running period** ~24.2 h in humans (clock runs slightly slow).

13. **Retinohypothalamic tracts** carry light to the SCN; photoreceptors are **melanopsin** ganglion cells (not rods/cones).

14. **Tau gene** (Ralph & Menaker, 1988) first identified; **Clock gene** first molecularly characterized (mice, 1997).

15. **Phase delays easier than advances**: east-bound flights/phase advances cause more jet lag; shift workers better moved to later schedules.
