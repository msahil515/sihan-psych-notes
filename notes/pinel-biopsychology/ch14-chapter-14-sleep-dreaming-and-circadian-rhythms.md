# Chapter 14: Sleep, Dreaming, and Circadian Rhythms

Sleep occupies more than 175,000 hours of a typical human lifetime, implying a critical biological function. This chapter covers the psychophysiology of sleep stages, theories of why and when we sleep, the effects of sleep deprivation, circadian timing mechanisms, and the neural substrates of sleep-wake regulation.

---

## 14.1 Stages of Sleep

### Three Standard Psychophysiological Measures of Sleep

Sleep research requires continuous monitoring of brain and body activity. Three instruments define standard sleep measurement:

- **Electroencephalogram (EEG)**: Records electrical brain activity; the primary measure for staging sleep. During sleep, EEG waves are generally high-voltage and slow, but periods of low-voltage, fast waves punctuate the night.
- **Electrooculogram (EOG)**: Records eye movements; discovered in the 1950s that **rapid eye movements (REMs)** occur under closed eyelids during periods of low-voltage, fast EEG activity.
- **Electromyogram (EMG)**: Records muscle activity; specifically neck muscle EMG. Berger and Oswald (1962) discovered loss of electromyographic (EMG) activity in neck muscles during the same periods as REMs and low-voltage EEG.

These three measures (EEG + EOG + EMG) became the **three standard psychophysiological bases for defining stages of sleep**.

> **Key Discovery Timeline:**
> - 1950s: REMs discovered during periods of low-voltage, fast EEG
> - 1962: Berger and Oswald discover loss of neck EMG activity during same periods

**First-night phenomenon**: Disturbed sleep observed during a participant's first night in a sleep laboratory. Standard practice is to have each participant sleep several nights in the lab before commencing a study.

---

### Four Stages of Sleep EEG

Sleep stages are defined by characteristic EEG patterns. The progression from wakefulness into deep sleep involves increasing voltage and decreasing frequency.

#### Pre-Sleep / Transition to Sleep

- **Alpha waves**: Waxing and waning bursts of **8-12 Hz** EEG waves; appear when eyes are shut and person prepares to sleep; punctuate the low-voltage, high-frequency waves of alert wakefulness.
- On falling asleep: sudden transition to **Stage 1 sleep EEG**.

#### Stage 1 Sleep EEG

- **Low-voltage, high-frequency** signal, similar to but slightly slower than alert wakefulness.
- No striking EMG or EOG changes at this initial occurrence (**initial stage 1 EEG**).

#### Stage 2 Sleep EEG

- Slightly higher amplitude and lower frequency than Stage 1.
- Characterized by two distinctive waveforms:
  - **K complexes**: Single large negative wave (upward deflection) followed immediately by a single large positive wave (downward deflection). (Cash et al., 2009)
  - **Sleep spindles**: 1-2 second waxing-and-waning burst of **12-14 Hz** waves.

#### Stage 3 Sleep EEG

- Defined by the **occasional presence of delta waves**: the largest and slowest EEG waves, frequency **1-2 Hz**.

#### Stage 4 Sleep EEG

- Defined by a **predominance of delta waves**.
- Deepest stage of NREM sleep.

#### Key EEG Summary Table

| Stage | Voltage | Frequency | Defining Feature |
|-------|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Alert wakefulness | Low | High | Beta waves |
| Pre-sleep | Low-Med | 8-12 Hz | Alpha waves |
| Stage 1 | Low | High-ish | Transition; no special waves |
| Stage 2 | Medium | Lower | K complexes + Sleep spindles |
| Stage 3 | High | Low (1-2 Hz) | Occasional delta waves |
| Stage 4 | Highest | Lowest (1-2 Hz) | Predominance of delta waves |

---

### The Cycling Architecture of a Night's Sleep

- Sleepers progress: Stage 1 → Stage 2 → Stage 3 → Stage 4, then retreat back through stages to Stage 1.
- **Initial stage 1 EEG**: First time through Stage 1; no REMs, no loss of core-muscle tone.
- **Emergent stage 1 EEG**: All subsequent returns to Stage 1; accompanied by REMs and loss of tone in core body muscles.
- Each sleep cycle is approximately **90 minutes** long.
- As the night progresses: more time in emergent Stage 1 (REM), less time in Stage 4.
- Brief periods of wakefulness occur throughout the night (usually not remembered in the morning).

#### Terminology Clarifications

- **REM sleep** (pronounced "rehm"): Sleep associated with emergent stage 1 EEG; characterized by rapid eye movements.
- **NREM sleep** (Non-REM sleep): All other sleep stages combined (Stages 1-4 excluding emergent Stage 1).
- **Slow-wave sleep (SWS)**: Stages 3 and 4 together; named after the delta waves that characterize them.

#### Other Physiological Correlates of REM Sleep

- Cerebral activity (oxygen consumption, blood flow, neural firing) increases to waking levels in many brain structures.
- General increase in variability of autonomic nervous system activity (blood pressure, pulse, respiration).
- Occasional twitching of extremity muscles.
- Often some degree of **penile erection** in males (present even in babies; not linked to sexual dream content).
- Core muscle tone is **lost** (atonia of body-core muscles).

### REM Sleep and Dreaming

**Nathaniel Kleitman's laboratory** at the University of Chicago was the site of the pivotal REM-dreaming discovery in 1953. Kleitman and his students hypothesized that REM sleep might be the physiological correlate of dreaming.

> **KEY STUDY: REM Awakening Study (Kleitman lab, 1953)**
> - Method: Woke sleepers during REM episodes
> - Finding: Vivid, narrative dream recall; subjects could report on 10-12 dreams per night
> - Significance: Opened dreaming to scientific investigation

Key statistics:
- **80%** of awakenings from REM sleep led to dream recall.
- Only **7%** of awakenings from NREM sleep led to dream recall.
- NREM dreams: isolated experiences (e.g., "I was falling").
- REM dreams: story-like narratives.

---

### Testing Common Beliefs about Dreaming

Five beliefs were scientifically tested using REM as a research window:

#### 1. External Stimuli Incorporation into Dreams

> **KEY STUDY: Dement and Wolpert (1958)**
> - Method: Sprayed water on sleeping volunteers after a few minutes of REM sleep, then awakened them seconds later.
> - Finding: In 14 of 33 cases (42%), the water was incorporated into the dream narrative.
> - Conclusion: External stimuli CAN be incorporated into dreams.

#### 2. Dreams Run on Real Time (Not Just an Instant)

> **KEY STUDY: Dement and Kleitman (1957)**
> - Method: Awakened volunteers 5 or 15 minutes after REM onset; asked them to judge duration.
> - Finding: Correct in **92 of 111 cases**.
> - Conclusion: Dream duration corresponds to real elapsed time.

#### 3. "I Don't Dream" Claims

> **KEY STUDY: Goodenough et al. (1959)**
> - Finding: People who claim not to dream have just as much REM sleep as normal dreamers; they report dreams when awakened during REM episodes (less frequently than normal dreamers, but they do occur).
> - Conclusion: Everyone dreams; non-dreamers simply fail to recall dreams.

#### 4. Penile Erections and Sexual Dream Content

> **KEY STUDY: Karacan et al. (1966)**
> - Finding: Erections are NO more complete during sexually explicit dreams than during non-sexual dreams.
> - Conclusion: Penile erection during REM is a physiological correlate of REM state, NOT an indicator of sexual dream content. Even babies show REM-related penile erections.

#### 5. Sleeptalking and Sleepwalking During Dreaming

- **Sleeptalking (somniloquy)**: Has NO special association with REM sleep; can occur during any stage, often during transitions to wakefulness. (Dyken, Yamada, & Lin-Dyken, 2001)
- **Sleepwalking (somnambulism)**: Usually occurs during Stage 3 or Stage 4 sleep; NEVER occurs during REM sleep (core muscles are relaxed during REM). (Usui et al., 2007)
- No proven treatment for sleepwalking (Harris & Grunstein, 2008).

---

### Interpretation of Dreams

#### Freudian Theory

- **Sigmund Freud**: Dreams are triggered by unacceptable repressed wishes, often sexual in nature.
- **Manifest dreams**: The dreams we actually experience; disguised versions of real dreams.
- **Latent dreams**: The true underlying dreams; hidden by an unconscious censor.
- An **unconscious censor** disguises and subtracts information from real dreams so we can endure them.
- Clinical implication: Understanding patients requires interpreting manifest dreams to reveal latent content.
- **Criticism**: No convincing evidence supports Freudian dream theory; the 1890s brain science that formed its foundation is obsolete.

#### Activation-Synthesis Theory (Modern Alternative)

- Proposed by **Hobson (1989)**.
- Based on observation that during REM sleep, many **brain-stem circuits** become active and bombard the cerebral cortex with neural signals.
- Essence: Information supplied to the cortex during REM sleep is largely **random**; the resulting dream is the cortex's effort to make sense of these random signals.
- Reference: Eiser (2005).

## 14.2 Why Do We Sleep, and Why Do We Sleep When We Do?

Two fundamentally different theoretical frameworks explain sleep:

### Recuperation Theories of Sleep

- **Essence**: Being awake disrupts the **homeostasis** (internal physiological stability) of the body; sleep is required to restore it.
- Sleepiness is triggered by deviation from homeostasis caused by wakefulness.
- Sleep is terminated by return to homeostasis.
- Common proposed restorative function: restoring energy levels.
- **Predictions**: Long wakefulness causes disturbances; disturbances worsen over time; lost sleep is recovered after deprivation.

### Adaptation Theories of Sleep (Evolutionary/Circadian)

- **Essence**: Sleep is not a reaction to wakefulness effects, but the result of an **internal 24-hour timing mechanism**.
- Humans are programmed to sleep at night regardless of daytime activity.
- Sleep evolved to protect early humans from accident and predation during the dark (before artificial lighting).
- Focus more on **when** we sleep than on its function.
- Some versions propose sleep plays NO role in efficient physiological functioning.
- Sleep is like reproductive behavior: highly motivated, but not needed for health.
- Sleep conserves energy and reduces susceptibility to mishap in the dark.
- References: Rattenborg, Martinez-Gonzales, & Lesku (2009); Siegel (2009).

---

### Comparative Analysis of Sleep

#### What Most Species Show

- Most **mammals and birds** sleep, with EEG characterized by high-amplitude, low-frequency waves punctuated by low-amplitude, high-frequency waves (Siegel, 2008).
- Evidence for sleep in **amphibians, reptiles, fish, and insects** is less clear; some display inactivity/unresponsiveness but its relation to mammalian sleep is not established.

#### Four Key Conclusions from Comparative Sleep Research

1. **Sleep serves an important physiological function** (not merely energy conservation/predation avoidance), because:
   - Animals at increased risk during sleep (e.g., antelopes) still sleep.
   - Some marine mammals (e.g., **dolphins**) evolved unihemispheric sleep (sleeping one hemisphere at a time to control resurfacing for air). (Rattenborg, Amlaner, & Lima, 2000)
   - Natural selection would not favor risk of predation unless sleep served a critical function.

2. **Sleep's primary function is not a special, higher-order human function**. Suggestions that sleep allows complex brain reprogramming or emotional release are improbable given comparative evidence.

3. **Sleep is not necessarily needed in large quantities** for survival. Horses and other animals thrive on 2-3 hours/day. Also, natural-environment sleep patterns can differ substantially from captivity patterns (Horne, 2009); e.g., some heavy captive sleepers sleep little in the wild during food scarcity or migration (Siegel, 2008).

4. **Energy expenditure does not predict sleep duration**: No strong relationship between species' sleep time and level of activity, body size, or body temperature (Siegel, 2005). Giant sloths sleep 20 hours/day despite low energy expenditure. In humans, energy expenditure has little effect on subsequent sleep (Driver & Taylor, 2000; Youngstedt & Kline, 2006). Adaptation theories correctly predict sleep time based on **vulnerability while sleeping** and **time needed for survival requirements** (e.g., zebras graze nearly continuously and are vulnerable when asleep: ~2 hours/day; African lions can sleep almost continuously for 2-3 days after a kill).

## 14.3 Effects of Sleep Deprivation

### Interpretation of the Effects of Sleep Deprivation: The Stress Problem

- Most people who sleep little in real life do so under **extreme stress** (illness, excessive work, shift work, drugs, exams).
- Stress can have adverse effects **independent of sleep loss**, making it difficult to isolate pure sleep-deprivation effects.
- Even in controlled lab studies, the deprivation procedure itself may be stressful.
- Many popular media studies confound **low sleep** with **high stress**, limiting conclusions about sleep's function.
- Sleep-deprivation studies must be interpreted with particular caution.

---

### Predictions of Recuperation Theories about Sleep Deprivation

Recuperation theories predict:

1. Long wakefulness will produce physiological and behavioral disturbances.
2. These disturbances will grow steadily worse as deprivation continues.
3. After deprivation, much of missed sleep will be recovered (sleep rebound).

---

### Two Classic Sleep-Deprivation Case Studies

#### The Case of the Sleep-Deprived Students (Kleitman, 1963)

- Observations from multiple subjects kept awake for several days.
- Night 1: Little sleepiness; able to study; attack of drowsiness between 3-6 AM.
- Day 1 after: Felt well except slight malaise when sitting still; normal activities masked fatigue.
- Night 2: Reading/study nearly impossible; sitting induced sleep; powerful urge to sleep in early morning hours; sleepiness again diminished later.
- Night 3, Day 4: Similar pattern; maximum sleepiness reached.
- Pattern: Wavelike increases and decreases in sleepiness; greatest drowsiness at the same time each night (indicating circadian influence).
- Source: Kleitman (1963), pp. 220-221.

#### The Case of Randy Gardner (Dement, 1978)

- **1965 science fair project**: Randy Gardner (San Diego, California) with two classmates.
- Goal: Break the then-world record of **260 hours** of consecutive wakefulness.
- William Dement joined the team after reading about the project.
- Duration: **264 hours and 12 minutes** (11 days) of continuous wakefulness.
- Behavior: Friendly, cooperative; held a press conference near end; "It's just mind over matter."
- Recovery sleep: Only **14 hours** on the first night; returned to usual 8-hour schedule thereafter.
- Key finding: **No substantial recovery sleep** despite 11 days deprivation (typical of such cases).
- No abnormal or disturbed behavior observed.
- Source: Dement (1978), pp. 38-39.

---

### Experimental Studies of Sleep Deprivation in Humans

Hundreds of studies since Dement and Kleitman in mid-20th century, assessing schedules from slight reduction to total deprivation for several nights (see Durmer & Dinges, 2005).

#### Consistent Effects of Even Moderate Sleep Deprivation (3-4 hours less than normal)

1. **Increased sleepiness**: Report feeling more sleepy; fall asleep faster when given opportunity.
2. **Negative affect**: Perform worse on written mood tests.
3. **Vigilance deficits**: Perform poorly on vigilance tasks (e.g., watching a screen and responding when a light flickers).

#### Effects on Cognitive Function

- Complex cognitive function effects have been **less consistent** (Drummond et al., 2004).
- Researchers prefer simple, monotonous tasks as they are most sensitive (Harrison & Horne, 2000).
- Growing evidence of complex cognitive task disruption, often requiring substantial deprivation (~24 hours) for consistent effects (Killgore, Balkin, & Wesensten, 2006; Strangman et al., 2005).
- **Immune to disruption**: Tests of logical deduction and critical thinking are largely spared.
- **Susceptible to disruption**: **Executive function** tests (Nilsson et al., 2005).
  - **Executive function**: Cognitive abilities dependent on the **prefrontal cortex**.
  - Includes: innovative thinking, lateral thinking, insightful thinking, assimilating new information to update plans and strategies.

#### Effects on Physical Performance

- Surprisingly inconsistent; only a few measures affected even after lengthy deprivation (Van Helder & Radomski, 1989).

#### Physiological Consequences

- Reduced body temperature.
- Increases in blood pressure.
- Decreases in some aspects of immune function.
- Hormonal and metabolic changes.
- Little evidence these changes have consequences for health or performance.

> **KEY STUDY: Cohen et al. (2009) — Sleep and Susceptibility to Infection**
> - Method: Exposed 153 healthy volunteers to a cold virus; assessed sleep duration and illness.
> - Finding: Those sleeping less than 8 hours/night were NOT less likely to become infected, but were MORE likely to develop cold symptoms.
> - Limitation: Correlational study; cannot directly implicate sleep duration as causal.
> - Significance: Focused directly on susceptibility to infection rather than immune function markers — a methodological model for future studies.

#### Microsleeps

- After 2-3 days of continuous deprivation, most participants experience **microsleeps**: brief periods of sleep (~2-3 seconds), during which eyelids droop and responsiveness decreases even while sitting/standing.
- Microsleeps disrupt vigilance performance.
- Performance deficits on vigilance tests also occur in sleep-deprived individuals NOT experiencing microsleeps (Ferrara, De Gennaro, & Bertini, 1999).

#### Comparison to Deprivation of Other Motivated Behaviors

- Food deprivation: starvation and death.
- Sex deprivation: total loss of reproductive capacity.
- Sleep deprivation effects: **subtle, selective, and variable** — puzzling given the powerful drive to sleep.
- Another puzzle: Performance deficits from extended deprivation disappear readily; in one study, **4 hours** of sleep eliminated deficits from **64 hours** of deprivation (Rosa, Bonnett, & Warm, 2007).

---

### Sleep-Deprivation Studies with Laboratory Animals

> **KEY STUDY: Carousel Apparatus (Rechtschaffen et al., 1983; Rechtschaffen & Bergmann, 1995)**
> - Design: Two rats (experimental + yoked control) placed in separate chambers.
> - Method: Whenever experimental rat's EEG shows sleep, the shared disk floor rotates, pushing the sleeping rat into a shallow pool of water if it doesn't awaken.
> - Yoked control: Exposed to identical disk rotations; can avoid water by walking in opposite direction IF awake.
> - Result: Experimental rats typically **died after ~12 days**; yoked controls remained reasonably healthy.
> - Confound: Repeated awakening by apparatus may kill through stress, not sleep deprivation per se.
> - Postmortem findings in experimental rats: **swollen adrenal glands, gastric ulcers, internal bleeding** (pathological stress signs).
> - Conclusion: Sleep deprivation may be one phenomenon that cannot be productively studied in nonhumans because of unavoidable confounding effects of extreme stress (Benington & Heller, 1999; D'Almeida et al., 1997; Horne, 2000).

---

### REM-Sleep Deprivation

Method: Wake participants each time REM sleep begins.

#### Two Consistent Effects of REM-Sleep Deprivation

1. **REM rebound**: Following deprivation, participants have MORE than their usual amount of REM sleep for the first 2-3 nights (Brunner et al., 1990).
2. **Increasing pressure for REM**: With each successive night, participants must be awakened more frequently to prevent REM accumulation.
   - Webb & Agnew (1967): Night 1 required **17 awakenings**; Night 7 required **67 awakenings**.

#### REM Sleep and Memory: Contested Evidence

- Many reviewers treated REM sleep's positive effect on **explicit memory storage** as established.
- **Vertes and Siegel (2005)** argued the evidence is unconvincing:
  - Numerous negative studies have been ignored.
  - Patients taking **antidepressant drugs that block REM sleep** show no obvious memory problems even after months/years.
  - Pharmacologically blocking REM sleep in volunteers did not disrupt consolidation of verbal memories, and actually **improved consolidation of motor task memory** (Rasch et al., 2008).

#### Default Theory of REM Sleep (Horne, 2000)

- Difficult to maintain continuous NREM sleep; brain periodically switches to one of two other states:
  - If immediate bodily needs exist (eating, drinking): switches to **wakefulness**.
  - If no immediate needs: switches to the **default state (REM sleep)**.
- REM sleep and wakefulness are similar states; REM is more adaptive when no immediate needs exist.
- Many similarities between REM and wakefulness support this view.

> **KEY STUDY: Nykamp et al. (1998) — Default Theory Support**
> - Method: Awakened young adults at each REM onset; substituted a **15-minute period of wakefulness** for each lost REM period (instead of immediately returning to sleep).
> - Finding: Participants were NOT tired the next day despite only 5 hours of sleep; displayed NO REM rebound.
> - Interpretation: No need for REM sleep if wakefulness substituted for it.
> - Replicated in rats: Oniani & Lortkipanidze (2003).
> - Consistent with finding that antidepressants reducing REM sleep increase nighttime awakenings.

---

### Sleep Deprivation Increases the Efficiency of Sleep (Neuroplasticity)

One of the most important findings: individuals deprived of sleep become **more efficient sleepers**, with a higher proportion of **slow-wave sleep (SWS; stages 3 and 4)**, which appears to serve the main restorative function.

#### Six Lines of Evidence for Increased Sleep Efficiency

1. After sleep deprivation, people regain only a small proportion of total lost sleep, but regain most of their **lost Stage 4 sleep** (Borbély et al., 1981; De Gennaro, Ferrara, & Bertini, 2000; Lucidi et al., 1997).
2. After sleep deprivation, slow-wave sleep EEG contains an even higher proportion than usual of slow waves (Aeschbach et al., 1996; Borbély, 1981).
3. People who normally sleep 6 hours or less get as much slow-wave sleep as those who sleep 8+ hours (Jones & Oswald, 1966; Webb & Agnew, 1970).
4. Morning naps after a full night's sleep show few slow waves and do NOT reduce the following night's sleep (Åkerstedt & Gillberg, 1981; Hume & Mills, 1977).
5. Gradually reducing sleep reduces Stage 1 and Stage 2 duration but **slow-wave sleep duration remains about the same** (Mullaney et al., 1977; Webb & Agnew, 1975).
6. Repeatedly waking people during **REM sleep** produces little increase in next-day sleepiness, whereas waking people during **slow-wave sleep** has major effects (Nykamp et al., 1998).

#### Implication for Sleep Research Methodology

- Conventional short-term sleep-deprivation studies are **virtually useless** for discovering how much sleep people truly need.
- Our bodies respond negatively to less sleep than we are used to, but this does not indicate that the lost sleep was needed.
- True sleep need can be assessed ONLY by **long-term sleep-reduction experiments** (many weeks) allowing adaptation and maximization of sleep efficiency.
- This is consistent with the plasticity and adaptiveness of the adult mammalian brain.

## 14.4 Circadian Sleep Cycles

### Overview of Circadian Rhythms

- **Circadian** (from Latin: "lasting about a day"): Approximately 24-hour biological cycles.
- Most surface-dwelling species have circadian rhythms adapted to the light-dark cycle (Foster & Kreitzman, 2004).
- **Circadian sleep-wake cycle**: Most obvious circadian rhythm.
  - Humans: active in light; sleep at night.
  - Nocturnal animals (e.g., rats): sleep during day, active at night.
- Almost every physiological, biochemical, and behavioral process shows some circadian rhythmicity (Gillette & Sejnowski, 2005).
- **Zeitgebers** (German: "time givers"; pronounced "ZITE-gay-bers"): Environmental cues that can entrain (control the timing of) circadian rhythms.
  - Most important zeitgeber for mammals: the **daily light-dark cycle**.
  - Other zeitgebers (active in continuous darkness/light): social interaction, hoarding, eating, exercise (e.g., in hamsters — Mistlberger et al., 1996; Sinclair & Mistlberger, 1997).
  - Artificial manipulation: Alternating 11.5-hour light/11.5-hour dark periods → circadian cycles conform to a **23-hour day**.

---

### Free-Running Circadian Sleep-Wake Cycles

- **Free-running rhythms**: Circadian rhythms that persist in environments devoid of zeitgebers (constant conditions).
- **Free-running period**: Duration of a free-running circadian rhythm.
  - Varies between subjects but is relatively constant within a subject.
  - Usually **longer than 24 hours**.
  - Typical in humans under constant moderate illumination: approximately **24.2 hours** (Czeizler et al., 1999).
  - Example: One subject fell asleep at intervals of approximately **25.3 hours** for an entire month (Wever, 1979).
- Implication: We all have an internal biological clock that runs a little slow unless entrained by environmental time cues.
- Free-running cycles do NOT need to be learned; even rats born and raised in unchanging environments (continuous light or dark) display regular free-running cycles slightly longer than 24 hours (Richter, 1971).
- Regularity of free-running cycles despite variations in physical and mental activity supports **circadian factors over recuperative factors** in sleep regulation.

#### Internal Desynchronization

- Many animals have a circadian body temperature cycle related to their sleep-wake cycle: tend to sleep during the **falling phase** of body temperature and wake during its **rising phase**.
- In constant lab environments, sleep-wake and body temperature cycles can **break away from each other**.
- **Internal desynchronization**: The phenomenon of two circadian rhythms in the same organism having different free-running periods simultaneously. (De La Iglesia, Cambras, & Díez-Noguera, 2008)
  - Example: One subject initially showed both cycles at 25.7 hours; sleep-wake shifted to 33.4 hours while body temperature shifted to 25.1 hours.
- Implication: More than one circadian timing mechanism exists; sleep is NOT causally related to the body temperature decreases normally associated with it.

#### Another Challenge to Recuperation Theories

- When subjects stay awake longer than usual, the **following sleep time is shorter, not longer** (Wever, 1979).
- Humans and animals are programmed for ~24-hour cycles; more wakefulness = less time for sleep.

---

### Jet Lag and Shift Work

Two main disruptions of circadian rhythmicity in modern industrialized societies:

#### Jet Lag

- **Jet lag**: Disruption caused when zeitgebers controlling circadian phases are rapidly shifted.
  - **East-bound flights**: **Phase advances** (clocks set forward; harder to adjust).
  - **West-bound flights**: **Phase delays** (clocks set back; easier to adjust).
- Typical adjustment: ~**10 days** to fully adjust to a 10.5-hour phase advance (e.g., Tokyo to Boston).
- Produces: sleep disturbances, fatigue, general malaise, deficits in physical and cognitive function.

#### Countermeasures for Jet Lag

1. **Gradual pre-flight schedule shifting**: Shift sleep-wake cycle in days prior to flight.
2. **Post-flight interventions**:
   - Exposure to intense light early in the morning after east-bound flight accelerates adaptation to phase advance.
   - Exercise-based strategy:

> **KEY STUDY: Mrosovsky & Salmon (1987) — Exercise and Phase Advance Adaptation**
> - Species: Hamsters
> - Method: Forced 3-hour bout of wheel running 7 hours before usual activity time on day of an 8-hour phase advance.
> - Finding: Hamsters that exercised adapted to the phase advance in **1 day** vs. ~10 days for controls.
> - Implication: A workout early in the morning after an east-bound flight might accelerate adaptation.

#### Shift Work

- Zeitgebers remain the same; workers must adjust their sleep-wake cycles to changing schedules.
- Produces: sleep disturbances, fatigue, malaise, performance deficits.
- **Better practice**: Schedule **phase delays** rather than phase advances (Driscoll, Grunstein, & Rogers, 2007).
  - Going to sleep 4 hours later and waking 4 hours later (phase delay) is much easier than going to sleep 4 hours earlier (phase advance).
  - Same principle explains why east-bound flights are more problematic than west-bound flights.

---

### A Circadian Clock in the Suprachiasmatic Nuclei

#### Discovery History

- **Richter (1967)**: First breakthrough — large **medial hypothalamic lesions** disrupt circadian cycles of eating, drinking, and activity in rats.
- Subsequent research: Specific lesions of the **suprachiasmatic nuclei (SCN)** of the medial hypothalamus disrupt various circadian cycles including sleep-wake cycles.
  - SCN lesions do NOT greatly affect **total sleep time** but DO **abolish circadian periodicity** of sleep cycles.
- SCN displays circadian cycles of electrical, metabolic, and biochemical activity entrained by the light-dark cycle (Mistlberger, 2005; Saper et al., 2005).

> **KEY EXPERIMENT: Ralph et al. (1990) — SCN Transplant Study**
> - Background: A strain of mutant hamsters with abnormally short **20-hour** free-running sleep-wake cycles.
> - Normal hamsters: 25-hour free-running period.
> - Procedure 1: Removed SCN from mutant hamster fetuses; transplanted into normal adult hamsters whose 25-hour free-running cycles had been abolished by SCN lesions.
> - Result 1: Restored free-running cycles of ~**20 hours** (the donor's rhythm, not the recipient's original).
> - Procedure 2 (reverse): SCN from normal hamster fetuses transplanted into SCN-lesioned adult mutant hamsters.
> - Result 2: Restored free-running cycles of ~**25 hours** (the donor's rhythm).
> - Conclusion: The SCN is unquestionably the major circadian clock in mammals; the rhythm belongs to the SCN itself.

#### The SCN Is Not the Only Circadian Clock

Three lines of evidence from 1980s-1990s research show other timing mechanisms exist:

1. Under certain conditions, bilateral SCN lesions leave some circadian rhythms unaffected while abolishing others.
2. Bilateral SCN lesions do not eliminate all environmental entrainment abilities: SCN lesions can block light entrainment but NOT food or water availability entrainment.
3. Cells from other body parts display free-running circadian cycles of activity in tissue culture, just like suprachiasmatic neurons.

---

### Neural Mechanisms of Entrainment

How does the light-dark cycle entrain circadian rhythms?

#### Retinohypothalamic Tract Discovery

- Starting point: the eyes (Morin & Allen, 2006).
- Cutting **optic nerves** before the optic chiasm: **Eliminated** light-dark entrainment of circadian rhythms.
- Cutting **optic tracts** at the point where they left the optic chiasm: **Did NOT eliminate** entrainment.
- Conclusion: Visual axons critical for circadian entrainment **branch off from the optic nerve in the vicinity of the optic chiasm**.
- Discovery: **Retinohypothalamic tracts** — leave the optic chiasm and project to the adjacent suprachiasmatic nuclei.

#### Photoreceptors for Entrainment

- Surprisingly: Neither **rods** nor **cones** are necessary for circadian entrainment via light.
- Mystery photoreceptors identified: A rare type of **retinal ganglion cells** with distinctive functional properties (Berson, 2003; Hattar et al., 2002).
- Evolutionary specialization: Sacrificed ability to respond quickly to rapid light changes in favor of consistent response to slowly changing **background illumination levels**.
- Photopigment: **Melanopsin** (Hankins, Peirson, & Foster, 2007; Panda et al., 2005).

---

### Genetics of Circadian Rhythms

#### Tau Gene (First Mammalian Circadian Gene Identified)

- **1988**: Routine screening of hamsters revealed some with abnormally short **20-hour** free-running circadian rhythms.
- Breeding experiments showed: Abnormality = genetic mutation.
- Gene named: **tau** (Ralph & Menaker, 1988).
- Molecular structure characterized: **2000** (Lowrey et al., 2000).

#### Clock Gene (First Characterized Molecularly)

- Discovered in **mice**.
- Molecular structure characterized: **1997**.

#### Three Key Discoveries from Circadian Gene Research

1. **Evolutionary conservation**: Same or similar circadian genes found in bacteria, flies, fish, frogs, mice, and humans; circadian genes evolved early and have been conserved across species (Cirelli, 2009).
2. **Molecular mechanism**: The fundamental molecular mechanism of circadian rhythms involves **gene expression** — transcription of proteins by circadian genes displays a circadian cycle (Dunlap, 2006; Hardin, 2006; Meyer, Saez, & Young, 2006).
3. **Ubiquitous cellular clocks**: Molecular circadian timing mechanisms similar to those in the SCN exist in **most cells of the body** (Green & Menaker, 2003; Hastings, Reddy, & Maywood, 2003; Yamaguchi et al., 2003). These cellular clocks are normally entrained by neural and hormonal signals from the SCN.

## 14.5 Four Areas of the Brain Involved in Sleep

### Two Areas of the Hypothalamus Involved in Sleep

Discovery credit: **Baron Constantin von Economo**, a Viennese neurologist, during **World War I**.

#### Encephalitis Lethargica

- **Encephalitis lethargica**: Serious viral brain infection that swept the world during WWI.
- Most victims: slept almost continuously.
- Minority of victims: had difficulty sleeping (insomnia).

#### Von Economo's Clinical Observations

| Brain Damage Location | Sleep Effect |
|----------------------|--------------|
| Posterior hypothalamus + adjacent midbrain | **Excessive sleepiness** (hypersomnia) |
| Anterior hypothalamus + adjacent basal forebrain | **Inability to sleep** (insomnia) |

- **Conclusion**: **Posterior hypothalamus promotes wakefulness**; **Anterior hypothalamus promotes sleep**.
- Confirmed by lesion and recording studies in experimental animals (Szymusiak, Gvilia, & McGinty, 2007; Szymusiak & McGinty, 2008).

### Reticular Formation and Sleep

Discovery involved comparison of two brain-stem transections in cats:

#### Cerveau Isolé Preparation (Bremer, 1936)

- **Cerveau isolé** (French: "isolated forebrain"; pronounced "ser-VOE ees-o-LAY"): Brain-stem severed between inferior and superior colliculi (midcollicular transection), disconnecting forebrain from most ascending sensory input.
- Retained: Visual and olfactory input to isolated forebrain.
- Finding: Cortical EEG showed **almost continuous slow-wave sleep**.
- Strong visual or olfactory stimuli could briefly produce desynchronized EEG, but effect barely outlasted stimuli.

#### Encéphale Isolé Preparation (Bremer, 1937)

- **Encéphale isolé** (French: "isolated brain"): Brain-stem transected at caudal level, disconnecting brain from spinal cord and rest of nervous system.
- Finding: **Normal sleep-wake cycle** of cortical EEG was preserved.
- Implication: A wakefulness-producing system lies between the two transection sites.

#### Lesion and Stimulation Studies (Lindsey, Bowden, & Magoun, 1949; Moruzzi & Magoun, 1949)

- Partial transections at the midcollicular level disrupted normal sleep-wake EEG ONLY when severing the **reticular formation core** of the brain stem.
- Lateral transections (sparing reticular formation, cutting ascending sensory tracts): Little effect on cortical EEG.
- Electrical stimulation of **pontine reticular formation** in sleeping cats: awakened them and produced lengthy EEG desynchronization.

#### Moruzzi and Magoun's Synthesis (1949)

Combined four findings:
1. Cerveau isolé effects on cortical EEG.
2. Encéphale isolé effects on cortical EEG.
3. Effects of reticular formation lesions.
4. Effects of reticular formation stimulation.

**Conclusion**: A wakefulness-producing area is located in the **reticular formation** between the two transection sites.

## Case Studies in This Chapter

### The Case of the Woman Who Wouldn't Sleep (Meddis, 1977)

- "Miss M": Approximately 23 hours of wakefulness per day; found this insufficient.
- Did not feel sleepy; active painter and writer; no drowsiness before sleep onset.
- Sleep onset: approximately 1:30-2:00 AM; fell asleep while still holding a book.
- Duration: approximately **99 minutes** total sleep per night.
- Lab study: EEG confirmed genuine sleep of short duration.
- "She does not ever report feeling sleepy."
- Source: Meddis (1977), The Sleep Instinct, Routledge & Kegan Paul.
- Implication: Challenges beliefs about the amount of sleep necessary for normal functioning.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **EEG (Electroencephalogram)** | Recording of brain electrical activity; primary measure for sleep staging |
| **EOG (Electrooculogram)** | Recording of eye movements; detects REMs |
| **EMG (Electromyogram)** | Recording of muscle electrical activity; specifically neck muscles in sleep research |

| **Stage 1 sleep EEG** | Low-voltage, high-frequency EEG; first sleep stage |
| **Stage 2 sleep EEG** | Medium-amplitude, lower-frequency EEG; features K complexes and sleep spindles |
| **K complexes** | Single large negative wave followed by a single large positive wave; characteristic of Stage 2 |
| **Sleep spindles** | 1-2 second bursts of 12-14 Hz waxing-and-waning waves; characteristic of Stage 2 |

| **Stage 4 sleep EEG** | Predominance of delta waves |
| **Delta waves** | Largest, slowest EEG waves; 1-2 Hz; define Stages 3 and 4 |
| **REM sleep** | Sleep associated with emergent Stage 1 EEG; accompanied by REMs, muscle atonia, and high cerebral activity |
| **NREM sleep** | All non-REM sleep stages combined |

| **First-night phenomenon** | Disturbed sleep observed on a participant's first laboratory night |
| **Rapid eye movements (REMs)** | Rapid movement of eyes under closed eyelids; marker of REM sleep |
| **Manifest dreams** | Dreams as actually experienced; Freud's term for the surface content |
| **Latent dreams** | True underlying dreams; Freud's term for hidden content disguised by the unconscious censor |
| **Unconscious censor** | Freudian concept; mechanism that disguises and subtracts from real dreams |
| **Activation-synthesis theory** | Hobson's (1989) theory; REM dreams are cortex's effort to make sense of random brain-stem signals |
| **Recuperation theories** | Sleep restores homeostasis disrupted by wakefulness |
| **Adaptation theories** | Sleep is controlled by an internal 24-hour clock; evolved to protect from nighttime predation |
| **Homeostasis** | Internal physiological stability of the body |
| **Somniloquy** | Sleeptalking; no special REM association; occurs in any stage |
| **Somnambulism** | Sleepwalking; occurs during Stage 3 or Stage 4; NEVER during REM |

| **Encephalitis lethargica** | Viral brain infection from WWI era; used by von Economo to map sleep-wake brain regions |

| **Encéphale isolé** | Caudal brain-stem transection; preserves normal sleep-wake cycle (Bremer, 1937) |
| **Reticular formation** | Brain-stem structure; wakefulness-producing area; stimulation produces EEG desynchronization |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Three standard psychophysiological measures of sleep** are EEG, EOG, and EMG; all three together define sleep stages. Berger and Oswald (1962) discovered the EMG loss component.

2. **Stage 2 sleep is specifically identified by K complexes and sleep spindles** (12-14 Hz); Stage 4 is defined by predominance of delta waves (1-2 Hz); Stage 3 by occasional delta waves.

3. **REM sleep** = emergent Stage 1 EEG + rapid eye movements + loss of core-muscle tone + increased cerebral activity. Initial Stage 1 has none of these additional features.

4. **80% of REM awakenings yield dream recall** vs. only 7% of NREM awakenings. NREM dreams are isolated; REM dreams are narrative.

5. **Sleepwalking (somnambulism) occurs during Stage 3/4 (SWS), never during REM**. Sleeptalking (somniloquy) has no special REM association and can occur in any stage.

6. **Freud's dream theory**: manifest (experienced) vs. latent (real) dreams; unconscious censor. Modern alternative: **Hobson's activation-synthesis theory (1989)** — dreams result from cortex interpreting random brain-stem signals.

7. **Recuperation theories** predict sleep need is proportional to prior wakefulness; **adaptation theories** predict sleep timing is governed by internal 24-hour programming regardless of prior wakefulness activity levels.

8. **Randy Gardner (1965)**: Stayed awake 264 hours 12 minutes (11 days); recovered with only 14 hours sleep; returned to normal 8-hour schedule. Demonstrates that sleep debt is not fully recovered after prolonged deprivation.

9. **Sleep deprivation selectively impairs executive function** (prefrontal cortex-dependent: innovative, lateral, insightful thinking) while sparing logical deduction and critical thinking.

10. **Sleep efficiency increases with deprivation**: People regain mostly Stage 4 sleep (not total sleep time); Stage 4 is considered the primary restorative stage. This makes brief deprivation studies poor indicators of true sleep need.

11. **SCN (suprachiasmatic nuclei)** = primary circadian clock. SCN lesions abolish circadian periodicity of sleep but do not greatly reduce total sleep time. Ralph et al. (1990) SCN transplant experiment definitively proved SCN contains the clock.

12. **Free-running period in humans** is approximately 24.2 hours under constant moderate illumination; slightly longer than 24 hours = biological clock naturally runs slow.

13. **Retinohypothalamic tracts** carry light-dark information from the optic chiasm to the SCN. The photoreceptors are specialized **retinal ganglion cells** containing **melanopsin** (NOT rods or cones).

14. **Tau gene** (Ralph & Menaker, 1988) = first mammalian circadian gene identified; **Clock gene** = first to have molecular structure characterized (mice, 1997).

15. **Phase delays are easier to adapt to than phase advances**: explains why east-bound flights (phase advance) cause more jet lag than west-bound flights (phase delay), and why shift workers are more productively transferred to later (delayed) schedules.
