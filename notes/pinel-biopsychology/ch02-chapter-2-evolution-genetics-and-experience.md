# Chapter 2: Evolution, Genetics, and Experience

**Overview:** This chapter challenges traditional dichotomous thinking (physiological vs. psychological; nature vs. nurture) in favor of an interactive model of behavioral biology. It covers human evolutionary history, the principles of Mendelian and molecular genetics, and how genetic factors and experience interact during behavioral development. Central argument: all behavior arises from the interaction of genetic endowment, experience, and perception of the current situation.

---

## 2.1 Thinking about the Biology of Behavior: From Dichotomies to Interactions

### Problems with Thinking about the Biology of Behavior in Terms of Traditional Dichotomies

Two long-standing debates have shaped how people think about behavior:

1. **Physiological-or-psychological dichotomy**: The assumption that some aspects of human psychological functioning are too complex to be produced by a physical brain (rooted in Cartesian dualism).
2. **Nature-or-nurture dichotomy**: The assumption that behavior is controlled by EITHER genetic/inherited factors OR environmental/experiential factors.

Both are now understood to be **fundamentally flawed**.

---

### The Physiological-or-Psychological Dichotomy

- **Cartesian dualism**: Descartes' view that mind and brain are separate — the mind (soul) is non-physical; the brain is physical. Officially sanctioned by the Roman Church in the 17th century.
- In **1747**, **Julien Offroy de la Mettrie** anonymously published a pamphlet arguing that thought is produced by the brain. He was forced to live in exile in Berlin. Considered a dangerous, scandalous claim at the time.

**Two lines of evidence against physiological-or-psychological thinking:**

1. Complex psychological changes (self-awareness, memory, emotion) can be produced by brain damage or brain stimulation — psychological phenomena have clear physiological substrates.
2. Non-human species (especially primates) possess abilities once assumed to be purely psychological and uniquely human (e.g., self-awareness).

---

### The Case of the Man Who Fell Out of Bed

> **Source:** Oliver Sacks (1985), *The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat*

**Clinical case illustrating the physiological basis of self-awareness:**

- Patient suffered from **asomatognosia**: a deficiency in the awareness of parts of one's own body.
- Typically involves the **left side of the body**.
- Usually results from **damage to the right parietal lobe**.
- Patient did not recognize his own left leg; thought a severed leg had been placed in his bed as a joke; threw it out of bed and landed on the floor still attached to it.
- When asked where his left leg was, he said he had no idea — it had "disappeared."
- **Key point:** Even very complex changes in self-awareness are clearly produced by brain damage. The full range of human experience can be produced by brain manipulations.

> **MCQ Target:** Asomatognosia = right parietal lobe damage = loss of awareness of LEFT side of body.

---

### The Case of the Chimps and the Mirrors

> **Researcher:** G. G. Gallup (1983); Parker, Mitchell, & Boccia (1994)

**Study on self-awareness in chimpanzees (Mirror/Mark Test):**

- **Operationalization:** An organism is self-aware to the extent it can become the object of its own attention.
- **Method:**
  - Preadolescent, group-reared chimpanzees exposed to mirrors.
  - Initial reaction: responded as if seeing another chimpanzee.
  - After ~**2 days**: began using mirror to groom/inspect previously unseen body parts; made faces, looked upside down, assumed unusual postures.
  - **Mark Test:** While anesthetized, a bright **red, odorless, alcohol-soluble dye** was painted on the uppermost portion of an eyebrow ridge and the top half of the opposite ear.
  - After recovery, mirror reintroduced.
- **Findings:**
  - All chimpanzees showed **mark-directed responses**: touched and inspected marked areas on their own face while watching the mirror.
  - Over **3-fold increase** in mirror viewing time.
  - Some chimps visually examined and smelled fingers used to touch the marks.
- **Key point:** Non-human primates demonstrate self-awareness — challenges the view that self-awareness is uniquely human and therefore requires a non-physical "mind."

> **MCQ Target:** Gallup's mark test = chimpanzee self-awareness = challenges physiological-or-psychological dichotomy.

---

### Nature-or-Nurture Thinking Runs into Difficulty

**Historical evolution of the debate:**

| Stage | Question | Problem |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Early | Is it genetic OR learned? | Ignored fetal environment, nutrition, stress, sensory stimulation |
| Broadened | Is it genetic OR experiential? | Still a false dichotomy |
| Quantitative | How much is genetic, how much experiential? | Assumes additive combination, not interaction |
| **Modern (correct)** | How do genetic and experiential factors INTERACT? | Interactive — the only valid framework |

- "Nurture" was originally equated with learning; later broadened to include all experiential factors.
- The "how much" question assumes genetic and experiential factors combine **additively** — this is incorrect.
- The correct framework: genetic factors and experience **interact** (like a musician and instrument producing music — you cannot ask how much of the music comes from the musician and how much from the instrument).

**Three general points about neural development (Pinel's interactive model):**
1. Neurons become active **long before** they are fully developed.
2. Subsequent development (number of connections formed, survival) depends greatly on neural **activity**, much of which is triggered by external experience.
3. **Experience continuously modifies genetic expression.**

---

### The Case of the Thinking Student

**Metaphor/Anecdote:**
- Student asks if intelligence is "one-third genetic and two-thirds experience."
- Pinel's response: Asking how much intelligence comes from genes vs. experience is as absurd as asking how much music comes from the musician vs. the instrument — **the music results from the interaction of both**.
- Same logic applies to: water + temperature for evaporation; males + females for copulation.
- **Conclusion:** Intelligence (and any behavioral trait) is a product of the **interaction** of genes and experience — not a proportional mixture.

---

### A Model of the Biology of Behavior

**The Interactive Model** (adopted by many biopsychologists):

All behavior is the product of interactions among:
1. The organism's **genetic endowment** (product of its evolution)
2. Its **experience**
3. Its **perception of the current situation**

**Seven steps in the model (Figure 2.3 — key for MCQs):**

1. Evolution influences the pool of behavior-influencing genes available to species members.
2. Experience modifies the expression of an individual's genetic program.
3. Each individual's genes initiate a unique program of neural development.
4. Development of each individual's nervous system depends on interactions with its environment (experience).
5. Each individual's current behavioral capacities and tendencies are determined by its unique patterns of neural activity (some experienced as thoughts, feelings, memories).
6. Each individual's current behavior arises from interactions among ongoing neural activity and perception of the current situation.
7. The success of each individual's behavior influences the likelihood that its genes will be passed on to future generations.

## 2.2 Human Evolution

### Darwin and the Theory of Evolution

- **Charles Darwin** published ***On the Origin of Species*** in **1859** — beginning of modern biology; the single most influential theory in the biological sciences.
- Darwin was NOT the first to suggest species evolve, but was the first to:
  - Amass a large body of supporting evidence.
  - Propose a mechanism: **natural selection**.

**Four kinds of evidence supporting evolution:**

| Evidence Type | Example |
|--------------|---------|
| 1. Fossil record | Fossils change systematically through geological layers (e.g., hominid skull evolution) |
| 2. Structural similarities among living species | Human hand, bird wing, cat paw share underlying skeletal structure |
| 3. Selective breeding | Major changes in domestic plants/animals through breeding programs |
| 4. Direct observation of evolution in progress | Grant (1991): Galápagos finch beak size increased after one season of drought |

---

### Natural Selection

- **Natural selection**: Heritable traits associated with high rates of survival and reproduction are most likely passed to future generations; repeated over generations → species better adapted to their niche.
- Darwin named it "natural selection" to emphasize similarity to artificial selective breeding.
- **Fitness (Darwinian)**: The ability of an organism to **survive and contribute its genes** to the next generation (NOT physical strength).

> A **scientific theory** is not vague speculation; it is an explanation providing the best current account based on available evidence. Evolution is as well-supported as the theories of gravity and electricity.

---

### Evolution and Behavior

#### Social Dominance

- Males of many species establish a **stable hierarchy of social dominance** through combative encounters.
- Once established, low-ranking males learn to avoid or submit quickly → hostilities diminish.
- **Why social dominance matters for evolution:**

  - **Dominant males copulate more** → more effective at passing on characteristics.
  - **McCann (1981):** Studied 10 bull elephant seals on same breeding beach.
    - Dominant male: **~37% of copulations**.
    - Rank 10 male: **~1% of copulations**.
    - Challenge behavior: raise to full height, push chest to chest; smaller male usually backs down; if not, vicious neck-biting battle ensues.

  - **Dominant females produce more/healthier offspring.**
  - **Pusey, Williams, and Goodall (1997):** High-ranking female chimpanzees produced more offspring; those offspring more likely to survive to sexual maturity — attributed to better access to food foraging areas.

#### Courtship Display

- Intricate **courtship display** series precedes copulation in many species.
- Signals may be olfactory, visual, auditory, or tactual.
- Copulation unlikely if either partner fails to react appropriately.
- **Courtship displays promote speciation:**
  - **Species**: Group of organisms reproductively isolated from others; produce fertile offspring only by mating within the same species.
  - New species branches off when a reproductive barrier forms between a subpopulation and the rest.
  - Barrier may be **geographic** (e.g., birds on isolated island) or **behavioral** (different courtship displays).
  - **Conspecifics**: Members of the same species.

---

### Course of Human Evolution

#### Vertebrate Evolution Timeline

| Milestone | Time |
|-----------|------|
| First complex multicellular organisms | ~600 mya |
| First chordates | ~450 mya |
| First vertebrates (primitive bony fishes) | ~425 mya |
| First amphibians | ~400 mya |
| First reptiles | ~300 mya |
| First mammals | ~180 mya |
| First hominins | ~6 mya |

- **Chordates**: Animals with **dorsal nerve cords**; one of ~20 **phyla**.
- **Vertebrates**: Chordates with **vertebrae** (spinal bones) protecting the dorsal nerve cord.
- Seven classes of vertebrates: 3 classes of fishes + amphibians + reptiles + birds + mammals.
- **Transitional fossil (Daeschler, Shubin, & Jenkins, 2006):** ~375 mya; found in northern Canada; had scales, teeth, and gills (fish) AND primitive wrist and finger bones (land vertebrate) — the predicted link between fish and land vertebrates.

#### Amphibians (~400 mya)
- Bony fishes ventured onto land (~410 mya) — could escape stagnant pools and access terrestrial food.
- Natural selection transformed fins → legs and gills → lungs.
- Larval form must live in water; only adults survive on land.

#### Reptiles (~300 mya)
- Evolved from a branch of amphibians.
- First vertebrates to lay **shell-covered eggs** (freed from ponds for first life stage).
- Covered by **dry scales** (greatly reduced water loss through skin).

#### Mammals (~180 mya)
- Evolved from one line of small reptiles during the height of the age of dinosaurs.
- Females feed young with secretions from **mammary glands** (hence "mammals").
- Eventually stopped laying eggs; females nurtured young internally until birth.
- **Exception: duck-billed platypus** — surviving mammalian egg-laying species.
- Internal gestation: long-term security and environmental stability for complex developmental programs.
- ~20 orders of mammals; humans belong to order **Primates** (Latin *primus* = "first" or "foremost").

#### Primates

- **~12 families** of primates; no single characteristic possessed by all primates but no others.
- Five notable families: Prosimians, New-World Monkeys, Old-World Monkeys, Apes, Hominins.

**Apes (gibbons, orangutans, gorillas, chimpanzees):**
- Evolved from Old-World monkeys.
- **Like** Old-World monkeys: long arms, grasping hind feet (arboreal travel), opposable thumbs (not long enough for precise manipulation).
- **Unlike** Old-World monkeys: no tails; can walk upright for short distances.
- **Chimpanzees**: Closest living relatives of humans; **~99% of genes identical** (Chimpanzee Sequencing and Analysis Consortium, 2005; Pollard, 2009).
- The actual ape ancestor of humans is likely long extinct (Jaeger & Marivaux, 2005).

#### Emergence of Humankind

- **Hominins**: Primates of the family that includes humans.
- **Two genera:** *Australopithecus* and *Homo*.
- **Homo species:**
  - *Homo erectus* (extinct)
  - *Homo sapiens* (modern humans — not extinct)

**Australopithecines:**
- *Australo* = southern; *pithecus* = ape.
- Evolved ~**6 million years ago** in Africa.
- Several species; roamed African plains for ~**5 million years** before extinction.
- Height: ~1.3 meters (4 feet); small brains; **fully upright posture** (confirmed by pelvis, leg bones, fossilized footprints).
- Fossilized footprints (~3.6 mya in African volcanic ash): two adults and a child; 70-meter trail; child often walked in adults' footsteps.
- Notable fossil: complete skull of a **3-year-old Australopithecus girl**, 3.3 million years old, found in Ethiopia.
- Fossil evidence of **3-foot-tall hominins** inhabiting Indonesian island Flores as recently as **13,000 years ago** (Diamond, 2004; Wong, 2005a).

**Early Homo:**
- Evolved from one Australopithecus species ~**2 million years ago**.
- Larger brain cavity than Australopithecus (but smaller than modern humans).
- Used **fire and tools** (Ambrose, 2001).
- Coexisted with Australopithecus species in Africa for ~500,000 years until australopithecines died out.

**Modern Homo sapiens:**
- Replaced early Homo species in African fossil record ~**200,000 years ago** (Pääbo, 1995).
- Began migrating out of Africa ~**50,000 years ago**.

**Timeline of human cultural accomplishments:**
- Artistic products (wall paintings, carvings): ~**40,000 years ago**
- Ranching and farming: ~**10,000 years ago**
- Writing: ~**3,500 years ago**

> **Paradox:** The "big three" attributes (large brain, upright posture, free hands with opposable thumb) existed for hundreds of thousands of years before most human accomplishments appeared.

### Thinking about Human Evolution (Corrected Misconceptions)

1. **Evolution is a dense bush, not a ladder.** It does not proceed in a single line.
2. **Humans have little reason to claim evolutionary supremacy.** Hominins have existed for only a "blip" of evolutionary time.
3. **Evolution can be rapid.** Rapid changes triggered by sudden environmental shifts or adaptive mutations. Whether human evolution was gradual or sudden is debated (paleontologists). Cooling of the earth coinciding with hominin emergence may have accelerated human evolution (Behrensmeyer, 2006).
4. **Fewer than 1% of all known species still exist.** Only tips of the evolutionary bush survive.
5. **Evolution is a tinkerer, not an architect.** Improvements occur through modifications to existing programs — never perfect designs (e.g., mammalian sperm ineffective at body temperature → evolution of the scrotum).
6. **Not all structures/behaviors are adaptive.** Non-adaptive by-products of evolutionary changes = **spandrels** (e.g., human belly button — by-product of umbilical cord; serves no adaptive function).
7. **Not all adaptive characteristics evolved for their current function.** **Exaptations** = evolved for one function, later co-opted for another (e.g., **bird wings** — evolved for walking, later for flight).
8. **Similarity does not imply common origin:**
   - **Homologous structures**: Similar because of **common evolutionary origin** (e.g., human arm and bat wing — same underlying skeletal structure).
   - **Analogous structures**: Similar because of **convergent evolution** — similar solutions to same environmental demands, but NO common origin (e.g., bird wing and bee wing — same function, few structural similarities).
   - **Convergent evolution**: Evolution in unrelated species of similar solutions to the same environmental demands.

### Evolution of the Human Brain

**Early approach — brain size alone — two problems:**
1. Humans do NOT have the biggest brains: Humans ~1,350g vs. whales/elephants 5,000–8,000g.
2. Brain size of acclaimed intellectuals (e.g., Einstein) was unremarkable.
- Healthy adult human brains: ~1,000–2,000g; no clear relationship between overall size and intelligence.

**Body-weight adjusted brain size (% of total body weight):**
- Humans: **2.33%**, Elephants: **0.20%**, **Shrews: 3.33%** (shrews "outrank" humans)
- This measure also has problems.

**Modern approach — compare brain regions:**
- **Brain stem**: Regulates reflex activities critical for survival (heart rate, respiration, blood glucose).
- **Cerebrum (cerebral hemispheres)**: More complex adaptive processes — learning, perception, motivation.

**Three key points about human brain evolution:**
1. Brain **increased in size** during evolution.
2. Most increase occurred in the **cerebrum**.
3. Increase in **convolutions** (folds on cerebral surface) greatly increased the volume of the **cerebral cortex** (outermost layer of cerebral tissue).

**Cross-species similarity:** All brains composed of neurons; same major structures connected in same ways across related species; similar structures perform similar functions. Human abilities = modification of abilities found in closest evolutionary relatives.

### Evolutionary Psychology: Understanding Mate Bonding

- **Evolutionary psychology**: Field that tries to understand human behaviors through consideration of the evolutionary pressures that led to their development (Schmitt & Pilcher, 2004).

**Mating systems:**

| System | Definition | Prevalence |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Promiscuity** | Members of both sexes copulate indiscriminately with many partners | Most common in all species |
| **Polygyny** | One male bonds with more than one female | Most prevalent in mammals |
| **Polyandry** | One female bonds with more than one male | Rare; only where male investment > female investment |
| **Monogamy** | Enduring bond between one male and one female | ~4% of mammalian species |

**Trivers (1972) theory of mammalian mate bonding:**
- Female mammals make far greater investment in offspring (carry young in body for months; suckle and care after birth).
- Males often contribute little more than sperm.
- Consequence: females can produce only a **few offspring** in a lifetime; males can sire **many offspring**.
- Females must be **selective** — mate with fit males to maximize fitness of their few offspring.
- Males have little evolutionary pressure to be selective — bond with **as many females as possible**.
- Inevitable result: **polygyny**.

**Why polyandry is rare:**
- Occurs only when male investment > female investment.
- Example: **sea horse** — female deposits eggs in male's pouch; he fertilizes and carries them to maturity.
- Does NOT occur in mammals.

**Why monogamy evolved (~4% of mammals):**
- Evolved when females could raise more/fitter young with **undivided male help**.
- Female behavior encouraging exclusive bonding (e.g., driving away rivals; withholding copulation until male remains for a period) changed optimal male strategy.
- Males: best reproductive outcome = bond with one fit female and invest in her offspring.

**Are humans monogamous?**
- Many human cultures do not favor monogamy.
- Many Westerners bond with multiple partners across a lifetime.
- Infidelity common, particularly among men.
- Humans cannot match truly monogamous species (e.g., geese).

---

### Thinking about Evolutionary Psychology

**Buss (1992) — Predictions from evolutionary mate-bonding theory confirmed in humans:**

1. Men in most cultures value **youth and attractiveness** (fertility indicators) more than women do.
2. Women value **power and earning capacity** more than men do.
3. Physical attractiveness best predicts which women will bond with men of **high occupational status**.
4. Women's major mate-attraction strategy: **increasing physical attractiveness**; men's: **displaying power and resources**.
5. Men are **more likely** than women to commit adultery.

> **Caveat:** Behavioral tendencies shaped by evolution exist without conscious awareness of their evolutionary origins. All inherited tendencies are modulated by experience.

> **Limitation:** Evolutionary psychology theories must include testable predictions about current behaviors; untestable theories have little scientific value.

## 2.3 Fundamental Genetics

### Darwin's Gap and Mendel

- Darwin did not know WHY conspecifics differ or HOW traits are transmitted to offspring.
- **Gregor Mendel** (1822–1884), an Augustinian monk, had sent Darwin a manuscript with the answers — it remained **unread** in Darwin's files.
- Significance of Mendel's research not recognized until early **20th century** — after both Darwin (d. 1882) and Mendel (d. 1884) had died.

---

### Mendelian Genetics

**Mendel's experimental design:**
- Studied **dichotomous traits**: traits that occur in one form or the other, never in combination (e.g., seed color = brown OR white).
- Used **true-breeding lines**: interbred members always produce offspring with the same trait, generation after generation.

**Seed color experiment:**
- **Cross 1:** True-breeding brown × true-breeding white → **all first-generation offspring = brown**.
- **Cross 2:** First-generation offspring × each other → **~3/4 brown : ~1/4 white** (second generation).
- Same result across all pairs of dichotomous traits.

**Terminology:**
- **Dominant trait**: Appears in all first-generation offspring (e.g., brown).
- **Recessive trait**: Disappears in first generation; reappears in ~1/4 of second generation (e.g., white).
- **Phenotype**: Observable traits of an organism.
- **Genotype**: Traits an organism can pass on through genetic material (including unexpressed traits).

**Mendel's four-part theory:**

| Component | Proposal | Modern Term |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Two kinds of inherited factors per dichotomous trait | Genes |
| 2 | Each organism has two genes per trait | Alleles |
| 3 | One gene dominates in heterozygous organisms | Dominance |
| 4 | Offspring randomly inherit one factor from each parent | Law of Segregation |

- **Gene**: Each inherited factor; basic unit of heredity.
- **Alleles**: The two genes controlling the same trait (one from each parent); located at the same chromosomal position.
- **Homozygous**: Two identical alleles for a trait (e.g., BB or bb).
- **Heterozygous**: Two different alleles for a trait (e.g., Bb); dominant allele is expressed.

---

### Chromosomes: Reproduction and Recombination

- Genes are located on **chromosomes** — threadlike structures in the cell nucleus.
- Chromosomes come in **matched pairs**.
- **Humans: 23 pairs (46 total).**
- Alleles for each trait sit at the same position on each chromosome of a pair.

**Meiosis:**
- **Meiosis**: Cell division producing **gametes** (egg cells and sperm cells).
- Each gamete receives ONE chromosome from each pair → **23 chromosomes** in humans.
- **Fertilization:** Sperm + egg → **zygote** (fertilized egg) with full 46 chromosomes.
- **Genetic recombination:** During meiosis, paired chromosomes **cross over** at random points, break, and **exchange sections** → each gamete contains unique recombinations of parental chromosomes.

**Mitosis:**
- **Mitosis**: Cell division in all non-reproductive body cells.
- Chromosomes double before division → both daughter cells receive **full complement** (46 in humans).

---

### Chromosomes: Structure and Replication

- Each chromosome = **double-stranded DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) molecule**.
- Each strand = sequence of **nucleotide bases** on a phosphate-deoxyribose backbone.
- **Four nucleotide bases:** adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), cytosine (C).
- The sequence of bases = the **genetic code**.

**Complementary base pairing:**
- **A–T** (adenine bonds with thymine)
- **G–C** (guanine bonds with cytosine)
- The two strands are exact complements; they coil around each other (**double helix**).

**DNA Replication:**
1. Two strands of DNA unwind.
2. Exposed bases on each strand attract complementary free-floating bases from nuclear fluid.
3. Result: **two identical double-stranded DNA molecules** (both identical to the original).

**Errors in replication:**
- **Down syndrome**: Extra chromosome in each cell (gross error).
- **Mutations**: Accidental alterations in individual genes (more common).
  - Most mutations: organisms less fit → disappear from gene pool within a few generations.
  - Rare fitness-increasing mutations: contribute to rapid evolution.

---

### Sex Chromosomes and Sex-Linked Traits

- **Autosomal chromosomes**: Typical chromosomes in matched pairs.
- **Sex chromosomes**: The pair determining sex; look different, carry different genes.
  - **Females (mammals):** XX
  - **Males (mammals):** XY
- **Y chromosome**: Small; carries few genes.
- **Sex-linked traits**: Traits influenced by genes on sex chromosomes; virtually all controlled by genes on the **X chromosome**.

**Rules:**
- **Dominant sex-linked traits**: More frequent in **females** (two X chromosomes = twice the chance of inheriting the dominant gene).
- **Recessive sex-linked traits**: More frequent in **males**:
  - Females must inherit TWO copies (one on each X) to manifest — very rare.
  - Males manifest with just ONE copy (only one X chromosome).
  - Classic example: **color blindness** — gene is rare so females almost never have two copies; every male with one copy is color blind.

---

### The Genetic Code and Gene Expression

- **Structural genes**: Genes containing information for **protein synthesis**.
- **Proteins**: Long chains of amino acids; control physiological activities and are structural components of cells.
- All cells in the body contain **exactly the same genes** — different cell types arise from differential **gene expression**.

**Enhancers (Promoters):**
- Stretches of DNA **without structural genes** (compose most of each chromosome; structural genes are a small portion).
- **Enhancers**: Determine whether structural genes initiate protein synthesis and at what rate.
- Act like switches — can be turned **up** or **down**.
- **Transcription factors**: Proteins that bind to DNA and influence gene expression; many are influenced by **signals from the cell's environment**.
- This is a major mechanism by which **experience interacts with genes to influence development** — central to the nature-nurture interaction.

**Steps in gene expression:**

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| 1 | Chromosome section with the gene unravels |
| 2 | One exposed DNA strand serves as template; **messenger RNA (mRNA)** is transcribed |
| 3 | mRNA carries genetic code out of nucleus into cytoplasm |
| 4 | mRNA attaches to a **ribosome** in the cytoplasm |
| 5 | Ribosome moves along mRNA, reading **codons** (3 consecutive bases) |
| 6 | Each codon = instruction to add 1 of 20 amino acids (carried by **transfer RNA, tRNA**) |
| 7 | Ribosome adds amino acids until a "stop" codon; completed **protein** released into cytoplasm |

**RNA vs. DNA:**
- RNA contains **uracil** instead of thymine.
- RNA has **ribose** backbone (DNA has deoxyribose).

**Two phases of gene expression:**
1. **Transcription**: DNA base sequence → RNA base sequence.
2. **Translation**: RNA base sequence → amino acid sequence (protein).

**Example codon:** Guanine-guanine-adenine → amino acid glycine.

---

### Mitochondrial DNA

- **Mitochondria**: Energy-generating organelles in the cytoplasm of every cell, including neurons.
- Mitochondria contain their own DNA: **mitochondrial DNA**.
- Inherited **solely from one's mother** (maternal inheritance).
- **Evolutionary clock**: Mutations in mitochondrial DNA accumulate at a consistent rate → used to date evolutionary divergences (Kaessmann & Pääbo, 2002).
- Analysis of mitochondrial DNA mutations: Supports that **hominins evolved in Africa** and spread over the earth (Goebel, Waters, & O'Rourke, 2008; Stix, 2008; Wallace, 1997).

---

### Modern Genetics

- **Human Genome Project:**
  - Began: **1990**
  - Completed: **2001**
  - Collaboration of major research institutions across several countries.
  - Goal: Map the sequence of all **~3 billion bases** composing human chromosomes.
  - Most surprising finding: Humans have a **relatively small number of genes** (~20,000–25,000 — far fewer than expected).
- Technical advances led to genome sequencing of many other species.

## 2.4 Behavioral Development: Interaction of Genetic Factors and Experience

### Selective Breeding of Maze-Bright and Maze-Dull Rats

> **KEY STUDY — Classic Demonstration of Gene-Environment Interaction**

- Rats selectively bred for maze-learning performance:
  - **Maze-bright**: Selectively bred for fast maze learning.
  - **Maze-dull**: Selectively bred for slow maze learning.
- **Critical finding:** Maze-bright rats outperformed maze-dull rats only in **restricted (impoverished) environments**.
- In **enriched environments**: maze-dull and maze-bright rats performed **equally well**.
- **Implication:** Genetic differences in a behavioral trait can be completely overridden by environmental enrichment — genes and environment interact, they do not simply add.

### Neuroplasticity

- **Neuroplasticity**: The ability of the nervous system to change its structure and function in response to experience.
- Neural development is not a fixed genetic program — it depends on experiential input throughout life.

---

### Phenylketonuria (PKU): A Single-Gene Metabolic Disorder

> **KEY STUDY — Gene-Environment Interaction with Clinical Implications**

- **PKU (Phenylketonuria)**: Genetic disorder caused by a **single gene mutation**.
- Defective gene: fails to produce the enzyme **phenylalanine hydroxylase** (needed to convert phenylalanine → tyrosine).
- Result: **Phenylalanine** accumulates in body and brain → severe **intellectual disability**.
- **Treatment:** Phenylalanine-free diet from birth **completely prevents** intellectual disability.
- **Key lesson:** A highly heritable, single-gene disorder can be entirely prevented by an environmental manipulation. Genotype does NOT equal phenotype. Genetic does NOT mean immutable.

> **MCQ Target:** PKU = single gene mutation + dietary treatment = prevented intellectual disability = best example of gene-environment interaction.

---

### Development of Birdsong

> **KEY STUDY — Interaction of Genetic Predisposition and Experience**

- Birds have a **genetic predisposition** to learn their own species' song.
- BUT: birds must hear their species' song during a **sensitive (critical) period** of development to sing it normally.
- Birds raised in isolation (no song exposure) develop **abnormal songs**.
- **Key lesson:** Both genetic factors AND environmental input (auditory experience) are required for normal development of birdsong. Neither alone is sufficient.

## 2.5 Genetics of Human Psychological Differences

### Development of Individuals versus Development of Differences among Individuals

**Critical distinction:**

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Development of an individual** | How a single organism grows and changes over a lifetime; always involves gene-experience interaction |
| **Development of differences among individuals** | Why individuals differ from one another; can have different determinants than individual development |

- A factor can be important for development in ALL individuals yet account for NO differences AMONG individuals (and vice versa).
- Example: All humans develop two eyes (both genes and normal experience required) — but this tells us nothing about why individuals differ from each other in eye color.

**Heritability:**
- **Heritability**: A statistical measure of the degree to which differences in a trait among individuals in a specific population can be attributed to genetic differences, given the range of environments in that population.
- Heritability is a **population statistic** — NOT a statement about any individual.
- Heritability can change if the environment changes.
- **Critical warning:** High heritability does NOT mean a trait is unresponsive to environmental intervention. PKU is highly heritable yet completely preventable by diet.

**Twin studies:**
- Standard method for estimating heritability of psychological traits.
- **Monozygotic (MZ) twins**: Identical twins; share **100% of genes**.
- **Dizygotic (DZ) twins**: Fraternal twins; share **~50% of genes** (like regular siblings, but same age).
- If MZ twins are more similar than DZ twins for a trait → suggests a genetic component.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Asomatognosia** = loss of awareness of the **left side** of the body, resulting from damage to the **right parietal lobe** (Oliver Sacks, 1985 case).

2. **Gallup's mirror/mark test (1983)**: Chimpanzees anesthetized, red dye painted on eyebrow/ear, then shown mirror — all showed mark-directed responses. Proves **self-awareness in non-human primates**; challenges the physiological-or-psychological dichotomy.

3. **Cartesian dualism** was officially sanctioned by the **Roman Church** in the **17th century**. **de la Mettrie** challenged it in **1747**, arguing thought is produced by the brain — he was forced into exile.

4. The nature-nurture debate's core flaw: asking "how much" is genetic vs. experiential incorrectly assumes **additive** (not interactive) contributions. Correct framework: **interaction** (musician + instrument analogy).

5. **Darwin's four types of evidence** for evolution: (1) fossil record progression; (2) structural similarities among species; (3) selective breeding effects; (4) direct observation (Grant, 1991 — Galápagos finch beak size after one drought season).

6. **Darwinian fitness** = ability to **survive and contribute genes** to the next generation (NOT physical fitness).

7. **Spandrels** = non-adaptive evolutionary by-products (e.g., belly button); **Exaptations** = evolved for one function, co-opted for another (e.g., bird wings — walking → flight).

8. **Homologous** = common evolutionary origin; **Analogous** = convergent evolution, no common origin.

9. **Chimpanzees share ~99% of genes** with humans. Hominins evolved ~**6 mya** in Africa. *Homo sapiens* migrated out of Africa ~**50,000 years ago**. Writing began ~**3,500 years ago**. Fewer than **1% of all known species** still exist.

10. **Mendel's ratios:** F1 cross = 100% dominant; F2 cross = **3:1 dominant:recessive**.

11. **Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes** (46 total). Meiosis → gametes with **23 chromosomes**. Mitosis → daughter cells with **46 chromosomes**. Fertilization: sperm (23) + egg (23) = zygote (46).

12. **Complementary base pairing:** A-T and G-C in DNA; RNA substitutes **uracil** for thymine. Sequence of bases = genetic code. Each group of 3 bases = **codon** = one amino acid instruction.

13. **Mitochondrial DNA** is inherited **only from the mother**; used as an evolutionary clock; supports **African origin of hominins**.

14. **Human Genome Project**: Began **1990**, completed **2001**; mapped ~**3 billion** base pairs; surprising finding: humans have a **relatively small number of genes**.

15. **PKU**: Single gene mutation → no phenylalanine hydroxylase → phenylalanine accumulates → intellectual disability. **Phenylalanine-free diet from birth = complete prevention**. Best example that "genetic" does NOT mean "immutable."

16. **Trivers (1972)**: Polygyny evolved because female mammals invest far more in offspring than males → females are selective, males are not. Polyandry (one female, multiple males) occurs only when male investment > female investment (e.g., sea horse).

17. **Buss (1992)** confirmed: men value **youth/attractiveness**, women value **power/resources**; men more likely to commit adultery — predictions from evolutionary theory.

18. **Heritability** is a **population statistic**, not a property of any individual or of the trait in isolation. It can change if environments change. High heritability does NOT mean unresponsive to environment.

19. **Maze-bright vs. maze-dull rats**: Genetic difference in maze performance was **abolished by environmental enrichment** — genetic differences in behavior can be overridden by the environment.

20. **Two phases of gene expression**: (1) **Transcription** (DNA → mRNA in nucleus); (2) **Translation** (mRNA → protein at ribosome in cytoplasm). **Transcription factors** (influenced by environmental signals) regulate which genes are expressed — this is the molecular mechanism of gene-environment interaction.
