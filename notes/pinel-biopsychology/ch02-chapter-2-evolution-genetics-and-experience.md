# Chapter 2: Evolution, Genetics, and Experience

**Overview:** Challenges dichotomous thinking (physiological vs. psychological; nature vs. nurture) in favor of an interactive model of behavioral biology. Covers human evolution, Mendelian and molecular genetics, and how genes and experience interact in development. Central argument: all behavior arises from the interaction of genetic endowment, experience, and perception of the current situation.

---

## 2.1 Thinking about the Biology of Behavior: From Dichotomies to Interactions

### Problems with Traditional Dichotomies

Two flawed debates have shaped thinking about behavior:

1. **Physiological-or-psychological dichotomy**: the assumption that some psychological functions are too complex to be produced by a physical brain (rooted in Cartesian dualism).
2. **Nature-or-nurture dichotomy**: the assumption that behavior is controlled by EITHER genetic OR experiential factors.

---

### The Physiological-or-Psychological Dichotomy

- **Cartesian dualism**: Descartes' view that the non-physical mind (soul) is separate from the physical brain. Sanctioned by the Roman Church in the 17th century.
- **1747**: **Julien Offroy de la Mettrie** anonymously argued thought is produced by the brain; forced into exile in Berlin.

**Two lines of evidence against the dichotomy:**
1. Complex psychological changes (self-awareness, memory, emotion) can be produced by brain damage or stimulation.
2. Non-human species (esp. primates) show abilities once assumed uniquely human (e.g., self-awareness).

---

### The Case of the Man Who Fell Out of Bed

> Source: Oliver Sacks (1985), *The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat*

- Patient had **asomatognosia**: deficient awareness of parts of one's own body; typically the **left side**; usually from **right parietal lobe damage**.
- He did not recognize his own left leg, thought it was a severed leg placed as a joke, and threw himself out of bed.
- **Key point:** even complex changes in self-awareness are produced by brain damage.

> **MCQ Target:** Asomatognosia = right parietal lobe damage = loss of LEFT-side body awareness.

---

### The Case of the Chimps and the Mirrors

> Gallup (1983); Parker, Mitchell, & Boccia (1994)

- **Operationalization:** an organism is self-aware to the extent it can become the object of its own attention.
- **Method:** group-reared chimps shown mirrors; first reacted as if to another chimp; after ~**2 days** used mirror to inspect their own bodies. **Mark Test:** while anesthetized, **red, odorless, alcohol-soluble dye** painted on eyebrow ridge and opposite ear; mirror reintroduced.
- **Findings:** all chimps made **mark-directed responses** (touched/inspected the marks while watching the mirror); >**3-fold increase** in viewing time; some smelled the touching fingers.
- **Key point:** non-human primates show self-awareness — challenges the view that it requires a non-physical "mind."

> **MCQ Target:** Gallup's mark test = chimpanzee self-awareness = challenges the physiological-or-psychological dichotomy.

---

### Nature-or-Nurture Thinking Runs into Difficulty

| Stage | Question | Problem |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Early | Genetic OR learned? | Ignored fetal environment, nutrition, stress, stimulation |
| Broadened | Genetic OR experiential? | Still a false dichotomy |
| Quantitative | How much genetic vs. experiential? | Assumes additive combination, not interaction |
| **Modern (correct)** | How do they INTERACT? | The only valid framework |

- Correct framework: genes and experience **interact** (like a musician + instrument producing music — you cannot split the music between them).

**Three points on neural development (Pinel's interactive model):**
1. Neurons become active **long before** fully developed.
2. Further development (connections, survival) depends on neural **activity**, much triggered by experience.
3. **Experience continuously modifies genetic expression.**

---

### A Model of the Biology of Behavior

All behavior is a product of interactions among: (1) **genetic endowment** (product of evolution), (2) **experience**, (3) **perception of the current situation**.

**Seven steps of the model (Figure 2.3 — key for MCQs):**
1. Evolution shapes the pool of behavior-influencing genes available to a species.
2. Experience modifies expression of an individual's genetic program.
3. Genes initiate a unique program of neural development.
4. Nervous system development depends on interactions with the environment.
5. Current behavioral capacities/tendencies are determined by unique patterns of neural activity (some experienced as thoughts, feelings, memories).
6. Current behavior arises from interactions of ongoing neural activity + perception of the current situation.
7. Behavioral success influences whether the individual's genes are passed on.

## 2.2 Human Evolution

### Darwin and the Theory of Evolution

- **Charles Darwin**, ***On the Origin of Species*** (**1859**) — the most influential theory in biology.
- Not the first to suggest evolution, but the first to amass large supporting evidence and propose a mechanism: **natural selection**.

**Four kinds of evidence for evolution:**

| Evidence | Example |
|--------------|---------|
| 1. Fossil record | Fossils change systematically through geological layers |
| 2. Structural similarities | Human hand, bird wing, cat paw share skeletal structure |
| 3. Selective breeding | Major changes in domestic plants/animals |
| 4. Direct observation | Grant (1991): Galápagos finch beak size rose after one drought season |

---

### Natural Selection

- **Natural selection**: heritable traits linked to high survival/reproduction are most likely passed on; over generations → species better adapted to its niche.
- **Fitness (Darwinian)**: the ability to **survive and contribute genes** to the next generation (NOT physical strength).

> A **scientific theory** is the best current evidence-based explanation, not vague speculation; evolution is as well-supported as gravity.

---

### Evolution and Behavior

#### Social Dominance

- Males of many species establish a **stable social dominance hierarchy** through combat; once set, low-rankers submit and hostilities drop.
- **Dominant males copulate more** — **McCann (1981)**: in 10 bull elephant seals, the dominant male had ~**37% of copulations**, rank-10 male ~**1%**.
- **Dominant females produce more/healthier offspring** — **Pusey, Williams, & Goodall (1997)**: high-ranking female chimps had more offspring with higher survival, via better food access.

#### Courtship Display

- Elaborate **courtship displays** (olfactory, visual, auditory, tactual) precede copulation; copulation fails if either partner does not react appropriately.
- **Courtship promotes speciation:**
  - **Species**: group reproductively isolated from others; produces fertile offspring only by mating within the species.
  - New species form when a reproductive barrier (**geographic** or **behavioral**, e.g., different courtship displays) separates a subpopulation.
  - **Conspecifics**: members of the same species.

---

### Course of Human Evolution

| Milestone | Time |
|-----------|------|
| First complex multicellular organisms | ~600 mya |
| First chordates | ~450 mya |
| First vertebrates (bony fishes) | ~425 mya |
| First amphibians | ~400 mya |
| First reptiles | ~300 mya |
| First mammals | ~180 mya |
| First hominins | ~6 mya |

- **Chordates**: animals with **dorsal nerve cords**; one of ~20 **phyla**.
- **Vertebrates**: chordates with **vertebrae** protecting the dorsal nerve cord. Seven classes: 3 fish classes + amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals.
- **Transitional fossil (Daeschler, Shubin, & Jenkins, 2006):** ~375 mya, N. Canada; had fish features (scales, teeth, gills) AND land-vertebrate wrist/finger bones.

**Amphibians (~400 mya):** bony fishes ventured onto land; selection turned fins→legs, gills→lungs; larvae must live in water.

**Reptiles (~300 mya):** from amphibians; first to lay **shell-covered eggs**; **dry scales** reduced water loss.

**Mammals (~180 mya):** from small reptiles; feed young via **mammary glands**; later nurtured young internally to birth. **Exception: duck-billed platypus** still lays eggs. Humans = order **Primates** (Latin *primus* = "first").

**Primates:** ~12 families; five notable: Prosimians, New-World Monkeys, Old-World Monkeys, Apes, Hominins.

**Apes (gibbons, orangutans, gorillas, chimpanzees):** from Old-World monkeys; long arms, grasping feet, opposable thumbs; unlike them, no tails and can walk upright briefly. **Chimpanzees** = closest living human relatives, **~99% of genes identical** (Chimpanzee Sequencing and Analysis Consortium, 2005). The actual ape ancestor is likely long extinct.

**Emergence of Humankind:**
- **Hominins**: family that includes humans. Two genera: *Australopithecus* and *Homo* (*Homo erectus* extinct; *Homo sapiens* modern).
- **Australopithecines:** evolved ~**6 mya** in Africa; roamed ~**5 million years**; ~1.3 m tall; small brains; **fully upright** (pelvis, leg bones, fossilized footprints ~3.6 mya). A 3.3-million-year-old skull of a 3-year-old girl found in Ethiopia. 3-foot hominins on Flores (Indonesia) as recently as **13,000 years ago**.
- **Early Homo:** from an Australopithecus species ~**2 mya**; larger brain cavity; used **fire and tools**; coexisted with australopithecines ~500,000 years.
- **Modern Homo sapiens:** appeared ~**200,000 years ago**; migrated out of Africa ~**50,000 years ago**.

**Cultural timeline:** art ~**40,000 ya**; ranching/farming ~**10,000 ya**; writing ~**3,500 ya**.

> **Paradox:** the "big three" (large brain, upright posture, free hands with opposable thumb) existed long before most human accomplishments.

---

### Thinking about Human Evolution (Corrected Misconceptions)

1. **Evolution is a dense bush, not a ladder** — not a single line.
2. **Humans cannot claim evolutionary supremacy** — hominins are a "blip" of evolutionary time.
3. **Evolution can be rapid** — driven by sudden environmental shifts or adaptive mutations; earth cooling may have accelerated human evolution (Behrensmeyer, 2006).
4. **Fewer than 1% of known species still exist.**
5. **Evolution is a tinkerer, not an architect** — modifies existing programs (e.g., sperm ineffective at body temperature → evolution of the scrotum).
6. **Not all traits are adaptive** — **spandrels** = non-adaptive by-products (e.g., the belly button).
7. **Not all adaptive traits evolved for their current function** — **exaptations** evolved for one function, co-opted for another (e.g., **bird wings**: walking → flight).
8. **Similarity ≠ common origin:**
   - **Homologous structures**: similar via **common origin** (human arm, bat wing).
   - **Analogous structures**: similar via **convergent evolution**, no common origin (bird wing, bee wing).
   - **Convergent evolution**: unrelated species evolve similar solutions to the same demands.

---

### Evolution of the Human Brain

**Brain size alone — two problems:**
1. Humans (~1,350 g) lack the biggest brains (whales/elephants 5,000–8,000 g).
2. Acclaimed intellectuals (e.g., Einstein) had unremarkable brain sizes. Adult human brains ~1,000–2,000 g; no clear size–intelligence link.

**Body-weight-adjusted (% of body weight):** Humans **2.33%**, elephants **0.20%**, **shrews 3.33%** (also flawed).

**Modern approach — compare regions:**
- **Brain stem**: regulates survival reflexes (heart rate, respiration, blood glucose).
- **Cerebrum (cerebral hemispheres)**: complex adaptive processes (learning, perception, motivation).

**Three points on human brain evolution:**
1. Brain **increased in size**.
2. Most increase in the **cerebrum**.
3. Increased **convolutions** greatly enlarged the **cerebral cortex** (outermost layer).

**Cross-species:** all brains are made of neurons; related species share major structures, connections, and functions. Human abilities = modifications of those in close relatives.

---

### Evolutionary Psychology: Mate Bonding

- **Evolutionary psychology**: understanding behaviors via the evolutionary pressures that shaped them (Schmitt & Pilcher, 2004).

| System | Definition | Prevalence |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Promiscuity** | Both sexes copulate indiscriminately with many partners | Most common overall |
| **Polygyny** | One male bonds with >1 female | Most prevalent in mammals |
| **Polyandry** | One female bonds with >1 male | Rare; only where male investment > female |
| **Monogamy** | Enduring bond between one male and one female | ~4% of mammalian species |

**Trivers (1972):** female mammals invest far more in offspring (gestation, suckling); males often contribute only sperm. So females produce few offspring and must be **selective**; males can sire many and bond with **as many females as possible** → **polygyny**.

- **Polyandry** occurs only when male investment > female (e.g., **sea horse**: female deposits eggs in male's pouch); not in mammals.
- **Monogamy (~4%)** evolved where females raise more/fitter young with **undivided male help**; female behaviors (driving off rivals, withholding copulation) made single-female investment the best male strategy.
- **Humans:** many cultures and individuals are not monogamous; infidelity is common (esp. men); humans do not match truly monogamous species (e.g., geese).

**Buss (1992) — confirmed predictions in humans:**
1. Men value **youth/attractiveness** (fertility cues) more than women.
2. Women value **power/earning capacity** more than men.
3. Physical attractiveness best predicts which women bond with high-status men.
4. Women's main strategy = increasing attractiveness; men's = displaying power/resources.
5. Men are more likely to commit adultery.

> **Caveat:** evolved tendencies operate without conscious awareness and are modulated by experience. Evolutionary theories must yield testable predictions.

## 2.3 Fundamental Genetics

### Darwin's Gap and Mendel

- Darwin did not know why conspecifics differ or how traits are transmitted.
- **Gregor Mendel** (1822–1884), an Augustinian monk, had the answers; significance recognized only in the early **20th century**, after both men died.

### Mendelian Genetics

- Studied **dichotomous traits** (one form or the other; e.g., seed color brown OR white) using **true-breeding lines**.
- **Seed color:** brown × white → **all F1 brown**; F1 × F1 → ~**3/4 brown : 1/4 white** (F2).

**Terminology:** **Dominant trait** (appears in all F1); **Recessive trait** (reappears in ~1/4 of F2); **Phenotype** (observable traits); **Genotype** (heritable traits, incl. unexpressed).

**Mendel's four-part theory:**

| Component | Proposal | Modern Term |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Two inherited factors per dichotomous trait | Genes |
| 2 | Two genes per trait | Alleles |
| 3 | One gene dominates in heterozygotes | Dominance |
| 4 | Offspring inherit one factor randomly from each parent | Law of Segregation |

- **Gene**: basic unit of heredity. **Alleles**: the two genes for a trait (one per parent), at the same chromosomal position. **Homozygous**: two identical alleles (BB/bb). **Heterozygous**: two different alleles (Bb); the dominant allele is expressed.

### Chromosomes: Reproduction and Recombination

- Genes sit on **chromosomes** (threadlike, in the nucleus), in **matched pairs**. **Humans: 23 pairs (46 total).**
- **Meiosis**: produces **gametes** (egg/sperm), each with ONE chromosome per pair → **23** in humans. **Fertilization**: sperm + egg → **zygote** (46).
- **Genetic recombination**: paired chromosomes **cross over** and exchange sections during meiosis → unique gamete combinations.
- **Mitosis**: division in non-reproductive cells; chromosomes double so each daughter gets the full **46**.

### Chromosomes: Structure and Replication

- Each chromosome = **double-stranded DNA**; strands are sequences of **nucleotide bases** on a phosphate-deoxyribose backbone.
- **Four bases:** adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), cytosine (C); their sequence = the **genetic code**.
- **Complementary base pairing:** **A–T**, **G–C**; strands are exact complements coiled as a **double helix**.
- **Replication:** strands unwind → exposed bases attract complementary free bases → **two identical DNA molecules**.
- **Errors:** **Down syndrome** (extra chromosome); **mutations** (gene alterations — most reduce fitness and disappear; rare beneficial ones drive rapid evolution).

### Sex Chromosomes and Sex-Linked Traits

- **Autosomal chromosomes**: matched non-sex pairs. **Sex chromosomes**: **XX** (female), **XY** (male, mammals). **Y** is small with few genes.
- **Sex-linked traits**: virtually all controlled by genes on the **X chromosome**.
- **Dominant sex-linked traits**: more frequent in **females** (two X's = twice the chance).
- **Recessive sex-linked traits**: more frequent in **males** (one X is enough; females need two copies). Classic example: **color blindness**.

### The Genetic Code and Gene Expression

- **Structural genes**: contain info for **protein synthesis**. **Proteins**: amino acid chains controlling physiology and cell structure.
- All cells share the same genes; cell types differ via **gene expression**.
- **Enhancers (promoters)**: DNA stretches (most of each chromosome) that determine whether/how fast structural genes make protein; act like adjustable switches.
- **Transcription factors**: proteins binding DNA to influence expression; many respond to **environmental signals** — the key mechanism by which **experience interacts with genes**.

**Steps in gene expression:**

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| 1 | Chromosome section with the gene unravels |
| 2 | One DNA strand is the template; **mRNA** is transcribed |
| 3 | mRNA carries the code out of the nucleus |
| 4 | mRNA attaches to a **ribosome** |
| 5 | Ribosome reads **codons** (3 bases) |
| 6 | Each codon adds 1 of 20 amino acids (carried by **tRNA**) |
| 7 | At a "stop" codon, the completed **protein** is released |

- **RNA vs. DNA:** RNA has **uracil** (not thymine) and a **ribose** backbone.
- **Two phases:** **Transcription** (DNA → RNA) and **Translation** (RNA → protein). Example: guanine-guanine-adenine → glycine.

### Mitochondrial DNA

- **Mitochondria**: energy-generating cytoplasmic organelles with their own DNA.
- **Mitochondrial DNA**: inherited **solely from the mother**; mutations accumulate at a steady rate → **evolutionary clock** (Kaessmann & Pääbo, 2002); supports **African origin of hominins**.

### Modern Genetics

- **Human Genome Project:** began **1990**, completed **2001**; mapped ~**3 billion bases**. Surprising finding: humans have **relatively few genes** (~20,000–25,000).

## 2.4 Behavioral Development: Genes × Experience

### Selective Breeding of Maze-Bright and Maze-Dull Rats

> **KEY STUDY — Gene-Environment Interaction**

- **Maze-bright** (bred for fast learning) outperformed **maze-dull** rats only in **impoverished environments**.
- In **enriched environments**, the two performed **equally**.
- **Implication:** genetic differences in behavior can be fully overridden by enrichment — genes and environment interact, not add.

### Neuroplasticity

- **Neuroplasticity**: the nervous system changes its structure/function in response to experience; development depends on experiential input throughout life.

### Phenylketonuria (PKU)

> **KEY STUDY — Gene-Environment Interaction with Clinical Implications**

- **PKU**: a **single-gene mutation** failing to produce **phenylalanine hydroxylase** (converts phenylalanine → tyrosine) → **phenylalanine** accumulates → severe **intellectual disability**.
- **Treatment:** a phenylalanine-free diet from birth **completely prevents** disability.
- **Lesson:** a highly heritable single-gene disorder is fully preventable environmentally. Genetic ≠ immutable.

> **MCQ Target:** PKU = single gene + dietary treatment = prevented intellectual disability = best example of gene-environment interaction.

### Development of Birdsong

> **KEY STUDY**

- Birds have a **genetic predisposition** to learn their species' song, but must hear it during a **sensitive (critical) period** to sing normally; isolation → abnormal song.
- **Lesson:** both genes AND experience are required; neither alone suffices.

## 2.5 Genetics of Human Psychological Differences

### Development of Individuals vs. Differences among Individuals

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Development of an individual** | How one organism grows/changes; always gene × experience |
| **Development of differences among individuals** | Why individuals differ; can have different determinants |

- A factor can matter for development in all individuals yet explain none of the differences among them (e.g., all humans develop two eyes, which says nothing about eye-color differences).

**Heritability:**
- **Heritability**: statistical measure of the degree to which trait differences in a population are due to genetic differences, given that population's range of environments.
- A **population statistic**, not about any individual; can change with environment.
- **Warning:** high heritability does NOT mean unresponsive to intervention (PKU is highly heritable yet preventable).

**Twin studies:**
- **Monozygotic (MZ) twins**: identical; share **100%** of genes.
- **Dizygotic (DZ) twins**: fraternal; share ~**50%** (like siblings, same age).
- MZ more similar than DZ → genetic component.
