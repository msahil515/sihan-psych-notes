# Item Writing

Chapter 3 covers the construction of test items: their definition, classification into essay and objective types, the rules for writing each variety well, the formats used in psychological and educational measurement, the distinctions between essay and objective tests, and the standard methods for scoring objective-test items efficiently.

---

## Meaning and Types of Items

### Definition of an Item

**Item** (Bean, 1953, p. 11): "a single task or question that usually cannot be broken down into any smaller units." An arithmetical problem, a manipulation task, or a mechanical puzzle can each be an item.

**Nunnally (1978)** described items as the **"lowest common denominator of a test"** — the irreducible unit from which tests are built.

### Characteristics of a Good Item

1. Phrased without ambiguity for both examinees and examiners.
2. Neither too easy nor too difficult.
3. Possesses **discriminating power** — distinguishes those who possess the measured trait from those who do not.
4. Measures only significant aspects of knowledge, not trivial content.
5. Does not encourage guesswork.
6. Presents no reading difficulty.
7. Is not dependent on another item for meaning and cannot be answered by referring to another item.

### Number of Items Required

There is no hard-and-fast rule, but research shows:

| Item Type | Items Needed | Reliability Coefficient |
|---|---|---|
| Dichotomous (two-alternative) | 25-30 | ~0.80 |
| Multipoint (polytomous) | 18-20 | ~0.80 |

Practical rule: the item writer should always write **approximately twice** the number of items intended for retention. For a speed test, **60 items** is a commonly cited figure.

### Two Broad Categories of Items

1. **Essay items** (free-answer items)
2. **Objective items** ("new-type" items)

## Essay Items (Free-Answer Items)

An **essay item** is one in which the examinee relies on memory and past associations to compose a response to a broad question. Also called **free-answer items**.

Essay items encourage **higher mental processes**: the ability to synthesize, analyze, evaluate, organize, criticize, and integrate information.

### Subtypes of Essay Items

| Subtype | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Short-answer essay item** | Answered in one or two sentences; concerns one central concept | "What is reliability?" |
| **Long-answer / extended-answer essay item** (A. Hinz, 1972) | Requires several paragraphs; covers more than one central concept | "Discuss the meaning of reliability and validity." |

**Primary merit**: requires recall, selection, and coherent organization of facts.

**Primary limitation**: **subjectivity in scoring** (inter-scorer and intra-scorer variability). Scores often correlate with answer length rather than accuracy.

**Additional limitations**: literary ability confounds factual knowledge; fewer questions lower content validity; writing speed affects performance.

---

### Variations in Essay Testing Environments

| Variation | Description | Key Merit | Key Demerit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Open-book examination** | Examinees may use textbooks during the test | Reduces pure memory demands | Less able examinees spend time reading rather than composing |
| **Take-home examination** | Answered outside the test hall with no time pressure | Allows thorough, unhurried work | Sacrifices test security; internet/cell-phone access is a concern |
| **Open Cheat Sheet** | Examinees bring prepared notes, outlines, or key points | Effective where formulas/facts dominate | — |
| **Study questions** | Examiner provides a larger list (e.g., 20); a subset (4-5) appears on the exam | Examinees can prepare thoroughly using any resources | — |

---

### Guidelines for Writing Good Essay Items

1. Define the problem explicitly so every examinee interprets it the same way.
2. Avoid overly broad questions.
3. Provide appropriate directions: time allotted, information required, weightage per item.
4. Allow sufficient construction time.
5. Begin with action verbs: **compare, contrast, criticize, explain how, differentiate**. Avoid "what," "when," "who," "list" (elicit only fact recall).
6. Know which mental processes are targeted before writing.
7. Use recall material in novel organization to prevent rote answers.
8. For controversial topics, evaluate on quality of evidence, not position taken.

### Guidelines for Constructing Essay Tests (Structural Level)

1. Do not make the test an exercise in writing speed.
2. Require all examinees to answer the same questions (choice reduces comparability).
3. Include a range of difficulty.
4. Provide clear general directions: answer format, time plan, evaluation criteria, time limit, score points per question.

### Scoring Methods for Essay Items

**Sorting method**: Papers sorted into groups (best, better, poor); examiner assigns weightage to each group. Best for: quick holistic evaluation, small number of examinees.

**Point-score method**: A **grading key** (best possible answer outline) is prepared in advance; scorer matches examinee answers against the key and assigns points. Best for: short-answer essays. **Limitation**: difficult to apply to extended/long-answer items.

### General Scoring Guidelines for Essay Tests

1. Decide which qualities are judged (grammar, content, organization) and evaluate each separately.
2. Read all answers to one question before moving to the next (maintains consistency; prevents halo effect).
3. Prepare a **model answer** for each question before grading.
4. Grade papers as anonymously as possible.
5. **Shuffle** answer sheets before scoring each new question (prevents contrast effects).
6. Write specific comments and corrections on papers.

## Objective Items ("New-Type" Items)

An **objective item** has one fixed correct answer that the examinee either supplies or selects from given options.

### Two Broad Categories

| Category | Subcategories | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Supply type** | Unstructured short-answer; Completion/fill-in | Examinee writes the answer |
| **Selection type** | Two-alternative; Multiple-choice; Matching | Examinee selects from given choices |

---

### Supply-Type Items

#### Unstructured Short-Answer Item

Direct question; examinee supplies a word, number, or phrase.
Example: *Who was the first President of the Indian Republic?* (Answer: Dr. Rajendra Prasad)

#### Completion Item (Fill-in Item)

Incomplete statement; examinee supplies the missing element.
Example: *The first President of the Indian Republic was ___.*

- Used in mathematics, chemistry, physics, biology, vocabulary tests, association-based learning.
- **Reduces guessing** to a minimum.
- **Scoring**: answer key; correct = +1; incorrect = 0 (no negative marking).
- **Important note**: completion items are NOT strictly objective items (scoring can become subjective).

#### Writing Completion Items: Key Rules

1. **Avoid ambiguity**: *Behaviourism was founded in ___* is ambiguous ("1913" and "America" are both correct). Better: *Behaviourism was founded in the year ___*

2. **Avoid direct-hint clues**: providing opposite blanks (theist/atheist) makes the second answer derivable from the first.

3. **Avoid too many blanks**: excessive blanking (e.g., multiple blanks in Allport's definition of personality) tests rote memory, not understanding.

---

### Selection-Type Items

#### Two-Alternative Item (Dichotomous Item)

**Probability of guessing = 50%.** Biggest limitation.

##### Subtypes

| Subtype | Format | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Yes-No** | Question answered Yes or No | "Do you suffer from insomnia? Yes/No" |
| **True-False** | Declarative statement: True or False | "Wilhelm Wundt is the father of experimental psychology. True/False" |
| **Right-Wrong** | Sentence judged grammatically right or wrong | "He needs not go to cinema. Right/Wrong" |
| **Correction Form** | Underlined wrong part replaced with correct answer; hybrid of supply + selection | "Wundt established the first lab in *1869* (1879)" |

Best suited for: personality tests; dates, technical terms, vocabulary comparisons.
Practical advantage: easiest to construct; quickest to score.

##### Guidelines for Writing Two-Alternative Items

(a) Simple and direct statements (not complex or indirect).

(b) Entirely true or entirely false — no ambiguous middle ground.
- Poor: *The capital of India is Delhi.* | Improved: *The capital of India is New Delhi.*

(c) Avoid **double negatives**.

(d) Cite authority explicitly for controversial statements.
- Poor: *Intelligence is the ability to learn or profit by past experience.*
- Improved: *According to Ebbinghaus, intelligence is the ability to learn or to profit by past experience.*

(e) Avoid **specific determiners**:
- Suggest FALSE: *never, nothing, always, all, none*
- Suggest TRUE: *usually, sometimes, often*
  Equalize their use across true and false items.

(f) Use a non-obvious sequence of true/false answers (not strict alternating patterns).

(g) Paraphrase rather than reproduce verbatim from textbooks — rewards understanding over rote memory.

---

#### Multiple-Choice Item

The **most popular, common, flexible, and effective** of all objective items. Also called **polytomous** or **polychotomous** item.

**Structure**:
- **Stem** (= *premise*): question or incomplete statement presenting the problem.
- **Alternatives / Options**: usually **3 to 5** (4-5 preferred); similar length and complexity.
- **Keyed answer**: the correct or best answer.
- **Foils / Distractors**: plausible but incorrect alternatives.

##### Advantages Over True-False Items

1. Respondent must know the correct answer, not just that a statement is wrong.
2. Relatively free from **response sets**.
3. Incorrect choices reveal misconceptions for diagnostic use.
4. **Higher reliability per item** — more alternatives reduce guessing (analogous to increasing test length).
5. Measures wide range of abilities: recall, application, discrimination, interpretation.

##### Limitations

1. Difficult to write; requires deep subject knowledge.
2. Takes longer to construct and review.
3. Risk of misleading or ambiguous distractors, especially for younger/less-vocabulary examinees.
4. Less suited for organizational ability, synthesis, creative thinking.

##### Guidelines for Writing Multiple-Choice Items

(a) **Keyed answer must not be noticeably shorter or longer** than distractors (length = inadvertent clue).

(b) **Randomly vary position** of keyed answer across items (habitual placement allows pattern detection).

(c) **Stem must present a clear problem**:
- Poor: *Learning (a) is change in attitude ...*
- Improved: *Learning ordinarily refers to: (a) change in attitude ...*

(d) **Avoid clang (phonological) associations** between stem and keyed answer:
- Poor: *Verbal intelligence test ... (b) Verbal responses* (verbal-verbal clang)
- Improved: *Verbal intelligence test ... (b) Written responses*

(e) **Ensure only one correct or best answer**:
- Poor: Includes both Kendall tau and Spearman rho as options (both correct for ranked data).
- Improved: Remove Kendall tau; only Spearman rho remains.

(f) **Use negation sparingly** (not, except); when used, underline or capitalize it.

(g) **Wrong answers must be plausible** — distractors should represent common errors, logically consistent with the stem.

(h) **"None of these" / "None of the above"**: use only when an unequivocal judgement is possible (spelling, mathematics). Avoid in best-answer formats. Prefer the form "none of the above" (grammatically correct) and pair with a question stem, not a sentence completion.

(i) **Avoid "All of the above"**: a shortcut when only three distinct distractors can be generated.

---

#### Matching Item

Two columns: **left = premises (stems)** | **right = options/responses**.

Measures **associative learning**: dates-events, terms-definitions, books-authors, names-diagrams.

Example (from text):
| Stem | Response |
|---|---|
| 1. Product-moment method | A. Fisher |
| 2. Rank difference method | B. Pearson |
| 3. Coefficient of concordance | C. Spearman |
| 4. F ratio | D. Kendall |

##### Limitations

1. Measures only factual/associative knowledge, not organizational ability.
2. **Process of elimination** — if all but one pair is known, the last pair is automatic.
3. Requires content homogeneity — mixing types provides extra clues.
4. Takes longer to answer than other objective types.

##### Guidelines for Writing Matching Items

(a) **Number of response options must exceed number of stems** (e.g., 4 stems : 6 responses) to reduce elimination guessing.

(b) **Clear directions** stating the basis of matching and mechanics.

(c) **Stems and responses must be homogeneous** (all stems = dates; all responses = event names). Mixing creates clues.

(d) Sets must be **neither too long nor too short** (long = inhomogeneous and time-consuming; short = high guessing probability).

## Other Item Formats Used in Psychological Tests

### The Likert Format

Introduced by **Likert (1932)** for attitude scale construction.

**Standard 5-point scale**:

| Option | Score (Positive Item) | Score (Negative Item) |
|---|---|---|
| Strongly agree | 5 | 1 |
| Agree | 4 | 2 |
| Neutral | 3 | 3 |
| Disagree | 2 | 4 |
| Strongly disagree | 1 | 5 |

**6-point variant** (no neutral): Strongly disagree / Moderately disagree / Mildly disagree / Mildly agree / Moderately agree / Strongly agree.

Responses are amenable to **factor analysis** to identify item clusters. Requires **item analysis** to assess discriminability.

---

### Category Format

Fixed scale with more options than Likert; most popular = **10-point scale**.

**Debate on optimal number of categories**:

| Researcher | Optimal Number | Key Point |
|---|---|---|
| Symonds (1925) | 7 | Classic recommendation |
| Champney & Marshall (1939) | ~21 (3 x 7) | Higher sensitivity |
| Lissak & Ladd (2011) | Maximum 7 | Reliability plateaus beyond 7 |
| Kaplan (1991) | Few categories adequate | Economy with no reliability loss |
| Clark & Watson (1994) | Caution with too many | May prevent discrimination |

---

### Visual Analogue Scale

Respondent marks a **100 mm horizontal line** with defined endpoints. Score = distance in mm from origin. Used for self-rated health, pain. **Disadvantage**: time-consuming for multi-item scales (Clark & Watson, 1994).

---

### Adjective Checklist

Associated with **Gough (1960)**. Long list of adjectives; respondent endorses or rejects each (binary). Used for self-description and description of others.

---

### Q-Sort

Developed by **Stephenson (1933)**. Respondents sort statements into categories in a forced distribution. Used for verbalized attitudes, preferences, self-description. Currently: Likert, multiple-choice, and Q-sort are most popular; checklists nearly obsolete; true-false is less popular.

## Difference Between Essay-Type Tests and Objective-Type Tests

Following **Ebel (1965)**:

### Key Differences

| Dimension | Essay Tests | Objective Tests |
|---|---|---|
| Response mode | Writes in own words | Selects from alternatives |
| Cognitive demands | Thinking + writing dominate | Reading + reflective thinking dominate |
| Scoring | Difficult; scorer variability | Easy; key-based, accurate |
| Quality dependency | Scorer's skill | Item constructor's skill |
| Guessing | Essentially eliminated | Allowed (varies by format) |
| Bluffing | Possible | Not possible |
| Score determination | Subjective (scorer decides) | Objective (responses determine via key) |
| Construction difficulty | Easy to construct | Difficult to construct well |
| Content coverage | Fewer items; lower content validity | Many items; higher content validity |
| Best for measuring | Synthesis, analysis, evaluation, organization | Piecemeal knowledge, recognition, recall |

### Key Similarities

1. Both involve subjectivity (objective = item construction; essay = scoring).
2. Both require objectivity of interpretation — scores must mean the same to all observers.
3. Both can measure most educational achievements; the boundary is increasingly blurred.

## General Guidelines for Item Writing

1. **Clarity**: simple, clear language; item must not be a language-comprehension test.

2. **No nonfunctional words**: words that contribute nothing toward identifying the correct answer lower validity.

3. **Avoid irrelevant accuracies**: embedded accurate but irrelevant details mislead examinees and reflect poor item-writer thinking.

4. **Appropriate difficulty level**: match examinee ability level. For objective items, homogeneous alternatives increase difficulty. Consider both typical and ideal examinees.

5. **No stereotyped words**: stereotyped phrasing fails to discriminate between knowers and non-knowers. Paraphrase to reward genuine understanding.

6. **No irrelevant clues**: consistent answer positioning, homogeneous foils, unique distractors, and specific determiners all artificially lower difficulty.

7. **No interlocking / interdependent items**: answering one item must not depend on knowing the answer to another. Violates equal opportunity.

### Remmers et al. (1960): Six Additional Guidelines

| # | Guideline |
|---|---|
| i | Define what is being assessed clearly; use substantive theory; make items specific |
| ii | Avoid exceptionally long items |
| iii | Use appropriate reading-difficulty level for the test-taking population |
| iv | Develop an item pool: write 3-4 items per dimension; avoid redundancy |
| v | Avoid **double-barrelled items** (conveying two or more ideas simultaneously) |
| vi | Mix negative and positive items to control **acquiescence response set** |

**Chan, Drasgov & Savin (1999)**: Items should be periodically revised; items focused on the meaning of concepts lose reliability over time.

## General Methods of Scoring Objective-Type Items

Criteria for selecting a scoring method:
1. Must not be time-consuming.
2. Must not require specially trained persons.

### Overlay-Key Method

A **cut-out key** (sheet with windows) is prepared. Correct answers are written below each window. The key is overlaid on each answer page; examinee's responses are visible through the windows and compared directly against correct answers below. Total score = sum of points for correct answers. Also usable for matching and completion items.

### Strip-Key Method

Correct answers are printed **vertically on a strip of paper or cardboard** aligned with item positions on the test. The strip is placed alongside the answer column; responses are marked in an adjacent vertical column. Total score = sum of points for each correct answer.

### Scoring Essay-Type Tests

No key-based method applies to essay tests. Short-answer essays can be treated key-like if model answers, acceptable lengths, and point allocations are established in advance. Extended-answer essay items resist key-based scoring.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Bean (1953)** defined an item as "a single task or question that usually cannot be broken down into any smaller units." **Nunnally (1978)** called items the "lowest common denominator of a test."

2. For reliability ~0.80: **25-30 dichotomous items** or **18-20 multipoint items** are needed (minimum after item analysis).

3. Item writers should always draft **approximately twice** the number of items intended for retention.

4. **Essay items** encourage higher mental processes (synthesis, analysis, evaluation, organization); their primary limitation is **subjectivity in scoring**.

5. Two scoring methods for essay items: (a) **sorting method** (holistic grouping) and (b) **point-score method** (grading key; suited to short-answer essays).

6. **Two-alternative / dichotomous item**: **50% guessing probability** — the least preferred objective type for knowledge assessment.

7. Four subtypes of two-alternative items: **Yes-No, True-False, Right-Wrong, Correction Form**. The Correction Form is a hybrid of supply and selection.

8. Multiple-choice item is also called **polytomous** or **polychotomous**. Correct answer = **keyed answer**; wrong alternatives = **foils / distractors**.

9. **Specific determiners** (never, always = suggest false; usually, sometimes = suggest true) must be equalized across true and false items.

10. In matching items: **response options must exceed stems** (to reduce elimination guessing); stems and responses must be **homogeneous**.

11. **Likert format** (Likert, 1932): positive items scored 5-4-3-2-1; negative items scored 1-2-3-4-5. Amenable to factor analysis.

12. Optimal scale points: Symonds (1925) = 7; Champney & Marshall (1939) = ~21; Lissak & Ladd (2011) = reliability plateaus beyond 7 for most topics.

13. **Adjective checklist** = Gough (1960); **Q-sort** = Stephenson (1933). Currently: Likert, multiple-choice, Q-sort most popular; checklists nearly obsolete; true-false less popular.

14. **Remmers et al. (1960)**: mix negative and positive items to control **acquiescence response set**.

15. Two objective scoring methods: (a) **overlay-key method** (cut-out windows over response positions) and (b) **strip-key method** (answers printed vertically on a strip alongside the test). Neither applies to extended essay tests.
