# Response Set in Test Scores

Chapter 8 examines the concept of response sets as a major source of invalidity in psychological test scores. It defines and classifies the major types of response sets, discusses their implications for test reliability and validity, and outlines practical methods for controlling or eliminating them in test construction and administration.

---

## Meaning of Response Sets

### Response Bias

**Response bias** (Guilford, 1954) is defined as a tendency whereby a response to any test item is altered by the examinee in a way that it indicates something other than what the test is intended to measure. It is one of several factors that tend to invalidate test scores.

### Response Set

A **response set** is a specific kind of response bias that occurs as a result of examinees adopting certain mental sets during testing. Example: an examinee may decide — for personal reasons — that "Yes" is always the correct alternative in a Yes/No test and answer all items "Yes" regardless of item content.

**Cronbach (1950, p. 474)** defined response set as:
> "The tendency causing a person to give different responses to items than he would if the same content were presented in a forced form."

In other words, when the same item content is presented in different formats (e.g., free-response vs. forced-choice), a respondent's answers may differ not because of true differences in the trait being measured but because of systematic response tendencies. Cronbach illustrates this by noting that three examinees A, B, and C may answer a two-alternative personality inventory item differently — not due to poor reliability of the test — but because different response sets (e.g., social desirability, faking-bad, acquiescence) are operating within each individual.

## Types of Response Sets

### Deviation Set

- **Proposer:** Berg (1961)
- **Definition:** The **deviation set** refers to a tendency to give very unusual or uncommon responses to test items.
- **Occurrence:** Particularly found in tests with non-verbal items such as:
  - The **Rorschach Test** (inkblot projective technique)
  - The **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)**
- Such unusual or rare responses deviate markedly from normative responses, introducing systematic distortion.

---

### Acquiescence Set

- **Definition:** **Acquiescence** (also called yea-saying / nay-saying) refers to a tendency to respond in some systematic way — consistently marking all items as "Yes," "True," or "Agree" (yea-saying) or consistently marking all items as "No," "False," or "Disagree" (nay-saying) — regardless of item content.
- **Occurrence:** Most common in personality inventories and interest inventories.
- **Conceptual structure:** Acquiescence is treated as a **continuum variable**:
  - One pole: examinees who consistently respond "Yes" / "True" (yea-sayers)
  - Other pole: examinees who consistently respond "No" / "False" (nay-sayers)
- **Consequences on total score:**
  - If "Yes"/"True" responses are keyed positively: yea-saying inflates the total score above the true score.
  - If "No"/"False" responses are keyed positively: nay-saying inflates the total score above the true score.
  - In either case, a distorted picture of the examinee emerges — the score does not accurately reflect the trait being measured.

---

### Faking-Good (Social Desirability Tendency)

- **Definition:** **Faking-good**, also known as the **social desirability tendency**, is a tendency to give answers in a way that creates a favourable impression of oneself.
- **Mechanism:** The examinee presents a better-than-true self-image to improve chances of selection, admission, or favourable evaluation.
- **Typical contexts:** Highly prevalent in:
  - Personality tests
  - Service/job selection tests
  - School/college admission tests
  - Tests measuring socially desirable traits: emotional stability, punctuality, trustworthiness, responsibility.
- **Edwards (1957):** Conducted extensive research on the **social desirability variable**. Argued that faking-good is not necessarily a deliberate "facade effect" or a conscious attempt to "put oneself in a good light"; examinees themselves may be unaware of the tendency.
- **Crowne & Marlowe (1964)** and **Frederiksen (1965):** Held that social desirability tendency is related to:
  - Need for social approval and recognition
  - Social conformity
  - Need to avoid social criticism
- **Experimental evidence:** When examinees are explicitly instructed to fake-good, they are able to do so effectively and improve their test scores compared to neutral-instruction conditions. This shows the test's effectiveness is drastically reduced when examinees are motivated to fake-good.

---

### Faking-Bad (Social Undesirability Tendency)

- **Definition:** **Faking-bad** (social undesirability tendency) refers to a tendency to select answers that give an unfavourable or socially undesirable impression of oneself.
- **Motivations:** May indicate the need for sympathy, attention, or to be helped by others in problem-solving.
- **Examples:**
  - A patient about to undergo psychotherapy may over-simulate maladjustment on an adjustment inventory.
  - A military officer seeking sick leave may fake responses to appear more mentally ill than he actually is.

---

### Semantics (Response Category Ambiguity)

- **Definition:** When response options are worded with terms such as "often," "sometimes," "seldom," "agree," "strongly agree," "strongly disagree," these terms carry **different meanings for different examinees**.
- **Effect:** What one examinee describes as "often" another may describe as "sometimes"; what one calls "sometimes" another may call "seldom." This ambiguity introduces error in response interpretation and affects the final score.
- This is not a volitional response set but a semantic-interpretive source of systematic error.

---

### Evasiveness

- **Definition:** **Evasiveness** is the tendency to mark "?", "Uncertain," "Indifferent," etc., rather than committing to a definitive response direction.
- **Occurrence:** Found when response alternatives include a middle or neutral category (e.g., "Agree–Indifferent–Disagree").
- **Effect:** Creates a bimodal-like avoidance pattern; examinees evade a true directional category.
- **Empirical note:** Cronbach (1946, p. 480) reported that a scale designed to measure the "doubtful category" response tendency had a **reliability coefficient of 0.73**, indicating this tendency is itself reliable (i.e., consistent across time).
- Guilford also developed categorical frameworks for such response patterns.
- Evasiveness is considered a consistent habit on the part of the examinee.

---

### Tendency to Work Speedily

- **Definition:** A tendency to respond rapidly to items, prioritising speed over accuracy — referred to as a **speed-oriented response set**.
- **Motivational basis:** Primarily ego-oriented motivation; common in ability and special-ability tests.
- **Context:** Lengthy tests (300 or more items) particularly encourage this tendency, as examinees begin to skip careful consideration of item content.
- **Effect:** Distorts test validity — the test ends up measuring speed of responding rather than the intended ability or trait.

---

### Tendency to Guess When Uncertain

- **Definition:** The tendency to **guess** a response when the correct answer is not known or the item is ambiguous or difficult.
- **Occurrence:** More prominent in ability tests than in personality tests, because ability tests are typically written in multiple-choice or matching formats (which present specific alternatives to guess from).
- **Effect:** Guessing contributes to the **error variance** of the score and therefore lowers the validity of the test.

---

### Cautiousness

- **Definition:** **Cautiousness** refers to the tendency to leave items unanswered or untouched, especially when the correct answer is not clear.
- **Occurrence:** Most common in ability tests and special-ability tests.
- **Effect:** A large number of unanswered items adversely affects test efficiency by reducing the amount of scored information.
- This is the opposite pole of the guessing tendency.

---

### Extremeness

- **Definition:** **Extremeness** refers to a tendency to mark only the extreme points of a rating scale (i.e., the highest or lowest values) rather than using the middle range.
- **Effect:** If this tendency operates consistently, it reduces the discriminative efficiency and lowers the overall validity of the rating instrument.

---

## Response Sets vs. Response Styles

The conceptualisation of response sets has evolved through distinct stages:

1. **Stage 1 (Early view):** Response sets were identified purely as sources of **error variance** to be eliminated from test scores.
2. **Stage 2 (Later view — Hodges, 1962):** Response sets were reconceptualised as indicators of **broad, durable personality characteristics** in their own right.
   - In this conceptualisation, response sets were relabelled as **response styles** — that is, stable, trait-like dispositions that reveal something genuine about personality (e.g., acquiescence as a trait, social desirability as a personality variable).
   - Supporting evidence: Studies by Swartz (1932), and follow-up studies by Hodges (1962) and Jackson & Messick (1962).

**Key distinction:**
- **Response set:** Temporary, situation-specific tendency that introduces error.
- **Response style:** Stable, trait-like tendency considered a genuine personality indicator.

---

## Implications of Response Sets

Response sets have the following implications for psychological testing:

1. **Alter individual differences:** Response sets may artificially reduce or enlarge individual differences in test scores.
2. **Introduce pseudo variance:** Response sets contribute **irrelevant or pseudo variance** to test scores — they dilute the test with factors unrelated to the test content, thereby **reducing construct validity**.
3. **Worsen with item difficulty/ambiguity:** Response sets become more pronounced when items are difficult, ambiguous, or unstructured. Such items should therefore be avoided.
4. **Consistency across administrations:** Response sets tend to be consistent from one test administration to another and from one test to another (with minor exceptions). Because of this consistency:
   - They may **raise test reliability** (a consistent factor, even an irrelevant one, contributes positively to reliability).
   - However, when a response set's contribution to **error variance** outweighs its contribution to true-score variance, it reduces validity and must be controlled.
5. **Reclassification potential:** Consistent response sets may come to be treated as response styles measuring genuine personality traits (Swartz, 1932; Hodges, 1962; Jackson & Messick, 1962).

## Methods to Eliminate Response Sets

### 1. Design of Test Items

- **Two-value formats** (Yes/No, True/False, Agree/Disagree) are known to encourage acquiescence and evasiveness.
- **Recommended alternative:** Use **5-point, 7-point, or 10-point rating scales** instead of two-value formats. Finer-grained scales increase reliability and validity by reducing acquiescence and evasiveness response-set variance.
- **Forced-choice items:** Require examinees to choose among options that appear equally acceptable or equally unacceptable, making it harder to respond according to social desirability.
  - **Edwards' Personal Preference Schedule (EPPS):** Uses forced-choice items with more than two items presented as phrases appearing equally acceptable or unacceptable. Examinees must choose.
  - **Gordon Personal Inventory (GPI):** Uses forced-choice items with two items; the examinee selects the item most descriptive of themselves and the item least descriptive of themselves.
  - **Limitation of forced-choice items:** Subsequent research shows they are not very effective at controlling social desirability; they also introduce unfavourable technical complications that further reduce the ability to control social desirability.

#### Controlling Social Desirability Specifically

- **Social Desirability (SD) Scale** developed by **Marlowe & Crowne (1960):** Scores on this scale may be correlated with scores on a new test to index how much social desirability is operating.
- **MMPI L and K scales:** Used to evaluate faking-good and faking-bad (see Correction Keys section).
- **Item selection by neutrality (Wiener, 1948; Hanley, 1961):** Select only items that are relatively **neutral** with respect to social desirability.
  - **Edwards (1957)** constructed a **9-point social desirability rating scale** (from "extremely undesirable" to "extremely desirable") to classify items:
    - Items rated 4 to 6 are considered **neutral** with respect to social desirability.
    - The theoretical median of the scale is 5.
    - Items rated above 6 are indicative of faking-good tendency.
    - Items rated below 4 are indicative of faking-bad tendency.

**Edwards' 9-Point Social Desirability Rating Scale:**

| Rating | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| 1 | Extremely undesirable |
| 2 | Strongly undesirable |
| 3 | Moderately undesirable |
| 4 | Mildly undesirable |
| 5 | Neutral |
| 6 | Mildly desirable |
| 7 | Moderately desirable |
| 8 | Strongly desirable |
| 9 | Extremely desirable |

### 2. Adapting Test Difficulty

- Response sets increase as item difficulty increases.
- Test items should be calibrated to match the **ability level of the examinees**.
- Items that are too difficult for the target group encourage response sets (especially guessing and speed); relatively easier, appropriately matched items reduce this.
- Tests calibrated to ability level (adaptive or ability-matched) are common in **psychophysics**.

---

### 3. Use of Correction Keys (Validity Scales)

Multiple correction methods and validity indices have been developed:

#### MMPI Validity Scales (Original)
The **Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)** contains four validity scales designed to detect response sets:

| Scale | Name | What a High Score Indicates |
|-------|------|-----------------------------|
| **?** | Cannot Say | Excessive item omissions |
| **L** | Lie Scale | Deliberate attempt to fake-good (naive, obvious faking) |
| **F** | Infrequency Scale | Deliberate attempt to fake-bad; malingering; random responding |
| **K** | Correction Scale | Subtle defensiveness / suppressor variable |

- The **K scale** acts as a **suppressor variable**: its score is added as a correction to the T-scale scores of certain clinical scales to adjust for defensiveness.
- Items are answered in one of three categories: **True, False, Cannot Say**.

#### MMPI-2 Additional Validity Scales
The revised **MMPI-2** added three further validity scales:

| Scale | Name | What it Detects |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| **Fb** | Back F Scale | Infrequency in the latter portion of the test |
| **VRIN** | Variable Response Inconsistency | Logically inconsistent responding (random responding); large number of items answered in a logically inconsistent manner |
| **TRIN** | True Response Inconsistency | Tendency to respond "True" to most items (yea-saying) or "False" to most items (nay-saying) |

---

### 4. Modifying the Directions

Written instructions can be crafted to pre-empt specific response sets:

| Response Set to Control | Direction Modification |
|-------------------------|------------------------|
| Acquiescence (yea-saying) | State explicitly that approximately 50% of True/False items are actually False, prompting examinees to distribute answers |
| Guessing when uncertain | Warn that incorrect answers will receive **negative marking** (penalty scoring), discouraging random guessing |
| Evasiveness | Instruct examinees to respond as **spontaneously as possible** and note that neutral/uncertain responses may be indicative of an undesirable tendency |
| Speed tendency | Direct examinees to answer all items and allow adequate time so accuracy is prioritised over speed |

**Caution:** Directing examinees to answer all items may increase **random error**. However, in most cases, this random error is less damaging than the **constant (systematic) errors** produced by response sets such as guessing, evasiveness, and acquiescence.

---

### 5. Use of Good and Appropriate Scoring Formulas

- Developing appropriate scoring formulas for correct, incorrect, and unattempted items can significantly reduce response-set variance.
- **Nunnally (1967)** recommended obtaining **factor analysis** information regarding these different score components and weighting them accordingly, as the best solution for reducing response-set contamination.

---

### 6. Use of Easy Multiple-Choice Items

- Two-alternative items (Yes/No, True/False, Agree/Disagree) have a **50% chance** of correct guessing, making them highly susceptible to the guessing response set.
- **Multiple-choice items** (3 or more options) automatically reduce guessing probability:
  - 3 options: ~33% chance of correct guess
  - 4 options: 25% chance of correct guess
  - 5 options: 20% chance of correct guess
- Multiple-choice items are considered by many investigators to be the **best format** for controlling response-set variance.

**Key construction rules for multiple-choice items:**
1. Items must **match the ability level** of the examinees; different ability-level groups may require different tests.
2. Correct answers must NOT be placed at the same position in every item. Their positions should be **randomly distributed** across items.
3. **Moser & Price (1945)** specifically recommended that both right and wrong answers should be **randomised with respect to their position** in the set of alternatives, and they provided a method for accomplishing this.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Response bias** | Any tendency whereby a test response is altered in a way that indicates something other than the intended construct (Guilford, 1954) |
| **Response set** | A kind of response bias arising from examinees adopting systematic mental sets; tendency to give different responses than they would give if the same content were presented in forced form (Cronbach, 1950) |
| **Response style** | A response set reconceptualised as a stable, durable personality trait (Hodges, 1962) |
| **Deviation set** | Tendency to give unusual or uncommon responses to test items (Berg, 1961) |
| **Acquiescence** | Tendency to respond "Yes/True/Agree" (yea-saying) or "No/False/Disagree" (nay-saying) to all items regardless of content |
| **Faking-good** | Tendency to answer items so as to create a favourable self-impression (social desirability tendency) |
| **Faking-bad** | Tendency to answer items so as to create an unfavourable self-impression (social undesirability tendency) |
| **Social desirability tendency** | Tendency to give socially approved responses; relates to need for approval, social conformity, avoiding criticism (Crowne & Marlowe, 1964; Frederiksen, 1965) |
| **Semantics (as response set)** | Ambiguity in response-category wording (often/sometimes/seldom) that creates different interpretations across examinees, introducing systematic error |
| **Evasiveness** | Tendency to mark "?", "Uncertain," or "Indifferent" options rather than taking a directional stance |
| **Cautiousness** | Tendency to leave items unanswered when the correct answer is uncertain |
| **Extremeness** | Tendency to mark only the extreme ends of a rating scale |
| **Speed tendency** | Tendency to prioritise rapidity of response over accuracy |
| **Guessing tendency** | Tendency to guess when uncertain about the correct answer, contributing to error variance |
| **Forced-choice items** | Items requiring selection among alternatives matched for desirability, designed to reduce social desirability bias |
| **Suppressor variable** | A variable (e.g., MMPI K scale) added as a correction to adjust scores on other scales by removing a contaminating factor |
| **Pseudo variance** | Irrelevant variance in test scores introduced by response sets; reduces construct validity |
| **Yea-saying** | Consistent tendency to agree or respond "True/Yes" regardless of content |
| **Nay-saying** | Consistent tendency to disagree or respond "False/No" regardless of content |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Response bias** (Guilford, 1954) = responses altered to indicate something other than what the test measures; response set is a subset of response bias triggered by mental sets.
2. **Cronbach (1950)** defined response set as giving different responses in free vs. forced format for the same content.
3. **Deviation set** was first proposed by **Berg (1961)**; associated with projective tests (Rorschach, TAT).
4. **Acquiescence** is a continuum: yea-sayers at one end, nay-sayers at the other; most common in personality and interest inventories.
5. **Faking-good = social desirability tendency; faking-bad = social undesirability tendency.** Edwards (1957) argued faking-good is often unconscious.
6. **Crowne & Marlowe (1964):** Social desirability linked to need for social approval, conformity, and avoiding criticism.
7. **Evasiveness** had a measured reliability coefficient of **0.73** (Cronbach, 1946), confirming it is a consistent habit.
8. **Response sets become response styles** when treated as stable personality traits (Hodges, 1962); supported by Swartz (1932) and Jackson & Messick (1962).
9. **Response sets can paradoxically raise test reliability** (because they are consistent across administrations) while simultaneously reducing validity.
10. **Edwards' 9-point social desirability scale:** Ratings 4–6 = neutral; above 6 = faking-good; below 4 = faking-bad; theoretical median = 5.
11. **MMPI has four validity scales:** L (Lie/faking-good), F (Infrequency/faking-bad), K (Correction/suppressor), and ? (Cannot Say). **K scale is a suppressor variable** added to clinical scale scores.
12. **MMPI-2 added three validity scales:** Fb (Back F), **VRIN** (Variable Response Inconsistency — random responding), and **TRIN** (True Response Inconsistency — yea/nay-saying).
13. **5-point, 7-point, or 10-point scales** reduce acquiescence and evasiveness better than two-value (Yes/No) scales.
14. **EPPS** (Edwards) uses forced-choice with multiple items; **GPI** (Gordon) uses forced-choice with two items (most vs. least descriptive). Research shows forced-choice items are **not fully effective** at controlling social desirability.
15. **Moser & Price (1945):** Correct and incorrect answers in multiple-choice items should be **randomly distributed by position** to prevent positional response bias; **Nunnally (1967)** recommended factor analysis to determine optimal scoring weights for correct/incorrect/omitted responses.
