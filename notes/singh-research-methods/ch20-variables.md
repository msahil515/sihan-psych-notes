# Variables

Chapter 20 provides an exhaustive classification of variables used in behavioural research — distinguishing dependent, independent, moderator, intervening, active, attribute, qualitative, quantitative, continuous, and discrete variables — and then details five techniques for controlling extraneous variables (elimination, constancy of conditions, balancing, counterbalancing, randomisation) and strategies for managing demand characteristics.

---

## Meaning and Types of Variables

A **variable** is defined as an attribute of objects, events, things, or persons that can be measured. More formally, variables are characteristics or conditions that are manipulated, controlled, or observed by an experimenter. Examples include intelligence, anxiety, attitude, teaching method, education, achievement, sex, and age.

Variables may be viewed from three standpoints:

1. From the viewpoint of **correlation**
2. From the viewpoint of the **design of study**
3. From the viewpoint of **unit of measurement**

---

## Dependent Variables, Independent Variables, and Intervening Variables

### The Four Sets of Variables in Causal Research

From the viewpoint of causation, four sets of variables operate in a study:

| Set | Also Called | Role |
|-----|-------------|------|
| Change variables | Independent variables (IV) | Responsible for bringing about change |
| Outcome variables | Dependent variables (DV) | The result or effect of the change |
| Consequential variables | Intervening variables | Affect the link between cause and effect |
| Covarying variables | Confounding variables (Gravetter, 1993) | Necessary to complete the cause-effect relationship |

### Dependent Variable (DV)

The **dependent variable** is defined as the variable about which a prediction is made; it is the outcome or response variable. It is the variable whose value changes as a result of manipulation of the independent variable. Requirements for a good DV:

- **Sensitive**: must change clearly in response to IV changes
- **Valid**: must measure what it is intended to measure
- **Reliable**: must behave consistently across occasions

Underwood (1966, p. 12) calls the DV the **response variable**.

### Independent Variable (IV)

The **independent variable** is the variable that is manipulated, measured, or selected by the experimenter to produce observable changes in the dependent variable. Prediction about the DV is made on the basis of the IV. An occasional synonym for the IV is the **controlled variable**. Underwood (1966) calls the IV the **stimulus condition**.

#### Type I and Type II Independent Variables

| Type | Description | Research Approach |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Type I IV** | Directly and experimentally manipulated by the experimenter | Experimentation (including laboratory experimentation) |
| **Type II IV** | Manipulated only through a process of selection (not direct manipulation) | Correlational research |

*Example of Type I IV:* Temperature is divided into high, medium, and low categories and its effect on industrial production rate is studied. Temperature is directly controlled by the experimenter.

*Example of Type II IV:* Age groups (20s, 30s, 40s) are selected and compared on production rate. Age cannot be directly manipulated; it is selected.

### Confounding Variable

A **confounding variable** is a variable that is unintentionally allowed to vary with the independent variable, making it impossible to draw unambiguous causal conclusions. A study in which such a variable operates is called a **confounded experiment**. (Gravetter, 1993).

*Example:* Two teaching methods taught by two different professors — the professor identity is a confounding variable because any group difference could be due to the method or the instructor.

### Floor and Ceiling Effects

**Floor effect**: Occurs when a task is so difficult (or a scale so insensitive at the bottom) that subjects cluster at the lowest end of the scale, obscuring true differences.

**Ceiling effect**: Occurs when a task is too easy, causing scores to cluster at the top of the scale, again obscuring true differences.

Both effects are especially problematic when the DV cannot track the full range of effects of the IV.

## Task Variables

**Task variables** refer to characteristics associated with the behavioural task presented to subjects. They include physical characteristics of apparatus, task features, and procedures. Tasks vary in complexity: increasing task complexity generally increases learning probability; in complex reaction time studies, increased complexity lengthens reaction time.

---

## Environmental Variables

**Environmental variables** are characteristics of the environment not the focus of research but potentially relevant to behaviour. Examples: noise, temperature, illumination level, social context. These are not studied as IVs but are held constant (controlled). For example, illumination may be held constant while colour is varied as an IV.

---

## Subject Variables

**Subject variables** are characteristics of subjects likely to produce changes in behavioural measures: sex, age, weight, anxiety, intelligence. These are generally not the primary interest of the experimenter. They are controlled by:

- Randomisation in subject selection
- Matched selection

Subject variables can also be used as Type II independent variables (e.g., comparing male vs. female performance).

### Relevant Variables (Extraneous Variables)

**Relevant variables** (also called **extraneous variables** or **control variables**) are variables that are not the focus of research but are likely to influence the DV. They are of three types:

1. **Subject relevant variables (Type-S)**: Characteristics of subjects (age, sex, intelligence, aptitude, personality). Controlled through selection, matching, or random assignment.

2. **Stimulus relevant variables (Type-E)**: Part of the experimental context, such as task complexity. Directly manipulated by the experimenter.

3. **Instrument/Response relevant variables**: Conditions that influence the measuring instruments or response, such as practice and fatigue. Controlled by counterbalancing design (D'Amato, 1970).

---

## Qualitative Variables and Quantitative Variables

### Qualitative Variables

**Qualitative variables** consist of categories that cannot be ranked in terms of magnitude. No quantitative relationship exists among their categories. They correspond to **nominal and ordinal scales**. Precise and accurate measurement is not possible; therefore, they are least preferred in scientific investigation.

### Quantitative Variables

**Quantitative variables** are those whose categories can be ordered or ranked according to magnitude. Examples: intelligence, age, height, weight, achievement. They correspond to **interval and ratio scales**. Precise numerical measurement along a continuum is possible. These are preferred in psychological and educational research.

---

## Continuous Variables and Discrete Variables

### Continuous Variable

A **continuous variable** is one capable of being measured in an infinitely precise manner; it can take any value over a defined range. Measured on interval or ratio scales. Examples: age, height, intelligence, reaction time.

- Age can be expressed in years, months, days, hours, minutes, seconds, or even smaller units.
- Any variable that can take intermediate fractional values is continuous.

### Discrete Variable

A **discrete variable** takes only whole number values and cannot be expressed in intermediate fractions. Values are determined by counting (D'Amato, 1970).

Examples: number of members in a family, number of students in a class (can be 35 or 36, never 35.5), number of books in a library, number of females in a district.

> In most psychological and educational research, both IVs and DVs are quantitative and continuous.

---

## Moderator Variables and Intervening Variables

### Moderator Variable

The **moderator variable** is a special type of independent variable (also called the **secondary independent variable**) selected by the experimenter because it is presumed to affect the relationship between the primary (main) IV and the DV.

*Definition:* Variables manipulated or measured because of their probable moderating relationship between the IV and DV.

*Example:* Studying lecture vs. demonstration teaching methods on science achievement. If the experimenter suspects that intelligence level moderates this relationship, students are subdivided into high, average, and low intelligence subgroups under each teaching method, yielding 6 subgroups. Intelligence is the moderator variable.

### Intervening Variable

The **intervening variable** is a variable that theoretically exists and influences the behavioural measure; it cannot be directly seen and can only be inferred. It is influenced by the IV and moderator variable and in turn affects the DV.

*Identification question (Kerlinger, 1978):* "What is it about the independent variable that will cause the predicted outcome?"

*Example:* IV = whether subjects can reach a goal; DV = number of aggressive acts; Intervening variable = frustration (cannot be seen, only inferred).

---

## Active Variables and Attribute Variables

### Active Variable

An **active variable** (also called an **experimental variable**) is a variable that can be directly manipulated by the experimenter. It can be assigned to participants by the researcher.

### Attribute Variable

An **attribute variable** (also called an **organismic variable**) is a pre-existing characteristic of the subject that the experimenter selects/measures but cannot directly manipulate. Examples: sex, age, intelligence level, personality traits.

*Example of attribute variable use:* Comparing ten-year-old boys vs. girls on numerical ability — sex is the attribute variable.

> Some variables can function as both: anxiety can be manipulated by instruction (active variable) or measured with a scale (attribute/organismic variable).

---

## Difference Between a Variable and a Concept

| Concept | Variable |
|---------|----------|
| An abstraction from observed events; a quality that varies markedly from person to person | Measurable with varying degrees of accuracy |
| Cannot easily be used in scientific research | Can be used in scientific research because it can be measured |
| Examples: honesty, courage | Examples: age, gender, attitude, intelligence |

**Measurability** is the main criterion of the difference between a concept and a variable. Concepts should be transformed into measurable form (operationalised as variables) for scientific use.

## Methods of Measuring Dependent Variables

Researchers use the following four approaches for measuring DVs:

### 1. Direct Observation of Targeted Behaviour

The DV involves direct observation and measurement of a specific behavioural response.

- Schachter (1968): Measured amount of food eaten by obese vs. normal subjects (eating behaviour).
- Latane and Darley (1968): Measured whether subjects would help others in bystander influence studies.

### 2. Indirect Measures of Unseen Internal Processes

When the behaviour cannot be directly observed, indirect (correlated) observable responses are used. The specific measure depends on the underlying theory.

- **Physiological measurements** (heart rate, breathing, blood pressure, perspiration) to infer anxiety/stress.
- **Duration of infant gaze** or smiling to infer positive emotional state (used with young children who cannot verbally report).
- **Reaction time** (Clark & Lockhart, 1972): Time a subject takes to respond to a stimulus; used to test cognitive processes of attention and memory.
  - Sternberg (1969): Measured reaction time to a probe word that was or was not part of a previously presented list, to study how subjects search memory.

### 3. Judgements About a Stimulus

Subjects make judgements about a stimulus; it is assumed judgements change as a function of experimental conditions.

- **Forced-choice techniques**: Subjects indicate position of an item from few options.
- Stuart and Day (1988): Manipulated visual stimulus characteristics; subjects reported whether they perceived a visual illusion.
- **Temporal judgements**: Subjects estimate duration of a time period after certain activities.
- Block (1989): Subjects drew a line to indicate perceived duration of a time interval (non-verbal method).

### 4. Self-Reports

Subjects provide descriptions of their own feelings, ideas, or thoughts, commonly via rating scales.

- Schwartz and Clore (1983): Manipulated subject health descriptions and asked subjects to report mood level and causes.
- Brandstatter and Frants (1973): Rating scales used to measure attitudes, perceptions, or confidence in memory.

## Important Considerations in Selection of Variables

Tuckman (1978) recommended three considerations for selecting variables:

### 1. Theoretical Considerations
- Evaluate the degree to which the IV produces changes in the DV.
- Consider frequency of interaction.
- Variable must be related to the theoretical framework being used.

### 2. Design Considerations
- The selected variable must fulfil the basic requirements of the experimental design.
- The IV must be manipulable within the experimental design's framework.

### 3. Practical Considerations
- Limit the number of variables (too many cannot be studied simultaneously).
- Consider available financial resources.
- Consider time constraints.
- Consider the nature of the variable (some are easier to study than others).
- Consider the degree of control the researcher can maintain over the experimental situation.

## Important Approaches to Manipulating Independent Variables

Seven approaches to manipulating IVs:

### (i) By Manipulating Context
The researcher manipulates the context in which the stimulus is presented.
- Inducing different moods via success/failure stories, or hypnosis, then examining effects on memory and thought.

### (ii) By Presenting Different Stimuli
Changing characteristics of a stimulus or presenting different stimuli as the IV.
- Green (1978): Exposed subjects to films depicting different levels of violence; measured subsequent aggression.
- Campbell and Harren (1978): Examined the effect of various furniture arrangements on social interactions.

### (iii) By Manipulating Instructions/Information Given to Subjects
The IV consists of different instructions or information.
- Craik and Lockhart (1975): Different information in questions about a word list influenced encoding.
- Gardner and Coleco (1971): Told subjects a painting's supposed artist was male or female; measured sex-biased evaluation.

### (iv) By Manipulating Social Settings Using Confederates
A **confederate** is a person instructed by the researcher to act in a pre-arranged fashion.
- Asch (1951): Confederates exerted group pressure on a subject in a line-matching task to demonstrate conformity.

### (v) By Manipulating Intervening Variables
**Intervening variables** are internal states mediating IV-DV relationships. Manipulating the IV creates an internal state (intervening variable) that then affects the DV.

Distinction between:
- **State characteristic**: Temporary, situationally influenced (e.g., state anxiety).
- **Trait characteristic**: Stable over time, less influenced by situational factors (e.g., self-esteem). Trait characteristics can still be influenced by experimental manipulations.

*Example:* Frustrating subjects by blocking a reward induces anger (intervening variable) which leads to aggression (DV).

### (vi) By Stressing or Overloading the Psychological System
Researchers manipulate the IV by stressing the subject's psychological system.
- Steelman and Gorkin (1980): Subjects produced a dissonant behaviour and had to resolve the conflict.
- Cray and VanRickhem (1960): Attention study — different messages presented simultaneously to each ear; IV = varied distractions/message content.

### (vii) By Mutilating or Removing Parts of Organs
Physiological manipulation through lesioning, ablation, or electrical stimulation.
- Tokonaga et al. (1986): Brain lesion studies examining effects on motivation, sexual drive, emotions.

## Techniques of Controlling Extraneous Variables

**Extraneous variables** operate alongside the IV and affect the DV. If not controlled, the result is a **confounded experiment**. Five techniques of control:

### 1. Technique of Elimination

The simplest method: eliminate the extraneous variable from the experimental situation entirely.
- Example: If noise is an extraneous variable, make the room soundproof.
- Limitation: Cannot eliminate variables like age, sex, or intelligence.

### 2. Constancy of Conditions

Hold the value of extraneous variables constant across all participants and conditions.
- Time of day held constant for all participants.
- Same lighting conditions, same apparatus for all.
- When variables like sex or intelligence cannot be varied, use only subjects alike on those variables (e.g., only males eliminates the effect of sex).

### 3. Balancing

Used when:
- (a) The researcher cannot identify the extraneous variables, OR
- (b) Extraneous variables are identified and special steps are taken to control them.

**Situation (a):** Same treatment to all, with a control group receiving no treatment. Extraneous variables affect both groups equally, so their effects cancel out in the comparison. Only the IV differentially affects the two groups (see Fig. 20.1).

**Situation (b):** Extraneous variable is known (e.g., sex). Assign equal numbers of males and females to each group, balancing the effect of sex.

*Key assumption of balancing:* Participants in experimental and control groups are initially equivalent and differ only in treatment.

### 4. Counterbalancing

Used to control practice and fatigue effects in **within-subjects designs** (where each subject serves under two or more conditions).

**General principle of counterbalancing:**
- Each condition must be presented to each subject an equal number of times.
- Each condition must occur an equal number of times at each practice session.
- Each condition must precede and follow all other conditions an equal number of times.

**Symmetrical transfer**: Transfer from A to B = Transfer from B to A (no differential transfer). Groups can be combined and compared.

**Asymmetrical transfer**: Transfer from A to B ≠ Transfer from B to A. Counterbalancing is used specifically to control asymmetrical transfer. In a perfectly counterbalanced design, transfer becomes symmetrical.

*Table 20.1 — Symmetrical vs. Asymmetrical Transfer:*

| | Symmetrical Transfer | | | Asymmetrical Transfer | | |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | A | B | Difference | A | B | Difference |
| One half of subjects | 6 | 9 | 3 | 6 | 9 | 3 |
| Other half of subjects | 4 | 7 | 3 | 4 | 9 | 5 |

In symmetrical: difference = 3 for both halves (can combine groups).
In asymmetrical: differences are 3 and 5 (cannot combine groups legitimately without counterbalancing).

**Difference between Balancing and Counterbalancing:**

| | Balancing | Counterbalancing |
|---|---|---|
| Subject assignment | Each subject receives only ONE treatment | Each subject receives MORE THAN ONE treatment |
| Purpose | Cancel effects of extraneous variables across groups | Equalize practice/fatigue effects across conditions |
| Design type | Between-subjects | Within-subjects |

### 5. Randomisation

**Randomisation** refers to a technique in which each member of the population has an equal and independent chance of being selected or assigned.

Used when:
- Extraneous variables are difficult to identify, OR
- Extraneous variables are known but their effects cannot be controlled by other techniques.

**Function:** Randomly distributes extraneous effects across experimental and control conditions. Even unidentified extraneous variables are equitably distributed when subjects are randomly assigned.

*Example:* Comparing reading ability under subdued vs. full lighting. If subjects are randomly assigned to morning/afternoon conditions, on average lighting affects both groups equally, eliminating confounding due to lighting.

> Of all five techniques, **randomisation, balancing, and counterbalancing** are the relatively more popular.

## Controlling Demand Characteristics

**Demand characteristics** are defined as cues provided by the research context that guide or bias a subject's behaviour in a particular direction. Subjects use demand characteristics to answer: What am I supposed to do? What is really going on here? What will my response mean?

### Four Components of Demand Characteristics

1. **Subject cues**: Subjects' own attitudes, biases, and motivations influence how they respond. Some subjects are compliant, some apprehensive, some antagonistic.

2. **Environment**: The physical and social setting of the study can distract subjects or cause self-conscious reactions. Environmental interruptions or changes lead subjects to guess what is being tested.

3. **Measurement procedure**: Task difficulty or specific procedures cue subjects to expected responses. Example (Campbell and Stanley, 1963): Subjects estimating mental travel time for distant vs. near map locations may "oblige" the experimenter by reporting longer times for greater distances.

4. **Experimenter**: The experimenter's dress, behaviour, voice, mannerism, and expectations create demand characteristics. The experimenter, knowing the study's prediction, may unconsciously communicate cues to subjects.

### Impact of Demand Characteristics

- **Loss of internal validity**: Subjects behave in line with demand cues rather than solely due to the IV, making it appear the manipulation worked when it is actually the demand characteristics driving the response (or vice versa).
- **Loss of external validity**: Results obtained under specific demand characteristics may not generalise to situations with different demand characteristics.
- **Replication problems**: Subjects reacting consistently to ubiquitous demand cues introduces systematic inconsistency.

### Strategies for Controlling Demand Characteristics

#### General Strategies (Three Strategies)

1. **Provide as few cues as possible**: Do not divulge the specific purpose; avoid extraneous distracting information; avoid revealing or suspicious experimenter behaviours; hide threatening equipment.

2. **Maximise neutral presentation**: Experimenter is neither friendly nor unfriendly (neutral). Does not imply the task is easy or difficult. Does not indicate what a "normal" response is.

3. **Seek experimental realism**: Design the procedure and task to be psychologically engaging so subjects focus on the task, not on demand cues. Define the problem in a credible and interesting way.

#### Deception

**Deception** refers to the creation of an artificial situation or cover story (fictitious situation). If subjects are persuaded to believe a fictitious scenario, they are unaware of the true IV and do not feel pressured to respond predictably. Ethically controversial but one of few options for strong demand characteristics.

*Orne (1962)*: Demonstrated an experiment in which demand characteristics were purposely manipulated by attaching brain-activity measuring devices to subjects and telling them posture was critical for accurate measurement.

#### Placebo

A **placebo** is a substance or condition presented to the control group that resembles the experimental treatment but lacks the active ingredient.

*Example*: Studying alcohol's effect on aggression — the control group receives a beverage that smells and tastes like alcohol but contains no alcohol. If both groups show the same aggression, it rules out demand characteristics. If the experimental (real alcohol) group shows more aggression, the difference is attributable to alcohol itself, not to the demand that receiving alcohol implies aggressive behaviour.

Placebo ensures demand characteristics are equalised across experimental and control groups.

#### Single-Blind and Double-Blind Procedures

- **Single-blind procedure**: Subjects are unaware of the specific experimental condition they are in; controls subject demand characteristics.
- **Double-blind procedure**: Both subjects and the experimenter are unaware of condition assignment; controls both subject and experimenter demand characteristics.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Underwood (1966)** termed IV as "stimulus conditions" and DV as "response variables" — a specific attribution frequently tested.

2. **Type I IV** = directly manipulated (experimentation); **Type II IV** = manipulated through selection (correlational research) — confusing these is a common MCQ trap.

3. **Moderator variable** is also called the **secondary independent variable**; it is distinguished from the intervening variable by being manipulable/measurable (not just inferred).

4. **Intervening variable** cannot be directly observed; it is only **inferred**. Kerlinger's (1978) identification question: "What is it about the IV that will cause the predicted outcome?"

5. **Attribute variable = organismic variable**: a pre-existing subject characteristic (sex, age) selected but not directly manipulated. Anxiety can be either active or attribute depending on whether it is induced by instruction or measured by scale.

6. **Qualitative variables = nominal/ordinal scales**; **quantitative variables = interval/ratio scales**. Continuous variables use interval/ratio scales; discrete variables are determined by counting (whole numbers only).

7. **Counterbalancing** addresses **practice and fatigue effects in within-subjects designs**; **balancing** addresses extraneous variables in **between-subjects designs** (each subject gets only one treatment).

8. **Asymmetrical transfer** = when transfer from A→B differs from B→A. Counterbalancing controls asymmetrical (not symmetrical) transfer. A perfectly counterbalanced design makes transfer symmetrical.

9. **Randomisation** is the technique used when extraneous variables are **unknown or unidentifiable** — it distributes their effects equally across conditions by chance.

10. **Demand characteristics (Orne)** have four components: subject cues, environment, measurement procedure, and experimenter expectations. They threaten both **internal and external validity**.

11. **Placebo** controls demand characteristics by ensuring control group experiences the same contextual cues (smell, taste of "alcohol") as the experimental group, without the active ingredient.

12. **Single-blind** = only subjects are unaware; **double-blind** = both subjects and experimenter are unaware. Double-blind controls experimenter expectancy effects.

13. **Floor effect**: scores cluster at the bottom (task too hard or scale ceiling too low). **Ceiling effect**: scores cluster at the top (task too easy). Both obscure true differences in the DV.

14. **Three strategies for controlling demand characteristics**: (a) provide minimal cues, (b) maintain experimenter neutrality, (c) maximise experimental realism (engage subjects psychologically with the task).

15. **Measurability** is the key criterion distinguishing a **variable** from a **concept**. Concepts (honesty, courage) cannot be directly measured; variables (age, anxiety, intelligence) can. Concepts must be operationalised into variables for scientific use.
