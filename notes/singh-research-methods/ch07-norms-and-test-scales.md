# Norms and Test Scales

Chapter 7 establishes the conceptual and practical framework for interpreting raw test scores by situating them within reference systems (norms or criteria). It covers the two fundamental interpretive frameworks (norm-referencing and criterion-referencing), the procedures for constructing norms, all major norm types and their associated standard-score systems, local and subgroup norms, criteria for evaluating norm quality, and the growing role of computers in psychological assessment.

---

## Meaning of Norm-Referencing and Criterion-Referencing

### Why Norms are Needed

A raw score in isolation is meaningless. A score of 50 on an arithmetic test or 30 on a history test carries no interpretive value until it is placed within a reference framework. Two broad frameworks exist for this purpose: **norm-referencing** and **criterion-referencing**.

### Norm-Referenced Testing

**Norm-referencing** is the process of comparing an individual's test score with the scores of a specific, defined group of examinees on that same test. The reference group is the **standardization sample**, defined as a sample that is a true representative cross-section of the target population, collected under controlled conditions, and used as the baseline against which individual scores are evaluated.

- The primary purpose of a norm-referenced test (NRT) is to classify and rank individuals from highest to lowest along a continuum.
- Scores are expressed as **derived scores** (standardized score, percentile score, or other converted forms), not raw scores.
- Applications: selection from a larger group, placement into remedial or gifted programs, ranking candidates.

**Derived scores** are scores obtained by converting raw scores so that they (a) allow direct comparison of an individual's performance against the standardization sample, and (b) permit comparison across different tests that may use different raw-score units.

Two purposes of derived scores:
1. Provide a common baseline for comparing raw performance against the norm group.
2. Express the individual's relative position within the standardization sample.

### Criterion-Referenced Testing

**Criterion-referenced testing** (Glass, 1963) is the process of establishing a fixed, external performance standard or criterion and comparing the examinee's score against that standard, not against other examinees.

- If an examinee correctly performs the required proportion of items, the inference is that the person has attained the pre-specified mastery standard.
- Performance is interpreted in absolute terms (what the examinee can or cannot do), not in relative terms.
- Emphasis is on **mastery** (or minimum competency) of specific skills.

Key features of a criterion-referenced test (CRT):
1. Based on a defined set of behavioral objectives it intends to measure.
2. Results in absolute scores interpreted in behavioral or performance terms.
3. Performance is not compared against other examinees; all examinees could theoretically exceed or all fall below the standard.
4. The critical factor is performance relative to what the examinee can do.

A test is NOT automatically a CRT simply because it has no norms. The defining feature is comparison to a predetermined behavioral criterion, not merely the absence of a norm table.

### Differences between NRT and CRT

| Dimension | Criterion-Referenced Test (CRT) | Norm-Referenced Test (NRT) |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Compare performance to a predetermined criterion/standard | Compare examinees' performance with each other |
| Item domain | Narrow domain of skills with direct real-world relevance | Broad domain of skills with indirect relevance |
| Score expression | Percentage correct with a pre-determined cut-off score | Standard score, percentile, or grade equivalent |
| Item difficulty | Most items are of average difficulty | Items vary widely in difficulty level |
| Item construction | Items selected based on difficulty level, correlation with total score, and other properties | Items constructed to maximally discriminate among respondents along the measured trait |
| Focus | Mastery of specific competencies | Relative standing in the group |

## Steps in Developing Norms

Developing norms is a complex process. Three principal steps are outlined:

### Step 1: Defining the Target Population

The first step is to define the composition of the target group for whom the test is intended. The intended use of the test determines who the target population is.

Example: For the **Test of English as a Foreign Language (TOEFL)**, the target population consists of students for whom English is a foreign language and who intend to study abroad where instruction is in English. Including PhD candidates where the test is intended for Class XII students would be an example of inappropriate population definition.

### Step 2: Selecting the Sample from the Target Population

A **representative sample** must be drawn from the target population. A **cross-sectional representation** is required, meaning individuals from all relevant sections of the population must be represented. General principles:

- Larger samples are better.
- **Random sampling** is theoretically the best method for constructing norms, but is rarely employed in practice.
- **Cluster sampling** (or a variation thereof) is generally preferred for constructing norms because it is more practical for large-scale data collection.

### Step 3: Standardizing Conditions for Test Administration

Without standardized administration conditions, valid comparisons of individual scores to norms are impossible. Conditions that must be controlled include:

- Adequate, soundproof testing environment
- Appropriate furniture arrangement
- Strict adherence to time limits
- Test security
- Adherence to test manual directions
- Ensuring examinees work on correct test sections

Without standardized conditions, norms cannot function as a valid comparative device.

| Age scores | Age norms |
| Grade scores | Grade norms |
| Percentile scores | Percentile norms |
| Standard scores | Standard-score norms |

## Age-Equivalent Norms

**Age-equivalent norms** are defined as the average performance of a representative sample of a certain age level on a measure of a given trait or ability. The age equivalent of a raw score is the age at which that raw score is the average performance.

Example: If the average weight of a representative sample of 10-year-old girls in Bihar is 32 kg, then 32 kg constitutes the age norm for weight at age 10 for that group.

### When Age Norms are Appropriate

Age norms are most appropriate for traits and abilities that show systematic, progressive increase with age, particularly:
- Physical traits: height, weight
- Cognitive abilities: general intelligence (especially during childhood and adolescence)
- Best applied at the **elementary level**

### Limitations of Age Norms

1. **Unequal units across the developmental span.** Growth in cognitive abilities (e.g., intelligence) is not uniform across age levels. Growth from age 1 to 3 is greater than growth from age 14 to 15. This unequal growth rate produces an inherently unequal measurement scale. Even within a specific age level, the rate of growth varies substantially among children, undermining the assumption of uniform age units.

2. **Non-comparability across traits.** Different traits have different growth trajectories. Progress in music learning typically stops after adolescence, while vocabulary growth may continue well into adulthood. Age norms for these two traits are therefore not comparable with each other.

3. **Not suitable for non-progressive traits.** Traits like **personality** do not exhibit regular progressive change with age and therefore cannot be meaningfully represented by age norms. Many other personality traits share this limitation.

---

## Grade-Equivalent Norms

**Grade-equivalent norms** are defined as the average performance of a representative sample at each grade level on a given test. The grade equivalent of a score indicates the grade level at which that score represents the average performance.

### How Grade Equivalents are Constructed

1. Representative samples are selected from each of several grade levels.
2. The average score is computed for each grade.
3. For scores falling between two grade-level averages, the grade equivalent is determined by **interpolation**.

Example: If the average number of arithmetic items answered correctly by sixth-grade students is 30, then a raw score of 30 yields a grade equivalent of 6.0. A student reading at grade 6 level is said to be reading at the 6th-grade level.

### Limitations of Grade-Equivalent Norms

1. **Non-comparability across subjects.** Grade equivalents from different subjects on the same test are not comparable. Social studies performance depends heavily on exposure and prior knowledge, whereas arithmetic performance depends primarily on formal classroom instruction. Therefore, a grade equivalent of 7.0 in social studies is not comparable to 7.0 in arithmetic for the same student.

2. **Assumption of uniform prior experience.** Grade norms assume all students at a given grade have roughly similar prior learning experiences. This assumption may hold in early elementary grades but fails in higher grades where students' experiences diverge significantly.

3. **Non-linear growth rate.** In many academic domains, growth is rapid in early years and slows gradually, so the meaning of one grade unit differs across the scale.

Despite these limitations, grade-equivalent norms are widely used, particularly in achievement tests and educational measurement. They are also used in some intelligence tests.

## Percentile Norms (Percentile-Rank Norms)

### Definitions of Related Terms

**Percentile** (also called **centile**): A point on a scale that divides a distribution into 100 equal parts. A percentile is a point, not a range. It indicates the rank of a score expressed as the percentage of cases in the distribution that scored at or below that score.

- Example: If Mohan scores 65 on an achievement test and 70% of the standardization sample scored at or below 65, then Mohan's **percentile rank (PR)** is 70, and his score is at the 70th percentile (P70).

**Decile**: A point that divides a distribution into 10 equal parts. The 5th decile (D5) is equivalent to the 50th percentile (P50).

**Quartile**: A point that divides a distribution into 4 equal parts. There are 3 quartiles:
- Q1 = 25th percentile (P25)
- Q2 = 50th percentile (P50) = median
- Q3 = 75th percentile (P75)

### Percentile Norms

**Percentile norms** indicate, for each raw score, the percentage of the standardization sample that falls at or below that raw score. They show the individual's standing relative to the normative group.

For percentile norms to be meaningful, the norm group must be:
- Fairly large
- Homogeneous with respect to relevant characteristics (age, grade, sex, occupation)
- If heterogeneous, separate percentile norm tables should be maintained for each subgroup

Percentile norms are sometimes reported through an **ogive**, a graph that plots cumulative percentage against raw scores. The ogive allows a person to read their raw score on the X-axis and directly identify the corresponding percentile rank.

### Advantages of Percentile Norms

- Easy to calculate
- Easy to understand
- Accessible to almost any user, including those without statistical training

### Limitations of Percentile Norms

1. **Confusion with percentage scores.** Laymen and even skilled practitioners sometimes confuse percentile with percentage of items correct. These are entirely different:
   - **Percentile**: a derived score expressing the percentage of persons scoring below a given score
   - **Percentage correct**: a raw score expressing the percentage of items answered correctly

2. **Inequality of units (the critical limitation).** In a normal distribution, raw scores cluster around the mean with fewer at the extremes. This means:
   - A small raw score difference near the middle of the distribution (around P50) produces a large change in percentile rank.
   - The same raw score difference at the extremes (e.g., near P2 or P98) produces a very small change in percentile rank.
   - Percentile units are therefore systematically **unequal** across the scale.
   - This non-linear mapping means percentile ranks are **highly affected by monotonic transformation** and cannot be treated as an interval scale.
   - Arithmetic operations (averaging, subtracting) on percentile scores are therefore inappropriate.

## Standard Score Norms

A **standard score norm** is a norm based on standard scores. The major advantage of standard score norms over percentile norms is that **units of the scale are equal** throughout the entire range, removing the critical inequality-of-units problem.

### Standard Score (z-Score)

A **standard score** is the number of standard deviation units a raw score is above or below the mean of its distribution. The most fundamental standard score is the **z-score** (also called the sigma score).

**Definition:** A z-score indicates how many standard deviations a score is above or below the mean of the distribution.

**Formula (Equation 7.1):**

```
z = (X - M) / σ
```

Where:
- z = z-score (sigma score)
- X = raw score
- M = mean of the distribution
- σ = standard deviation of the distribution

**Properties of z-score distribution:**
- Mean = 0
- Standard deviation = 1

**Worked examples:**

- Mohan scores 60; M = 30, SD = 15: z = (60 - 30)/15 = 30/15 = **+2.0** (2 SDs above the mean)
- Another person scores 20: z = (20 - 30)/15 = -10/15 = **-0.67** (0.67 SDs below the mean)
- A person scoring exactly 30 (the mean): z = (30 - 30)/15 = **0**

**Primary use:** Comparing scores from two different distributions. Direct comparison of raw scores from different tests is meaningless without first converting to a common unit. Z-score conversion enables such comparison.

Example: If Bobby scores 60 in psychology (M = 48, SD = 4), z = (60-48)/4 = **+3.0**. In geography (details in text), z = +4.1. Bobby performed better in geography relative to the class, despite different raw scores.

### Limitation of z-Scores

Z-scores involve small decimal numbers with positive and negative signs, which are cumbersome to work with. Various derived standard scores overcome this by multiplying z by a constant and adding a constant to eliminate negative values.

### Linear vs. Normalized Transformations

**Linear transformation:** When raw scores are converted to standard scores through a linear function (e.g., z-score), all characteristics of the original distribution are preserved. The shape of the distribution does not change. This means if the original distribution is skewed, the standard scores will also be skewed.

**Normalized standard scores:** When raw score distributions are non-normal, a normalized transformation adjusts the distribution to approximate a normal curve. **Marshall and Hale** define normalized standard scores as scores "adjusted to produce a normal distribution." These are standard scores derived from a normalized distribution with mean = 0 and SD = 1.

The three most commonly used normalized standard scores are:
1. T scores
2. Stanine scores
3. Deviation IQ scores

## McCall's T Score

**T score** was devised by **McCall (1922)** and named after **L.L. Thurstone**. It is a normalized standard score based on a mean of 50 and standard deviation of 10, drawn from the shape of the normal distribution curve.

**Formula:**

```
T = 50 + 10z
```

Where z is the z-score computed from Equation 7.1.

**Properties:**
- Mean = 50
- SD = 10
- Range in most distributions: 20 to 80 (representing −3 to +3 SDs from mean)
- The T-scale extends from −5 to +5 standard deviations

**Conversion table example (from Table 7.1):**

| Raw Score (X) | z-score | T score |
|---|---|---|
| 7 | +2 | 70 |
| 6 | +1 | 60 |
| 5 | 0 | 50 |
| 4 | -1 | 40 |
| 3 | -2 | 30 |
(M = 5, SD = 1 for this example)

**Use:** T scores are used to compare performances across different tests on a common scale by converting all scores to the same mean and SD. When raw scores are grouped into a frequency distribution, conversion tables to T scores are available.

---

## Stanine Scores

**Stanine scores** were developed by the **United States Air Force during World War II**. The term is a contraction of **"standard nine"**. Stanine scores range from **1 to 9** in single-digit integers.

**Properties:**
- Mean = 5
- SD ≈ 2
- Range: 1 to 9

A variation of the stanine scale was devised by **Canfield (1951)**, which includes 10 units above the mean and 5 units below.

### Conversion of Raw Scores to Stanines

Raw scores are arranged in order and assigned to stanine categories based on the following normal-curve proportions:

| Stanine | Percentage of cases |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4% |
| 2 | 7% |
| 3 | 12% |
| 4 | 17% |
| 5 | 20% |
| 6 | 17% |
| 7 | 12% |
| 8 | 7% |
| 9 | 4% |

**Mnemonic for proportions:** 4-7-12-17-20-17-12-7-4 (symmetric around stanine 5).

**Worked example:** With 300 students:
- Lowest 12 scores (4% of 300) = Stanine 1
- Next 21 scores (7% of 300) = Stanine 2
- And so on upward

**Advantage:** Single-digit scores are computationally convenient, especially for computer analysis. When combined scores on a C-scale, the distribution is normal.

## Deviation IQ Score

**Deviation IQ** is a normalized standard score with:
- Mean = **100**
- Standard deviation = typically **15** (but ranges from 12 to 20 depending on the intelligence test used)

**Key distinction from Ratio IQ:**
- The **ratio IQ** (traditional IQ) = (Mental Age / Chronological Age) × 100
- The **deviation IQ** is a normalized standard score, not a true ratio; it does not involve mental age
- Many experts have objected to the term "deviation IQ" as misleading because it is calculated differently from ratio IQ but uses the same name and scale
- The term is retained to maintain continuity with the historically familiar IQ concept

**The first test to use deviation IQ was the Wechsler Intelligence Scale**, which used a mean of 100 and SD of 15. The Binet scales subsequently adopted the deviation IQ to circumvent problems with the ratio IQ.

**Interpretation example (with SD = 15):**
- Deviation IQ of 115 = +1 SD above the mean
- Deviation IQ of 85 = −1 SD below the mean (84.13th percentile from below)

**Interpretation example (with SD = 12):**
- Deviation IQ of 112 = +1.0 SD above the mean
- Deviation IQ of 84 = −1.33 SD below the mean

**Important note:** When SD differs across intelligence tests, the same deviation IQ score represents a different relative standing, so caution is required when comparing IQ scores from different tests.

### Comparison of Standard Score Systems

| Score Type | Mean | SD | Developer/Origin | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| z-score | 0 | 1 | Statistical convention | Linear transform; decimals with +/- signs |
| T score | 50 | 10 | McCall (1922), named after Thurstone | Normalized; range 20-80 |
| Stanine | 5 | ~2 | US Air Force, WWII | Range 1-9; single digits |
| Deviation IQ | 100 | 15 (typically) | Wechsler (first use) | Normalized; range 12-20 SD by test |

## Local and Subgroup Norms

### Local Norms

**Local norms** are norms derived from a representative sample of local examinees rather than from a national sample. They represent performance within a specific geographic or institutional context.

Example: Norms derived from Hindi-speaking people of Bihar only (rather than Hindi-speaking people of all of India) constitute local norms for that specific community.

### Subgroup Norms

**Subgroup norms** are norms derived from scores obtained from a specific subgroup defined by variables such as:
- Sex
- Ethnic background
- Geographic region
- Urban vs. rural environment
- Socioeconomic status
- Any other relevant subgroup variable

### When to Use Each Type

The choice between national norms and local/subgroup norms depends entirely on the purpose of testing:

- **National norms** are useful when the goal is to identify a child's competence relative to the national pool (e.g., selecting students for national-level gifted programs).
- **Subgroup/local norms** are more effective for predicting competence in relation to a specific school's instructional program or a particular community context.

## Computer Applications in Psychological Testing and Assessment

The use of computers in psychological testing and assessment is a relatively recent development that has fundamentally expanded the capabilities of assessment.

### Historical Background

Early impetus came from research on **adaptive testing** and **computerized backward masking procedures**, in which a target stimulus is followed closely by a masking stimulus. These procedures were used to measure the speed, capacity, or efficiency of information processing.

Key researchers:
- **Jenkinson (1982)** and **Nettelbeck (1982)**: Applied psychophysical approaches to ability testing
- **Saccuzzo and Braff (1981)**; **Rimland and Munsinger (1977)**: Used backward masking to measure information-processing speed
- Findings: Individuals differing in psychometric intelligence show different information-processing speeds
- **Sternberg (1985)** and **Campione (1989)**: Used similar response-time and backward masking tasks via personal computers
- **Saccuzzo and Larson (1987)**; **Saccuzzo, Larson, and Rimland (1986)**: Extended these applications

### Major Applications of Computers (Farrell, 1992)

**Farrell (1992)** identified nine major applications of computers to psychological assessment:

1. Presenting test stimuli
2. Professional consultation
3. Scoring
4. Collecting self-report data
5. Directly recording behaviors
6. Coding observational data
7. Helping individuals interpret test performance
8. Storing and managing assessment data
9. Providing individual assessment data and facilitating decision-making

### Levels of Computerized Testing

1. **Basic level:** Group-administration tests adapted for computer-based individual administration. Computer disks allow test users to score tests on their own systems. Example: **MMPI** (computer-scored).

2. **Advanced level:** Narrative computer interpretation of test scores (Moreland, 1987). Computer programs generate explanatory narratives based on particular patterns of test responses. These assist clinical experts in interpretation. Test users can obtain diagnostic and interpretive printouts about the test taker.

3. **Interactive level:** Individualized interpretation through interactive response patterns. The examinee communicates with the computer in a dual interaction mode. Test scores are combined with other information (academic background, occupational history, medical history) to assist individual decision-making. Example: **SIGI** (System for Interactive Guidance Information).

### Computer Assessment in Specialized Areas

- **Clinical neuropsychology**: Computers allow recording and evaluation of time-response parameters (e.g., reaction timing) that cannot be measured by hand.
- **Attention disorders and learning disabilities**: New assessment tools enabled (Gokali and Couch, 1992).
- **Virtual reality**: Computer technologies have led to the development of virtual reality as an assessment tool.
- **Cognitive screening**: Newly developed computer-administered batteries screen for signs of cognitive impairment and neurological dysfunction.

### Reliability and Validity of Computer-Based Testing

**Lee, Moreno, and Sympson (1986)**: Reported that computer-based presentation and scoring does not appear to affect the reliability or validity of test scores.

**Butcher (1987)** and **Matarazzo (1986)**: Warned that certain computer applications may lead to misuse and inaccurate test interpretation.

**Mazzeo et al. (1992)**: When a test is administered in both computerized and traditional printed modes, comparability of scores must be carefully investigated, as reliability and validity may vary.

**APA recommendation:** Specific norms should be developed for computer-based test scores. The American Psychological Association developed guidelines for computer-based testing formats ("The Testing Standards").

### Obstacles to Widespread Adoption

1. Resistance from clinicians trained in traditional psychotherapy procedures (Matarazzo, 1986; Moreland, 1992)
2. Vendors' reluctance to make products available for independent review
3. Many clinicians lack the quantitative skills needed to combine clinical and statistical procedures
4. Information needed to evaluate specific interpretive systems is often unavailable due to proprietary concerns

## Criteria for Good Test Norms

**Anastasi (1976)** and **Aiken (1997)** identified the following criteria that good test norms must satisfy:

### 1. Relevance

Norms must be relevant and meaningful to the types of persons for whom the test is intended. Norms based on a rural sample may not be appropriate for an urban population. National norms may be too broad when the purpose requires fine differentiation (e.g., selecting students for a specific accelerated academic program). Specialized norms for specific subgroups are preferable in such cases.

### 2. Currency (Up to Date)

Norms must be current. When educational achievement levels rise over time, a given raw score represents a higher percentile rank against old norms than against new norms. Norms must therefore be periodically revised and updated to remain meaningful.

### 3. Comparability

Norms from different tests should be comparable if the researcher wants to directly compare scores across tests. Comparability is only possible if tests have been normed on the same (or statistically equivalent) samples. A table of comparable norms enables cross-test comparisons, which is essential for guidance, counseling, and educational placement decisions.

### 4. Adequate Description of the Normative Group

Sufficient information about the normative group must be provided for norms to be properly interpreted. Required information includes:

(a) Method of sampling used
(b) Geographical distribution of cases in the norm sample
(c) Demographic characteristics of the norm group: age, sex, socioeconomic status, caste groups, school size and ability levels, and other relevant factors
(d) Conditions of administration and standardization during data collection

4. **Q1 = P25 (25th percentile); Q2 = P50 (median); Q3 = P75 (75th percentile).** D5 (5th decile) = P50.

6. **z-score formula: z = (X - M)/σ.** Distribution: M = 0, SD = 1. A positive z means above the mean; negative means below. A normalized z of +1 means the examinee surpassed 84% of the group.
