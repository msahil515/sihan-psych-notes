# Measurement of Intelligence, Aptitude and Achievement

Chapter 9 surveys the major theoretical viewpoints on the nature of intelligence, provides a detailed account of individual and group intelligence tests (Stanford-Binet, Wechsler, Kaufman, and nonverbal scales), and distinguishes intelligence from aptitude, achievement, and creativity. It covers the full psychometric infrastructure of each major scale — subtests, scoring units, reliability, validity, and normative standards — making it a high-density MCQ target.

---

## Historical Background

- The term **mental test** was first used by **J.M. Cattell** in **1890**.
- Cattell was an American psychologist who studied under **Wilhelm Wundt** (Germany) and **Francis Galton** (Britain).
- Cattell championed the **"brass instrument" approach**, which assumed that keen sensory abilities were essential to intelligence; tests measured sensory thresholds and reaction times.
- A student of Cattell later demonstrated that sensory test results (reaction time, colour naming) had **little or no relationship to college grades**, leading psychologists to abandon sensory measures as indices of intelligence.
- **Tuckman (1975):** "The modern history of testing is the history of testing for intelligence or mental ability."

## Different Viewpoints Towards Intelligence

### Intelligence As Whatever an Intelligence Test Measures

- Proposed by **Boring (1923)**.
- Definition: **Intelligence is whatever the intelligence tests measure.** When an intelligence test is administered, whatever it measures is implicitly accepted as intelligence.
- This was favoured by **psychometricians** who wanted to avoid theoretical disputes.
- **Criticisms:**
  1. Many tests measure intelligence — which is the standard measure? Boring's definition does not answer this.
  2. Many intelligence tests have **cultural bias**, so this definition would produce different meanings of intelligence across cultures.

---

### Intelligence As General Intellectual Capacity

- Proposed by **Binet (1919)**.
- Intelligence is a **general intellectual capacity** consisting of five abilities:
  1. To reason well with abstract materials
  2. To comprehend well
  3. To have a clear direction of thought
  4. To relate thinking to the attainment of a desirable end
  5. To be self-critical
- Stanley and Hopkins (1978) synthesised this as: intelligence is dependent upon **comprehension**, marked by **purposefulness** and **self-corrective judgement**.
- Binet conceptualised intelligence as a **single but complex mental process**, measurable by various integrated mental process tasks; his empirical approach was that ability develops with age.
- Binet developed the Binet-Simon scale with **Simon**; the 1908 edition grouped items into **age levels**.
- Binet died in **1911** at age 54.
- **William Stern**, a German psychologist, introduced the term **intelligence quotient** in **1911** to represent a child's status relative to age-mates.

#### Spearman's Two-Factor Theory (continuation within this section)

- **Spearman** postulated the existence of a **g factor** (general factor) and an **s factor** (specific factor) underlying intelligence.
- **g factor:** Mental energy required in all mental tasks, possessed by all individuals in varying degrees. A positive correlation between two or more functions indicates the presence of g.
- **s factor:** Specific factor unique to a particular type of mental activity. A low correlation between two or more functions indicates the presence of s.
- **Two-factor theory of intelligence:** All mental activities share a common general component (g) and each activity also has its own specific component (s); g is more important.
- Spearman proposed that intelligence tests should measure **g** because g predicts behaviour across different situations; measuring s is folly since it is unique to a specific activity.
- Good measures of g: **Raven's Progressive Matrices** and **Cattell Culture Fair Intelligence Test**.
- **Problem with the two-factor theory:** The existence of **group factors**. In 1906, Spearman noted that dissimilar tests could yield correlations higher than values predicted from their g loadings, implying shared common abilities between tests — a "halfway" between g and s.

### Intelligence As a Combination of Groups of Traits or Factors

#### Thurstone's Group-Factor / Multiple-Factor Theory

- Proposed by **L.L. Thurstone and T.G. Thurstone (1938; 1943)**.
- Also called the **group-factor theory** or **multiple-factor theory** of intelligence.
- Intelligence is not the expression of g (Spearman) but rather an expression of a **combination of groups of traits or factors**; these factors are intermediate — not as universal as g, not as truly specific as s.
- Each group of common mental abilities shares a **primary factor** that gives them functional cohesiveness.
- On the basis of extensive **factor-analytic research**, Thurstone and Thurstone (1941) postulated **seven Primary Mental Abilities (PMA)**:

| Symbol | Factor | Definition |
|--------|--------|------------|
| N | Number factor | Ability to do numerical calculations rapidly and accurately |
| V | Verbal factor | Verbal comprehension |
| S | Space factor | Manipulation of an imaginary object in space |
| W | Word fluency factor | Thinking of isolated words at a rapid rate |
| R | Reasoning factor | Discovering a rule or principle in series or groups of letters |
| M | Rote memory factor | Ability to memorise quickly |
| P | Perceptual Speed | Noting visual details rapidly |

- **Factor analysis** is a statistical procedure that identifies groups of tests which correlate in such a way that they seem to share a common dimension.
- Originally, each PMA was thought to be independent; later, **positive and significant correlations (range: 0.13 to 0.59)** were found among these factors.
- To account for these correlations, Thurstone introduced the concept of **second-order general factors**, thereby acknowledging Spearman's viewpoint regarding generality to some extent.
- **Key synthesis:** Intelligence is partly an expression of different factors (Thurstone) and partly an expression of a general factor (Spearman). Spearman emphasised g as the major determinant; Thurstone reversed these priorities.

#### Vernon's Hierarchical Factor Theory

- Proposed by **Vernon (1960)**.
- Attempted a **rapprochement** between Spearman and Thurstone.
- Structure:
  - **Top:** Single g factor
  - **Second level:** Two major group factors:
    - **v:ed** (verbal-educational)
    - **k:m** (practical-mechanical-spatial-physical)
  - **Below:** Several minor group factors resembling Thurstone's PMAs
  - **Bottom:** Specific factors

#### Cattell-Horn Fluid and Crystallized Intelligence Theory

- Developed by **Raymond Cattell and John Horn (1966)**.
- Two most important factors of intelligence:
  - **Fluid intelligence (Gf):** General, culture-reduced form of mental ability; problem-solving and information-processing ability largely independent of experience. Symbol: **G_f**.
  - **Crystallized intelligence (Gc):** Things learned through the investment of fluid intelligence in culture (e.g., trigonometry, biology); primarily used for tasks requiring a learned response; not culturally dependent. Symbol: **G_c**.
- Fluid and crystallized intelligence are **positively correlated**: moderate correlation of **r = 0.50** (Nisbett, 2000).
- Horn and colleagues later expanded to **nine broad abilities**:

| Symbol | Ability | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| G_f | Fluid intelligence | Reasoning in novel situations |
| G_c | Crystallized intelligence | Depth of general knowledge |
| G_q | Quantitative ability | Comprehending numerical concepts |
| G_a | Auditory processing | Discriminating sounds, detecting sound patterns |
| G_v | Visualization processing | Seeing spatial relationships and patterns |
| G_p | Processing speed | Quick decisions, maintaining attention |
| G_sm | Short-term memory | Using information over a short period |
| G_lm | Long-term memory | Transferring material to permanent storage and retrieving later |
| CDS | Correct decision speed | Arriving at correct decisions quickly |

#### Carroll's Three-Stratum Theory

- **John Carroll (1993)** reanalysed data from **more than 400 factor-analytic studies**.
- Result: A **hierarchical model** similar to Vernon's.
- **Three strata:**
  - **Stratum III (top):** General intelligence (g)
  - **Stratum II (middle):** A small number of general-ability factors similar to the Cattell-Horn model
  - **Stratum I (bottom):** A large number of specific factors for narrow test classifications
- Carroll and Horn combined their theories into the **Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) theory of intelligence**, noting many similarities between the Gf-Gc theory and the three-stratum theory.

### Intelligence As Structure of Intellect

- Proposed by **J.P. Guilford (1967)** based on approximately **20 years** of factor-analytic research.
- Model: **Structure of Intellect (SI) model** — a three-dimensional, boxlike model.
- Three dimensions: **Contents, Operations, and Products**.

#### Dimensions and Subcategories

**OPERATIONS** (what the person does mentally):

| Symbol | Operation | Definition |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| C | Cognition | Discovery, rediscovery, recognition of information |
| M | Memory Recording | Ability to readily encode information |
| — | Memory Retention | Ability to retain encoded information (added in 1988 expansion) |
| D | Divergent Production | Searching for multiple, creative, novel solutions |
| E | Evaluation | Placing value judgement on knowledge and thought |
| N | Convergent Production | Searching for a correct/single solution |

Original: 5 operations; Expanded (Guilford 1988): 6 operations (memory split into recording and retention).

**CONTENT** (type of material on which operations are performed):

| Symbol | Content | Definition |
|--------|---------|------------|
| V | Visual | Concrete visual materials |
| A | Auditory | Nature and characteristics of perceived sound |
| S | Symbolic | Letters, words, digits, other signs |
| M | Semantic | Verbal meanings or ideas |
| B | Behavioural | Knowledge regarding other persons |

Always 5 content categories.

**PRODUCTS** (results of performing operations on contents):

| Symbol | Product | Definition |
|--------|---------|------------|
| U | Units | Single word, definition, isolated bit of information |
| C | Classes | A concept |
| R | Relations | Any form of relationship (analogy, opposite, etc.) |
| S | Systems | An internally consistent set of classifications |
| T | Transformations | Changes in meaning, organization, or other arrangements |
| I | Implications | Information beyond what is presented |

Always 6 product categories.

#### Formula for Total Abilities

- **Original (Guilford 1967):** 5 operations × 6 products × 5 content = **150 abilities**
- **Expanded (Guilford 1988):** 6 operations × 5 content × 6 products = **180 abilities**
- Notation for each cell: three-letter code (operation–content–product order). Example: **CVU** = Cognition of Visual Units; **EBU** = Evaluation of Behavioural Units; **CMR** = Cognition of Behavioural Relations.
- Over **100 test items** identified within the model.
- A test based directly on this model: **Concept of Intellect Learning Abilities (J.P. Guilford and R. Hoepfner, 1971, 1983)**.
- **Convergent thinking** (measured by intelligence tests): reduces many inputs to one or two correct answers.
- **Divergent thinking** (measured by creativity tests): expands from limited information to many correct answers.

### Intelligence As Learning Ability

- **Piaget (1950, 1952)** defined intelligence on the basis of **assimilation** and **accommodation**.
  - **Assimilation:** Changes occurring in what has entered the mind from the external world to fit an existing frame of reference.
  - **Accommodation:** Changes occurring in the internal structure of a person as a function of new experiences.
- Together, assimilation and accommodation determine a person's ability to learn.
- **Feuerstein (1975)** developed a procedure for measuring a child's learning ability after proper instruction for a period of time (dynamic assessment).

---

### Intelligence As a Two-Level Process

- Proposed by **Jensen (1968)**.
- Two levels:
  - **Associative intelligence (Level I):** Depends upon memory and simple verbal associations; includes verbal associations, memory for temporal sequences, spatial positions, stimulus-response learning. Related to **biological maturation**; shows less variation across social classes and races.
  - **Abstract intelligence (Level II):** Includes concept learning, thinking, problem-solving, multiple discrimination, principle learning (Gagne 1965; Freeman 1962). Dependent on **education and culture**; responsible for observed differences in abilities across social classes and races.
- Most intelligence tests are a combination of both levels.

---

### Intelligence As a Global Capacity

- Proposed by **Wechsler (1944)** and **Stoddard (1943)**.
- **Wechsler's definition:** Intelligence is "the aggregate or global capacity of the individual to act purposefully, to think rationally, and to deal effectively with his environment."
  - Key feature: Intelligence is displayed by **behaviour of the individual as a whole**; intelligent behaviour is **goal-directed and purposeful**.
  - Wechsler implicitly included **drive** and **incentive** ("to act purposefully," "to deal effectively").
  - Criticism (Freeman 1962): Including incentive and drive introduces personality traits into a mental ability test, creating confusion.
- **Stoddard's definition:** Intelligence is "the ability to undertake activities that are characterized by (i) **difficulty**, (ii) **complexity**, (iii) **abstractness**, (iv) **economy**, (v) **adaptiveness to goal**, (vi) **social value**, and (vii) the **emergence of originals**, and to maintain such activities under conditions that demand concentration of energy and resistance to emotional forces."
  - Three characteristics deserving special attention:
    - **Economy:** Rate at which a mental task is done; if A solves faster than B, A has greater economy.
    - **Social value:** Whether tasks correspond to socially desirable norms (criticised as subjective).
    - **Emergence of originals:** Ability to discover something new (creativity and originality).
  - Criticisms of Stoddard: Including non-intellective traits — concentration of energy and resistance to emotional forces — confuses and invalidates intelligence measurement.

### Intelligence As a Process of Integrating Physiology and Information Processing (PASS Theory)

- Proposed by **Das, Naglieri, and Kirby (1994)**.
- Model: **PASS theory (model) of intelligence**.
- Based on the **neurophysiological model of brain** proposed by **A.R. Luria**.
- Intellectual performance is divided into four basic processes:

| Letter | Process | Description |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| P | Planning | Deciding where to focus attention and what type of processing is needed; monitors success of problem solving; modifies approach until solution is found; analogous to Sternberg's metacomponents |
| A | Attention | Most basic function; inability to focus attention is the primary reason for poor intellectual performance |
| S | Simultaneous scanning | Processing where elements are interrelated and accessible simultaneously; involved in language comprehension and perception of whole stimulus situations |
| S | Successive processing | Elements combined in a particular order; required for serial recalling and skilled movements |

- **Luria's three functional units** (basis for PASS):
  1. Controls arousal and attention
  2. Codes information using simultaneous and successive processes
  3. Provides, self-monitors, and structures cognitive activities
- All four PASS processes operate within an existing **knowledge base** (cumulative result of an individual's experiences stored in memory), which influences all cognitive processes.

## Types of Intelligence Tests

### Classification by Administration: Individual vs. Group Tests

| Dimension | Individual Test | Group Test |
|-----------|----------------|------------|
| Administration | One person at a time | More than one person at a time |
| First example | Binet-Simon scale | Army Alpha and Army Beta (World War I) |
| Administrator skill | Highly skilled and experienced required | Moderate experience sufficient (mostly self-explanatory) |
| Setting | Clinical settings | Educational, industrial, civil services, military |
| Age suitability | Very young children (preschool) | Adolescents and adults |
| Construction difficulty | More difficult, time-consuming, expensive | Easier to construct |
| Rapport | Better; can detect transient conditions (headache, fatigue) | Limited opportunity for rapport; harder to detect transient conditions |
| Norms | Based on smaller samples (less dependable) | Based on very large samples (more dependable) |

- **Army Alpha test:** First group intelligence test; verbal test for general screening and placement.
- **Army Beta test:** First group nonlanguage/nonverbal test; for illiterate or foreign-language soldiers.

---

### Classification by Nature of Items: Verbal, Performance, and Nonverbal Tests

#### Verbal (Paper-and-Pencil) Tests

- Instructions and items are presented through **written language**.
- Requires the examinee to be **literate**.
- Examples: **Stanford-Binet, Army Alpha**, Mohsin General Intelligence test, Bhatia General Mental Ability test, Jalota's Group General Mental Ability test.

#### Performance Tests

- Language is used only to **impart instructions**; items are **manipulative** in nature.
- No written language required; examinee responds by manipulating given materials.
- Can be administered to illiterates, speech-defective, preschool children, culturally deprived.
- **All performance tests are individual tests** by nature.
- Examples: Knox Beta test, Pinter-Patterson scale, Cornell-Coxe scale, Arthur Point scale, Pass-along test, Kohs Block Design test.

#### Nonverbal (Nonlanguage / Culture-Free / Culture-Fair) Tests

- **No language** used in either instructions or item content.
- Instructions given via pantomime, gestures, blackboard demonstrations, charts.
- Items are figural; examinees discover relations between figures/designs **without actual manipulation**.
- Paper and pencil used only to underline or cross out (does not require reading or writing ability).
- **Not usually group tests.**
- Most suitable for: subnormals, physically handicapped, foreign-language groups.
- First nonlanguage test: **Army Beta examination** (WWI, for illiterate soldiers).
- Examples: Leiter International Performance Scale, **Cattell Culture Fair Intelligence Test**, **Raven Progressive Matrices**, **Goodenough Draw-a-Man test** (now Goodenough-Harris Drawing Test).
- Note: No test has fully succeeded as a truly culture-free test. More accurate terms would be **culture-common** or **culture-general** rather than "culture-free."

## Stanford-Binet Scale: History and Versions

**Alfred Binet (1857–1911):** Established the first psychological laboratory in **1889 in France**. Asked by the French Government to diagnose slow learners and mentally retarded children. Worked with **Simon** (physician at the asylum at Saint-Yon).

### Chronological Milestones

| Year | Test / Authors | Key Feature |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| 1905 | Binet and Simon | First Binet-Simon scale: 30 items in ascending order of difficulty |
| 1908 | Binet and Simon | First age scale; mental age concept introduced; items grouped into age levels |
| 1911 | Binet and Simon | Age range extended from 3 years to adult level; last revision by Binet in his lifetime |
| 1916 | Stanford-Binet (Terman and associates) | First important American revision; IQ concept first introduced in a psychological test; standardized on 1,400 (1,000 children + 400 adults); one-third of items were new |
| 1937 | Stanford-Binet-2 (Terman and Merrill) | Two parallel forms: L and M; better standardized and validated; extended upper and lower range |
| 1960 | Stanford-Binet Form L-M (Terman and Merrill) | Single form combining best items from L and M; retained 1937 characteristics; range: 2 years to 22 years 2 months of mental age; deviation IQ introduced |
| 1972 | Restandardization (Thorndike) | New normative sample of 100 individuals per age level (each one-third Binet age level); first to include nonwhites |
| 1986 | Stanford-Binet-4 / SB-4 (Thorndike, Hagen, and Sattler) | Complete restructuring into 15 subtests; incorporated gf-gc theory (Horn and Cattell); three-level hierarchical model |
| 2003 | Stanford-Binet-5 / SB-5 (Roid) | Five factors; verbal and nonverbal domains; SD changed from 16 to 15; screened for religious bias; routing procedure introduced |

### Stanford-Binet Fourth Edition (SB-4 / 1986 Edition)

- Incorporated **gf-gc theory** (Horn and Cattell) as its theoretical base.
- **Three-level hierarchical model:**
  - **Top:** g factor (general intelligence) — reflects common variability of all tasks.
  - **Second level:** Three group factors:
    - **Crystallized abilities** (reflecting learning) — further divided into verbal reasoning and quantitative reasoning.
    - **Fluid-analytic abilities** (reflecting original problem-solving potential) — reflected in abstract/visual reasoning.
    - **Short-term memory** — person's ability to retain information after a single brief presentation.
  - **Third level (subtests):** 15 tests organized into four content areas.

- **Four content areas and subtests:**

| Content Area | Subtests |
|--------------|----------|
| Verbal reasoning | Vocabulary, Comprehension, Absurdities, Verbal relations |
| Quantitative reasoning | Quantitative, Number series, Equation building |
| Abstract/visual reasoning | Pattern analysis, Copying, Matrices, Paper-folding-and-cutting |
| Short-term memory | Bead memory, Memory for sentences, Memory for digits, Memory for objects |

- **Scoring:**
  - Raw scores on each of 15 tests converted to **standard age scores** with **mean = 50, SD = 8**.
  - Four content area scores each with **mean = 100, SD = 16**.
- **Basal age:** Lowest level point where two consecutive items of approximately equal difficulty are passed.
- **Ceiling:** Point at which at least 3 out of 4 items are missed.
- **Adaptive format:** Entry point for each test determined by the subject's vocabulary test score combined with chronological age.
- **Limitations:** Disagreement about factor structure (shifts from 2-factor to 4-factor structure with age); difficult to establish basal with younger impaired children; administration is complex.

### Stanford-Binet Fifth Edition (SB-5 / 2003 Edition)

- Released in **2003** by **Roid**.
- Screened for gender, ethnic, cultural, socio-economic, and **religious tradition** biases (first time in intelligence testing history to include religious tradition: Christian, Jewish, Muslim, Hindu, Buddhist).
- Suitable for **ages 2 through 85+**.
- **Five factors of intelligence** (each assessed in both Verbal and Nonverbal domains):
  1. Fluid reasoning
  2. Knowledge
  3. Quantitative reasoning
  4. Visual-spatial processing
  5. Working memory
- **10 subtests** (5 factors × 2 domains = 10).
- **Scores produced:**
  - 10 subtest scores (mean = 3, SD = 3... wait, text says "mean and SD of 3" — typical standard scores for subtests are mean 10, SD 3; "mean and SD of 3" may be a text error. Report as in text.)
  - 3 IQ scores: Full Scale IQ, Verbal IQ, Nonverbal IQ
  - 5 factor scores (fluid reasoning, knowledge, quantitative reasoning, visual-spatial processing, working memory)
  - IQ and factor scores: **mean = 100, SD = 15** (SD changed from 16 to 15 compared to earlier SBS editions)
- **Routing procedure:** Estimates general cognitive ability before the full test; items also provide an **Abbreviated IQ** used for screening.
- **Working memory factor** (verbal and nonverbal items) aids assessment of ADHD.

## Wechsler Scales

**David Wechsler** developed scales to address limitations of the Stanford-Binet, particularly the criticism regarding measurement of adult intelligence. He worked at **Bellevue Hospital, New York City**.

### Wechsler-Bellevue Intelligence Scale (1939)

- Developed in **1939**; designed for adults and children aged 7–16.
- Two equivalent forms: **Form I** and **Form II**.
- Each form: 10 subtests (verbal + nonverbal/performance).

### WAIS, WAIS-R, WAIS-III

| Year | Name | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| 1955 | **Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (WAIS)** | Revised Wechsler-Bellevue; 11 subtests: 6 verbal, 5 performance |
| 1981 | **WAIS-R** | Revised WAIS |
| 1997 | **WAIS-III** | 7 verbal + 7 performance subtests; 4 index scores |

#### WAIS-III (1997) — Verbal Subtests and Functions

| Subtest | Function Measured |
|---------|-------------------|
| Vocabulary | Level of word knowledge |
| Similarities | Abstract thinking |
| Arithmetic | Concentration |
| Digit Span | Short-term memory, anxiety |
| Information | Range of knowledge |
| Comprehension | Judgement |
| Letter-Number Sequencing | Freedom from distractibility |

#### WAIS-III — Performance Subtests and Functions

| Subtest | Function Measured |
|---------|-------------------|
| Picture Completion | Visual alertness |
| Digit Symbol-Coding | Visual-motor functioning |
| Block Design | Perceptual reasoning ability |
| Matrix Reasoning | Inductive reasoning |
| Picture Arrangement | Planning ability |
| Symbol Search | Perceptual processing speed |
| Object Assembly | Analysis of part-whole relationship |

#### WAIS-III Scoring

- Raw scores converted to **scaled scores** with **mean = 10, SD = 3**.
- Two sets of norms: **age-adjusted norms** and **reference-group norms** (ages 20–34).
- **Verbal IQ (VIQ):** Sum of age-corrected scaled scores on verbal subtests; deviation IQ with **mean = 100, SD = 15**.
- **Performance IQ (PIQ):** Sum of age-corrected scaled scores on performance subtests; deviation IQ with **mean = 100, SD = 15**.
- **Full Scale IQ (FSIQ):** Age-corrected scaled scores of verbal + performance subtests combined.

#### WAIS-III — Four Index Scores

| Index Score | Subtests | What It Measures |
|-------------|----------|------------------|
| Verbal Comprehension Index (VCI) | Vocabulary, Similarities, Information, Picture Completion | Crystallized intelligence |
| Perceptual Organization Index (POI) | Block Design, Matrix Reasoning, Arithmetic | Fluid intelligence |
| Working Memory Index (WMI) | Digit Span, Letter-Number Sequencing, Digit Symbol-Coding | Holding and manipulating information in mind |
| Processing Speed Index (PSI) | Symbol Search | Speed at which the mind works |

#### WAIS-III Reliability

| Score | Split-Half | Test-Retest |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Full Scale IQ | .98 | .94 |
| Verbal IQ | .97 | .94 |
| Performance IQ | .94 | .88 |

- Validity correlations (with WAIS-R, WISC-III, and individual tests): ranged from **.45 to .90**.
- Age range: **16 to 85–89 years**.
- **Pattern analysis:** Evaluates large differences between subtest scaled scores (e.g., schizophrenia shows low arithmetic and comprehension scores).

#### WAIS-III Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages: Multidimensional; allows pattern analysis; appropriate for adults; uses deviation IQ; impressive reliability/validity; uses point scale; includes performance scale; index scores support multidimensional intelligence.

Disadvantages: Poor measure of extreme intelligence levels; does not incorporate Gardner's (1983) multiple intelligences; poor reliability for individual subtests.

### WAIS-IV (2008)

- Released in **2008**.
- **10 core + 5 supplemental subtests**; FSIQ based on 10 core subtests.
- Verbal/Performance subscales from previous versions **deleted**; replaced by four index scores:
  1. **Verbal Comprehension Index (VCI)**
  2. **Perceptual Reasoning Index (PRI)**
  3. **Working Memory Index (WMI)**
  4. **Processing Speed Index (PSI)**
- **General Ability Index (GAI):** Based on Similarities, Vocabulary, Information (from VCI) + Block Design, Matrix Reasoning, Visual Puzzles (from PRI); less affected by processing and working memory impairments.
- Two broad scores: **FSIQ** (based on VCI + PRI + WMI + PSI) and **GAI** (based on 6 subtests of VCI + PRI).
- Age range: **16–90 years**.

#### WAIS-IV Subtests

| Verbal Comprehension (VCI) | Perceptual Reasoning (PRI) | Working Memory (WMI) | Processing Speed (PSI) |
|---------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Similarities (Core) | Block Design (Core) | Digit Span (Core) | Symbol Search (Core) |
| Vocabulary (Core) | Visual Puzzles (Core) | Arithmetic (Core) | Coding (Core) |
| Information (Core) | Matrix Reasoning (Core) | Letter-Number Sequence (Core) | Cancellation (Supplemental) |
| Comprehension (Supplemental) | Picture Completion (Supplemental) | | |
| | Figure Weights (Supplemental) | | |

### Downward Extensions: WISC and WPPSI

#### WISC-III (Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children, Third Edition)

- First published: **1949**; revised **1974**; most recent revision: **1991**.
- Age range: **6 years through 16 years, 11 months, 30 days**.
- **11 subtests** (3 supplementary):
  - **Verbal subtests:** Information, Comprehension, Arithmetic, Similarities, Vocabulary (+ Digit Span as supplementary)
  - **Performance subtests:** Picture Completion, Picture Arrangement, Block Design, Object Assembly, Coding (+ Maze and Symbol Search as supplementary)
- Scaled scores: **mean = 10, SD = 3**; IQ scores: **mean = 100, SD = 15**.

**WISC-III Reliability:**

| Score | Split-Half |
|-------|-----------|
| Full Scale IQ | .96 |
| Verbal IQ | .95 |
| Performance IQ | .91 |

- Validity correlations with WAIS-III and WPPSI-R: **.70s–.80s** (for FSIQ, VIQ, PIQ); correlations with Stanford-Binet: **.60s–.70s** for individual subtests, **.80s–.90s** for the three IQs.

**Criticisms of WISC-III:**
1. Fails to incorporate recent developments in cognitive science (information processing, planning, metacognition).
2. Lacks **treatment validity** (Witt and Gresham 1985): specific deficits on WISC-III don't guide specific remedial interventions.
3. **Selection bias** toward certain cultural/racial groups (Saccuzzo and Johnson 1995): White children score higher; African-American children overselected for mental retardation classes, underselected for gifted classes.

#### WISC-IV (2003; UK version 2004; India standardized 2012)

- **15 subtests** (10 from WISC-III; 5 new: Picture Concepts, Letter-Number Sequencing, Matrix Reasoning, Cancellation, Word Reasoning).
- **Four composite scores:**
  1. **Verbal Comprehension Index (VCI)** — formerly Verbal IQ
  2. **Working Memory Index (WMI)**
  3. **Perceptual Reasoning Index (PRI)** — formerly Perceptual Organization Index
  4. **Processing Speed Index (PSI)** — formerly Performance IQ

**WISC-IV Index Subtests:**

| VCI | PRI | WMI | PSI |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| Vocabulary (1) | Block Design (4) | Digit Span (2) | Coding |
| Similarities (3) | Picture Concepts (new*) | Letter-Number Sequencing (new*) | Symbol Search (8) |
| Comprehension (6) | Matrix Reasoning (new*) | Arithmetic | Cancellation (new*) |
| Information (8) | Picture Completion (supp.) | | |
| Word Reasoning (new supp.) | | | |

#### WPPSI (Wechsler Preschool and Primary Scale of Intelligence)

### A Comparison between Binet Scales and Wechsler Scales

Two profound differences:

#### 1. Age Scale vs. Point Scale

- **Binet scales (1908–1972): Age scales.** Items grouped by age level; two-thirds to three-fourths of individuals at an age level expected to pass its tasks. Arrangement concerns content (perceptual speed, memory, reasoning tasks may all appear at one age level). Subjects do not receive credit for partially completed sections (e.g., passing 3 out of 5 required items gives no credit). Modern Binet: has a basal score but has **not adopted the point scale concept** (passing 3 or all 4 gives the same basal credit).
- **Wechsler scales: Point scales.** Credits or points are assigned to each item; subject receives specific amount of credit for each item passed.

#### 2. Performance Scale

- **Binet:** Emphasis is on verbal skills and language. Some performance tasks in early editions were restricted to younger age levels; performance task results were difficult to separate from verbal task results.
- **Wechsler:** Explicitly includes a **performance scale** (separate from verbal scale). Wechsler scale was the **first to offer direct comparison** of verbal and nonverbal intelligence (both scales standardized on the same sample and maintained in comparable units).

**Concluding remark on Wechsler scales:** The weakest point is their **lack of theoretical grounding**, making it hard to provide a coherent basis for interpretation.

## The Kaufman Scales

- Developed in the 1980s and 1990s; individually administered; designed for many age groups.
- Incorporate recent developments in test construction.
- Three types:
  1. **Kaufman Assessment Battery for Children (K-ABC)**
  2. **Kaufman Adolescent and Adult Intelligence Scale (KAIT)**
  3. **Kaufman Brief Intelligence Test (K-BIT)**

### K-ABC (Kaufman Assessment Battery for Children)

- Authors: **Kaufman and Kaufman (1983a, 1983b)**.
- Age range: **2½ to 12½ years**.
- **16 subtests** combined into **5 global scales:**
  1. Sequential processing
  2. Simultaneous processing
  3. Mental processing composite (sequential + simultaneous)
  4. Achievement
  5. Nonverbal

**Theoretical foundations (Kaufman 1984):**
- **Luria's (1966) neuropsychological model** of brain functioning
- **Sperry's (1968) split-brain functioning** theory
- **Neisser's (1967) information processing** theories

- **Sequential processing:** Children's ability to solve problems by mentally analyzing inputs into a **serial/temporal order** (e.g., number and word-order recall).
- **Simultaneous processing:** Children's ability to **synthesize and integrate information** in parallel for problem solving.
- Main purpose: Identify children's unique strengths and problem-solving strategies to develop **intervention strategies**.
- Also provides independent **achievement scale** (measuring intelligence and achievement separately is a major advantage).
- **Nonverbal scale:** For children who are linguistically different or handicapped.

**Scoring:**
- Subtests: standard scores with **mean = 10, SD = 3**.
- Global scales: standard scores with **mean = 100, SD = 15**.

**K-ABC Reliability:**
- Subtest split-half: **.72** (preschool); **.71–.85** (school-age).
- Global scale split-half: **.86–.93** (preschool); **.89–.97** (school-age).

**Criticisms of K-ABC:**
- Jensen (1984): Not as valid for predicting school achievement as Binet or Wechsler scales; limited for assessing general intelligence.
- Highly visual — limits use with visually handicapped.
- Does not measure extremes of intelligence (Kamphaus 1993).
- Sternberg (1984): Noncorrespondence between definition and measurement; overemphasis on rote learning at the expense of higher-order thinking.

### KABC-II (2004)

- Measures processing and cognitive abilities for **ages 1 to 18** (approx.).
- **18 subtests** (core and supplemental).
- Based on two theoretical models (examiner chooses before testing):

| Luria's Model | CHC Model |
|--------------|-----------|
| Simultaneous processing scale | Visual processing (Gv) |
| Sequential processing scale | Short-term memory (Gsm) |
| Learning ability scale | Long-term storage retrieval (Glr) |
| Planning ability scale | Fluid reasoning (Gf) |
| — | Crystallized ability (Gc) / Knowledge (5th scale, CHC only) |

**KABC-II Subtests:**

| Scale | Subtests |
|-------|----------|
| Simultaneous/Gv | Triangles, Face recognition, Block counting, Conceptual thinking, Rover, Gestalt closure, Pattern reasoning (ages 5–6), Story completion (ages 5–6) |
| Sequential/Gsm | Word order, Number recall, Hand movements |
| Planning/Gf | Pattern reasoning (ages 7–18), Story completion (ages 7–18) |
| Learning/Glr | Atlantis, Atlantis delayed, Rebus, Rebus delayed |
| Knowledge/Gc (CHC only) | Riddles, Expressive vocabulary, Verbal knowledge |

- **Composite scores:** Mental Processing Index (MPI) — Luria model; Fluid-Crystallized Index (FCI) — CHC model.
- Administration time: Luria model ~25–40 minutes; CHC model ~30 minutes.

---

### KAIT (Kaufman Adolescent and Adult Intelligence Test)

- Authors: **A. Kaufman and Kaufman (1993)**.
- Age range: **11 to 85 years or older**.
- Integrates **Horn and Cattell's (1966) gf-gc theory** with ideas from Luria (1980) and Piaget (1972).
- Composed of a **crystallized scale** (concepts from schooling and acculturation) and a **fluid scale** (ability to solve novel problems).

**Structure:**

| Battery | Content | Time |
|---------|---------|------|
| Core battery | 3 fluid + 3 crystallized subtests (6 total) | ~1 hr 15 min |
| Expanded battery | Core + 4 additional subtests | ~1 hr 30 min |

**Core battery fluid subtests:** Logical steps (sequential reasoning), Mystery codes (rule learning), Rebus learning (long-term memory and learning).

**Core battery crystallized subtests:** Word knowledge and language development, Double meanings (language comprehension), Auditory comprehension (listening ability).

**Expanded battery additions:** Memory for block designs (visual processing/fluid), Famous faces (cultural knowledge/crystallized), Rebus delayed recall, Auditory delayed recall.

- Also includes an optional **supplemental mental status examination**.
- Yields IQ scores in a wide range; often used for assessing individuals with exceptional abilities (e.g., gifted children).

---

### K-BIT (Kaufman Brief Intelligence Test)

- Authors: **Kaufman and Kaufman (1990)**.
- Purpose: **Quick screening** for estimating intellectual functioning level.
- Age range: **4 to 10 years** (first edition).
- Not a shortened version of K-ABC or KAIT.
- **Three scores:** Verbal, Nonverbal, Composite — expressed as **deviation IQ units**.
- Verbal scale: 45 expressive vocabulary items + 17 definitions.
- Nonverbal scale: 48 matrices items.

### KBIT-2 (2004)

- Age range: **4 to 90 years**.
- Verbal scale: Receptive vocabulary, general information, reasoning, comprehension, vocabulary knowledge (Riddles subtest).
- Nonverbal scale: Matrices subtest (visual analogies and relationships).
- Completion time: **15–30 minutes** (none of the three subtests are timed).

## Nonverbal Intelligence Scales

### Raven's Progressive Matrices (RPM)

- One of the best-known, most popular intelligence tests; usable individually or in a group.
- Age range: **5 years to elderly adults**.
- **60 multiple-choice questions** arranged in order of difficulty (Kaplan and Saccuzzo 2005).
- Designed to measure **Spearman's g factor** (Raven 1983; Raven, Raven and Court 1995).
- Items: sets of matrices with a missing part; subject selects the correct insert from alternatives. Matrix sizes: 6×4, 4×4, 3×3, 2×2.
- No time limits.

**Three forms:**

| Form | Edition Year | Suitable For |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| Standard Progressive Matrices (SPM) | 1996 | Average individuals aged 6–80 |
| Coloured Progressive Matrices (CPM) | 1990 | Younger children, mentally retarded; cannot be tested with SPM |
| Advanced Progressive Matrices (APM) | 1994 | Above-average adolescents and adults |

**Reliability and Validity:**
- Test-retest reliability: **.70–.90**
- Internal consistency: largely in the **.80s–.90s**
- Validity with intelligence tests: **.40–.75** (higher with performance tests than verbal tests)

---

### Goodenough Draw-a-Man Test (Goodenough-Harris Drawing Test)

- Originally developed in **1926** by **Goodenough**.
- Revised in **1963** by **Harris** — renamed the **Goodenough-Harris Drawing Test**.
- Procedure: Examinee instructed to make a picture of a man as accurately as possible.
- Emphasis: Accuracy of observation and development of **conceptual thinking** (not artistic skills).
- **Scoring:** Each detail gives 1 point; possible total = **73 points (Harris scale)**.
- **Three scales in revised form:**
  - **Man Scale:** Draw a man.
  - **Woman Scale:** Draw a woman.
  - **Self Scale:** Draw self (treated as a **projective test of personality**).
- Point scores on each scale translated into **standard scores: mean = 100, SD = 15**.
- Age range: **3 years to 15 years 11 months** (preferred: **3 to 10 years**).

**Reliability and Validity:**
- Test-retest, split-half, and scorer reliability are adequate.
- Validity correlations with other intelligence tests: majority **above .50** (vary widely).

## Types of Intelligence Test Scores

### Mental Age

- Concept introduced by **Binet and Simon (1908)** in the first age scale.
- **Mental age (MA):** A score representing the level of intellectual development corresponding to the average performance of children of a given chronological age.
- Items on the Binet scale were arranged into **age levels** — tasks that the average child at a specific age could complete.

### Intelligence Quotient (IQ)

- The term **intelligence quotient** was introduced by **William Stern** (German psychologist) in **1911**.
- The **IQ concept was first introduced in a psychological test** in the **1916 Stanford-Binet** (Terman).
- **Ratio IQ (Mental Age formula):**
  > IQ = (Mental Age / Chronological Age) × 100
- **Deviation IQ:** Used from the 1960 Stanford-Binet revision onward and in all Wechsler scales.
  - **Wechsler scales:** IQ scores expressed as deviation IQ with **mean = 100, SD = 15**.
  - **Stanford-Binet 4th edition:** Area scores with **mean = 100, SD = 16**; standard age scores **mean = 50, SD = 8**.
  - **Stanford-Binet 5th edition:** IQ and factor scores with **mean = 100, SD = 15**.
- **Deviation IQ** expresses a person's score relative to the normative group for their age, using a standard score distribution rather than the ratio formula.

---

## Distinction between Aptitude Tests and Achievement Tests

- **Intelligence tests** (also called **IQ tests, scholastic aptitude tests, or academic aptitude tests**) evaluate the general level of cognitive functions; they measure **general cognitive ability**.
- **Aptitude tests** measure potential for future learning or performance in specific areas.
- **Achievement tests** measure what has already been learned in specific content areas.
- A key distinction: intelligence/aptitude tests measure **convergent thinking** (reducing information to one or two correct answers), whereas **creativity tests** measure primarily **divergent thinking** (expanding from limited information to many correct answers).

---

## Tests of Creativity

- **Convergent thinking:** Individual reduces large amounts of information to one or two possible correct answers. Measured by **intelligence tests**.
- **Divergent thinking:** Individual expands from a limited amount of information to many correct answers. Measured by **creativity tests**.
- Examples of creativity tests:
  - **Torrance Tests of Creativity**
  - **Remote Associates Test**

## Likely Exam Points

1. **J.M. Cattell (1890)** coined the term "mental test"; studied under Wundt and Galton; championed the brass instrument approach.
2. **Boring (1923):** Intelligence = whatever intelligence tests measure — the only definition that avoids theory but is criticised for cultural bias and lack of a standard test.
3. **William Stern (1911)** introduced the term "intelligence quotient"; **Terman (1916)** first introduced IQ in a psychological test (Stanford-Binet).
4. **Spearman's two-factor theory:** g (general mental energy, most important, goal of testing) + s (specific, unique to each activity); g measured by Raven's Matrices and Cattell Culture Fair Test.
5. **Thurstone's 7 PMAs:** N (number), V (verbal), S (space), W (word fluency), R (reasoning), M (rote memory), P (perceptual speed); inter-correlations ranged **0.13–0.59**, necessitating second-order general factors.
6. **Guilford's SI model (1967):** 5 operations × 6 products × 5 content = **150 abilities**; expanded (1988) to 6 operations × 5 content × 6 products = **180 abilities**; each cell identified by 3-letter code (operation-content-product).
7. **Cattell-Horn (1966):** Fluid (Gf) and crystallized (Gc) intelligence; they correlate **r = 0.50**; expanded to **9 broad abilities** (Gf, Gc, Gq, Ga, Gv, Gp, Gsm, Glm, CDS).
8. **Carroll (1993):** Three-stratum theory from 400+ factor analytic studies; combined with Cattell-Horn to form **CHC theory**.
9. **PASS theory (Das, Naglieri, Kirby 1994):** Planning, Attention, Simultaneous, Successive; based on Luria's three functional units; Planning is analogous to Sternberg's metacomponents.
10. **Stanford-Binet milestones:** 1905 (30-item scale), 1908 (mental age), 1916 (IQ introduced; n=1,400), 1937 (Forms L & M), 1960 (L-M form; deviation IQ), 1972 (2,100 sample; nonwhites included), 1986 (15 subtests; gf-gc; SAS mean=50 SD=8; area score mean=100 SD=16), 2003 (SB-5: 5 factors; SD=15; religious bias screening; routing procedure; age 2–85+).
11. **WAIS-III (1997) reliability:** Split-half FSIQ = **.98**, VIQ = **.97**, PIQ = **.94**; test-retest FSIQ = **.94**, VIQ = **.94**, PIQ = **.88**; 4 index scores: VCI (crystallized), POI (fluid), WMI (working memory), PSI (processing speed).
12. **Binet vs. Wechsler scales:** (1) Age scale (Binet) vs. point scale (Wechsler); (2) No separate performance scale (Binet) vs. separate verbal and performance scales standardized on the same sample (Wechsler — first to allow direct comparison).
13. **K-ABC (1983):** Sequential vs. simultaneous processing; based on Luria, Sperry, and Neisser; ages 2.5–12.5; subtest scores mean=10 SD=3; global scores mean=100 SD=15; K-ABC-II (2004) has dual models (CHC and Luria); MPI and FCI composites.
14. **Raven's Progressive Matrices:** 60 items; measures Spearman's g; SPM (ages 6–80), CPM (young/mentally retarded), APM (above-average adolescents/adults); test-retest reliability .70–.90; validity with intelligence tests .40–.75.
15. **Goodenough-Harris Drawing Test (1963):** 73-point Harris scale; Man, Woman, and Self scales (Self = projective); standard scores mean=100 SD=15; ages 3–15 yr 11 mo; validity correlations mostly above .50.
16. **Army Alpha** (verbal) and **Army Beta** (nonlanguage/nonverbal): First group intelligence tests; developed for WWI; Army Beta was the first nonlanguage group test, designed for illiterate/foreign-language soldiers.
17. **WISC-IV (2003; India 2012):** 15 subtests; VCI (formerly Verbal IQ), PSI (formerly Performance IQ), PRI (formerly Perceptual Organization Index), WMI.
18. **WPPSI-IV (2012):** Ages 2 yr 6 mo – 7 yr 7 mo; includes FRI and VSI as new primary index scales alongside VCI, WMI, PSI; Ancillary scales: VAI, NVI, GAI, CPI.
