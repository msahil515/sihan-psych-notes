# Introduction to Measurement

This chapter establishes the conceptual and historical foundations of psychological measurement. It defines measurement and evaluation, traces the evolution of mental testing from ancient China through the 20th century, systematically covers the four levels (scales) of measurement with their permissible statistics, identifies the properties that differentiate scales, and distinguishes psychological measurement from physical measurement, assessment, and testing.

---

## Measurement and Evaluation

**Measurement** refers to the process of assigning numerals to events, objects, etc., according to certain rules (Eber, 1996).

A more precise definition is given by **Nunnally (1972, p. 2)**: "Measurement consists of rules for assigning numbers to objects to represent quantities of attributes."

### Key Properties Derived from Nunnally's Definition

1. **Numbers are assigned according to explicit rules.** Rules are procedures that transform qualities of attributes into numbers. Rules may be obvious and explicit (e.g., measuring height with a ruler) or vague and implicit (e.g., measuring extraversion or intelligence). For psychological, sociological, and educational attributes the rules are generally vague and less explicit (Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994; Kaserfeld, Kaernee & Rubin, 2005).

2. **Measurement concerns attributes, not objects themselves.** One measures the aptitude, intelligence, or attitude of a person, not the person. Two difficulties arise: (a) an attribute whose existence is doubtful (e.g., extra-sensory perception) may be unmeasurable; (b) an attribute that is not unitary (e.g., school achievement influenced by home, school, and emotional factors) requires separate instruments for each sub-attribute.

3. **Numerals represent quantities (quantification).** Numerals may indicate actual amount (e.g., 85% marks in arithmetic) or relative position. Measurement always involves quantification.

### Measurement vs. Evaluation

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Measurement** | Assigning numerals to events/objects according to rules; answers "How much?" |
| **Evaluation** | Appraising an event or attribute against a standard; involves value judgment |

**Evaluation** (Backman, 1971, p. 1): "A process wherein the parts, processes or outcomes of a programme are examined to see whether they are satisfactory, particularly with reference to the programme's stated objectives, our own needs, a set of standards, or some combination of these."

Examples:
- A child who is 4'3" tall — that is **measurement**.
- Describing the child as "short" relative to age-group norms — that is **evaluation**.
- A typist doing 80 wpm — measurement; classifying him as a "Grade A" typist — evaluation.

## The History of Psychological Measurement and Mental Testing

### Ancient Origins

- The history of psychological and educational measurement dates to approximately **206 BC**, when Chinese officials were examined every third year to determine fitness for office (Gould, 1981).
- During the **Han dynasty (206 BC–220 AD)**, test batteries became common for civil law, military affairs, revenue, and agriculture.
- During the **T'ang dynasty (618–906 AD)**, written examinations became the norm; a national multistage testing programme involved local, provincial, and national levels. Only those passing all three rounds were declared fit for public office.

### Spread to Western Countries

- The British government encouraged the East India Company as early as **1832** to follow the Chinese testing model for revenue services.
- Great Britain adopted a similar civil service testing system in **1855**.
- France and Germany followed afterward.
- The **US Civil Service Reform Act** was established in **1883**.

### The Psychophysics Movement (Pre-1850 to 1900)

- Before 1850, psychology was not independent of philosophy. After 1850, measurement of human behaviour through experiments was called **psychophysics**.
- Beginning in **1860**, psychophysicists began forming alliances with biological scientists.
- Three major influences on psychological measurement from 1850 to 1900:
  1. **Psychophysics**
  2. **Darwinian thinking**
  3. **Clinical work**

Key figures in early psychophysics and experimental psychology:

- **Franz Joseph Gall (1758–1828):** German physician who used the shape of the memory drum to test patients with brain injury; patients could recognize words but could not identify them when displayed via a moving dot.
- **Herman Ebbinghaus (1850–1909):** German psychologist who administered a battery of tests; the test took over 100 hours to administer and went out of use.
- **Wilhelm Wundt:** Established the **first psychological laboratory in 1879** at the University of Leipzig, Germany. Developed the "thought meter" (a calibrated pendulum with needle striking bells) to assess the swiftness of thought. The difference between the observed and actual pendulum position was used to determine quickness of thought.

The **Kinnebrook incident**: An assistant at the Royal Observatory at Greenwich was dismissed because his assessment of stellar crossing times was about **one second slower** than the astronomer's (Boring, 1950). This highlighted the problem of individual differences in reaction time.

### The Darwinian Influence and Galton

- Darwin's theory of evolution emphasized that members of a species differ, providing the conceptual basis for measuring **individual differences**.
- **Sir Francis Galton** (half-cousin of Darwin): First to systematically study individual differences in human beings. Focused on "human ability" as a dimension of difference. Laid the foundation for **psychometrics**.
- Galton attempted to measure intelligence via **reaction time (RT)** and **sensory discrimination tasks**.
- Key works:
  - *Hereditary Genius* (1869) — emphasized genetic factors in eminence.
  - *Inquiries into Human Faculty and its Development* (1883) — essays on individual differences in mental faculties.
- Galton set up a **psychometric laboratory in London at the International Health Exhibition in 1884**.
- Galton is regarded by many historians as the **father of mental testing** (Goodenough, 1949).

### Karl Pearson and J.M. Cattell

- **Karl Pearson** developed sophisticated statistical techniques for analyzing Galton's data, including the **product-moment method for computing the correlation coefficient**.
- **J.M. Cattell** studied with both Wundt and Galton. He introduced the term **"mental tests"** in **1890** in the paper *Mental Tests and Measurement*. He started his own research laboratory at Columbia University. His test battery was primarily sensory in character, reflecting Galtonian influence.
- **Clark Wissler (1901):** Among Cattell's students, showed that mental test scores had virtually no correlation with academic performance (e.g., RT and colour naming correlated -.03; colour naming and hand movement correlated only 0.19). This **deflected the mental testing movement away from brass instruments** and paved the way for Binet's approach.

### Alfred Binet and the Intelligence Test Movement

- **Alfred Binet (1905):** Tasked with measuring the general ability of school-going children in France. Developed a test to identify retarded children who could not benefit from regular instruction.
- The **Binet-Simon Intelligence Scale** was the first psychological test to appear — a ~30-question scale for measuring intelligence in children.
- Its **1912 revision** was notable: **Stern** introduced the concept of **IQ (Intelligence Quotient)**.

### The Six Periods of Mental Testing in the 20th Century

(Thorndike and Thorndike-Christ, 2011)

#### (i) The Early Period (1901–1915)

- Period of tentative exploration and discovery.
- Binet Scale originated in France and was revised twice by Binet.
- Goddard worked at the Training School for handicapped individuals in Vineland; the test was brought to the USA.
- **Arthur Otis** began work on group testing of mental ability.
- **S.D. Porters** developed a nonverbal test of intelligence in Australia for people with hearing or language handicaps.
- **Charles Spearman** (British psychologist): Developed two theories of human abilities. Proposed the **g factor (general intelligence)** — a single dimension of ability underlying all human performance.

#### (ii) The Boom Period (WWI era, up to ~1930)

- World War I created urgent need for large-scale psychological assessment.
- A group of psychologists led by **Robert Yerkes** developed two tests:
  - **Army Alpha test** — verbal test for literate recruits.
  - **Army Beta test** — non-verbal test for illiterates.
  - These tests were used for selecting officer trainees and screening out intellectually unfit military personnel.
- The **Woodworth Personal Data Sheet** — the **first objective measure of personality** — was developed during World War I.
- Post-war: **E.K. Strong** developed the **Strong Vocational Interest Blank (SVIB)** for assessing vocational interests.
- **L.L. Thurstone (1929):** Proposed methods of scaling for measuring attitudes and values.
- **Walter Lippmann** criticised both the tests and the theory underlying the mental testing movement.

#### (iii) The First Period of Criticism (1930s)

- New tests published: **Wechsler-Bellevue Intelligence Scale**, **Kuder Preference Record**, **Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)**.
- **L.L. Thurstone** developed the technique of **factor analysis** and the theory underlying it.
- **Oscar Buros** observed that many psychological and educational tests had no scientific merit. In **1938**, he started publishing critical reviews of tests (forerunner of the Mental Measurements Yearbook).

#### (iv) The Battery Period (1940s–1950s)

- Widespread use of **Multiple Aptitude Batteries** based on Thurstone's factor-analytic theory.
- **Bloom** and **Guilford** provided taxonomies of ability describing types of mental functioning.
- Psychological and educational testing became a major industry.
- Civil service systems, schools, and mental hospitals increasingly relied on test batteries.
- A **Central Aptitude Test Battery (CAT)** was developed.

#### (v) The Period of Accountability (from the 1960s)

- Some tests were challenged as **biased against minority groups** and against females (due to items favouring spatial and mechanical ability).
- The **Elementary and Secondary Education Act of 1965** (US) mandated that all public school students achieve high standards, increasing reliance on standardised tests.
- Legal proceedings were held in the USA over use of personality and ability tests in admission and hiring.

## Levels of Measurement (Measurement Scales)

### Postulates of Measurement

**Guilford (1954, p. 11)** outlined the general postulates of measurement scale under three headings:

1. Postulates relating to equalities or identities (3 variations)
2. Postulates relating to rank order (2 variations)
3. Postulates relating to additivity (4 variations)

**Total: 9 postulates**

| # | Postulate | Category |
|---|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Either a = b or a ≠ b (not both) | Equality |
| 2 | If a = b, then b = a (symmetry of equality) | Equality |
| 3 | If a = b and b = c, then a = c (transitivity of equality) | Equality |
| 4 | If a > b, then b ≠ a (asymmetry of rank) | Rank order |
| 5 | If a > b and b > c, then a > c (transitivity of rank) | Rank order |
| 6 | a + b = b + a (commutative law of addition) | Additivity |
| 7 | a + b + c = (a + b) + c = a + (b + c) (associative law) | Additivity |
| 8 | Identical numbers or objects may be substituted for one another without changing results | Additivity |
| 9 | If a = b and c = d, then a + c = b + d (substitution in addition) | Additivity |

Following **Stevens (1951)**, there are four levels of measurement, each differing in the statistical operations they permit.

---

### (i) Nominal or Classificatory Scale

- **Lowest level** of measurement.
- Numbers are used to **name, identify, or classify** persons, objects, or groups.
- Members within any group are equivalent; members across groups are not.
- Examples: classification by religion (Hindu, Muslim, Christian), sex (male/female), political party affiliation, clinical groups (schizophrenia, manic-depressive, neurosis, phobia).

**Permissible statistics:**
- Counting / frequency
- Percentage
- Proportion
- Mode
- Coefficient of contingency

**Not permissible:** Addition, subtraction, multiplication, division.

**Drawback:** Most elementary and least precise; some experts argue it is not measurement at all.

---

### (ii) Ordinal Scale

- **Second level** of measurement; has the property of **magnitude** but lacks equal intervals and absolute zero.
- Numbers denote **rank order** of objects/individuals (highest to lowest or vice versa).
- Reflects "greater than" or "less than" relationships.
- Examples: socio-economic status (upper, middle, lower class); student rank in class; ranks on any psychological trait.

**Does NOT assume:**
- Equal distances between ranks.
- Actual magnitude of differences between ranks.
- Absolute zero.

Example of the limitation: If X = 20, Y = 50, Z = 80 on a test, the difference between Y and X = 30, and between Z and Y = 30. Both differences are numerically equal but the underlying trait may not be equally spaced.

**Permissible statistics:**
- All nominal statistics, plus:
- Median
- Percentile

**Not permissible:** Arithmetic mean, standard deviation, Pearson r, parametric tests (because intervals are not equal).

**Drawback:** Ordinal measures are not absolute quantities; they cannot reflect the actual magnitude of difference or incorporate absolute zero.

---

### (iii) Interval (Equal-Interval) Scale

- **Third level** of measurement; includes all characteristics of nominal and ordinal scales.
- Additional characteristic: **equal intervals** — the unit of measurement remains constant throughout the scale.
- Numbers can be legitimately **added and subtracted**.
- Example: Scores 20, 16, 12, 8 on an interval scale — the difference A–C (20–8=12) equals B–D (16–4=12). The intervals are additive.

**Key limitation — arbitrary zero point:**
- The zero point is **arbitrary** and does not represent true absence of the trait.
- Ratios of magnitudes are therefore meaningless.
- A score of 0 on a numerical aptitude test does not mean zero numerical ability.
- A score of 80 on an IQ test does not mean twice the intelligence of a score of 40.

**Permissible statistics:**
- Arithmetic mean
- Standard deviation
- Pearson product-moment correlation (r)
- t-test
- Other parametric tests of significance

**Not permissible:** Coefficient of variation (because it is a ratio of SD to mean, and the mean shifts with any change in the arbitrary zero point).

**Examples:** Intelligence tests, aptitude tests, most psychological tests.

---

### (iv) Ratio Scale

- **Highest level** of measurement; has all properties of nominal, ordinal, and interval scales, plus an **absolute (true) zero point**.
- The ratio of any two numbers is independent of the unit of measurement (e.g., 4:8 = 2:4; 10/28 = 4/7... [note: 10/28 ≠ 4/7; the text uses this as an illustrative example of ratio consistency]).
- All four postulates of measurement can be applied.
- **All statistical operations** including the coefficient of variation can be computed.

**Examples:** Weight, width, length — physical measures.
- A stick 6 feet long is twice as long as a stick 3 feet long (true zero makes the ratio meaningful).
- 40°C is NOT twice as warm as 20°C, because 0°C is not a true zero point (that is why temperature in Celsius/Fahrenheit is an **interval** scale, not a ratio scale).

**Key distinction from interval scale:** In interval scale, zero point is arbitrary; in ratio scale, zero point is absolute (true zero).

## Properties of Scales of Measurement

Three properties differentiate scales from one another:

1. **Magnitude:** The property of "moreness." A scale has magnitude if, for any two individuals, we can determine which represents more, less, or an equal amount of the attribute ("greater than or equal to" relationship).

2. **Equal Intervals:** A scale has equal intervals if the difference between any two points on the scale has the same meaning as the difference between two other points that differ by the same number of units. Example: the difference between 4 kg and 6 kg = the difference between 10 kg and 12 kg (both = 2 kg).

3. **Absolute Zero:** A scale has an absolute zero if zero on the scale represents the true and complete absence of the attribute being measured. Only ratio scales possess this property.

## Functions of Measurement

### In Selection

Measurement tools predict the ability of individuals for personnel selection in industry and institutions. They also provide data for disciplinary decisions (training, dismissal, suspension).

### In Classification

Measurement helps classify individuals into categories. Examples:
- School psychologist classifying problem children as: remedial, borderline, backward.
- Clinical psychologist classifying patients into diagnostic categories.
- Industry classifying personnel by job satisfaction, accident proneness, absenteeism.

### In Comparison

Measurement enables comparison of individuals or groups on traits, mental processes, habits, tendencies, and educational achievement. Reveals individual differences (per the pioneering work of Galton and Darwin).

### In Guidance and Counselling

Measurement helps individuals know their strengths and weaknesses, supports counsellor-client understanding, enables prediction of future adjustment problems, and diagnoses mental disabilities and deficiencies.

### In Research

Measurement is the fundamental basis of all psychological and educational research. It enables control of variables, accurate design, statistical calculations, and valid results.

### In Improving Classroom Instruction

By measuring the outcome of instruction, teachers can determine how many students benefit from instruction and make quantitative and qualitative modifications accordingly.

## Distinction between Psychological Measurement and Physical Measurement

| Dimension | Physical Measurement | Psychological Measurement |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **Unit of measurement** | Fixed and constant (e.g., 1 metre is always 1 metre) | Not fixed; varies by method (IQ, manipulative tasks, speed) and examiner |
| **Zero point** | True (absolute) zero — zero length means no length | Arbitrary (relative) zero — zero score does not mean complete absence of trait |
| **Accuracy and predictability** | More accurate and predictable (e.g., disk of 60° is thrice as long as 20°) | Less accurate; ratio statements about psychological traits are invalid |
| **Directness** | Direct — length, weight, height measured directly | Indirect — traits like extraversion measured through observable responses (verbal, manipulative) |
| **Completeness** | Entire quantity can be measured (e.g., all tables in a room) | Only a sample representing the trait is measured (census of all class X students in Bihar is impractical) |

## Steps in Measurement Process

Three common steps in any measurement (psychological or physical):

### (i) Identifying and Defining the Quality or Attribute

- First, select the **relevant attribute** to be measured (e.g., for truck drivers: eye-hand coordination, depth perception, motor accident proneness — not verbal comprehension).
- Then, **define the attribute precisely**. Abstract, hard-to-observe properties are called **constructs** (e.g., intelligence). The definition must specify what behaviour counts as the attribute.

### (ii) Determining Operations for Isolating and Displaying the Attribute

- Develop a set of **operations** that isolate the attribute and correctly display it.
- When an attribute is defined by how it is measured, it is said to have an **operational definition**.
- Physical measurement: straightforward (e.g., a ruler to measure length).
- Psychological measurement: complex, because:
  - Intelligence is multi-faceted.
  - No single universally accepted test of intelligence exists.
  - Different tests include different tasks.
  - Ambiguity in what procedures correctly elicit intelligent behaviour.

### (iii) Quantifying the Attribute

- Express the operations in **quantitative terms** by assigning numerical values according to a set of rules.
- Two key advantages of using numerical values:
  1. Enables **communication** — makes description meaningful and precise (e.g., "Ram's height is 5 ft, weight is 65 kg").
  2. Allows **mathematical operations** (adding, subtracting, finding averages) for summarizing quantitative information.

---

## Problems Related to the Measurement Process

Three categories of problems, corresponding to the three steps:

1. **Problem in defining the attribute:** For complex constructs (intelligence, anxiety, adjustment, cooperativeness), there is diversity in definition. Multiple sub-abilities may or may not be included (e.g., for intelligence: ability to memorize, to profit from past experience, to reason abstractly, to adjust to situations, to generalize from events).

2. **Problem in devising measurement procedures:** For non-cognitive domains (client's anxiety, fear, cooperativeness), no satisfactory standardized measuring procedures exist, whereas for cognitive abilities standardized procedures are available.

3. **Problem of equality of units:** In psychological measurement, units are not equal by definition (e.g., an analogy item in an intelligence test is not the same "unit" as an arithmetic series item). This makes addition and subtraction of scores suspect.

---

## General Problems of Measurement

### (i) Indirectness of Measurement (Inadequateness)

Most psychological and educational variables cannot be observed directly. Measurement of intelligence, for example, depends on a **sample of behaviour** representative of intelligent acts. This sample may itself be unreliable, invalid, impractical, or not a true representative of the behaviour being measured. Measurement of any trait thus becomes perpetually difficult.

### (ii) Incompleteness of Measurement

Psychological and educational measures are generally **incomplete**. For example, measuring attitude towards co-education requires constructing a scale with samples of relevant behaviour — but the number of possible behaviour samples is unlimited. Any measurement is therefore partial and incomplete, creating a misleading index of the attitude.

### (iii) Relativity of Measurement

Psychological and educational measurements are **relative**, not absolute. A student's score of 60% in English is meaningful only in relation to a reference group. If no one else got below 90%, that 60% is actually a poor performance. Similarly, a score of zero on an arithmetic test does not mean zero arithmetic knowledge — the test does not reflect absolute knowledge level. Measurement must always be compared against a reference group to be meaningful.

## Sources of Errors in Measurement

Five sources of errors:

| Source | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **(i) Respondent** | Respondent may be reluctant to report true feelings, or may not express himself clearly, lowering accuracy. |
| **(ii) Observer (Abstruseness)** | The behaviour, style, and looks of the measurer/observer can influence respondent's replies (encouraging or discouraging certain types of answers). |
| **(iii) Measure (Researcher)** | Incorrect coding, faulty tabulation, inappropriate statistical analysis by the supervisor/researcher introduces errors. |
| **(iv) Situation** | Situational factors (unnecessary strain, lack of anonymity) — e.g., an interview situation — introduce errors. |
| **(v) Test Instrument** | Poor psychometric qualities (poor reliability and validity) of the psychological instrument introduce measurement errors. |

## Difference among Assessment, Testing and Measurement

| Term | Definition | Answered Question |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Assessment** | General term; includes paper-and-pencil tests, extended responses, performance tasks, laboratory experiments, teacher observation, and self-evaluation. Evaluates current and future functioning against normative standards. | "How well does the individual perform?" |
| **Testing** | Systematic procedure for measuring a sample of behaviour by putting questions in a uniform manner. Tests are a form of assessment. A technique is called a **test** only when: (1) administration, scoring, and interpretation are standardized; (2) there is a standardization sample; (3) there is evidence of reliability and validity. | "How well does the individual perform in comparison with others or at another time?" |
| **Measurement** | Process of obtaining a **numerical description** of the degree to which an individual possesses a particular characteristic, according to some rules. | "How much?" |

Key distinction: Not all assessment techniques are tests. Many so-called tests are more appropriately called assessment devices because they fail to meet minimum requirements (standardization, normative sample, reliability/validity evidence).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Nunnally's (1972) definition of measurement**: "Rules for assigning numbers to objects to represent quantities of attributes."

2. **Four levels of measurement** in ascending order: Nominal → Ordinal → Interval → Ratio. Stevens (1951) proposed this classification.

3. **Nominal scale** — only permissible statistics: frequency, percentage, proportion, mode, coefficient of contingency. Arithmetic operations are not permissible.

4. **Ordinal scale** — adds median and percentile to permissible statistics; does not have equal intervals or absolute zero.

5. **Interval scale** — has equal intervals but arbitrary zero; permits arithmetic mean, SD, Pearson r, t-test; does NOT permit coefficient of variation.

6. **Ratio scale** — only scale with true/absolute zero; permits all statistical operations including coefficient of variation; common in physical measurement, rare in psychological measurement.

7. **The coefficient of variation** is NOT applicable to interval scale data because the mean shifts with changes in the arbitrary zero point, invalidating the ratio SD/mean.

8. **Galton** is the father of mental testing; established the London psychometric laboratory in 1884; measured individual differences; influenced Pearson and Cattell.

9. **Wundt** founded the first psychological laboratory in **1879** at Leipzig, Germany.

10. **J.M. Cattell** coined the term "mental tests" in **1890** in the paper *Mental Tests and Measurement*.

11. **Clark Wissler (1901)** showed RT and colour naming correlated only -.03, and effectively ended the brass-instrument approach to mental testing.

12. **Army Alpha** = verbal test; **Army Beta** = non-verbal test for illiterates; developed in WWI under Robert Yerkes.

13. **Stern** introduced the concept of **IQ** in the 1912 revision of the Binet-Simon Scale.

14. **Assessment vs. Testing vs. Measurement**: Assessment = "How well?"; Testing = standardized form of assessment, requires standardization sample + reliability/validity evidence; Measurement = "How much?" (numerical description only).

15. **Three general problems of psychological measurement**: (1) Indirectness — traits cannot be observed directly; (2) Incompleteness — only a behaviour sample is captured; (3) Relativity — scores are meaningful only relative to a reference group.
