# Sampling

Chapter 14 covers the theory and practice of selecting subsets from populations for research, classifying all major sampling strategies into probability, nonprobability, and mixed categories. It defines foundational concepts (statistic, parameter, sampling error, confidence interval) and evaluates the advantages and disadvantages of each method in detail. The chapter also explains how to draw random samples and how to estimate population parameters from sample statistics.

---

## Meaning and Types of Sampling

A **population** (also called **universe**) is a well-specified and identifiable group from which a researcher wishes to draw conclusions. Examples include all high school teachers, all college students, all housewives.

A **sample** is any number of persons selected to represent the population according to some rule or plan. It is the smaller representation of the population.

A **parameter** is a measure based upon the entire population.

A **statistic** is a measure based upon a sample. A statistic is frequently referred to as an estimated parameter.

### Population Types

| Dimension | Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Size | **Finite population** | All members can be counted | University teachers |
| Size | **Infinite population** | Size is unlimited; members cannot all be counted | Fish in a river |
| Existence | **Real population** | Actually exists | Population of a city |
| Existence | **Imaginative population** | Exists only in the imagination | Hypothetical test-takers |

### Definition of Probability (Basis of Sampling Theory)

**Probability** in sampling theory is used as equivalent to **relative frequency**. It is expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage. For example, the probability of a tail on a single coin toss = 1/2 = 0.5 means that over many tosses, relative frequency of a tail will be approximately 0.5.

### Classification of Sampling Methods

Following **Blalock (1960)**, most sampling methods can be categorized into two broad types:

**(A) Probability Sampling Methods**
**(B) Nonprobability Sampling Methods**

An additional mixed category exists:
**(C) Mixed Sampling**

---

## (A) Probability Sampling Methods

**Probability sampling methods** are those that clearly specify the probability or likelihood of inclusion of each element or individual in the sample.

### Conditions That Must Be Satisfied

1. The size of the parent population or universe must be known to the investigator.
2. Each element or individual in the population must have an equal chance of being included in the sample.
3. The desired sample size must be clearly specified.

If a population has 500 elements, each individual has a 1/500 chance of being selected.

### Positive Points of Probability Sampling

- Chosen samples are considered representative.
- Conclusions are worth generalization and are comparable to similar populations.

### Negative Points of Probability Sampling

- A certain amount of **sampling error** exists because the researcher has only a limited element of the entire population.
- Sampling error refers to the degree to which sample characteristics approximate characteristics of the parent population.
- The smaller the sample, the greater the sampling error.

### Types of Probability Sampling

1. Simple random sampling
2. Stratified random sampling
   - (a) Proportionate stratified random sampling
   - (b) Disproportionate stratified random sampling
3. Area or cluster sampling

---

## (B) Nonprobability Sampling Methods

**Nonprobability sampling** is a method in which there is no way of assessing the probability of elements or groups of elements being included in the sample. Such samples do not provide a basis for estimating how representative sample characteristics are of the population parameters because they do not use techniques of random selection.

### Types of Nonprobability Sampling

1. Quota sampling
2. Accidental sampling
3. Judgemental or purposive sampling
4. Snowball sampling
5. Saturation sampling
6. Dense sampling

---

## (C) Mixed Sampling

**Systematic sampling** is classified as a mixed sampling method, incorporating features of both probability and nonprobability methods.

---

## Need for Sampling

Sampling is needed for the following important reasons:

1. **Economy of time and money**: A research study based on sampling is completed within lesser time and incurs less expenditure than a study based on the entire population.
2. **Accuracy in measurement**: Sampling studies are generally conducted by trained and experienced investigators, providing greater accuracy.
3. **Necessity with large populations**: Sampling remains the only feasible way when a population is very large or practically infinite.
4. **Making inferences about populations**: Sampling is needed to make judgments about the composition of the population and to allow generalization about population members.

## Fundamentals of Sampling

### (1) Statistic and Parameter

- **Statistic**: A statistical value calculated from a sample. Example: the mean calculated from a sample.
- **Parameter**: A statistical value based on the entire population. Example: the arithmetic mean of the whole population.
- The aim of sampling is to obtain an estimate of a parameter from a statistic.

### (2) Sampling Error

**Sampling error** is the error that occurs because a particular sample is drawn from a population rather than studying the whole population. It is the error that occurs due to chance because a portion of the population is studied instead of the whole.

Formal definition (Lindquist, 1940, p. 8): "A sampling error is the difference between a parameter and an estimate of that parameter which is derived from a sample."

Key points:
- The term "sampling error" does not imply that any mistake has been committed.
- It refers to chance fluctuations in values that are inevitable when randomly selected samples are taken from the same population.
- The amount of fluctuation in the sample mean depends on two factors:
  - (i) Variability in the population
  - (ii) Size of the sample

### (3) Confidence Interval and Population Mean

**Confidence interval** is an interval estimate based on sampling theory that gives a range within which the population parameter is expected to fall with a specified probability.

- **95% confidence interval**: 95% of sample means will be within ±1.96 standard errors of the population mean.
- **99% confidence interval**: 99% of sample means will be within ±2.58 standard errors of the population mean.
- **68% confidence interval**: Sample means within ±1 standard error of the population mean.

**Confidence limits** are the values defining the upper and lower boundaries of the confidence interval.

**Confidence level**: If the confidence level is 95%, then 100 - 95 = 5% or 0.05 indicates the rejection region (alpha level).

#### Formulas for Confidence Intervals

**95% CI:**
```
CI₉₅ = X̄ ± 1.96 × SE
```

**99% CI:**
```
CI₉₉ = X̄ ± 2.58 × SE
```

**68% CI:**
```
CI₆₈ = X̄ ± 1.00 × SE
```

Where SE = Standard Error of the Mean

#### Worked Example

Given: Sample mean (X̄) = 120, SD = 20, N = 100, SE = 2

- **95% CI**: 120 - 1.96(2) = **116.08** to 120 + 1.96(2) = **123.92**
- **99% CI**: 120 - 2.58(2) = **114.84** to 120 + 2.58(2) = **125.16**

The normal curve percentages for standard deviations:
- 1 SD from mean: 68% (approximately 67.26%)
- 2 SD from mean: 95% (approximately 95.44%)
- 3 SD from mean: 99% (approximately 99.74%)

### (4) Sampling Design

A **sampling design** is a plan for obtaining a sample from a sampling frame.

Characteristics of a good sampling design:
1. Must provide a sample that is the best representative of the population.
2. Should result in small sampling error.
3. Must allow for controlling systematic bias in sampling.
4. Must be viable in light of available funds.
5. Results must be applicable to the population with a reasonable confidence level.

There are two broad types of sample designs: probability sampling and nonprobability sampling.

### (5) Point Estimation vs. Interval Estimation

**Point estimation**: When researchers estimate parameters using a single sample value. A single sample value drawn from a population provides an estimate of the population parameter.

**Interval estimation**: Estimating the range of possible values that are likely to include the population mean. Social scientists generally prefer interval estimates over point estimates.

The **confidence interval** is the interval within which a parameter is hypothesized to be tenable. The **confidence limits** are the values defining the boundaries of this interval. The greater the size of the interval, the greater is the feeling of certainty that the value is encompassed between these limits.

## Principles of Sampling

There are three fundamental principles of sampling:

**Principle 1:** In most cases of sampling, there will be a difference between the sample statistic and the true population parameter, attributable to the selection of units in the sample.

*Illustration:* Given 5 individuals with ages 20, 22, 19, 24, 20 (population mean = 21 years), selecting all possible samples of size 2 (n=10 combinations: AB, AC, AD, AE, BC, BD, BE, CD, CE, DE) shows that each sample mean differs from the population mean of 21.

| Sample | Mean | Difference from Population Mean (21) |
|---|---|---|
| A+B | 21.0 | 0 |
| A+C | 19.5 | -1.5 |
| A+D | 22.0 | +1 |
| A+E | 20.0 | -1 |
| B+C | 20.5 | -0.5 |
| B+D | 23.0 | +2 |
| B+E | 21.0 | 0 |
| C+D | 21.5 | +0.5 |
| C+E | 19.5 | -1.5 |
| D+E | 22.0 | +1 |

This demonstrates that sample means vary around the true population parameter.

**Principle 2:** Parameters of a population are not ordinarily known. Estimates about population parameters are made in the form of sampling distributions. If the sample distribution is normal, we expect the population distribution to also be normal.

**Principle 3:** Random sampling provides a good approximation to the population, and the accuracy of this approximation depends on the size of the sample. As sample size increases, the sample distribution approaches the population distribution more closely.

---

## Aims of Sampling

The aims of sampling are:

1. To obtain a sample that is **representative** of the population it purports to represent.
2. **Consistency and reproducibility** of answers across samples.
3. Flexibility in research design.
4. Ensures every unit in the specified population has a specified probability of being selected.
5. Allows the researcher to compute **unbiased estimates** of population parameters and compute sampling errors.

---

## Factors Influencing Decision to Sample

The decision to sample rather than conduct a census is influenced by:

1. When a census costs too much or is wasteful of time.
2. When the data are to be used for estimating characteristics of the population.
3. When **destructive testing** is required (as in quality control inspection) and it is impossible to test all items.
4. Convenience, since a sample is a small portion of the population and is easier to manage.

---

## How Large Should a Sample Be?

Key principles regarding sample size:

- **Samples of 30 or more** are usually considered **large samples**; those fewer than 30 are considered **small samples**.
- At a sample size of 30, the magnitude of Student's t values begins to approach the Z critical values of the normal probability table used for large samples.
- **Samples larger than necessary** can also produce problems: higher costs, administrative and logistic burdens.
- A sample of **100 and above** is considered adequate for most educational research. Beyond this threshold, gains in sampling adequacy become increasingly slow.
- For subgroup analyses, the sample from each subgroup must also be sufficiently large to be representative of that subgroup.

## Methods of Drawing Random Samples

Randomness is a major concern in sample selection. Three methods ensure randomness:

### 1. Fishbowl Draw Method

- Elements/individuals of the population are labelled on pieces of equal size.
- Pieces are placed in a bowl.
- A blindfolded person or child draws the required number of units.
- Simple to apply but limited by population size.

### 2. Table of Random Numbers Method

- Assign numbers from 1 to N to all elements in the population.
- Enter the table of random numbers at any point.
- Move systematically (right, left, up, down, or diagonally) through the table.
- Select elements whose numbers appear; skip numbers exceeding N, duplicates, and 00.
- **Most popular method** for ensuring randomness in sample selection.
- Limitation: becomes difficult when population size exceeds 5 digits.

**Illustration (Table 14.1):** From a population of 80 (numbered 01-80), a sample of N=10 is drawn. Numbers exceeding 80 (83, 84, 97, 93), duplicates (01, 74, 17), and 00 are all excluded.

### 3. Computer-Determined Randomness Method

- Used when the size of the population is large.
- Data is fed into a computer to obtain a random sequence of N elements.
- Easiest to adopt when computer facilities are available.

## Simple Random Sample

A **simple random sample** (also known as an **unrestricted random sample**) is one in which:
- Each and every individual in the population has an **equal chance** of being included.
- The selection of one individual is **in no way dependent** upon the selection of another individual.

**Practical procedure:** Write names or roll numbers of all N individuals on equal-sized, equal-coloured pieces of paper; place in a box; reshuffle; a blindfolded person draws the required number.

**Sampling with replacement:** When an element once drawn is returned to the population before the next draw. For a population of 4 (A, B, C, D), sampling with replacement yields 4² = 16 possible samples of size 2 (AA, AB, AC, AD, BA, BB, ..., DD). In practice, cases like AA, BB, CC, DD (same element drawn twice) are ignored.

**Biased sample:** A sample drawn in a way that does not ensure equal chance for all elements, thereby increasing or decreasing the likelihood of particular elements being included.

### Advantages of Simple Random Sampling

1. The sample is regarded as **representative** of the population because all elements have equal and independent chances of inclusion.
2. The investigator need **not know the true composition** of the population beforehand.
3. It is the **easiest and simplest** technique among all probability sampling plans.
4. It serves as the **foundation** upon which all other types of random sampling are based.
5. **Sampling error** associated with any sample can be easily assessed.
6. The investigator does **not commit classification error** because prior knowledge of population characteristics is not required.

**Classification error**: The error resulting from improper classification of population characteristics or segments.

### Disadvantages of Simple Random Sampling

1. Does **not ensure** that elements existing in small numbers in the population will be included.
2. Does **not fully exploit** the investigator's knowledge about population segments (e.g., known proportions of males/females, graduates/undergraduates, high SES/low SES).
3. **Sampling error** is generally larger than in a stratified random sample of the same size, because stratified sampling reduces heterogeneity within each stratum.

## Stratified Random Sample

In **stratified random sampling**, the population is first divided into a number of **subgroups or strata**. A random sample is then drawn from each stratum.

**Rationale:** When the population consists of subgroups that differ with respect to the variable under study, simple random sampling may not give adequate representation to all strata. By stratifying and sampling from each stratum, the composition of the sample can be made to resemble the composition of the whole population.

The strata are **non-overlapping** categories.

---

### (A) Proportionate Stratified Random Sampling

**Proportionate stratified random sampling** is a technique in which the sample drawn from each stratum is in the **same proportion** as the stratum is in the total population. The **same sampling fraction** is used for every stratum.

**Formula:**
```
Sample from stratum = (N_stratum / N_total) × n_sample
```

**Example (Table 14.1 and 14.2):**

Population of 10,000 students:

| Class | N | Proportion |
|---|---|---|
| PhD | 1,000 | 0.10 |
| MA | 2,000 | 0.20 |
| BA | 7,000 | 0.70 |
| **Total** | **10,000** | **1.00** |

Sample of 100 students:

| Class | Sample N | Calculation |
|---|---|---|
| PhD | 10 | 1,000 × 100/10,000 |
| MA | 20 | 2,000 × 100/10,000 |
| BA | 70 | 7,000 × 100/10,000 |
| **Total** | **100** | |

Key properties:
1. Uses the **same sampling fraction** for each stratum.
2. Preserves the **proportional characteristics** of the population in the sample.
3. Gives a **self-weighting sample**: the overall sample mean is an **unbiased estimate** of the population mean.
4. Also called **proportionate allocation**.

#### Disadvantages of Proportionate Stratified Random Sampling

1. **Time-consuming**: Samples are drawn according to the proportion of each stratum, requiring careful calculation and execution.
2. Risk of **classification error**: The investigator may misclassify an element into the wrong stratum, affecting validity and reliability.

---

### (B) Disproportionate Stratified Random Sampling

**Disproportionate stratified random sampling** is similar to proportionate stratified sampling, but the substrata are **not necessarily sampled according to their proportionate weight** in the population. Some strata may be overrepresented and others underrepresented.

This occurs in two ways:
- (a) The investigator gives **equal weight to each substratum** regardless of its actual size.
- (b) The investigator gives **greater representation to some substrata** and not enough to others.

**Example:** Population of 10,000 with 6,000 males and 4,000 females. Drawing a sample of 1,000:
- **Proportionate**: 600 males + 400 females (reflects true population ratio)
- **Disproportionate (equal weight)**: 500 males + 500 females (overrepresents females, underrepresents males)

#### Advantages of Disproportionate Stratified Random Sampling

1. **Less time-consuming** than proportionate stratified sampling because the investigator does not need to maintain proportionate representation.
2. Allows the investigator to give **appropriate weight to particular groups** that are underrepresented in the population (gives adequate sample size for rare subgroups).

#### Disadvantages of Disproportionate Stratified Random Sampling

1. Certain strata are **overrepresented** and others **underrepresented**, potentially introducing **bias** into the sample.
2. Requires the investigator to **know the composition** of the original population.
3. Risk of **misclassification error**: placing an element into the wrong stratum.

Despite these limitations, disproportionate stratified random sampling is **common in social science research** and is **more popular than proportionate** stratified sampling because of ease and convenience.

## Area (or Cluster) Sampling

**Area (or cluster) sampling** is a probability sampling method where **geographical divisions** of territories (communities, neighbourhoods, counties, etc.) are made on a map, and a certain number of these geographical units are selected (randomly or non-randomly) to represent all elements.

**Origin**: Area sampling originated in the field of agriculture (farming experiments on fertilizers, soil treatments, and crop varieties). It has been extensively used in social sciences, geography, and rural research.

**Key feature**: Each selected section/cluster represents all elements of the geographical unit, which is why it is also known as **cluster sampling**.

**Example:** To study attitudes of Tamil Nadu residents toward family planning:
1. Divide total area into sections.
2. Randomly select sections (using table of random numbers).
3. Study all persons/families in selected sections.
4. If sections contain different types of families, apply additional random selection within each section.

This process of multiple-stage selection is called **multi-stage sampling** (attributed to **Moser**).

### Research Applications of Area Sampling

1. **Large-scale surveys** of political, religious, and social behaviour.
2. Where **complete lists of specific individuals** are unattainable or too costly to obtain.
3. **Public opinion polls**.

### Advantages of Area Sampling

1. **Easier for large geographical areas**: The investigator only needs a listing of families/people in a given area; simply draws geographical units and interviews all persons in randomly drawn locations.
2. **Respondents can be substituted**: Since clusters of elements (not individuals) are sampled, respondents within the same section can replace others.
3. **Saves time and money**: The investigator need not travel widely; the cost is much less compared to other probability sampling methods.

## Quota Sampling

**Quota sampling** is a nonprobability sampling method. It is described as a "common and poor version of stratified sampling."

**Procedure:**
1. The investigator divides the population into different groups according to specified characteristics (age, sex, occupation, etc.).
2. The investigator determines the number of individuals (**quota**) to be included from each group.
3. Interviewers then find respondents who fit the criteria until the quota is reached.

**Example:** Selecting 1,000 people from a large city across 10 age groups (below 15, 15-25, 25-35, ..., 95+) with proportional quotas: Below 15=100, 15-25=200, 25-35=200, 35-45=150, 45-55=100, 55-65=80, 65-75=70, 75-85=50, 85-95=30, 95+=20.

**Key characteristic**: Quota sampling is a combination of stratified and purposive sampling strategies.

### Advantages of Quota Sampling

1. **Convenience** and ease of administration.
2. **Economy**: Less costly than more complex sampling designs.
3. Can be used when complete population lists are unavailable.
4. Allows representation of specific subgroups in the sample.

### Limitations of Quota Sampling

The notable limitation is that the **selection of respondents by interviewers may result in a biased sample** because the interviewer, not a random process, ultimately chooses who participates.

---

## Purposive or Judgemental Sampling

**Purposive or judgemental sampling** is a nonprobability sampling procedure whereby the researcher **deliberately selects units to be sampled based on his/her knowledge and professional judgment**.

The researcher has their own bias and judgment in selecting the sample from the population. For example, a researcher may select respondents whom they believe are capable and well-qualified members of the group.

**Usefulness**: Especially useful when:
- The population is small.
- The researcher needs results from highly specific, expert, or typical informants.

Two procedures exist within purposive sampling:
1. **Quota-guided purposive procedure**: The investigator has the freedom to select individuals from each cell of the population according to his/her judgment.
2. **Criteria-based purposive procedure**: The researcher selects the sample according to specified criteria aligned with the research purpose.

**Key distinction**: In quota sampling, proportional representation of the population is targeted; in purposive sampling, a hand-picked sample of the population is selected based on researcher judgment.

---

## Accidental Sampling

**Accidental sampling** is the most **unrestricted** form of sampling. Those persons who happen to be available to the researcher are selected as the sample. Individuals are chosen because they are in the right place at the right time where the researcher is conducting the study.

Since the sample is selected out of convenience, this is also known as **convenience sampling**.

**Examples**: Conducting exit surveys; polling people in a shopping centre who happen to walk by; interviewing those available near the research site.

**Properties**:
- Possesses the **least amount of control** of all sampling methods.
- **Most convenient** and **least time-consuming** method.
- **Most unreliable** method.
- Data accumulated can be **biased and unreliable**.
- Can be used to collect basic data and opinions.
- Useful in exploratory studies.

---

## Snowball Sampling

**Snowball sampling** is a nonprobability method used to find **hard-to-reach or hidden populations**.

**Procedure:**
1. An individual or small group with certain characteristics is identified.
2. That person is asked to recommend others for the study who share the same characteristics.
3. Those individuals identify still others, and so on.
4. The sample grows progressively larger, like a snowball gathering snow as it rolls downhill.

**Based on the concept**: Individuals usually know others of their kind (social network referral).

**Usefulness**: Particularly useful when:
- The population of interest is small and specific.
- There is **no known way to systematically identify** qualifying individuals.
- Reaching hidden or stigmatised populations.

### Disadvantages of Snowball Sampling

1. It is **not possible to determine sampling error** or make statistical inferences from the sample to the whole population.
2. The sample **may not be representative** of the total population.
3. Difficult to determine whether individuals are being **left out or over-represented** because the researcher lacks complete knowledge of the population.

---

## Saturation Sampling and Dense Sampling

### Saturation Sampling

**Saturation sampling** is a nonprobability method in which the researcher tries to **include as many available subjects as possible**. The whole population of a limited group is used as the sample.

**Example**: A school has 1,000 students; the researcher selects 900 or 950 students.

### Dense Sampling

**Dense sampling** is a method in which **all or nearly all members of the accessible population** are included in the sample. It requires a great deal of time, money, and resources.

**Appropriate when**: The number of accessible population is not very large.

### Comparison: Saturation vs. Dense Sampling

| Feature | Saturation Sampling | Dense Sampling |
|---|---|---|
| Category | Nonprobability | Nonprobability |
| Goal | Include as many subjects as possible | Include nearly all accessible population members |
| Scope | Whole limited group used as sample | Near-census of accessible population |
| Resource requirement | Moderate | Very high |
| Use in practice | Rare | Rare |

Both provide a **census-like account** of the accessible population. Both satisfy the needs of certain types of researchers in particular situations but are **rarely used in practice**.

## Requisites of a Good Sampling Method

A good sample must fulfil two basic conditions:
- (a) It must be a **true representative** of the population.
- (b) It must have **adequate size**.

The requisites of a good sampling method are:

1. **Representativeness**: The sample must ensure that it is representative of the population. A representative sample contains the relevant characteristics of the population in the same proportions as they exist in the total population.
2. **Adequacy**: The sample must be of adequate size. A sample too small will fail to include relevant population characteristics in adequate amounts.

As a general rule, the larger the sample, the more reliable the results and the more likely it is to represent the total population.

Note: There is no guarantee that any given sample will be perfectly representative. The statistician's way to achieve representativeness is by **random selection** of subjects.

As stated by a cited authority (1974): "Inadequate representation is usually the result of careless or improper procedures in selecting the sample."

General merits of sampling as a method of studying problems in education and psychology:

1. Provides focus on the group of interest.
2. **Economy**: Economical in terms of time, money, and energy.
3. Convenience in conducting the study.
4. Allows better control of confounding factors.
5. Makes more thorough investigation possible within available resources.

## Sampling Distribution

**Sampling distribution** is a theoretical or hypothetical frequency distribution that lists all possible distributions of a statistic calculated from all possible samples of a given size actually taken from the population.

**Key concepts:**

- If we take all possible samples of a given size from a population (e.g., 100 samples of 100 parents each), we get 100 different sample means and 100 different standard deviations.
- If these 100 means are grouped into a frequency distribution, we get the **sampling distribution of means**.
- If these 100 standard deviations are grouped, we get the **sampling distribution of standard deviations**.
- The sampling distribution is a distribution of **standardized statistics** (mean of means = 0, standard deviation = 1).

**Types of distributions in sampling:**

| Distribution | Description |
|---|---|
| **Population distribution** | Distribution based on the entire population; parameters (mean, SD) are usually unknown |
| **Sample distribution** | An observed frequency distribution based on a single drawn sample |
| **Sampling distribution** | Theoretical distribution of all possible values of a sample statistic across all possible samples of a given size |

**Important inference**: If the sample distribution is normal, it can be inferred that the population distribution is also normal, and the parameters of the sampling distribution can be used to make inferences about the population.

**Illustration**: A random sample of 100 men with resting heart rate: mean = 70, SD = 10. This sample distribution is a basis for inferring the population distribution of resting heart rates.

## Sampling Error

**Sampling error** is the difference between the parameter and the statistic.

**Formal definition (Lindquist, 1940, p. 8):** "A sampling error is the difference between a parameter and an estimate of that parameter which is derived from a sample."

```
Sampling Error = Parameter - Statistic
```

A **sampling problem** exists whenever conclusions from a limited number of individuals (sample) are applied to a larger number (population). Such generalised conclusions are called **statistical inferences**.

- The **higher** the sampling error, the **poorer** the statistical inference.
- Sampling error does NOT imply a mistake was committed; it refers to **inevitable chance fluctuations** in values when randomly selected samples are drawn from the same population.

### Factors Affecting Sampling Error

1. **Variability in the population**: If the population is naturally unstable and tends to vary, successive samples will differ more.
2. **Size of the sample**: As the size of the sample increases, the sampling error decreases and the sample better reflects the population.

### Applications of Sampling Distribution to Sampling Error

The sampling distribution is used to:
1. Determine the **probability** that a particular sample value has resulted from sampling fluctuations.
2. Judge the **worthiness of a hypothesis** regarding the values of statistics in a population.

When the sampling distribution of a mean is normal, the investigator can determine the probability that any given sample value will occur in random sampling from a population.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Blalock (1960)** classified sampling methods into probability and nonprobability categories. This is the most cited classification in the chapter.

2. **Sampling error definition (Lindquist, 1940)**: "A sampling error is the difference between a parameter and an estimate of that parameter which is derived from a sample." The formula is: Sampling Error = Parameter - Statistic.

3. **Statistic vs. Parameter**: Statistic = measure from a sample; Parameter = measure from the whole population.

4. **Three conditions for probability sampling**: (i) population size known, (ii) each element has equal chance, (iii) desired sample size clearly specified.

5. **Quota sampling** is defined as "a common and poor version of stratified sampling" and is a **nonprobability** method, unlike stratified random sampling (which is probability).

6. **Proportionate vs. Disproportionate Stratified Sampling**: Proportionate uses the same sampling fraction; gives a self-weighting sample. Disproportionate overrepresents some strata; more popular in practice due to ease and convenience.

7. **Confidence interval Z-values**: 95% CI uses ±1.96 SE; 99% CI uses ±2.58 SE; 68% CI uses ±1.00 SE.

8. **Accidental sampling = Convenience sampling**: Most unreliable, least time-consuming; least amount of control; used in exit surveys and shopping centre polls.

9. **Snowball sampling** is used for **hard-to-reach or hidden populations**; based on the principle that individuals know others of their kind. Cannot determine sampling error; sample may not be representative.

10. **Area sampling originated in agriculture** (farming experiments on fertilizers and crop varieties). In social sciences, it has been used extensively in geography and rural research. Multi-stage version is associated with **Moser**.

11. **Simple random sampling**: n=30 or more = large sample; below 30 = small sample. At n=30, Student's t values approach Z critical values.

12. **Saturation sampling**: Includes nearly all members of a limited group (e.g., 900/1,000 students). **Dense sampling**: Includes nearly all of the accessible population. Both are nonprobability methods. Both are rarely used in practice.

13. **The table of random numbers** is the most popular method for ensuring randomness. Limitation: difficult to use when population exceeds 5 digits.

14. **Systematic sampling** is classified as a **mixed sampling** method (neither purely probability nor nonprobability).

15. **Simple random sampling** is the foundation upon which all other probability sampling types are based. Its major disadvantage is that rare subgroups may not be adequately represented, and the investigator cannot exploit prior knowledge about population composition.
