# Measurement of Personality

Chapter 10 covers the meaning and purpose of personality measurement, the major tools of assessment (self-report inventories, observational methods, projective techniques), the five strategies used to construct personality inventories, methods for overcoming distortions in self-reports, situational tests, and the measurement of interests, values, and attitudes. It is a high-yield chapter for MCQs on specific tests, their authors, item counts, scales, and construction strategies.

---

## Meaning and Purpose of Personality Measurement

**Personality** refers to the total functions of an individual as he or she interacts with the environment. **Traits** are the fundamental units of personality: they are defined as the *observed consistency of behaviour* of a person. Traits are not directly observed; they are *inferred* from consistent behaviour.

The most general trait categories are: what a person does, how a person does it, and how well a person does it.

**Nunnally (1978)** identified four broad types of traits that personality measurement aims to study:

### Social Traits
**Social traits** determine how persons interact with others in society. Examples: friendliness, honesty, dominance, responsibility, shyness. Social traits include **temperament** and **character**.

### Motives
**Motives** include non-biological drives such as need to earn money and prestige, need for academic achievement, need for affiliation, aggression, etc. These non-biological needs are collectively called **personality dynamics**.

### Personal Conceptions
**Personal conceptions** include methods/dimensions that determine a person's attitude toward the self and others, their values, interests, etc.

### Adjustment
**Adjustment** includes freedom from emotional worries, instability, and other disruptive behaviour. **Maladjustment** (opposite of adjustment) includes pathological traits such as hallucinations, hysteria, imaginary illness, psychoses, etc.

### Interrelationship of the Four Trait Types
All four types are *correlated* (not independent). For example:
- A social trait like dominance influences motives, personal conceptions, and adjustment.
- Motive influences social traits, personal conception, and adjustment.
- Maladjustment produces extreme social traits, eccentric motives, and personal conceptions.

For social traits, motives, and adjustment, similar measurement principles apply. For personal conception (attitudes, interests, values), a *different* set of principles is necessary.

## Tools of Personality Assessment

There are three major tools:

1. **Self-report personality inventories** (Structured personality tests)
2. **Observational methods** (including situational tests)
3. **Projective techniques**

### Self-report Inventories

**Self-report inventories** (also called **personality inventories**) are self-rating questionnaires where the individual describes their own feelings, environment, and reactions. The person reports about themselves in response to measurement questions.

Five types of self-report inventories:

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Social/specified trait inventories | Measure traits like self-confidence, dominance, ego-strength, extroversion, responsibility | Bernreuter Personality Inventory; Eysenck Personality Questionnaire; Differential Personality Inventory |
| 2. Adjustment inventories | Evaluate adjustment to school, home, health, etc. | Bell Adjustment Inventory |
| 3. Pathological trait inventories | Evaluate hysteria, paranoia, hypomania, depression, schizophrenia | Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI) |
| 4. Screening inventories | Screen individuals into two or three groups | Cornell Index (screens for psychosomatic difficulties vs. normal) |
| 5. Attitude/Interest/Value inventories | Measure attitudes, interests, and values | Educational inventories; Strong Vocational Interest Blank; Allport-Vernon Study of Values |

All five types share the underlying principle: behaviours are manifestations of traits, and the presence/absence of a trait can be assessed by measuring behaviour.

### Observational Methods

**Observational methods** provide either a structured (controlled) or unstructured (uncontrolled) situation. Persons are placed in these situations and careful, impartial, accurate observations are made by observers. Differences in observations by different observers reflect **subjectivity**. (Full discussion in Chapter 12.)

### Projective Techniques

**Projective techniques** are the most popular method of personality assessment. The person is asked to describe an unstructured stimulus or situation, and through their responses, their needs, drives, motives, and fears are revealed. (Full discussion in Chapter 11.)

## Popular Strategies for Construction of Personality Inventories

### Personality Self-report Inventories (Overview)

**Structured personality tests** (the formal name for self-report inventories) evaluate:
- **Personality traits**: relative disposition to think, feel, and act consistently
- **Personality types**: general descriptions of people
- **Personality states**: emotional reactions that vary from situation to situation
- **Self-concept**: a relatively organized and consistent set of assumptions a person has about himself or herself

In a structured personality test, the subject responds to written statements by marking them 'True' or 'False'. Items are structured and lack ambiguity (unlike projective tests where stimuli are ambiguous).

**Five construction strategies:**
1. Logical-Content Strategy
2. Criterion-Group Strategy
3. Factor Analytic Strategy
4. Theoretical Strategy
5. Combination Strategy (Mixed Strategy)

---

### The Logical-Content Strategy

In the **logical-content strategy**, the test constructor uses reason and deductive logic to develop items. The core assumption is that items describe the person's personality and behaviour at face value (i.e., the strategy assumes **face validity**).

**Historical origin:** The first personality inventory ever developed used this strategy.
- **Woodworth Personal Data Sheet** (1920) by **Woodworth** — purpose: identify military recruits likely to develop emotional problems in combat. It had **116 items** with Yes/No responses. Items were selected from known symptoms of emotional disorders and from questions asked by clinical psychologists and psychiatrists in screening interviews. Produced a **single overall score** (global measure). Developed during **World War I**.

Other early logical-content tests:
- **Bell Adjustment Inventory**: assessed home life, social life, health life, and emotional life. Published in the 1930s. Yielded multiple scores.
- **Bernreuter Personality Inventory**: measured sociability, confidence, and introversion. Published in the 1930s. Yielded multiple scores.

**Weaknesses of logical-content strategy** (criticized sharply by 1940s; Landis and McNavmar & Landis, 1935):
1. Assumes subject complies with instructions and gives honest responses.
2. Assumes subject can evaluate their response to each item.
3. Assumes test constructor and interpreter define items similarly.
None of these assumptions is necessarily true. Tests based on face validity were almost completely discredited by the 1940s, leading to the criterion-group strategy.

---

### The Criterion-Group Strategy

The **criterion-group strategy** is an empirical approach that relies on data collection and statistical analyses to determine the meaning of a test response (Kaplan & Saccuzzo 2001).

**Steps:**
1. Select a **criterion group** (persons with specific characteristics, e.g., hysteria or schizophrenia) and a **control group** (represents general population).
2. Administer a set of items and select only those that **distinguish** criterion from control group. Face validity of items is irrelevant.
3. **Cross-validate** the selected items by testing them on an independent criterion group vs. control group.
4. Use data from normal control groups to obtain **standard scores**.
5. Conduct further research to interpret what endorsing many items on a dimension means.

Two major tests developed using criterion-group strategy:

#### Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)

- Developed by **Hathaway & McKinley (1951)** in the **early 1940s**.
- A **true-false self-report questionnaire**.
- Called **multiphasic** because it detects several psychiatric/pathological problems.
- Original version: **550 items**; three response options per item: True, False, Cannot say.
- **10 clinical scales** and **4 validity scales**.

**10 Clinical Scales:**

| Code | Scale Name |
|---|---|
| Hs | Hypochondriasis |
| D | Depression |
| Hy | Hysteria |
| Pd | Psychopathic Deviate |
| Mf | Masculinity-Femininity |
| Pa | Paranoia |
| Pt | Psychasthenia |
| Sc | Schizophrenia |
| Ma | Mania |
| Si | Social Introversion |

**4 Validity Scales:**

| Scale | Purpose |
|---|---|
| L (Lie scale) | Detects attempts to fake good; items scored infrequently (<10%) by normal population |
| F (Infrequency Scale) | Detects faking bad; high F score invalidates the profile (Shores & Carstairs 1988) |
| K (Correction Scale) | Empirically constructed; high K = defensiveness or faking good; low K = excessive frankness/faking bad |
| ? (Cannot Say) | Number of items omitted/double-marked; >30 omissions makes record suspect/invalid (Anastasi & Urbina 1997); has no items of its own |

Validity scales are checks on carelessness, misunderstanding, malingering, and response sets. Raw scores converted to **McCall's T scores** (mean = 50, SD = 10).

**MMPI-2** (Butcher et al. 1989):
- **567 affirmative statements**, True/False response.
- First 370 items identical to original MMPI (with editorial changes); retains all 10 clinical and 4 validity scales.
- Remaining 197 items (107 new) make up 104 new, revised content, and supplementary scales/subscales.
- Additional scales: **15 content scales**, **27 content component scales**, **21 supplementary scales**, **28 Harris-Lingoes subscales**.
- Normed on **2600 adults** (ages 18-84).
- Content scale examples: HEA (health concerns), TPA (Type-A personality), FAM (family problems), WRK (work interference).
- Supplementary scale examples: Anxiety, Repression, Ego-Strength, Mac Andrew Alcoholism Scale-Revised.
- **3 new validity scales** in MMPI-2:
  - **F(b) (Back F)**: extension of F scale for second half of inventory; checks validity throughout.
  - **VRIN (Variable Response Inconsistency Scale)**: evaluates random responding; consists of matching pairs with similar content — opposite-direction marking earns a score.
  - **TRIN (True Response Inconsistency Scale)**: assesses acquiescence (tendency to mark True regardless of content); consists of matching pairs with opposite content.
- Reliability: median test-retest coefficients range from low .50s to .90s; split-half reliability ran in the .70s.
- Problems: item overlap among scales, imbalanced true-false keying, high intercorrelation (scales 7 and 8 correlate .64-.87), lack of generalizability across demographics.
- Used in over **10,000 studies** (Graham 1993); preferred by ~**90% of clinical psychologists** (Aiken 1988).
- Exception: Social Introversion (Si) was developed by contrasting college students high/low in social introversion, not the criterion-keyed method used for the other 9 clinical scales.

**MMPI-A** (Butcher et al. 1992):
- Developed specially for **adolescents**, age range **14-18 years**.
- **478 items**.
- Retains 10 clinical and 4 validity scales.
- Has its own validity scales F1 and F2, plus unique content, supplementary, and subscales.

#### California Psychological Inventory (CPI)

- Developed by **Harrison Gough** (originally in **1957**).
- Original length: **480 items**; revised in **1987** to **462 items**; third edition (Gough & Bradley 1996): **434 items**.
- All 434 items answered True/False; yields scores on **20 scales** (17 personality scales + 3 validity scales).
- More than a third of items drawn from **MMPI**.
- Unlike MMPI, CPI is used for **normal adult populations** (MMPI is for abnormal populations).
- **3 validity scales:**
  - **Well-being (Wb)**: normals asked to fake bad.
  - **Good Impression (Gi)**: normals asked to fake good.
  - **Communality (Cm)**: based on frequency count of highly popular responses (random responding check).
- Personality dimensions include: Dominance (Do), Sociability (Sy), Capacity for Status (Cs), Social Presence (Sp), Self-acceptance (Sa), Independence (In), Empathy (Em), Responsibility (Re), Socialization (So), Self-control (Sc), Tolerance (To), Achievement via Independence (Ai), Intellectual Efficiency (Ie), Psychological-Mindedness (Py), Flexibility (Fx), Feminity (Fe), and others.
- Scores converted to standard score scales: mean = 50, SD = 10.
- Useful for predicting: high school and college achievement, GPA in medical institutions, teacher/student effectiveness, police/military effectiveness, leadership success, executive success; also identifies delinquent/criminal adolescents and adults.
- Reliability: short-term test-retest .49-.90; long-term .38-.77.
- Shares some limitations with MMPI: scale intercorrelation, imbalanced true-false keying.

### The Factor Analytic Strategy

The **factor analytic strategy** uses **factor analysis** to derive empirically the major dimensions of personality. Factor analysis is a statistical technique for revealing redundancy in a set of intercorrelated scores.

**Principal-components method** (Hotelling 1933): finds the minimum number of common factors that account for an interrelated set of scores.

**Process:**
1. Start with intercorrelations of a large number of items or tests.
2. Factor analyze intercorrelations to find minimum number of factors accounting for maximum variance.
3. Label factors based on what items loaded on each factor have in common.

Key psychologists: **Guilford**, **Cattell**, **Eysenck**.

#### Guilford's Factor Analytic Inventories

- Guilford (1940) found **5 factors** from factor analysis of introversion-extroversion tests: social introversion, thinking introversion, depression, cycloid tendencies, and restraint. Known as the **Inventory of factors STDCR**.
- Further work led to: **Guilford-Martin Inventory factors GAMIN (1943a)** and **Guilford-Martin Personality Inventory (1943b)**.
- After combining overlapping factors, a single **10-factor inventory** was developed: **Guilford-Zimmerman Temperament Survey (1956)** (Guilford & Zimmerman).
- **10 factors of Guilford-Zimmerman Temperament Survey:**
  1. General activity (G)
  2. Restraint (R)
  3. Ascendance (A)
  4. Sociability (S)
  5. Emotional stability (E)
  6. Objectivity (O)
  7. Friendliness (F)
  8. Thoughtfulness (T)
  9. Personal relations (P)
  10. Masculinity (M)
- Each dimension measured by **30 items**; Yes/No response format.
- Includes **three verification keys** to detect falsification.
- Currently serves only historical interest; was overshadowed by MMPI.

#### Cattell's 16 Personality Factor Questionnaire (16PF)

- Developed by **R. Cattell** (and co-workers); originally published in **1949**; currently in its **fifth edition** (R. Cattell, A. Cattell & E. Cattell, Russell Sage, 1994).
- Meant for ages **16 and over**.
- **185 items** (fifth edition); forced-choice format with 2 or 3 options.
- Yields **16 scores** representing **16 source traits** (each bipolar).
- Forms A, B, C, D available; fifth edition is single-form.
- Includes **15 Problem Solving items** (Reasoning scale: a quick measure of mental ability).
- Includes measures of three response style indices: random responding, acquiescence, and social desirability/undesirability.

**Cattell's 16 Source Traits (Table 10.1):**

| Factor | Low Pole | High Pole | Letter Symbol |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warmth | Reserved | Outgoing | A |
| Intelligence | Less Intelligent | More Intelligent | B |
| Ego Strength | Stable/Ego Strength | Emotionality/Neuroticism | C |
| Dominance | Humble | Assertive | E |
| Impulsivity | Sober | Happy-go-lucky | F |
| Conformity | Expedient | Conscientious | G |
| Boldness | Shy | Venturesome | H |
| Emotional Sensitivity | Tough-minded | Tender-minded | I |
| Suspiciousness | Trusting | Suspicious | L |
| Imagination | Practical | Imaginative | M |
| Shrewdness | Forthright | Shrewd | N |
| Guilt Proneness | Placid | Apprehensive | O |
| Rebelliousness | Conservative | Experimenting | Q1 |
| Self-Sufficiency | Group-tied | Self-Sufficient | Q2 |
| Self-Discipline | Casual | Controlled | Q3 |
| Tension/Anxiety | Relaxed | Tense | Q4 |

Key details on select scales:
- **Scale B** distinguishes **crystallized intelligence** (cultural learning/training) from **fluid intelligence** (innate abilities). Cattell also developed the **Cattell Fair Intelligence Test** to assess fluid intelligence independently.
- **Scale G** (Conscientious): items similar to MMPI L scale.
- **Scale N** (Shrewdness): correlates significantly with Hysteria Scale of MMPI; considered least useful by Karson & O'Dell (1976).
- **Scale O** (Guilt Proneness): plays an important role in clinical analysis.
- **Scale Q4** (Tension): the best indicator of neurotic anxiety.
- Men and boys tend to score higher on **Scale E** (Dominance) than women and girls (Cattell 1965).

Reliability: short-term test-retest reliability range **.65 to .93**, median **.83**. Factor intercorrelations can reach as high as **.75**. Second-order factor analysis yielded **4 second-order factors**.

**Parallel inventories derived from 16PF:**
- **Junior Senior High School Personality Questionnaire**: ages 12-18.
- **Children's Personality Questionnaire**: ages 8-12.
- **Clinical Analysis Questionnaire (CAQ)** (Delhees & Cattell 1971): extends 16PF to psychopathology assessment; adds **12 abnormal source traits** to the original 16.

**CAQ's 12 abnormal factors (Table 10.2):**

| D2 | Zestfulness/Suicidal disgust |
| D3 | Brooding discontent |

| D5 | Energy euphoria |
| D6 | Guilt and resentment |
| D7 | Bored depression |
| P | Paranoia |
| Pa | Psychopathic deviation |
| Sc | Schizophrenia |
| As | Psychasthenia |
| Ps | General psychosis |

#### Eysenck Personality Questionnaire (EPQ)

- Developed by **Eysenck and Eysenck (1975)** based on a lifelong factor analytic programme.
- Measures **3 major personality dimensions**: Neuroticism (N), Extraversion (E), Psychoticism (P).
- **90 statements**, Yes/No format; for persons aged **16 and older**.
- Includes a **Lie (L) scale** to assess social desirability.
- **Junior EPQ**: for children aged **7 to 11**; consists of **81 statements**.

**Three EPQ scales:**

| Scale | Dimension | High Score | Low Score |
|---|---|---|---|
| P Scale | Psychoticism | Impulsivity, aggression, hostility, empathy defects, cold/unusual traits; high scores in psychopathic/schizoid personalities | Lower psychoticism |
| E Scale | Extraversion-Introversion | Extraverted: sociable, fun-loving, gregarious, novelty-seeking | Introverted: preference for solitude, quiet, introspective, serious |
| N Scale | Neuroticism | Nervous, maladjusted, overemotional | Stable, confident |

**Extraversion-Introversion empirical correlates (key MCQ targets):**
1. Extroverts have greater need for entertaining external stimulation.
2. Extroverts are readily conditioned to stimuli associated with sexual arousal.
3. Extroverts are more suggestible than introverts.
4. Extroverts are vigilant in watch-keeping.
5. Introverts' performance on signal detection tasks is comparatively more improved.
6. Introverts are less tolerant of pain but more tolerant of sensory deprivation.

Reliability: one-month test-retest: P = .78, E = .89, N = .86, L = .84. Internal consistency: P in the .70s; E, N, L in the .80s.

### The Theoretical Strategy

In the **theoretical strategy**, items are selected to assess variables or constructs specified by a *major theory of personality*. Construct-related validity evidence is then sought. Psychometricians adopted this strategy to avoid biases stemming from factor analytic approaches. The strategy creates homogeneous scales.

#### Edwards Personal Preference Schedule (EPPS)

- Developed by **Edwards (1954, 1959)**.
- Theoretical basis: **need theory of personality** by **Murray and Morgan (1938)**.
- Edwards selected **15 needs** from Murray's list.
- Each need is paired with every other 14 needs **twice** on the test.
- Total inventory: **210 pairs** of statements.
- **1 additional consistency scale**: 15 repeated items; testees should answer at least 9/15 consistently (score below 9 indicates interpretive problems).
- Total scales: **15 need scales + 1 consistency scale = 16 scales**.
- Forced-choice format: within each pair, testee chooses the statement more characteristic of themselves.
- Maximum possible raw score per scale: 2 × 14 = **28**; minimum = 0.

**EPPS produces ipsative scores**: strength of each need is expressed in relation to the strength of the testee's *other* needs, not in absolute terms. Ipsative scores allow **intraindividual comparison** only; normative reference data are for **interindividual comparison**. Combining both makes interpretation confusing.

**15 Needs of EPPS (Table 10.3):**

| Need | Description |
|---|---|
| n Achievement | To do one's best, accomplish something difficult |
| n Deference | To conform to what is expected |
| n Exhibition | To be the center of attention |
| n Intraception | To analyze motives and feelings of oneself and others |
| n Dominance | To influence others, be regarded as a leader |
| n Nurturance | To help others in trouble, treat others kindly |
| n Order | To make plans before starting difficult tasks |
| n Autonomy | To come and go as desired, do/say what one wants |
| n Affiliation | To be loyal to others, participate in friendly groups |
| n Succorance | To seek encouragement, gain sympathy from others |
| n Abasement | To feel guilty when wrong, accept blame |
| n Change | To do new and different things, experience novelty |
| n Endurance | To keep at a task until finished, work hard |
| n Heterosexuality | To engage in activities with opposite sex |
| n Aggression | To attack contrary views, tell others what one thinks |
| Consistency | Checks whether responses on matching pairs are consistent |

Raw scores converted to **percentile equivalents** using norms for college and general adult populations.

Criticisms of EPPS: social desirability effects not satisfactorily controlled; can be faked despite forced choice; ipsative vs. normative conflict in interpretation.

#### Personality Research Form (PRF) and Jackson Personality Inventory (JPI)

- **PRF** (Jackson 1967) and **JPI** (Jackson 1976a, 1976b) — both based on **Murray's theory of needs**.
- PRF was developed in two sets of parallel forms:
  - Shorter forms A and B: **15 scales** (including 1 validity scale), **300 items**.
  - Longer forms AA and BB: **22 scales** (including 2 validity scales: Desirability and Infrequency), **440 items**.
  - Later Form E: **22 scales**, **352 items** (condensed, extended to other populations).
- **22 PRF scales:** abasement, achievement, affiliation, aggression, autonomy, change, cognitive structure, defendence, dominance, endurance, exhibition, harm avoidance, impulsivity, nurturance, order, play, sentience, social recognition, succorance, understanding, desirability, infrequency.
- Scales are bipolar: low score indicates presence of opposite trait.
- Validity scales (like MMPI): detect scoring errors, careless test-taking, social desirability.
- Used primarily with college students.

- **JPI**: one form, **320 true-false items**, **15 scales** (including 1 validity scale: infrequency); for high school through college students and adults.
- JPI scales: anxiety, breadth of interest, complexity, conformity, energy level, social innovation, interpersonal affect, organization, responsibility, risk-taking, self-esteem, social admittance, tolerance, social participation, value orthodoxy, infrequency.
- Both PRF and JPI feature **balanced true-false keying** and **no item overlap**.

#### The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI)

- Developed by **Myers and Briggs** (Myers 1962; Myers & McCaulley 1985).
- Theoretically based on **Carl Jung's theory of Psychological Types**.
- Four bipolar dimensions (dichotomies):
  1. **E-I**: Extroversion vs. Introversion (from Jung's dichotomy of attitudes)
  2. **S-N**: Sensation vs. Intuition (opposing ways of perceiving, from Jung)
  3. **T-F**: Thinking vs. Feeling (contrasting approaches to judgement, from Jung)
  4. **J-P**: Judgement vs. Perception (orientation toward outer world; not explicit in Jung's original theory)
- Results in **16 possible type formulae** (combinations of preferred letters, one per dimension).
- Example: **INTP** = introverted, intuitive, thinking, perceptive type.
- Underlying assumption: every person has specific preferences in how they combine experiences; these preferences underlie needs, values, interests, and motivation.
- Applications: financial success, leadership, vocational perception, communication styles, career choices, sensitivity and purpose in life.

#### Measures of Self-concept

**Self-concept** broadly refers to the set of assumptions a person has about himself or herself. Several tools exist:

- **Gough's Adjective Checklist** (Gough & Heilbrun 1980): **300 adjectives** in alphabetical order (from 'absent-minded' to 'zany'); based on 15 Murray need categories, EPPS, Berne's Transactional Analysis theory (1961, 1966), and Welsh's theory of creativity and intelligence.
- **Student Self-concept Scale (SSCS)**: based on **Bandura's theory of Self-efficacy** (Gresham, Elliott & Evans-Fernandez 1993); assesses three domains: academic, social, and self-image.
- **Personal and Academic Self-Concept Inventory (PASCI)**: developed by Fleming and Whalen (1990); for high school and college students.
- **SDQ scale**: investigates the hierarchical, multifaceted model of self-concept by **Shavelson and colleagues**; includes a global self-esteem scale and six facet scales (two dealing with social aspects).
- **Pier-Harris Children's Self-Concept**: contains **80 self-assessment items**, Yes/No format.
- **Tennessee Self-Concept Scale**: formal paper-and-pencil test.
- **Q-Sort Technique** (Rogers): person sorts self-descriptive cards from least to most personally descriptive; makes two sorts: **real self** (who the person really is) and **ideal self** (what the person believes they should be). Major discrepancy between real and ideal self reflects low self-esteem and poor adjustment (Rogers 1961).

### Combination Strategies

Modern test developers use **combination strategies**, mixing multiple approaches. The two most prominent combined-strategy tests are the Millon Inventories and the NEO-PI-R.

#### The Millon Inventories (MCMI)

- Developed by **Millon (1994)**.
- Full name: **Millon Clinical Multiaxial Inventories (MCMI)**.
- Originally published in **1977** (MCMI); revised in **1987** (MCMI-II); current version **MCMI-III**.
- Ranks second only to MMPI/MMPI-2 as a measure of pathological traits (Piotrowski 1997).
- MCMI-III: **175 true-false items** (85 from MCMI-II, 90 from the original MCMI).
- Compatible with **DSM-IV** (American Psychiatric Association).
- Two advantages of MCMI-III over MMPI-2: (1) shorter (175 items), (2) organized to identify clinical syndromes compatible with DSM-IV.
- MCMI-III includes **24 clinical scales** + **4 modifying indices**.

**MCMI-III Scale Categories (Table 10.4):**

| Category | Scales |
|---|---|
| Clinical Personality Patterns (11 scales) | Schizoid, Avoidant, Depressive, Dependent, Histrionic, Narcissistic, Antisocial, Aggressive-Sadistic, Compulsive, Passive-Aggressive-Negativistic, Self-Defeating |
| Severe Personality Pathology (3 scales) | Schizotypal (S), Borderline (C), Paranoid (P) |
| Clinical Syndromes (7 scales) | Anxiety (A), Somatoform (H), Bipolar: Manic (N), Dysthymia (D), Alcohol Dependence (B), Drug Dependence (T), PTSD (R) |
| Severe Syndromes (3 scales) | Thought disorder (SS), Major Depression (CC), Delusional disorder (PP) |
| Modifying Indices (4 scales) | Disclosure (X), Desirability (Y), Debasement (Z), Validity (V) |

Modifying indices: Disclosure, Desirability, and Debasement scores are used to adjust other scale scores upward/downward for defensiveness or symptom exaggeration. Validity scale (3 items with unusual content) detects random responding or oppositional test-taking.

**Additional Millon instruments:**
- **Millon Adolescent Clinical Inventory (MACI)**: ages **13-19**; derived from the earlier **Millon Adolescent Personality Inventory (MAPI)** (Millon, Green & Meagher 1982); for clinical assessment as well as counselling/guidance/career planning.
- **Millon Index of Personality Styles (MIPS)**: for normal adults with family, work, or social problems; non-clinical use.

#### The NEO Personality Inventory-Revised (NEO-PI-R)

- Developed by **Costa and McCrae (1992)**.
- Uses both **factor analysis** and **theory** in item development.
- Measures the **Big Five personality factors**:
  1. **N** — Neuroticism
  2. **E** — Extraversion
  3. **O** — Openness to Experience
  4. **A** — Agreeableness
  5. **C** — Conscientiousness
- **'Big'** refers to the fact that each factor subsumes a large number of more specific traits.
- Big Five are as broad and abstract as Eysenck's factors.

**NEO-PI-R Facet Scales (Table 10.5):**

| Factor | Facets |
|---|---|
| Neuroticism | Anxiety, impulsivity, angry hostility, depression, self-consciousness, vulnerability |
| Extraversion | Activity, excitement seeking, positive emotions, warmth, gregariousness, assertiveness |
| Openness to Experience | Actions, ideas, values, fantasy, aesthetics, feelings |
| Agreeableness | Altruism, trust, straightforwardness, compliance, modesty, tendermindedness |
| Conscientiousness | Competence, dutifulness, order, achievement-striving, self-discipline, deliberation |

NEO-PI-R has good reliability and validity across different data sources and with other instruments (e.g., Goldberg's adjective inventories, 1992). Currently considered one of the **three dominant tests** of personality alongside MMPI-2 and Millon Inventories.

## Overcoming Distortions in Self-report Inventories

**Distortions** in self-report responses are a major problem. Four main strategies to overcome them:

### 1. Establishment of Rapport
Before administration, the tester should establish a warm, cooperative relationship (**rapport**) with testees. This reduces discomfort and promotes honest, unbiased responses.

### 2. Use of Forced-Choice Technique
**Forced-choice technique**: subject is forced to choose between two or more equally desirable or undesirable items. This controls faking-good or socially desirable response sets because both options are equally appealing. However, forced-choice items have drawbacks: they require more time, and can reduce reliability (difficult choices lower consistency), which may offset gains in validity.

### 3. Concealing the Main Purpose of the Test
Two methods:
- **Plausible false purpose**: describe a personality test as an ability test to reduce faking.
- **Embedded false information**: insert fictitious items among true ones (e.g., asking students to endorse book titles including fictitious ones; higher endorsement of fictitious titles = higher deceit/boasting).
Note: concealment may be ethically problematic as it can make psychologists appear deceptive.

### 4. Use of Verification and Correction Keys
Correction and verification keys indicate whether the examinee is presenting a true picture of themselves. Examples:
- MMPI's four validity scales check carelessness, evasiveness, misunderstanding, and response sets.
- EPPS uses 15 paired statements presented twice; high inconsistency in response indicates a response set.

## Situational Tests

A **situational test** is a compromise between standardized tests and observational methods. Personality traits are measured by observing what a person thinks and does in a situation resembling everyday life. The person being examined typically does not know they are being tested. Situations are social in nature and designed to emphasize traits under study.

**Historical origin:** The first situational test of personality was developed by the **United States Office of Strategic Services (OSS)** during **World War II** to screen men for military assignments.

**Suitable traits:** leadership, dominance, responsibility, extroversion-introversion.

**Behavioural tests**: Situational tests that use directly observable units of behaviour (honesty, self-control, cooperation). These traits can be grouped under **character**.

**Character Education Inquiry (CEI)**: First major attempt to study character traits; conducted by **Hartshorne, May & Shuttleworth (1930)**. Used natural school situations (games, classroom exams). Students were unaware of being studied. Primarily designed to measure honesty, altruism, and self-control.

**Duplicating technique** (used in CEI): Children's responses to tests (e.g., arithmetic, vocabulary) were duplicated secretly. When students scored their own papers using a key, comparison with duplicate copies revealed cheating.

**Self-control measurement** (CEI): Measured by the extent to which a distractor in doing a task is resisted, or the total time a child persisted in solving a puzzle. Example task: reading a story with words run together (e.g., THEKINGORDEREDTHEKNIGHTTOBRINGTHECULTPRITBEFOREHI).

### Limitations of Situational Tests

1. **Time-consuming, costly, and laborious**: require trained observers; creating real-life situations takes great effort.
2. **Subjectivity and bias**: observers are human; subjective elements cannot be completely eliminated.
3. **Isolated bit of behaviour**: meaning of a single observed behaviour is difficult to determine without context (e.g., a child hitting another could be aggression, dominance, or superiority).
4. **Selection problem**: deciding "what to observe" is challenging; only limited behaviours can be selected.
5. **Observer visibility**: presence of the observer may alter group behaviour; fitting the observer into the setting is problematic.

Overall, situational tests should be supplemented with other standardized tests; observational methods alone are insufficient for definitive conclusions.

## Measurement of Interests, Values, and Attitudes

### Meaning and Types of Interest Tests

**Interest**: a preference for one activity over another. Two key points: (1) interests involve selection and ranking along a like-dislike dimension, and (2) interests involve activities/behaviours (expressed using action verbs).

**Super & Crites (1962)** classified interests into three types:

| Type | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Expressed Interest** | When a person verbally states preference for one activity over another (simplest/most direct method) |
| **Manifest Interest** | When a person voluntarily participates in an activity (demonstrated by action, e.g., attending Yoga classes) |
| **Inventoried Interest** | Interests measured by interest tests/inventories that compare interest in different activities |

Note: expressed and manifest interest do not necessarily correspond. Most individuals engage in some activities they dislike, and may refuse to engage in activities they claim to enjoy.

**Problems with direct measurement of interests** (especially in children and adolescents):
1. Young people are not fully aware of activities required in different occupations.
2. Occupational preferences are guided by stereotypes formed from popular media.

**Historical note:** The first interest inventory was introduced in **America in 1921** and was called the **Carnegie Interest Inventory**.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Woodworth Personal Data Sheet (1920)**: the **first personality inventory** ever developed; 116 Yes/No items; used for WWI military screening; single score; logical-content strategy.
2. **MMPI clinical scales mnemonic**: Hs, D, Hy, Pd, Mf, Pa, Pt, Sc, Ma, Si (10 scales); **Social Introversion (Si)** was constructed differently (college students high/low in social introversion), not by criterion-keyed method.
3. **MMPI validity scales**: L (Lie), F (Infrequency), K (Correction), ? (Cannot Say); ? has no items of its own; >30 omissions = invalid; high F = faking bad; high K = faking good or defensive; low K = excessive frankness.
4. **MMPI-2**: 567 items; 3 new validity scales: F(b), VRIN (random responding), TRIN (acquiescence); normed on 2600 adults (ages 18-84).
5. **MMPI-A**: for adolescents **ages 14-18**; 478 items; developed by Butcher et al. (1992).
6. **CPI**: developed by **Harrison Gough (1957)**; for **normal adults** (unlike MMPI which is for abnormal); 434 items (3rd ed.); 20 scales; validity scales Wb, Gi, Cm.
7. **Cattell's 16PF**: 185 items; 16 source traits; ages 16+; **Q4 scale = best indicator of neurotic anxiety**; **Scale N = least useful** (Karson & O'Dell); **Scale B** distinguishes fluid vs. crystallized intelligence; **CAQ adds 12 abnormal factors**.
8. **EPQ**: **90 items** (16+); **81 items** (Junior EPQ, ages 7-11); 3 dimensions: N, E, P + Lie scale; Introverts are less tolerant of pain but more tolerant of sensory deprivation.
9. **EPPS**: based on **Murray's need theory**; 210 pairs; **15 needs + 1 consistency scale**; **ipsative scoring**; max raw score per scale = 28; consistency: at least 9/15 repeated items must be consistent.
10. **Millon MCMI-III**: 175 true-false items; 24 clinical scales + 4 modifying indices; aligned with **DSM-IV**; ranks second to MMPI/MMPI-2; MACI (ages 13-19) and MIPS (normal adults) are extensions.
11. **NEO-PI-R (Costa & McCrae 1992)**: measures **Big Five** (N, E, O, A, C); 'Big' = each factor subsumes many specific traits; uses combined factor analytic + theoretical approach.
12. **MBTI**: based on **Jung's typology**; developed by **Myers & Briggs**; 4 dimensions (E-I, S-N, T-F, J-P); yields **16 type formulae**; J-P polarity was NOT in Jung's original theory.
13. **Super & Crites (1962)**: three types of interests — **expressed** (verbal statement), **manifest** (voluntary participation), **inventoried** (measured by inventory); first American interest inventory = **Carnegie Interest Inventory (1921)**.
14. **Character Education Inquiry (CEI)**: Hartshorne, May & Shuttleworth (1930); first major study of character (honesty, self-control, altruism) using situational methods in school settings; used the **duplicating technique** to measure cheating.
15. **Forced-choice technique limitation**: reduces reliability because difficult choices increase response variability, which may offset gains in validity; cannot fully eliminate social desirability effects.
