# Reviewing the Literature

Chapter 19 covers the purpose, types, and sources of literature review in scientific research,
along with guidance on how to write and organise a literature review, how to decide recency
requirements, and how to prepare index cards and abstracts following APA format.

---

## Purpose of the Review

**Literature** refers to the collected body of works produced by earlier scientists in a given
field. The **review of literature** is a well-established procedure in scientific research that
documents, generalises, and synthesises prior knowledge, and is regarded as an essential
component of research design.

### Four Specific Purposes

1. **Making a solid foundation for research**
   - Helps the researcher understand fundamental strategies, data-collection methods, and
     research designs used in related works.
   - Assists in pinpointing areas of agreement and disagreement among prior studies.
   - Helps define variables that are categorically significant and practically important.
   - Enables the researcher to select an appropriate research design for the study problem.

2. **Avoidance of repetition**
   - Prevents the researcher from conducting an exact replication of work already done.
   - A review can provide justification and foundation for the current study by demonstrating
     that it addresses a gap identified in earlier studies.
   - Helps the researcher gain acquaintance with the quantity and nature of related studies.

3. **Facilitation of prior works**
   - Enables the researcher to establish and synthesise prior studies related to the present
     study.
   - Helps address unanswered questions in the existing body of knowledge.
   - Identifies significant overlaps and gaps among prior works.

4. **Improvement of methodology and variables**
   - Helps in discovering important variables relevant to the area of study.
   - Clarifies inter-relationships among variables.
   - Guards against overlooking variables that would lead to poorly designed studies.
   - Supports building a good conceptual framework for subsequent analysis.

### Three General Purposes

(a) To provide the context for one's own methodological approach.
(b) To establish one's credibility and proficiency as a knowledgeable researcher.
(c) To judge the adequacy and appropriateness of one's own approach.

## Types of Literature Review

The focus and extent of a literature review is to integrate and summarise what is known in a
field. Five main types are described:

### 1. Primary Review
- The researcher traces almost all literature on a given specific topic.
- Normally adopted to demonstrate how a concept, theory, or research method has developed
  and progressed over time.

### 2. Content Review
- Links a specific study to the larger body of related works.
- Covers **content analysis**: summarises and synthesises existing knowledge in a particular
  area.
- Examines the role of concepts, theories, frameworks, and methodologies previously employed.

### 3. Methodological Review (first use)
- Focuses on how different researchers have applied different types of methodologies to a
  specific content area.
- Systematically compares different methodologies used across research works through content
  analysis.

### 4. Methodological/Integrative Review (second use — systematic/integrative type)
- A type of **integrative review** in which the researcher brings together the outcomes of
  empirical results.
- **Systematic review**: attempts to study and compare different research findings related to
  a specific problem to yield an integrated or general interpretation.
- Typically forms part of the methodology section of a research paper.

### 5. Theoretical Review
- Provides a summary of existing theories and how different methodologies have been applied
  in different contexts.
- Deals with important sub-concepts: research assumptions, scope of explanation, logical
  consistency.
- The researcher highlights similarities and differences in theoretical frameworks used across
  studies.

## Sources of the Review

### Journals and Books

- **Research journals** and **books** are the primary sources for literature review.
- A **research journal** contains the publication of research reports prepared with detailed
  methodology and findings.
- A **refereed journal** is a journal of original research reports that are examined and
  selected (peer-reviewed) before publication; editors carefully review manuscripts and
  select only a few for publication.
- Research books (textbooks, handbooks, readings) are also direct sources.
- Journals are regarded as more important than books because they provide the latest and most
  up-to-date information in the field.

### Reviews

- **Reviews** are short articles giving brief information on work done in a particular area
  over a period of time.
- Commonly published in journals, yearbooks, handbooks, and encyclopedias.
- Reviewers select research articles, then analyse their conclusions and recommendations.
- Useful for investigators who wish to have all relevant publications at one place without
  individually searching each source.
- Review articles also provide the advantage of a prior review already completed.

### Abstracts

- **Abstracts** provide a summary of research reports done in different fields.
- Key examples:
  - **Psychological Abstracts** (Washington DC: American Psychological Association) — first
    published in **1927**.
  - **Sociological Abstracts** (New York: Sociological Abstracts).
  - **PsyLit disc** — CD-ROM database of psychological literature.
  - **Educational Resources Information Centre (ERIC)** — education-focused abstracts.
  - **Dissertation Abstracts International** — abstracts of doctoral dissertations.
  - **Children Research Abstract**.
  - **Indian Education Abstracts** — published by **NCERT, New Delhi**.
- An abstract provides: summary, name of author and journal, and additional information about
  the research article.
- Limitation: abstracts do not fully satisfy researchers who need detailed methodology and
  results.

### Indexes

- **Indexes** show the titles of research reports without any abstract.
- Titles are arranged alphabetically by category.
- Key example: **Education Index** published by **H. W. Wilson**.
- Because they provide no detailed information, they leave many researchers dissatisfied.
- Best regarded as bibliographical sources that, when combined with other sources, provide
  valuable information.

### Internet

- A quick and easy source of literature review.
- Provides easy access to original writings, updated information not available in libraries,
  useful bibliographies, professional societies, and academic associations.
- Some publishers place latest content and extracts from recently published books online.
- Encyclopedias accessed online are useful as background reading but are generally not
  suitable for citing in a thesis.

### Doctoral Dissertations

- Doctoral dissertations are available in university libraries and are a very good source for
  literature review.
- Most dissertations contain chapters devoted to the review of literature, as well as
  methodology, findings, discussion, summary, and conclusion.
- **Dissertation Abstracts International (DAI/DATRI)**: publishes abstracts of doctoral
  dissertations submitted to different universities; listings are in terms of key words
  appearing in dissertation titles.
- In India: **Survey of Research in Education** edited by **M. B. Buch** serves the same
  function. The second survey covering **1972–1988** is an excellent resource.

### Supervisors / Research Professors

- University professors know the literature well and can guide researchers in the right
  direction.
- They are recognized authorities on the topic or research problem.
- Their suggestions and advice should be carefully analysed.
- Other research professors who have recently reviewed the literature in an area close to the
  study can also be very helpful.
- **Limitation of all reviews**: the interpretation of findings, insight into implications,
  and overall assessment are dependent on the judgement of the reviewer, making all reviews
  susceptible to **subjective judgement**.

## Types of Literature

### Primary Specific Books

- Introductory and advanced **textbooks** and **research reports** provide important
  background and context.
- Also provide information about theory and method in the researcher's field.

### Grey Literature

- **Grey literature** refers to both published and unpublished materials that do not have an
  **International Standard Book Number (ISBN)** or an **International Standard Serial Number
  (ISSN)**.
- A broad category including: unpublished research, theses, newspaper articles, conference
  papers, and pamphlets.
- **Official publications**: from government bodies and statistics agencies.
  - Can be a valuable source of background, contextual information, and secondary data.
  - Document and secondary data analysis are often based on this type of literature.
- Most researchers tend to overlook grey literature during the research process.

### Writing Aids

- Literature that offers significant support during the writing process and improves
  linguistic style.
- Includes: dictionaries, bibliography works, encyclopaedias, thesauruses, textbooks, books
  of quotes, almanacs.

### Journal Articles

- The most common and popular type of literature among researchers.
- Reasons for popularity:
  1. Very credible.
  2. Targeted for academic audiences.
  3. Present the "cutting edge" of knowledge.
  4. Produced regularly, ensuring currency.
- Of all types of literature, **only journal articles are peer-reviewed** (reviewed in the
  research box).

### Why Reviewing Literature Matters (Summary)

(i) Provides a strong theoretical background to the study.
(ii) Helps the researcher contextualise findings.
(iii) Demonstrates the researcher's contribution to the existing body of knowledge.
(iv) Establishes a link between what is being proposed and what has already been studied
     (refines the problem statement).
(v) Helps clarify and focus the research problem statement.

## Writing Process of the Literature Review

Since the literature review may be a very long chapter, structure is essential. Options for
structuring include:
- By topics.
- In chronological order.
- By classification of publications.
- From most recent to oldest publications.
- **Saunders et al. (2009)**: journal articles and books by single authors should be
  separately grouped and structured; undergraduate texts and research articles should also be
  distinguished.

**O'Leary (2009)** recommended the following **seven steps** for writing the literature
review:

### Step 1: Read a Few Good, Relevant Reviews
- Look at literature reviews in well-known journals and articles.
- Sort good and relevant reviews based on reading skills.

### Step 2: Write Critical Annotations
- Write critical annotations while going through various reviews.
- Sort and organise annotations by themes, issues, or categories.
- Themes, issues, or categories reflect: topical themes, arguments to refute or validate, or
  approaches researchers use in a field.
- **Annotation** helps in developing the researcher's own argument.

### Step 3: Write Purposefully
- A literature review cannot be written well without a definite agenda or aim.
- Readers must know the reason why and what the researcher is addressing.

### Step 4: Use the Literature, Not Reproduce It
- Do not use the review merely to repeat or borrow arguments from other researchers.
- Present the literature in a **synthetic** way.

### Step 5: Make the Literature Review an Ongoing Process
- Ongoing reading sharpens the researcher's questions, theories, and methods.
- The ongoing literature review is a critical and continuous process.

### Step 6: Get Plenty of Feedback
- Pass written drafts to supervisors and colleagues.
- Their views and responses provide good guidance for refining and improving the draft.

### Step 7: Be Critical and Constructive
- Can only be achieved through feedback.
- Introduce the review in a coherent manner so that the argument is reasonably valid and
  critical.

## How Old Should the Literature Be?

- The answer depends entirely on the **scope and purpose** of the review.
- Research is a **cumulative activity**: each generation of researchers learns from the
  previous generation.
- Preference should be for the **most recent publications** wherever possible.
- However, in any discipline there are **seminal works** that are centuries old but remain
  significant (e.g., the works of **Freud** and **Marx** are still cited in psychology and
  social science research despite their age).
- Healthy suggestion: researchers should take advantage of the most recent publications in
  their field and then draw the seminal theories from them.

## Organising the Index Cards for Reviewing and Abstracting

- After reviewing different literature sources, researchers prepare their own review and
  abstract on an **index card**.
- Recommended size: **4" x 10" index card**.
- Most journal articles contain an abstract of approximately **150 words** that can be
  incorporated.
- A more detailed summary is prepared when the article is particularly relevant and useful.

### Five Parts of the Abstract on Index Card

| Part | Content |
|------|---------|
| 1 | Name, number, and purpose of the study; hypothesis being tested |
| 2 | Methodology: sample size, population values, methods for measuring/manipulating variables, designs, and statistical methods |
| 3 | Findings: detailed statistical results |
| 4 | Conclusion: brief statement relating to each hypothesis |
| 5 | Full reference written exactly as it will appear in the reference/notes section |

- **APA (American Psychological Association) style** is the widely popular citation and
  formatting style used in psychology and social research journals. It has been adopted by
  many such journals. The 5th edition of the APA manual is referenced for index card format.
- If a source is mentioned during note-making, the full reference must be clearly written on
  the index card.

### Sample Abstract (APA 5th ed. format, from text)

> Singh, A. K. (1980). Extrasensory/Paranormal Associations in Proactive Inhibitions.
> *Journal of Psychological Research, 20*(1): 17–21.
>
> **Purpose**: Explores the possibility of extrasensory/paranormal associations in one means
> of reducing proactive inhibition.
>
> **Methodology**: 30 subjects (college students) required to memorise word lists using the
> method of serial anticipation; subjects divided into three groups of 10 each; analysis of
> variance conducted.
>
> **Findings**: Highest amount of proactive inhibition (PS) reflected by NS condition; CS2
> produced the highest amount of PS while CS1 produced the least.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Psychological Abstracts was first published in 1927** by the American Psychological
   Association (Washington DC) — a commonly tested date.

2. **Education Index** is published by **H. W. Wilson** and is an example of an *index*
   (not an abstract) — the distinction between indexes and abstracts is a frequent MCQ.

3. **Grey literature** is specifically defined as material lacking both an **ISBN and an
   ISSN** — includes theses, conference papers, pamphlets, and newspaper articles.

4. **Refereed journal**: only journals that undergo peer review/editorial selection before
   publication qualify; journals are considered more important than books as a literature
   source because they provide the latest information.

5. **O'Leary (2009)** proposed the **seven steps** for writing a literature review;
   **Saunders et al. (2009)** recommended separate grouping of journal articles and books.

6. The recommended index card size for abstracting literature is **4" x 10"**; standard
   journal abstracts are approximately **150 words**.

7. **Dissertation Abstracts International (DAI/DATRI)** lists dissertation abstracts by
   **key words appearing in the title**; India's equivalent is the **Survey of Research in
   Education** edited by **M. B. Buch** (second survey: 1972–1988).

8. **Five types of literature review**: Primary, Content, Methodological (x2 — including
   the integrative/systematic variant), and Theoretical — all five are MCQ targets.

9. The five parts of an index card abstract are: (1) name/number/purpose/hypotheses,
   (2) methodology, (3) findings/statistical results, (4) conclusions per hypothesis,
   (5) full APA-style reference.

10. **APA style** is the dominant citation format in psychology and social research journals;
    it is associated with the American Psychological Association's publications.

11. The **Survey of Research in Education** (M. B. Buch) is an Indian doctoral-dissertation
    resource; the **second survey** specifically covers **1972–1988**.

12. **Indexes vs. Abstracts**: Indexes list only titles (no summaries); abstracts provide a
    summary plus author and journal information. Indexes are considered "bibliographical
    sources" with limited utility on their own.

13. **ERIC** (Educational Resources Information Centre) is a major abstract database for
    educational research — frequently appears in MCQs alongside Psychological Abstracts.

14. The overarching limitation of all literature reviews is **subjective judgement**: the
    reviewer's interpretation, insight, and overall assessment cannot be fully objective.

15. **Seminal works** (e.g., Freud, Marx) justify including older literature; however, the
    general recommendation is to prioritise the **most recent publications** first, then
    draw from seminal theories — this nuance is a likely MCQ qualifier.
