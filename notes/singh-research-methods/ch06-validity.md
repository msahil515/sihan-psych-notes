# Validity

Chapter 6 covers validity as the core quality of a psychological test — the degree to which a test measures what it purports to measure. It distinguishes three classical types (content, criterion-related, construct), presents statistical methods for computing validity coefficients, details correction procedures for attenuation, and explores the modern unified view of validity that subsumes all categories into a single evaluative judgement.

---

## Meaning of Validity

**Validity** refers to the truth or fidelity of a measuring instrument. As applied to psychological and educational tests, it is defined as the degree to which a test measures what it purports to measure.

Validity is not the same as **reliability**; rather, validity is the correlation of a test with some outside, independent measure regarded by experts as the best measure of the trait or ability the test is intended to assess.

### Key Definitions

| Author | Year | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **Anastasi** | 1968 | "The accuracy with which it measures what it purports to measure." |
| **Lindquist** | 1955 | Validity in terms of how well a test serves its intended purpose (exact wording partially illegible in source). |
| **Magnusson & Saccuzzo** | 2001 | "The degree to which a test score or measure and the quantity it is believed to measure." |
| **AERA, APA & NCME** | 1999 | "Valid is a test that measures what it claims to measure, and measures it well, and useful." |

The correlation coefficient computed between the test and the criterion is known as the **validity coefficient**.

### Five Properties of Validity

1. **Validity is relative**: A test is valid only for a particular purpose and for a particular statistical population. A test valid for measuring geography knowledge is not valid for measuring mathematical ability.

2. **Validity is not a fixed property**: Validation is a never-ending process. As new concepts emerge and old constructs are reinterpreted, the test must be continuously revised.

3. **Validity is a matter of degree**: Like reliability, it is not an all-or-none property. A test cannot be labeled as "perfectly valid" or "not valid at all."

4. **Validity is an omnibus concept**: The 1999 Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing (AERA, APA, NCME) combined all aspects of validity into a unitary concept rather than separating them into discrete subcategories.

5. **Validity involves an overall evaluative judgement**: It requires evaluation of the degree to which empirical evidence and theoretical rationale justify inferences, including the consequences of those interpretations and uses.

### Messick's Sources of Evidence (1989)

**Messick** (1989, Psychological Bulletin) identified five sources of evidence for evaluating validity of a specific use or interpretation:

- **(a) Content** — the content of test items
- **(b) Substantive** — response format and processes
- **(c) Structural** — relationships among different aspects of test scores
- **(d) Generalizable** — generalizability of scores across contexts
- **(e) External** — relationships of performance to other measures

Messick also included the **consequences of testing**, meaning validity encompasses the social and practical repercussions of test use. This is known as **Messick's expanded theory of validity**.

### Historical Development

- **1966**: American Psychological Association monograph "Standards for Educational and Psychological Tests and Manuals" — first formal typology of validity into three types (content, criterion-related, construct).
- **1985**: Joint APA/AERA/NCME committee began discussing validity as based on evidence for inferences, not the test itself.
- **1999**: Same committee formally restricted validity to evidence supporting inferences from test scores; rejected separate categories in favor of a unified concept with different categories of evidence.

## Aspects of Validity

Since a test is valid to the extent that it serves the purpose for which it is used, and since there are many purposes of testing, there are different aspects (types) of validity corresponding to each purpose.

### Three Main Purposes of Testing

1. Representation of a specified content area (e.g., English spelling test).
2. Measurement of present level of performance or prediction of future performance on a variable (e.g., mechanical aptitude for a machinist job).
3. Measurement of a psychological construct that cannot be directly observed (e.g., neuroticism, intelligence, emotional adjustment).

### Three Main Types of Validity

Corresponding to the above purposes (based on APA Standards monograph, 1966):

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Content validity | Representativeness of the content domain | Achievement tests, proficiency tests |
| Criterion-related validity | Correlation with an external criterion (present or future) | Aptitude tests, selection tests |
| Construct validity | Measurement of a theoretical construct | Personality tests, intelligence tests |

> **Caution (1999 Standards)**: The categories are not mutually exclusive. Validity should be viewed as a unitary concept; the categories are conveniences, not distinctly different forms.

## Content or Curricular Validity

**Content validity** (also called **intrinsic validity**, **relevance**, **circular validity**, or **representativeness**) is a non-statistical type of validity primarily associated with achievement tests.

A test has good content validity when its items measure what the whole domain measures — i.e., the items collectively and individually cover a representative sample of the behaviour domain being assessed.

**Anastasi (1968, p. 100)**: "Content validity involves essentially the systematic examination of the test content to determine whether it covers a representative sample of the behaviour domain to be measured."

### Item Validity vs. Sampling Validity

- **Item validity**: Each test item individually represents measurement in the intended content area (e.g., all items in a biology test deal with biological knowledge).
- **Sampling validity**: The full set of items adequately samples the entire content area — not just valid individual items, but adequate coverage of the whole domain.

Both item validity and sampling validity are required for adequate content validity.

### Content-Coverage and Content-Representation Validity

Introduced in the 1999 edition of Standards for Educational and Psychological Tests:

- **Content-coverage validity**: Related to failure to incorporate certain relevant content (e.g., a history test covering only ancient history, omitting modern history).
- **Content-representation validity**: Related to whether the test adequately represents the full construct domain.

### Content-Related and Construct-Irrelevant Variance

- **Content-related variance** (also called construct under-representation): Occurs when relevant content is omitted from the test.
- **Construct-irrelevant variance**: Occurs when test scores are influenced by factors irrelevant to the construct being measured (e.g., an intelligence test influenced by test anxiety or reading comprehension).

### Steps for Establishing Content Validity

Content validity is examined by two methods: (i) expert judgement, and (ii) statistical methods.

**By Expert Judgement:**
- Submit test items to a group of subject-matter experts.
- Experts judge whether items represent all important topics, whether additional items are needed, and whether the weight assigned to topics is appropriate.

**By Statistical Methods:**
- Ensure all items measure the same thing (homogeneous cluster of items).
- Correlate scores on two independent tests covering the same content — a high correlation demonstrates content validity.
- Compute item-discriminating power: items that correlate positively with total test scores provide circumstantial evidence for content validity.

**Comprehensive content validation requires:**
1. Explicitly specifying the content area so all major portions are covered in proportion.
2. Defining the content area clearly (objectives, factual knowledge, principles, concepts) before item writing begins.
3. Establishing relevance of items based on examinee responses, not just the apparent face of the items.

### Face Validity

**Face validity** is not to be confused with content validity. It refers to whether a test appears relevant and appropriate to the test takers — not what the test actually measures, but what it looks like it measures.

- Face validity is not a technical form of validity.
- It is important for test-taker motivation: when a test looks appropriate for its purpose, examinees are more likely to cooperate and perform well (Sackett et al., 2001; Kaplan & Saccuzzo, 2001).
- Face validity helps improve test quality by improving wording and structure, but should not substitute for technical validity.

## Criterion-Related Validity

**Criterion-related validity** is a very common type of validity obtained by correlating test scores with a criterion — an external and independent measure of the trait the test is intended to assess.

**Cronbach (1971)**: The validity of a test is an empirical question based on perfectly reliable criterion scores.

The **criterion** is defined as an external and independent measure against which the test is validated.

There are two subtypes:
- **(a) Predictive validity**
- **(b) Concurrent validity**

---

### Predictive Validity

**Predictive validity** (also called **empirical validity** or **statistical validity**) is established when a test is correlated against a criterion measured at some point in the future. There is always a **time gap** between the test administration and criterion measurement.

The correlation between test scores and future criterion scores constitutes the validity coefficient, expressed as a **Pearson r**.

**Marshall & Hales (1972, p. 110)**: Predictive validity "is a method which determines the relationship between the scores on the test and an independent criterion measure, where the criterion measure is obtained later."

**Example**: An intelligence test administered at college admission is correlated with grade point average (GPA) obtained two years later. High correlation indicates good predictive validity.

**Applications**: Long-range prediction of academic achievement, vocational adjustment, vocational interest, and aptitude-based job selection.

**Important note**: The reliability of the criterion must be considered when establishing the predictive validity coefficient.

---

### Concurrent Validity

**Concurrent validity** is another subtype of criterion-related validity. It differs from predictive validity in that there is **no time gap** — both the test and the criterion measure are administered at the same time (concurrently).

**Example**: Scores on a newly developed intelligence test correlated with scores on an existing standardized intelligence test administered simultaneously. If the correlation is too high, the new test is considered a duplication.

**Applications**: Diagnosis of present status, classifying or grouping individuals.

**Two methods for computing concurrent validity:**

1. **Relationship method**:
   - Administer the new test to a defined group.
   - Administer the criterion (established valid test) to the same group.
   - Correlate the two sets of scores.
   - If coefficient is 0.70 or above, the test has good concurrent validity.

2. **Discrimination method**:
   - Determine whether test scores can discriminate between those who possess and those who do not possess a characteristic (e.g., a mental adjustment inventory discriminating between well-adjusted and maladjusted individuals).

### Predictive vs. Concurrent Validity: Key Comparison

- Concurrent validity is generally **higher** than predictive validity.
- Reason: In predictive validity, the criterion is gathered later, allowing scores to change over time, reducing correlation. Scores correlate more highly with present achievement than achievement five years later.
- If a test has good content validity, its predictive validity may be close to zero.

### The Criterion Problem

Whether computing predictive or concurrent validity, the researcher faces the challenge of selecting an appropriate criterion. An inappropriate or inadequate criterion reduces the validity coefficient. Sources of contamination include: interest, motivation, health, emotional adjustment, grading practices, working conditions, light, heat, speed of machines, electricity supply, quality of materials.

## Correction for Attenuation

When the criterion is unreliable (attenuated), the observed validity coefficient underestimates the true theoretical relationship. The obtained validity coefficient should be **corrected for attenuation**.

### Full Correction (Both Test and Criterion)

**Equation 6.1:**

```
        r_tc
r_c = ─────────────
      √(r_tt × r_cc)
```

Where:
- `r_c` = validity coefficient corrected for attenuation in both test and criterion
- `r_tc` = obtained validity coefficient (observed correlation between test and criterion)
- `r_tt` = reliability coefficient of the test
- `r_cc` = reliability coefficient of the criterion

**Illustration**: If `r_tt = 0.70`, `r_cc = 0.80`, `r_tc = 0.50`:

```
r_c = 0.50 / √(0.70 × 0.80) = 0.50 / 0.836 = 0.60
```

The theoretical upper limit of the correlation between the test and the criterion equals `√(r_tt × r_cc)`. In the above example: `√(0.70 × 0.80) = 0.836`, meaning the test and criterion cannot correlate beyond 0.836.

### One-Way Correction (Criterion Only)

When the criterion is far less reliable than the test, most researchers prefer to correct only the criterion. This is **one-way correction for attenuation**.

**Equation 6.2:**

```
        r_tc
r_c = ────────
       √r_cc
```

**Illustration**: If `r_tc = 0.50`, `r_cc = 0.67`:

```
r_c = 0.50 / √0.67 = 0.50 / 0.818 = 0.55
```

## Major Qualities Desired in a Criterion Measure

For a criterion to be satisfactory, four qualities are required:

**(i) Relevance**: The criterion must reflect the important behaviours of ultimate interest. Relevance corresponds to the content validity of the criterion measure.

**(ii) Freedom from Bias**: Each person should have the same opportunity to score high or low regardless of age, gender, or social class. A biased criterion confounds the meaning of the correlation between test and criterion scores (Cornilli, 2006).

**(iii) Reliability**: The criterion score must be stable and reproducible — it must not fluctuate widely from one situation to another or from day to day.

**(iv) Availability (Practicality)**: The criterion must be convenient and economical. It should not be so costly or difficult to obtain that computing the validity coefficient becomes impractical.

## Construct Validity

**Construct validity** is the third major type of validity and the most comprehensive and complex. It evaluates whether a test measures the theoretical construct it is intended to measure.

The term was first formally used in 1954 in the **Technical Recommendations for Psychological Tests** published by the American Psychological Association.

**Cronbach & Meehl (1955, p. 282)**: Construct validity is required when "no criterion measure is available and the test is used to measure some quality to be meaningful."

Also referred to as **factorial validity** and **trait validity**.

### What is a Construct?

**Ghiselli (1964, p. 114)**: "A construct is a label attached to a cluster of interrelated behaviors."

A **construct** is a nonobservable trait (such as anxiety, intelligence, or creativity) that explains observed behaviour.

**Mackinney's formulation**: "A variety of behaviours will correlate with one another in studies of individual differences and/or will vary together, as verified by experimental treatments."

Examples of constructs: anxiety, intelligence, verbal ability, scholastic aptitude, introversion, dominance.

### Steps in Determining Construct Validity

1. **Specify the possible different measures** of the construct.
2. **Determine the extent of correlation** between all or some of those measures.
3. **Evaluate whether** all or some measures act as if they were measuring the construct.

**Example — Specifying measures of intelligence**: If intelligence is defined as the overall ability of an individual to behave purposefully and adapt to the varying demands of the environment, specifications would include:
- Quick decisions in complex and difficult tasks
- Ability to operate with symbols and figures
- Ability to learn
- Purposeful / goal-related behaviour
- Comprehension of verbal materials

### Evidence for Construct Validity (Gregory)

A new test has construct validity if:
1. The test appears homogeneous and measures a single construct.
2. The test correlates highly with related tests and poorly with unrelated tests.
3. Developmental changes over time (across age groups) are consistent with the theory of the construct.
4. Differences among subgroups on the test are theory-consistent.
5. Interventions produce theory-consistent changes in test scores.
6. Factor analysis of test scores produces results understandable in light of the theory underlying the test construction.

### Cronbach's Unified Position

**Lee Cronbach (1989, p. 91)**: "All validation is one and in a sense, all is construct validation."

The 1999 Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing also rejected separate categories and recognizes different categories of **evidence** for validity, not different types of validity.

---

## Convergent Validation and Discriminant Validation

Introduced by **Campbell (1959)** as essential components of establishing construct validity.

- **Convergent validity**: A test correlates well with other measures assessing the same construct or concept. When a measure correlates well with constructs believed to assess the same concept, convergent validity is obtained.
- **Discriminant validity** (discriminant validation): A test does NOT correlate (or correlates poorly) with measures of unrelated constructs. Low correlations with unrelated measures become evidence of validity.

**Example**: A numerical aptitude test should:
- Correlate highly with a mathematical reasoning test and algebra test (convergent).
- NOT correlate with a history or geography test (discriminant).

---

## Multitrait-Multimethod Matrix (MTMM)

**Campbell & Fiske (1959)** proposed the **multitrait-multimethod matrix (MTMM)** as a special experimental design for studying convergent and discriminant validities simultaneously.

**Requirement**: Two or more traits must be measured by two or more different methods.

**Structure of the MTMM Matrix (Table 6.1 — Hypothetical Example)**:

| | Method 1 | | | Method 2 | | | Method 3 | | |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Traits | A1 | B1 | C1 | A2 | B2 | C2 | A3 | B3 | C3 |
| A1 | (.89) | | | | | | | | |
| B1 | .51 | (.89) | | | | | | | |
| C1 | .38 | .37 | (.76) | | | | | | |
| A2 | .57 | .22 | .09 | (.93) | | | | | |
| B2 | .22 | .57 | .10 | .68 | (.94) | | | | |
| C2 | .11 | .11 | .46 | .59 | .58 | (.84) | | | |
| A3 | .56 | .22 | .11 | .67 | .42 | .33 | (.94) | | |
| B3 | .23 | .58 | .12 | .43 | .66 | .34 | .67 | (.92) | |
| C3 | .11 | .11 | .45 | .34 | .32 | .58 | .58 | .60 | (.85) |

### Terminology

- **Reliability diagonals** (monotrait-monomethod): Values in parentheses on the main diagonal — same trait, same method.
- **Validity diagonals** (monotrait-heteromethod): Correlations between the same trait measured by different methods — evidence for convergent validity.
- **Heterotrait-monomethod triangles** (solid-line triangles): Correlations between different traits measured by the same method.
- **Heterotrait-heteromethod triangles** (broken-line triangles): Correlations between different traits measured by different methods.
- **Monomethod block**: Reliability diagonals + heterotrait-monomethod triangles.
- **Heteromethod block**: Validity diagonals + heterotrait-heteromethod triangles.

### Four Requirements for MTMM Validity

1. **Values in validity diagonals must be large and statistically significant** — evidence for convergent validity.
2. **Values in validity diagonals must be higher than heterotrait-heteromethod values (broken-line triangles)** — evidence for discriminant validity.
3. **Values in validity diagonals must be higher than heterotrait-monomethod values (solid-line triangles)** — if opposite, method-specific factors (social desirability, test anxiety, etc.) are contaminating scores. Evidence for discriminant validity.
4. **Both monomethod blocks and heteromethod triangles should show marked patterns of intercorrelation** — further evidence for discriminant validity.

For satisfactory construct validity, requirements 1, 2, and 3 must be met at minimum.

## The Unified View of Validity

The unified view of validity emerged in the 1980s (Cronbach 1988; Messick 1989; Shepard 1993):

- Validation is a **never-ending process**, not a one-time event.
- Validity requires evaluating whether interpretations and uses of test results are justified by supporting evidence AND in terms of the consequences of those interpretations and uses.

### Types of Inferences Requiring Validation

- **Interpretation inferences**: What the test score means — how scores are interpreted.
- **Action inferences**: Whether test scores appropriately and usefully inform specific actions or decisions.

**Messick's expanded theory** extends validity beyond score meaning to include:
- Value implications of score interpretation
- Relevance and utility of test scores
- Consequences of test interpretation and use (both actual and potential)
- Social consequences of score interpretation

## Statistical Methods for Calculating Validity

### Correlation Methods

The validity coefficient is a correlation between the test and an independent criterion. Appropriate correlation statistics depend on the scale of measurement:

| Data Type | Appropriate Statistic |
|---|---|
| One variable continuous, other dichotomous (Pass/Fail, True/False, Above/Below average) | Pearson r or **point biserial r** |
| Both variables dichotomous | **Tetrachoric r** or **phi coefficient** |
| More than two measures involved | **Multiple correlation (R)** |

- **Multiple correlation (R)**: The relationship between one measure and a composite of two or more sets of measures.
- **Pearson r** is the most commonly used validity coefficient.

### Expectancy Tables

An **expectancy table** is a bivariate distribution showing the relationship between test scores and criterion measures. For each test score range, the percentage of examinees achieving each criterion category is tabulated.

**Purpose**: To estimate the **predictive efficiency** of a test — the probability that an examinee scoring at a particular level on the test will obtain a specified score or rating on the criterion.

**Examples of questions answered by expectancy tables:**
- What is the probability that a student with an IQ of 100 will secure the first rank in class X?
- What is the probability that a person at the 90th percentile on a clerical speed test will perform in the top one-third as a clerk?
- What is the probability that a person scoring 60 (out of 139) on a medical entrance test will be rated "excellent" after completing the course?

**Table 6.2 (Illustrative)**: Medical Entrance Test scores of 200 students vs. criterion grades (Worse, Poor, Average, Good, Excellent) at Patna Medical College Hospital. Higher MET score bands show higher percentages of "Good" and "Excellent" ratings; lower bands show higher "Worse" and "Poor" ratings.

### Cut-off Score

A **cut-off score** is the score on a test that separates potentially superior examinees from potentially inferior ones. It is used to:
- Select the maximum number of potentially good examinees.
- Eliminate the maximum number of inferior or poor examinees.

Limitation: No cut-off is perfect because no test is perfectly valid and errors of measurement exist. Some inferior examinees will be selected and some superior ones rejected.

### Miscellaneous Techniques

Additional infrequently used procedures for establishing validity:
- Comparison of test scores of **contrasted groups** (e.g., normals vs. neurotics, institutionalized mental defectives vs. normal school children).
- Study of the relationship between scores and after-college records.
- Grouping students of a particular age with similar-percentage students in another age group to establish validity.

## Factors Influencing Validity

### Length of the Test

Lengthening (or repeatedly administering) a test increases both reliability and validity.

**Formula for validity of a test lengthened n times (Equation 6.3):**

```
              n × r_c1
r_cv_n = ──────────────────────────────
          √[n × r_11 × (1 + (n-1) × r_11)]
```

Where:
- `r_cv_n` = validity coefficient of the test lengthened n times
- `r_c1` = validity coefficient of the test at original length
- `n` = number of times test is lengthened (or repeated)
- `r_11` = reliability coefficient of the test

**Illustration**: Test with validity = 0.50, reliability = 0.40, lengthened 4 times:
- Result: validity increases from 0.50 to approximately 0.63.

**Formula for determining how many times to lengthen (Equation 6.4):**

```
        r_cv_n (1 - r_c1²)
n = ──────────────────────────
     r_c1² (1 - r_cv_n²) × r_11
```

(Rearrangement of Equation 6.3 to solve for n.)

**Formula when both test and criterion are lengthened (Equation 6.5):**

```
                       r_tc
r_yx' = ──────────────────────────────────────────────
         √[(1 - r_11 + n_t × r_11)(1 - r_cc + n_c × r_cc)]
```

Where `n_t` = times test is lengthened, `n_c` = times criterion is lengthened, `r_cc` = reliability of criterion.

**Illustration**: Test lengthened 3 times, criterion lengthened 3 times, `r_11 = 0.58`, `r_cc = 0.56`, `r_tc = 0.65`:
- Validity coefficient increases from 0.65 to approximately 0.92.

**Maximum validity when test is lengthened indefinitely (Equation 6.6):**

```
          r_yc
r_max = ────────
         √r_tt
```

Where `r_max` = maximum validity when lengthened indefinitely; `r_yc` = obtained validity coefficient; `r_tt` = reliability coefficient of the test.

This formula uses the **index of reliability** as its denominator, establishing the absolute upper limit of validity.

---

### Range of Ability (Sample Heterogeneity)

Like reliability, validity is affected by the range of ability of the sample:
- **Narrow range of ability** (restricted range) → low validity coefficient.
- **Wider range of ability** → higher validity coefficient.

---

### Ambiguous Directions

Ambiguous test directions lead to different interpretations by different examinees, encourage guessing, and consequently lower validity.

---

### Socio-Cultural Differences

Cultural differences across societies affect test validity. A test developed in one culture may not be valid for another due to differences in socioeconomic status, sex ratios, social norms, etc. This factor does not apply when a test is specifically designed to be cross-cultural.

---

### Addition of Inappropriate Items

Items with difficulty values that differ widely from original items, or vague items, lower both the reliability and validity of the test.

---

## Concept of Cross-Validation

**Cross-validation** is the process of recalculating the validity of a test on a new sample of the same population — a sample different from the one used in item analysis (standardization sample).

### Why Cross-Validation Is Necessary

When validity is computed on the same standardization sample used for item selection, the validity coefficient is **spuriously high** because:
- Items were selected to maximize correlation with the criterion on that specific sample.
- The coefficient is inflated by chance factors and specific characteristics of the standardization sample (chance sampling errors and item selection bias).

### Classic Demonstration

Empirical study (1940, Kurz 1948) with the **Rorschach test** and 80 sales managers:
- Items (Rorschach signs) were selected based on differentiating between an upper criterion group (42 managers most helpful to companies) and a lower group.
- On the standardization sample (n=80): 76 of 80 managers classified correctly; validity coefficient ≈ 1.00.
- On the cross-validation sample (n=41): validity coefficient dropped to **0.02**.

This demonstrates that items selected to discriminate in one sample cannot be assumed to discriminate in a new sample.

### Shrinkage of Validity Coefficient

**Validity shrinkage**: A phenomenon wherein a test predicts the relevant criterion less accurately with a new sample than with the original standardization sample.

**Factors affecting the amount of shrinkage:**
- **Large initial item pool + small proportion of items retained** → greater opportunity to capitalize on chance → greater shrinkage.
- **Small sample size** → greater validity shrinkage.
- Item selection strategy (rational vs. empirical) also affects shrinkage (Lawshe, Bolda & Brune, 1958; Klimoski, 1966).

Cross-validation is also the measure of the **extent of shrinkage** of the validity coefficient.

## Extravalidity Concerns

Even when a test is fair, valid, and unbiased for its intended use, users must consider additional issues. These are called **extravalidity concerns** (Cole & Moss, 1989).

### The Four Main Extravalidity Concerns

1. What is the purpose for which the test is used?
2. To what extent are those purposes accomplished by the actions taken?
3. What are the likely side effects of testing or using tests in decision-making?
4. What possible alternatives to the test may serve the same purpose?

### Unintended Side Effects of Testing

Psychological tests may produce unintentional negative consequences. Examples:

**School setting** (psychological testing of children):
- Teachers may not educate identified children and rate them as unworthy of attention.
- Identified children may feel stigmatized.
- Other children may call such children names.
- May encourage creation of special classes segregated by race or social class.

**Employment setting** (MMPI used to screen police candidates):
- Candidates rejected based on high scores on Psychopathic Deviation, Paranoia, or Depression may be permanently labeled in medical records.
- Such labels may bar them from other employment or educational opportunities.

### Functionalist Definition of Validity

Due to renewed sensitivity to extravalidity concerns, many test publishers have adopted a **functionalist definition**:

> A test is valid if it serves the purpose for which it is used.
> (Messick, 1988, 1995)

This contrasts with the technical definition ("a test is valid if it measures what it intends to measure").

**Moss (1992)** and supporters of the functionalist perspective assert that a valid test must satisfy:
1. Sufficient evidence for construct validity (convergent and discriminant validity).
2. Analysis of the value implications of test interpretation.
3. Evidence for the usefulness of test interpretation.
4. Assessment of both intended and unintended test consequences, including negative side effects.

## Relation of Validity to Reliability

Reliability and validity are both measures of efficiency:
- **Reliability** = self-correlation of the test (efficiency within itself).
- **Validity** = correlation of the test with an outside independent criterion (efficiency against an external standard).

A test that correlates nothing with itself cannot be expected to correlate well with external criteria. Therefore: **high reliability is necessary before high validity is possible.**

> **Reliability is a necessary but not sufficient condition for validity.**

However, for **heterogeneous tests** (e.g., biographical data blanks), validity may be high even without high internal consistency reliability, because heterogeneous tests measure different traits simultaneously.

### Upper Limit of Validity

Given the reliability of a test, validity can never exceed the **index of reliability** (square root of reliability):

```
Maximum validity ≤ √r_tt
```

**Theoretical maximum validity coefficient** between two variables:

```
r_12 = √(r_11 × r_22)
```

Where `r_11` and `r_22` are the reliabilities of the two test variables.

**Example**: If `r_11 = 0.80` (test reliability) and `r_22 = 1.00` (criterion reliability):

```
r_12 = √(0.80 × 1.00) = √0.80 = 0.89
```

Maximum validity = **0.89**.

### The Apparent Conflict Between Reliability and Validity

- **High reliability** requires: item homogeneity, high inter-item correlations.
- **High validity** requires: items of different difficulty values, low inter-item correlations.

These requirements appear contradictory.

**Tucker (1946)** resolved this conflict: If inter-item correlations range from **0.10 to 0.40**, one can expect both satisfactory reliability and validity simultaneously.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Validity | The degree to which a test measures what it purports to measure |
| Validity coefficient | Correlation coefficient between a test and an independent criterion |
| Content validity | The degree to which test items represent a representative sample of the intended content domain |
| Item validity | Whether individual test items represent measurement in the intended content area |
| Sampling validity | Whether the full set of test items adequately samples the entire content area |
| Face validity | Whether a test appears relevant and appropriate to test takers (not a technical form of validity) |
| Criterion | An external and independent measure against which a test is validated |
| Criterion-related validity | Validity established by correlating test scores with an external criterion (present or future) |
| Predictive validity | Criterion-related validity where the criterion is measured at a future time point |
| Concurrent validity | Criterion-related validity where test and criterion are administered at the same time |
| Construct validity | Validity of a test as a measure of a theoretical, nonobservable construct |
| Construct | A nonobservable trait (anxiety, intelligence) that explains observed behavior |
| Convergent validity | Evidence that a test correlates well with other measures of the same construct |
| Discriminant validity | Evidence that a test does NOT correlate with measures of unrelated constructs |

| Validity diagonals | Monotrait-heteromethod correlations in the MTMM matrix |
| Reliability diagonals | Monotrait-monomethod correlations in the MTMM matrix |
| Correction for attenuation | Statistical adjustment of a validity coefficient to correct for unreliability in the test or criterion |
| Full correction | Corrects for unreliability in both test and criterion |
| One-way correction | Corrects for unreliability in the criterion only |
| Cross-validation | Recalculating validity on a new sample different from the standardization sample |
| Validity shrinkage | Reduction in validity coefficient when the test is applied to a new sample |
| Expectancy table | Bivariate table showing the probability of achieving a criterion score for each test score range |
| Cut-off score | Score that separates potentially superior from potentially inferior examinees |
| Extravalidity concerns | Considerations beyond technical validity: purpose, side effects, consequences of test use |

| Content-related variance | Omission of relevant content from the test (construct under-representation) |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Validity is NOT the same as reliability**: Validity is correlation with an outside independent criterion; reliability is the self-correlation of the test.

2. **Three types of validity (APA 1966)**: Content validity, criterion-related validity, and construct validity. The 1999 Standards rejected these as separate categories, viewing validity as unitary with different categories of evidence.

3. **Cronbach (1989)**: "All validation is one and in a sense, all is construct validation."

4. **Correction for attenuation formula**: `r_c = r_tc / √(r_tt × r_cc)`. One-way correction (criterion only): `r_c = r_tc / √r_cc`. Both increase the observed validity coefficient.

5. **Convergent validity vs. discriminant validity (Campbell & Fiske, 1959)**: Convergent = test correlates with related measures; discriminant = test does NOT correlate with unrelated measures. Both established via the MTMM matrix.

6. **MTMM matrix rule**: Validity diagonal values (monotrait-heteromethod) must exceed both heterotrait-heteromethod and heterotrait-monomethod values for satisfactory construct validity.

7. **Cross-validation**: Validity is always recomputed on a new sample because validity on the standardization sample is spuriously inflated. The drop in validity coefficient is called validity shrinkage. Greater shrinkage with larger item pools, lower proportion retained, and smaller samples.

8. **Maximum validity formula**: `r_12 = √(r_11 × r_22)`. Validity can never exceed the index of reliability (`√r_tt`) for a homogeneous test.

9. **Tucker (1946)**: Inter-item correlations of 0.10 to 0.40 yield both satisfactory reliability and satisfactory validity simultaneously — resolves the apparent contradiction between reliability and validity requirements.

10. **Predictive validity is generally lower than concurrent validity** because the criterion in predictive validity is measured later, allowing scores to drift.

11. **Face validity is not a technical form of validity**: It refers to whether a test appears appropriate to examinees; it is important for motivation but should not substitute for technical validity.

12. **Messick's five sources of validity evidence (1989)**: Content, substantive, structural, generalizable, external — plus consequences of testing.

13. **Construct-irrelevant variance** (e.g., test anxiety affecting an intelligence test) and **content-related variance** (construct under-representation) are the two main threats to validity introduced in the 1999 Standards.

14. **Qualities of a good criterion**: Relevance, freedom from bias, reliability, availability (practicality).

15. **Functionalist definition of validity (Messick, 1988)**: A test is valid if it serves the purpose for which it is used — contrasted with the technical definition that validity is about measuring what is intended.
