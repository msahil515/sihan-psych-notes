# Ch 3: Psychological Testing & Assessment

**Overview:** Covers Singh's Part Two (Chapters 9-13): measurement of intelligence, aptitude, and achievement; personality assessment tools and construction strategies; projective techniques (Rorschach, TAT, word association, expressive methods); techniques of observation and data collection (questionnaire, interview, rating scales, content analysis); and psychophysical and psychological scaling methods including attitude scales. ## SECTION 1: Measurement of Intelligence, Aptitude, and Achievement (Ch. 9)

### 1.1 Viewpoints Toward Intelligence

- **Two-factor theory (Spearman, 1904):** intelligence = **g** (general factor) + **s** (specific factors). g is common to all cognitive tasks; s is unique to each task.
- **Group-factor theory (Thurstone, 1938):** no single g; instead **7 Primary Mental Abilities (PMA):** verbal comprehension, word fluency, number, space, associative memory, perceptual speed, reasoning.
- **Structure of Intellect (Guilford, 1967):** **3D model** -- **Operations** (5) x **Contents** (4) x **Products** (6) = **120 factors** (later extended to 150+). Operations: cognition, memory, divergent production, convergent production, evaluation.
- **Hierarchical models:** Vernon's two-strata (g at top; major group factors v:ed and k:m; minor group factors; specifics). Carroll's Three-Stratum Theory (1993): Stratum III = g, Stratum II = broad abilities (fluid, crystallized, etc.), Stratum I = narrow.
- **Fluid vs. Crystallized intelligence (Cattell & Horn):** **Gf** = biologically based, culture-fair, peaks early, declines with age; **Gc** = learned, accumulated knowledge, relatively stable with age.

### 1.2 Types of Intelligence Tests

| Type | Basis | Examples |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Individual** | One examiner : one examinee | Binet-Simon, Stanford-Binet, WAIS, WISC |
| **Group** | One examiner : many examinees | Army Alpha (verbal), Army Beta (non-verbal) |
| **Verbal** | Language-based items | Stanford-Binet, Verbal subtests of WAIS |
| **Non-verbal / Performance** | No language required | Raven's Progressive Matrices, Kohs Block Design |
| **Culture-fair** | Minimizes cultural bias | Cattell Culture-Fair Test, Raven's |

**Key Indian tests:** Bhatia Battery of Performance Tests (5 subtests: Koh's Block Design, Alexander Passalong, Pattern Drawing, Immediate Memory, Picture Construction).

### 1.3 Types of Intelligence Test Scores

- **Mental Age (MA):** concept by **Binet**; the age level at which a child performs. A child's MA = the age group whose tasks they pass.
- **Intelligence Quotient (IQ):** introduced by **Stern (1912)**, popularized by **Terman**.
  - **Ratio IQ:** (MA / CA) x 100. Problem: cannot be computed meaningfully beyond ~16 years; MA ceases to increase but CA continues.
  - **Deviation IQ (Wechsler):** converts raw score to a standard score with **mean = 100, SD = 15**. Solves the ceiling problem. WAIS uses deviation IQ.
- **Percentile Rank:** percentage of the normative sample scoring below a given score.
- **Standard Score:** z-score, T-score (mean 50, SD 10), stanine (1-9 scale, mean 5, SD 2).

> **Key Point:** Wechsler replaced ratio IQ with **deviation IQ** because ratio IQ becomes meaningless once MA stops growing. Deviation IQ has the same mean (100) and SD (15) at every age level, making cross-age comparisons valid.

### 1.4 Aptitude vs. Achievement Tests

| Dimension | Aptitude Test | Achievement Test |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Purpose** | Predict future learning/performance | Measure past learning/attainment |
| **Content** | Relatively novel tasks | Taught curriculum or skills |
| **Time orientation** | Future-predictive | Past-reflective |
| **Examples** | DAT, GATB, SAT | Board exams, classroom tests |
| **Specificity** | Often multi-aptitude batteries | Domain-specific |

**Differential Aptitude Test (DAT; Bennett et al.):** 8 subtests: verbal reasoning, numerical ability, abstract reasoning, clerical speed and accuracy, mechanical reasoning, space relations, spelling, language usage. Widely used for vocational guidance.

**General Aptitude Test Battery (GATB; USES):** 12 tests yielding 9 aptitudes (G, V, N, S, P, Q, K, F, M).

### 1.5 Tests of Creativity

- **Guilford:** creativity linked to **divergent production** in his SOI model. Tests: Unusual Uses, Consequences, Remote Associations.
- **Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking (TTCT):** **figural** (Draw and Guess) and **verbal** (ask, guess, product improvement). Scored for **fluency** (number of responses), **flexibility** (number of categories), **originality** (statistical rarity), **elaboration** (detail added).
- Creativity distinct from intelligence (threshold hypothesis: IQ above ~120 has little relationship with creativity).

## SECTION 2: Measurement of Personality (Ch. 10)

### 2.1 Meaning and Purpose

**Personality measurement** aims to describe, classify, and predict behavior patterns and traits systematically. Purposes: (1) clinical diagnosis; (2) personnel selection; (3) research; (4) counseling and guidance; (5) understanding normal vs. abnormal functioning.

### 2.2 Tools of Personality Assessment

| Tool Category | Examples |
|--------------|---------|
| **Self-report inventories** | MMPI, 16PF, EPQ/EPI, NEO-PI-R |
| **Projective techniques** | Rorschach, TAT, Word Association, Sentence Completion |
| **Situational tests** | Leaderless group discussion, in-basket, stress interview |
| **Behavioral observation** | Rating scales, behavioral checklists, time sampling |
| **Psychophysiological measures** | GSR, heart rate, EEG in anxiety/stress |
| **Interview** | Structured, semi-structured, unstructured clinical interviews |

### 2.3 Strategies for Constructing Personality Inventories (Singh's Taxonomy)

Singh identifies **four major strategies**:

#### (A) Content/Logical/Rational Strategy
- Items are selected based on **face validity** and **logical content relevance** to the trait being measured.
- Expert judgment determines item inclusion; no empirical criterion group needed.
- **Example:** Woodworth Personal Data Sheet (earliest personality inventory, WWI); Edwards Personal Preference Schedule (EPPS -- measures Murray's needs).
- **Limitation:** Items are transparent, easily faked; susceptible to response sets.

#### (B) Empirical Criterion-Keying Strategy
- Items selected **purely by their ability to differentiate** a criterion group from a normal group, regardless of face validity.
- **Process:** Administer large item pool to criterion group (e.g., depressives) and normals; retain items that statistically discriminate.
- **Prime example: MMPI (Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory):** Hathaway & McKinley, 1943. 550 true/false items; keyed against clinical criterion groups.
  - **10 Clinical Scales:** Hs (Hypochondriasis), D (Depression), Hy (Hysteria), Pd (Psychopathic Deviate), Mf (Masculinity-Femininity), Pa (Paranoia), Pt (Psychasthenia), Sc (Schizophrenia), Ma (Hypomania), Si (Social Introversion).
  - **4 Validity Scales:** ? (Cannot Say), L (Lie), F (Infrequency), K (Correction/Defensiveness).
  - MMPI-2 (1989): updated norms; 567 items.
- **Advantage:** Predictive validity; **Limitation:** items may lack theoretical meaning; cross-validated needed.

#### (C) Factor-Analytic Strategy
- Uses **factor analysis** to identify underlying dimensions; items grouped into statistically independent factors.
- **16PF (Cattell, 1949):** 16 source traits (primary factors) derived from factor analysis of personality descriptors. Examples: A (Warmth), B (Reasoning), C (Emotional Stability), E (Dominance), F (Liveliness), G (Rule-Consciousness), H (Social Boldness), I (Sensitivity), L (Vigilance), M (Abstractedness), N (Privateness), O (Apprehension), Q1 (Openness to Change), Q2 (Self-Reliance), Q3 (Perfectionism), Q4 (Tension). Second-order factors include Extraversion and Anxiety.
- **Eysenck Personality Questionnaire (EPQ) / EPI:** Eysenck's **3 super-factors**: **Extraversion-Introversion (E)**, **Neuroticism-Stability (N)**, **Psychoticism (P)**. Biological basis: E linked to cortical arousal; N linked to autonomic reactivity.
- **Big Five (NEO-PI-R; Costa & McCrae):** **Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism (OCEAN).**
- **Advantage:** Parsimonious, independent factors; **Limitation:** factors depend on the item pool analyzed; rotating solutions.

#### (D) Theoretical/Construct Strategy
- Items derived from a specific **psychological theory** of personality.
- **Example:** Edwards Personal Preference Schedule (EPPS) -- based on Murray's manifest needs theory; 225 forced-choice pairs; 15 needs.
- Jackson Personality Research Form (PRF) -- also Murray-based; refined via sequential system combining rational and empirical methods.
- **Advantage:** conceptually grounded; **Limitation:** validity depends on validity of the underlying theory.

> **Key Point:** Singh's four strategies are: (1) Content/Logical, (2) Empirical Criterion-Keying (MMPI), (3) Factor-Analytic (16PF, EPQ), (4) Theoretical. The MMPI is the prototypical empirical criterion-keyed inventory.

### 2.4 Overcoming Distortions in Self-Report Inventories

**Response sets/styles that distort scores:**
- **Social desirability:** tendency to respond in culturally approved direction. Controlled by: Marlowe-Crowne Social Desirability Scale, forced-choice format, L and K validity scales (MMPI).
- **Acquiescence (yea-saying):** tendency to agree regardless of content. Controlled by: balanced keying (equal true/false items for each trait).
- **Faking good / faking bad (malingering):** Controlled by: F scale (MMPI infrequency scale); repeated-measurement consistency checks.
- **Extreme response style:** using only scale endpoints. Controlled by: forced-choice format.
- **Careless/random responding:** detected by consistency/VRIN scales in MMPI-2.

**Structural solutions:** forced-choice format (EPPS), validity scales, subtle items (not face-valid), randomized item ordering.

### 2.5 Situational Tests

Observe behavior in **standardized, realistic situations** to bypass self-report limitations.
- **Leaderless Group Discussion (LGD):** group given a problem; no leader designated; observer rates leadership, communication, cooperation.
- **In-Basket Test:** simulates managerial work; how the examinee handles memos, letters, decisions in a set time.
- **Stress Interview:** examiner is deliberately hostile; measures emotional resilience.
- **OSS Assessment (WWII):** pioneered comprehensive situational assessment for personnel selection.
- **Advantages:** high ecological validity, hard to fake; **Disadvantages:** expensive, time-consuming, observer bias.

### 2.6 Measurement of Interests, Values, and Attitudes

**Interests:**
- **Strong Vocational Interest Blank (SVIB) / Strong Interest Inventory:** compares examinee's interests to occupational criterion groups. Empirical criterion-keying (same logic as MMPI).
- **Kuder Preference Record:** 168 forced-choice triads; 10 vocational areas (Outdoor, Mechanical, Computational, Scientific, Persuasive, Artistic, Literary, Musical, Social Service, Clerical).
- Holland's RIASEC typology (Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional).

**Values:**
- **Allport-Vernon-Lindzey Study of Values (SOV):** 6 Spranger values: Theoretical, Economic, Aesthetic, Social, Political, Religious. Forced-choice; ipsative scoring.
- **Rokeach Value Survey:** 18 terminal (end-state) + 18 instrumental (mode of conduct) values; ranking format.

**Attitudes:** measured via scaling (Thurstone, Likert, Guttman, Semantic Differential) -- see Section 5.

## SECTION 3: Projective Techniques (Ch. 11)

### 3.1 Meaning

**Projective technique:** a method using ambiguous, unstructured stimuli in which the respondent "projects" their own needs, wishes, fears, and personality dynamics. Based on the **projection** defense mechanism (Frank, 1939 -- coined the term "projective methods"). The ambiguity of the stimulus maximizes projection of inner states.

> **Key Point:** Frank (1939) coined "projective methods." The rationale is that ambiguous stimuli compel the subject to impose their own structure, thereby revealing unconscious personality dynamics.

### 3.2 Classification of Projective Techniques (Singh's Taxonomy)

Singh presents **two classification systems**:

**Classification 1 (Frank's original 5 types):**

| Type | Stimulus/Task | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **Constitutive** | Give structure/form to amorphous material | Rorschach Inkblot Test |
| **Constructive** | Construct/arrange objects to make something | World Test, Mosaic Test |
| **Interpretive** | Interpret/tell a story about a stimulus | TAT, CAT, SAT |
| **Cathartic** | Express feelings through play or drama | Play therapy, psychodrama |
| **Refractive** | Reveal unconscious through handwriting/expressive movements | Graphology, drawing tests |

**Classification 2 (Lindzey's widely cited 5 types):**

| Type | Task | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| **Association** | Respond immediately to stimulus | Rorschach, Word Association Test |
| **Construction** | Create/build a product | TAT (story construction), Make-a-Picture Story |
| **Completion** | Complete an incomplete stimulus | Sentence Completion Test, Rosenzweig PFT |
| **Choice/Ordering** | Select or arrange stimuli | Szondi Test, Luscher Color Test |
| **Expressive** | Express self through drawing/play | HTP, DAP, Play techniques |

### 3.3 Pictorial Techniques

#### 3.3A THE RORSCHACH INKBLOT TEST

**Developer:** Hermann Rorschach (Swiss psychiatrist), 1921. Published "Psychodiagnostik."

**Stimulus Material:**
- **10 cards** (18 x 24 cm), each with a symmetrical inkblot.
- **Cards I, IV, V, VI, VII:** **Achromatic** (black and gray only).
- **Cards II, III:** Achromatic **with red** color.
- **Cards VIII, IX, X:** **Fully chromatic** (multiple colors).
- Card **IV** is the "Father card" (dark, threatening, large); Card **VII** is the "Mother card" (light, feminine).

**Administration (Klopfer's system):**

1. **Free Association Phase (Performance Proper):** Cards presented one at a time; examiner asks "What might this be?" or "What do you see?" Responses recorded verbatim; card position and time noted. No limit on number of responses.
2. **Inquiry Phase:** After all 10 cards, examiner goes back through responses; asks "Where did you see it? What made it look like that?" to clarify location and determinants.
3. **Testing of Limits (optional):** After inquiry; examiner may suggest areas or ask whether movement/color was seen if not spontaneously reported; used to assess potential, not scored.

**Major Scoring Systems:**
- **Klopfer** (most detailed; popular in USA historically)
- **Beck** (more objective, conservative)
- **Exner's Comprehensive System (1974 onwards):** most empirically validated; now standard.

**Rorschach Scoring Categories (Exner/Klopfer):**

| Category | What it Scores | Symbol Examples |
|----------|---------------|----------------|
| **Location** | Where on the blot the response is seen | W (whole), D (large detail), Dd (small/unusual detail), S (white space) |
| **Determinants** | What features determined the percept | F (form), M (human movement), FM (animal movement), m (inanimate movement), C (pure color), CF (color-form), FC (form-color), C' (achromatic color), T (texture), V (vista/depth), Y (shading-diffuse) |
| **Content** | What the percept represents | H (human), Hd (human detail), A (animal), Ad (animal detail), An (anatomy), Art, Bl (blood), Bt (botany), Cl (clouds), Ex (explosion), Fi (fire), Fd (food), Ge (geography), Hh (household), Ls (landscape), Na (nature), Sc (science), Sx (sex), Xy (X-ray) |
| **Popularity-Originality** | How common or unique | P (popular response, seen by >1/3 of normative subjects), O+ (original, creative), O- (original, pathological) |
| **Form Level / Form Quality** | Accuracy of form | F+ (good form), F- (poor form), Fu (unusual but acceptable) |
| **Z-score** | Organizational activity | Scored when response integrates multiple blot areas |

> **Key Point:** The **5 major scoring categories** are: Location, Determinants, Content, Popularity-Originality, Form Level. Memorize the symbols: W/D/Dd/S for location; F/M/C/T/V/Y for determinants.

**Interpretation (Exner's Structural Summary):**

| Index/Ratio | Interpretation |
|-------------|---------------|
| **R (total responses)** | Ideally 17-27; low R = guardedness or limited productivity |
| **W:M ratio** | Ambition : capacity to fulfil it |
| **F%** (form responses) | >70% = overcontrol/constriction; <50% = impulsivity |
| **FC:CF+C** | FC > CF+C = emotional control; CF+C dominant = emotional lability |
| **M (human movement)** | Fantasy life, empathy, capacity for ideation |
| **Experience Balance (EB = M : Sum C)** | Introversive (M > C) vs. extratensive (C > M) vs. ambiequal |
| **Lambda (L = F / all non-F)** | High L = defensive simplification; avoidant style |
| **Afr (Affective ratio)** | VIII+IX+X responses / I-VII responses; affective responsiveness |
| **Egocentricity Index** | (2 x reflection + pair responses) / R; self-focus |
| **SCZI / PTI** | Perceptual-Thinking Index; schizophrenia/psychotic indicators |
| **DEPI** | Depression Index |
| **CDI** | Coping Deficit Index |
| **S-CON** | Suicide Constellation |

**Reliability and Validity:**
- Inter-rater reliability is **moderate to good** for location/determinants with Exner system (r ~ 0.85-0.95).
- Content validity is questionable; predictive validity for many signs is low.
- Criticized: Rorschach scoring norms by Exner may have been inflated (Wood et al., 2003).

#### 3.3B HOLTZMAN INKBLOT TECHNIQUE (HIT)

**Developer:** Wayne Holtzman, 1961.

**Improvements over Rorschach:**
- **45 cards per form** (Form A and Form B for alternate-form reliability); **only 1 response per card** (controls R).
- Allows **parallel form** and **test-retest reliability** computation.
- **22 scored variables** (Location, Space, Form Definiteness, Form Appropriateness, Color, Shading, Movement, Pathognomic Verbalization, Integration, Human, Animal, Anatomy, Sex, Abstract, Anxiety, Hostility, Barrier, Penetration, Balance, Popular, Reaction Time, Rejection).
- Standardized on large normative samples.
- **Limitation:** Less rich clinically than Rorschach; reduced projective pull with one response per card.

#### 3.3C THEMATIC APPERCEPTION TEST (TAT)

**Developer:** Henry Murray and Christiana Morgan, Harvard, **1935** (published 1943 by Murray).

**Theoretical Basis:** Murray's **Need-Press Theory.**
- **Need:** an internal force that organizes perception and directs behavior (e.g., need for achievement, affiliation, dominance, autonomy).
- **Press:** environmental forces acting on the person -- **Alpha press** (objective environment) vs. **Beta press** (subjectively perceived environment).
- **Thema:** recurring interaction patterns between need and press.

**Stimulus Material:**
- **31 cards** total (including one blank card).
- Cards are divided by gender and age suitability; standard administration uses **20 cards** across two sessions.
- Stimuli: black-and-white ambiguous pictures of interpersonal scenes.
- Card **16** = blank card; examinee imagines and describes a picture -- thought to be maximally projective.

**Administration:**
- "Tell me a story about this picture. What led up to this? What is happening now? What are the characters thinking and feeling? What will happen?" (Murray's standard instructions).
- Stories recorded verbatim; typically 20 cards, 10 per session.

**Scoring:**
- **Murray's formal system:** analyze hero (protagonist), needs, press, outcomes, themes.
- **McClelland's n-Ach scoring:** reliable, standardized; widely used in research.
- **Bellak's scoring:** ego functions, defense mechanisms, object relations.

**Children's version: CAT (Children's Apperception Test; Bellak, 1949):** 10 cards with animal figures for children 3-10 years. Senior Apperception Test (SAT) for the elderly.

**Reliability and Validity:**
- Inter-rater reliability moderate with standardized scoring; informal use has low reliability.
- Validity: best established for specific variables (n-Ach) rather than global clinical use.

### 3.4 Verbal Techniques

#### Word Association Test
**Origins:** Galton (1879); Wundt; **Jung (1906)** -- developed diagnostic value (Complex Indicator). Kent-Rosanoff Free Association Test (1910): 100 stimulus words with normative data; unusual responses suggest disturbance.

**Scoring in Jung's system:** reaction time (prolonged = complex indicator); response type; physiological measures (GSR).

#### Sentence Completion Test (SCT)
- Examinee completes incomplete sentence stems (e.g., "My mother..."; "I feel most anxious when...").
- **Rotter Incomplete Sentences Blank (RISB):** 40 stems; scored on conflict scale (0-6 per item).
- **Sacks Sentence Completion Test (SSCT):** 60 stems covering 15 attitude areas (family, sex, interpersonal, self-concept).
- More structured than Rorschach/TAT; easier to score; amenable to content analysis.
- Half-way between objective and projective; relatively transparent.

### 3.5 Expressive Techniques

**Draw-a-Person Test (DAP; Machover, 1949):** "Draw a person." Then "Draw a person of the opposite sex." Interpreted via body parts, proportions, shading, placement. E.g., large head = intellectualization; shaded hands = guilt; omitted features = anxiety about that body part. Reliability and validity questionable.

**House-Tree-Person Test (HTP; Buck, 1948):** Draw a house, tree, and person; interpreted symbolically (house = home life; tree = self in relation to environment; person = self/significant other).

**Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD; Burns & Kaufman, 1970):** "Draw your family doing something." Action (kinetic) element reveals family dynamics.

**Play Techniques:** Erikson's play therapy; Lowenfeld's World Test (miniature toys in sand tray); Mosaic Test (colored pieces arranged in a design) -- constructive projective.

### 3.6 Evaluation of Projective Techniques

**Strengths:**
1. Reveal **unconscious material** not accessible via self-report.
2. Resistant to **faking** (ambiguity prevents clear strategies).
3. Rich **qualitative data** about personality dynamics.
4. Useful in clinical assessment, particularly with children and uncooperative patients.
5. Holistic picture; captures idiographic personality.

**Weaknesses/Criticisms:**
1. **Low reliability:** inter-rater, test-retest, and internal consistency often poor (except with standardized systems like Exner).
2. **Questionable validity:** many specific signs lack criterion-related validity (Wood et al., 2003 critique of Rorschach).
3. **Examiner effects:** scorer's theoretical orientation biases interpretation.
4. **Norms:** often based on small, unrepresentative samples.
5. **Subjectivity:** interpretation varies widely; not amenable to actuarial use.
6. **Time-consuming:** administration + scoring + interpretation = several hours.
7. **Training required:** years of supervised experience needed.

> **Key Point:** The single biggest criticism of projectives is low **reliability** -- without reliable scoring, validity is undermined. Exner's Comprehensive System improved Rorschach reliability substantially.

## SECTION 4: Techniques of Observation and Data Collection (Ch. 12)

### 4.1 Methods of Data Collection

Singh identifies principal methods: (1) **questionnaire**, (2) **schedule**, (3) **interview**, (4) **content analysis**, (5) **observation**, (6) **rating scales**, (7) **psychological tests.** Each has distinct properties for data quality, cost, feasibility.

### 4.2 Questionnaire vs. Schedule

| Feature | Questionnaire | Schedule |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| **Administration** | Self-administered; mailed or distributed | Interviewer fills in while interacting with respondent |
| **Literacy required** | Yes | Not necessarily |
| **Response rate** | Lower (30-50% typical for mail) | Higher (face-to-face contact) |
| **Coverage** | Can reach wide geographic area cheaply | Limited by interviewer time and travel |
| **Clarification** | Respondent cannot ask clarifier | Interviewer can clarify and probe |
| **Anonymity** | Greater | Less |
| **Observer bias** | Absent | Possible (interviewer effect) |
| **Cost** | Lower | Higher |
| **Data quality** | Variable (incomplete, misunderstood) | Generally higher |

**Characteristics of a good questionnaire:** clear, unambiguous language; simple/single questions; no leading questions; appropriate length; pilot tested; sequenced logically (funnel technique: general to specific); includes instructions.

### 4.3 Interview

**Meaning:** A face-to-face verbal interchange in which one person (interviewer) attempts to elicit information from another (interviewee).

**Types of Interview:**

| Type | Structure | Use |
|------|-----------|-----|
| **Structured** | Fixed, standardized questions; identical for all | Survey research, epidemiology; high reliability |
| **Semi-structured** | Core questions fixed; probes flexible | Clinical research, qualitative studies |
| **Unstructured/Non-directive** | No fixed questions; open exploration | Exploratory research; psychotherapy intake |
| **Focused/Depth interview** | Focuses on specific experience; probes deeply | Case studies, qualitative research |
| **Clinical interview** | Diagnostic; mental status examination | Psychiatric/clinical assessment |
| **Stress interview** | Deliberate pressure on interviewee | Personnel selection (controversial) |
| **Telephone interview** | Remote; no visual cues | Survey research; convenient but limited |

**Advantages of interview:** flexible, high response rate, can observe non-verbal cues, clarify ambiguity, adapt to respondent.
**Disadvantages:** costly, interviewer bias, social desirability, halo effect in ratings.

**Factors affecting interview reliability:** interviewer characteristics, type of questions, recording method, inter-interviewer consistency.

### 4.4 Content Analysis

**Definition (Berelson, 1952):** "A research technique for the objective, systematic, and quantitative description of the manifest content of communication."

**Steps:** (1) Define the research question; (2) select/sample documents; (3) develop coding categories; (4) train coders; (5) code content; (6) check reliability; (7) analyze and interpret.

**Units of analysis:** word, theme, character, item, space-and-time measure.

**Uses:** analyzing projective test responses, media content, historical documents, therapy transcripts, TAT stories (McClelland's n-Ach).

**Reliability:** inter-rater reliability is essential; computed via Cohen's kappa or percentage agreement.

### 4.5 Observation as a Tool of Data Collection

**Participant vs. Non-participant Observation:**

| Feature | Participant Observation | Non-participant Observation |
|---------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Role of observer** | Joins the group being studied | Observes from outside without participating |
| **Data depth** | Rich, insider perspective | More objective, outsider view |
| **Bias risk** | High (going native) | Lower but observer effect still present |
| **Reactivity** | May alter behavior through participation | Observer presence may still alter behavior |
| **Use** | Ethnography, qualitative research | Behavior rating, controlled observation |

**Naturalistic observation:** observing behavior in its natural setting without intervention. High external validity; low control.

**Controlled observation:** behavior observed under standardized conditions (lab). High internal validity; lower ecological validity.

**Time sampling:** observations at specified time intervals. **Event sampling:** observe whenever a specific event occurs.

**Observer drift:** gradual systematic change in how observers apply coding criteria over time. Controlled by periodic retraining and inter-rater checks.

**Hawthorne effect (reactivity):** behavior changes because subject knows they are being observed. Controlled by habituation, one-way mirrors, video recording.

### 4.6 Rating Scales

**Definition:** instruments that require the rater to assign a numerical or categorical value to a person on a specified characteristic.

**Types of Rating Scales (Singh's classification):**

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Numerical Rating Scale** | Numbers with brief descriptors at poles and midpoint | 1-5, 1-7, 1-10 |
| **Graphic Rating Scale** | Horizontal/vertical line with descriptors; mark placed on continuum | Most widely used |
| **Descriptive Graphic Rating Scale** | Graphic scale with verbal descriptions at several points, not just ends | Improves consistency |
| **Standard Rating Scale** | Comparison to known standards or exemplar persons/behaviors | Man-to-man comparison scale (US Army) |
| **Rank-Order Method** | Raters rank all ratees from highest to lowest on a trait | Simple but breaks down with large N |
| **Paired Comparison Method** | Each ratee compared with every other; most thorough | n(n-1)/2 comparisons needed |
| **Forced-Choice Rating Scale** | Rater chooses between equally desirable options differing in validity | Reduces social desirability; complex |
| **Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scale (BARS)** | Anchors are specific behavioral examples at each scale point | High validity; developed participatively |
| **Checklist** | Rater checks traits/behaviors present or absent | Simple; dichotomous |
| **Q-Sort** | Cards sorted into distribution (usually quasi-normal) | Forced distribution; 9 or 11 piles |

**Man-to-man comparison (Military Rating Scale):** a master scale created by rating known individuals; all new ratees compared to these standards on each trait.

### 4.7 Errors in Ratings

| Error | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Halo Effect** | Overall impression of ratee colors all specific ratings | Attractive person rated high on unrelated traits |
| **Leniency Error (Generosity Error)** | Tendency to rate all ratees too positively | Rater reluctant to give poor ratings |
| **Severity Error (Strictness Error)** | Opposite of leniency; consistently harsh ratings | Rater who thinks no one is outstanding |
| **Central Tendency Error** | Avoiding extremes; clustering around midpoint | All ratings cluster at "3" on a 5-point scale |
| **Logical Error** | Rating similar on traits assumed to be logically related | Rating high intelligence = high creativity |
| **Contrast Error** | Rating influenced by immediately preceding ratee | Outstanding ratee makes next one look poor |
| **Proximity Error** | Traits near each other on form rated more similarly | Adjacent scale items correlate too highly |
| **Recency Effect** | Recent behavior weighted more heavily than earlier | Last month's performance dominates annual rating |
| **Primacy Effect** | Early information dominates later information | First impression bias |

> **Key Point:** The **halo effect** (Thorndike, 1920) is the most commonly cited rating error. It inflates inter-item correlations and reduces discriminant validity of ratings.

### 4.8 Methods of Improving Rating Effectiveness

1. **Train raters** in observation skills and awareness of errors (RATER training: Rater Error Training; Frame-of-Reference Training -- FOT).
2. **Behavioral anchors (BARS):** replace vague adjective anchors with specific behavioral examples.
3. **Multiple raters:** average across raters reduces individual bias (360-degree feedback).
4. **Forced-choice format:** removes transparency of evaluative direction.
5. **Separate ratings for different traits:** prevents halo by assessing one trait across all ratees before moving to next trait.
6. **Reverse scoring:** some items stated negatively to detect acquiescence.
7. **Keep ratings close to observation period:** reduces recency/primacy errors.

## SECTION 5: Psychophysics and Psychological Scaling (Ch. 13)

### 5.1 Psychophysical vs. Psychological Scaling

| | Psychophysical Scaling | Psychological Scaling |
|-|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Focus** | Relationship between physical stimulus and psychological sensation | Scaling of psychological/social stimuli with no clear physical metric |
| **Examples** | Absolute threshold, JND, loudness scales | Attitude scales, preference scales, value scales |
| **Methods** | Method of limits, constant stimuli, average error | Thurstone, Likert, Guttman, semantic differential |

### 5.2 Classical Psychophysical Methods

**Absolute Threshold (RL -- Reiz Limen):** minimum stimulus intensity detectable 50% of the time.

**Difference Threshold / JND (Just Noticeable Difference / DL -- Differenz Limen):** minimum change in stimulus detectable 50% of the time.

#### Method of Limits (Method of Minimal Change)
- Stimuli presented in **ascending and descending series** alternately.
- Ascending: start below threshold, increase until subject detects; note transition point.
- Descending: start above threshold, decrease until subject no longer detects; note transition point.
- **Threshold = average of transition points** across series.
- **Errors:** ascending series: **habituation** (delayed report); descending series: **anticipation** (early report). Alternating series balances these.

#### Method of Constant Stimuli (Constant Method)
- Fixed set of stimuli (usually 5-9) presented **many times in random order.**
- For each stimulus, record proportion of times detected (or judged greater than standard).
- Plot **psychometric function** (S-shaped/ogive); threshold = 50% point (or 75% for two-alternative forced choice).
- Most **accurate** but most time-consuming.
- For DL: comparison stimuli presented with standard; percent "heavier/louder" plotted; DL = (75th percentile - 25th percentile) / 2.

#### Method of Average Error (Method of Adjustment / Reproduction)
- Subject **adjusts** a variable stimulus until it **matches** the standard.
- Many trials; average error = mean deviation of adjustments from standard.
- Quick; active involvement of subject; but subject may develop preferred adjustment patterns.

| Method | Subject's Task | Best For | Main Error |
|--------|---------------|----------|-----------|
| **Limits** | Report detection at each step | RL and DL | Habituation, anticipation |
| **Constant Stimuli** | Detect/compare randomly presented stimuli | Most precise threshold | Time-consuming |
| **Average Error** | Adjust variable to match standard | PSE, constant error | Motor habits, time error |

### 5.3 Weber's Law and Fechner's Law

**Weber's Law (Ernst Weber, 1834):**
- The **JND is a constant fraction (K) of the standard stimulus (I).**
- **DL / I = K (Weber fraction)** -- constant for a given sensory modality.
- Examples: **weight ~ 1/50; brightness ~ 1/60; pitch ~ 1/333.**
- Holds well in middle ranges; breaks down at very high or very low intensities.

**Fechner's Law (Gustav Fechner, 1860):** derived from Weber's Law by integration.
- **S = K log I** (sensation magnitude = constant x log of stimulus intensity).
- "The magnitude of sensation increases as the **log** of the stimulus."
- Rationale: since DL is constant fraction of I, each successive JND requires proportionally larger stimulus increment; summing JNDs gives log function.
- Limitation: assumes all JNDs are subjectively equal (questionable).

**Stevens' Power Law (S.S. Stevens, 1957):**
- **S = kI^n** (sensation = constant x stimulus raised to exponent n).
- n < 1: brightness (0.33), loudness (0.67) -- compressive; n = 1: line length; n > 1: electric shock (3.5) -- expansive.
- Based on **magnitude estimation** (direct scaling); overcomes Fechner's assumption.
- **Prothetic continua** (intensity dimensions): power law with exponent < 1 usually. **Metathetic continua** (quality/pitch): better described by log.

### 5.4 Psychophysical Signal Detection Theory (SDT)

- Classical psychophysics assumed a fixed threshold; SDT replaces threshold with **d' (d-prime):** measure of sensitivity independent of response bias (beta).
- **Hit, Miss, False Alarm, Correct Rejection** -- ROC curve.
- Eliminates criterion problem; separates sensitivity from decision criterion.

### 5.5 Psychological Scaling Methods

**Scaling** assigns numbers to psychological objects/stimuli according to rules reflecting their properties.

**Category Scaling:** subject assigns each stimulus to one of n ordered categories. Simple; subjective.

**Rank-Order Scaling:** subject ranks stimuli; ordinal scale.

**Pair Comparison:** each stimulus compared to every other; derives ratio/interval-level scale.

**Magnitude Estimation (Stevens):** subject assigns numbers proportional to perceived magnitude; yields ratio scale.

### 5.6 Attitude Scales (Methods of Opinionnaires)

**Attitude:** a learned predisposition to respond in a consistently favorable or unfavorable manner toward an object, person, or idea. Three components: **cognitive, affective, behavioral (CAB/ABC model).**

| Scale | Developer | Method | Level | Key Features |
|-------|-----------|--------|-------|-------------|
| **Thurstone Equal-Appearing Intervals** | L.L. Thurstone, 1928 | Judges sort 100+ statements into 11 piles (most unfavorable to most favorable); median = scale value; items with small Q (IQR) selected; respondents endorse items they agree with; score = median scale value of endorsed items | Interval (approximately) | Most rigorous; judges needed; laborious; 20-22 final items |
| **Likert Summated Rating Scale** | R. Likert, 1932 | Items rated on 5-point scale (Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree); item analysis to select discriminating items; sum all item scores | Ordinal (often treated as interval) | Most widely used; simpler to construct; higher reliability than Thurstone; no judges needed |
| **Guttman Scalogram Analysis** | L. Guttman, 1944 | Items arranged in order of difficulty; perfect scale = endorsing item N means endorsing all items below N; cumulative/unidimensional | Ordinal | Tests unidimensionality; Coefficient of Reproducibility (CR) > 0.90 required; rare in practice |
| **Semantic Differential** | C.E. Osgood, Suci & Tannenbaum, 1957 | Concept rated on 7-point bipolar adjective scales (e.g., good-bad, strong-weak, active-passive); three factors: **Evaluation (E), Potency (P), Activity (A) -- EPA** | Interval (approximately) | Measures connotative meaning; versatile; not limited to attitudes; EPA structure replicates across cultures |
| **Bogardus Social Distance Scale** | E. Bogardus, 1925 | Cumulative; measures willingness to admit outgroup members to varying degrees of closeness (to country, neighborhood, marriage); Guttman-type | Ordinal | Specifically for social/ethnic attitudes; hierarchical |

> **Key Point:** Thurstone = equal-appearing intervals, uses judges, interval level. Likert = summated ratings, no judges, widely used. Guttman = scalogram, tests unidimensionality, CR > 0.90. Semantic Differential = EPA factors (Osgood). These four + Bogardus are Singh's attitude scale methods.

**Likert vs. Thurstone:**
- Thurstone: interval scale; requires judges (time-consuming); sensitive to judge bias.
- Likert: ordinal but higher reliability; no judges; simpler; scores range across all items (not just endorsed).
- Both yield similar rank orderings in practice despite theoretical scale-level differences.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Singh's four strategies** for constructing personality inventories: Content/Logical, Empirical Criterion-Keying (MMPI), Factor-Analytic (16PF/EPQ), Theoretical. Know which test exemplifies which strategy.

2. **MMPI:** 550 true/false items; Hathaway & McKinley, 1943; 10 clinical scales (Hs, D, Hy, Pd, Mf, Pa, Pt, Sc, Ma, Si) + 4 validity scales (?, L, F, K); empirical criterion-keying strategy. MMPI-2 = 567 items, 1989.

3. **Rorschach:** 10 cards; I,IV,V,VI,VII = achromatic; II,III = achromatic + red; VIII,IX,X = fully chromatic. Five scoring categories: **Location (W/D/Dd/S), Determinants (F/M/C/T/V/Y), Content (H/A/An etc.), Popularity-Originality, Form Level**. Exner's Comprehensive System is the standard.

4. **Frank's 5 types of projective techniques:** Constitutive (Rorschach), Constructive (World Test), Interpretive (TAT), Cathartic (play therapy), Refractive (graphology). Lindzey's types: Association, Construction, Completion, Choice/Ordering, Expressive.

5. **TAT:** Murray & Morgan (1935); 31 cards, 20 administered; based on need-press theory; alpha press vs. beta press; blank card 16 = maximally projective.

6. **Holtzman:** 45 cards per form, 1 response per card, 22 scored variables, parallel forms (A and B). Key improvement over Rorschach = allows standard reliability computation.

7. **Deviation IQ vs. Ratio IQ:** Ratio IQ = (MA/CA) x 100 (Stern); Deviation IQ = M=100, SD=15 (Wechsler). Deviation IQ solves ceiling problem for adults.

8. **Rating errors:** Halo (Thorndike, 1920), Leniency, Severity, Central tendency, Logical error, Contrast, Recency, Primacy. Most important = halo effect.

9. **Questionnaire vs. Schedule:** Schedule = interviewer fills in (higher data quality, lower response rate distortion risk); Questionnaire = self-completed (cheaper, lower response rate).

10. **Three classical psychophysical methods:** Limits (ascending/descending; errors: habituation/anticipation), Constant Stimuli (most accurate; random presentation; psychometric function), Average Error (subject adjusts; also called method of adjustment).

11. **Weber's Law:** DL/I = K. **Fechner's Law:** S = K log I. **Stevens' Power Law:** S = kI^n (n < 1 for most senses; n > 1 for pain/electric shock).

12. **Attitude scales comparison:** Thurstone (interval, judges, 1928), Likert (summated, no judges, 1932, most used), Guttman (cumulative, CR > 0.90, 1944), Semantic Differential (EPA, Osgood 1957), Bogardus (social distance, cumulative).

13. **Guilford's SOI:** Operations (5) x Contents (4) x Products (6) = 120 factors. Creativity = **divergent production**. TTCT (Torrance): fluency, flexibility, originality, elaboration.

14. **16PF:** Cattell; 16 source traits from factor analysis; second-order factors = Extraversion and Anxiety. Eysenck's 3 super-factors = E, N, P (biological basis: E = cortical arousal; N = autonomic reactivity).

15. **Content analysis (Berelson):** "objective, systematic, quantitative description of manifest content of communication." Used with TAT stories for n-Ach (McClelland).
