# Techniques of Observation and Data Collection

Chapter 12 surveys the major primary-data-gathering tools used in behavioural research: questionnaires, schedules, interviews, content analysis, direct observation, and rating scales. It examines the construction, administration, advantages, limitations, and error sources of each method, and introduces specialized rating techniques (numerical, graphic, forced-choice, standard, cumulated-point scales) along with the rating errors that compromise their validity.

---

## Methods of Data Collection

**Primary data** are data collected originally, for the first time, directly from the source by the researcher. Examples: assessing job satisfaction of employees, measuring student attitudes toward teachers.

**Secondary data** are data collected in a second-hand manner from pre-existing sources (census records, hospital files, books, journals, newspapers, periodicals). Example: using census data to study the age-sex structure of a population.

### Broad Classification of Data Collection Methods

| Dimension | Types |
|---|---|
| By source | Primary data / Secondary data |
| By technique (primary) | Interview (structured / unstructured), Questionnaire (mailed / face-to-face), Observation (participant / nonparticipant) |

---

## Questionnaire and Schedule

A **questionnaire** is a form containing a series of questions that **respondents themselves fill in**. It measures attitudes or beliefs and is therefore also called an **attitude scale** or **opinionnaire**.

A **schedule** is a form containing a series of questions that are **asked and filled in by the investigator** in a face-to-face situation.

An **interview guide** consists of a set of basic interview topics to be covered by the interviewer during the interview (not a verbatim question list).

Key distinction:
- Questionnaire: used when factual information is desired; self-administered.
- Schedule: investigator-administered, face-to-face.
- Opinionnaire/attitude scale: used when opinions rather than facts are desired.

Group administration of questionnaires is efficient and economical when several persons are available simultaneously; it also helps researchers establish rapport and obtain richer attitudinal data.

---

### Wording of Questions in a Questionnaire

Eight critical factors identified in the literature:

1. **Simplicity in language**: Questions must match the vocabulary of the target population. Technical jargon should be avoided for general-population samples. Marquis (1960) showed experimentally that **longer questions had higher validity** than shorter ones (answers better agreed with physicians' reports in a health questionnaire), contradicting the instinct to keep questions brief.

2. **Ambiguity**: Questions must convey the same meaning to all respondents. Double-barrelled questions (asking about two things at once) introduce ambiguity and must be split into separate questions. The **split-ballot technique** is used when no single wording can be accepted universally: different equivalent groups receive different wordings of the same question and responses are compared.

3. **Vague words**: Words like "often," "generally," "fairly," "on the whole" should be avoided because they invite vague answers. "Why" questions also introduce vagueness (Lazarsfeld, 1935) because the answer depends on a host of factors not controlled by the question.

4. **Embarrassing questions**: Questions about private matters (sexual behaviour, cheating, stealing) threaten respondents and produce refusals or distorted answers. Remedies: (a) ask respondents to express views about others rather than themselves; (b) use sentence-completion techniques. Oppenheim (1966) used sentence completion to study psychiatric nurses' attitudes toward a mental hospital and found it effective in reducing threat.

5. **Double negatives**: Double negatives cancel each other and create confusion. Example: "Do you not approve the idea that college girls should not engage in domestic affairs?" must be avoided.

6. **Leading questions**: A **leading question** is one whose content or structure pushes the respondent toward a specific answer. "Should something not be done about...?" typically elicits a positive response; "You don't think...do you?" elicits a negative response. Payne (1951) and Cantril (1944) documented numerous subtle leading words (e.g., "involved" in "Do you think the party should get involved in...?" leads to a negative answer).

7. **Presuming questions**: Questions that assume the respondent has knowledge or has participated in activities they may not have. Must be preceded by a filter question (e.g., "Have you ever used nutri-nuggets? If yes, do you find them tasty?").

8. **Hypothetical questions**: Questions phrased as "Would you like to...?" or "What would you do if...?" reflect little concrete reality and should be avoided.

---

### Characteristics of a Good Questionnaire

1. Concerned with specific topics perceived as relevant by respondents; objectives and significance clearly stated.
2. As short as possible; very lengthy questionnaires are often discarded.
3. Directions and wording simple and clear; each question addresses a single idea.
4. Questions objective; no hints or suggestions about the desired answer.
5. Embarrassing, presuming, and hypothetical questions avoided.
6. Questions in logical order, proceeding from general to specific (or favourable to unfavourable).
7. Attractive in appearance: neatly printed/duplicated and clearly arranged.

---

### Order of Questions

Order affects both the **validity of answers** and the **refusal rates** (Cantril 1944; Whitfield 1950).

- Opening questions: simple, general, rapport-building; no sensitive or embarrassing items at the start.
- Questions move logically from general to specific.
- Sensitive questions that cannot be omitted should be placed near the end (so that if refusal occurs, only a few questions remain unanswered).

**Funnel sequence** (Kahn & Cannell, 1975): Start with broad questions, gradually narrow to specific questions. This has become the **standard norm** for questionnaires in behavioural research.

---

### Functions of a Questionnaire

1. **Description**: Provides individual or group characteristics (age, sex, marital status, occupation, income, political/religious affiliation, group membership). These descriptive data enable the researcher to draw inferences about group behaviour (e.g., clique formation, inter-group rivalry).

2. **Measurement**: Measures individual and group variables such as attitude, opinion, and personality traits through multiple items designed to assess these constructs.

---

### Types of Questionnaires

#### Based on Type of Response Required

**1. Fixed-response questionnaire** (also called **closed-form questionnaire** or **pre-coded questionnaire**): Contains statements or questions with a fixed set of options; respondent checks the most applicable option.

Assumptions:
- The target sample has adequate knowledge of the subject matter.
- The researcher can anticipate the likely response categories.

Advantages:
- Easily scored and coded; facilitates statistical analysis.
- Requires no writing; advantageous for respondents who struggle with verbal expression.
- Less time-consuming; lengthy questionnaires can be completed rapidly.
- Higher return rate when mailed.

Disadvantages:
- Researcher may fail to provide all relevant response alternatives; if the respondent is forced to choose a non-fitting option, data will be misleading.
- Encourages **response set or bias** (e.g., respondent checks only the first option to complete quickly).

**2. Open-end questionnaire**: Questions require short or lengthy written answers; respondents express themselves in their own words.

Champion (1969) constructed a questionnaire combining both fixed and open-ended items, administered to seniors at a small college.

Advantages:
- Useful when the investigator has little information about subjects.
- Provides flexibility; elicits unanticipated and insightful replies.

Disadvantages:
- **Educational bias**: Subjects adept at self-expression are unfairly compared with poor expressors.
- **Socio-economic bias**: Respondents of different socio-economic backgrounds use different vocabularies.
- More time-consuming than fixed-response.
- Lower mail return rate than fixed-response.
- Difficult to score or code objectively; apparent similarities in responses may mask different meanings.

---

#### Based on Method of Administration

**1. Mail questionnaire**: Mailed to designated subjects with instructions and a return envelope. Researcher typically waits a fortnight or more. Survey evidence: approximately **70% of mailed questionnaires are not returned**.

**2. Face-to-face administered questionnaire**: Selected subjects complete the questionnaire in the presence of the investigator or associates. More common than the mail questionnaire.

Advantages of face-to-face administration:
- Less time-consuming; high completion and return rate.
- Investigator knows who is completing the questionnaire (increases validity).
- Researcher present to clarify queries.
- Allows probe questions for detailed analysis.

Disadvantages of face-to-face administration:
- Respondents may become overly conscious in the investigator's presence, adversely affecting validity.
- Travel for personal contact is costly and time-consuming.
- Sample chosen by accessibility and convenience; no longer representative (reduces external validity).

## Interview

An **interview** is a face-to-face situation between the **interviewer** (researcher) and the **respondent** (interviewee) intended to elicit desired information. It is a social process involving at least two persons.

Three important conditions for interview success: **accessibility**, **cognition**, and **motivation** (discussed in Chapter 15 of the book).

---

### Types of Interviews

**1. Formal interview** (also: structured, patterned, standardized, inflexible, mass, controlled, extensive interview): Pre-prepared questions asked in a set order; answers recorded in a standardized way. Less trained interviewers can conduct it. Reported validities are usually higher than for informal interviews. Limitations: (a) expensive and time-consuming; (b) validities are usually less than those obtained by biodata analysis and standardized psychological tests (Guilford 1966).

**2. Informal interview** (also: unstructured, qualitative, formative, flexible, intensive, uncontrolled interview): No predetermined questions or order; the interviewer decides what to ask around key themes. More commonly used than the formal interview. Allows "digging deeper" into respondents' personality.

Limitations of informal interview:
1. Greater scope for personal influence and bias; "missing" the standard question; less reliable.
2. Requires greater skill (tactfulness, intelligence, social sense, subject-matter knowledge).
3. Data is difficult to quantify and analyze: (a) different questions may be asked of different respondents; (b) language differences between interviewers make responses non-comparable; (c) results not amenable to statistical analysis.

---

### Major Functions of an Interview

1. **Description**: Provides insight into the interactive quality of social life through verbal interaction; helps the researcher understand the complete view of the subject under investigation.

2. **Exploration**: Provides insight into unexplored dimensions of a topic; helps develop new variables and hypotheses for subsequent research; sharpens conceptual clarity.

Of the two, **exploration is considered more important than description**.

---

### Factors Affecting the Uses of Interviews

Three major factors (Gordon 1969; others):

**(1) Characteristics of interviewers**

- **Subjective characteristics**: Inquisitive mind; ability to attune quickly to newly emerging facets of a problem; ability to integrate scattered information into a coherent whole. Especially important in exploratory research.
- **Objective characteristics**: Sex, age, race, manners, clothing, culture, education, social class, speech. These interfere with the verbal dialogue and affect the effectiveness of the interview (Gordon 1969).

**(2) Characteristics of interviewees**

(i) **Capacity to verbalize**: Very young children, mentally retarded or ill individuals, those with limited formal training or isolated social experience lack the capacity to verbalize meaningfully.

(ii) **Willingness to verbalize**: Interviewees must be willing to express their views. Lane (1962) suggested **paying interviewees** and conducting the interview on the interviewer's terms to increase willingness.

**(3) Nature of the problem under study**

Problems touching respondents' private lives (e.g., income, sexual behaviour) or requiring difficult verbalization affect the scientific quality of interviewing (Cressey 1953; Sutherland & Cressey 1936).

---

### Advantages and Disadvantages of Interviews

**Advantages** (Goode 1969):

1. Greater flexibility in questioning; probe questions possible.
2. Rapid and ready acquisition of desired information.
3. Ensures interviewees have interpreted and answered the questions themselves (increases validity).
4. Allows control over the context of questioning and answering.
5. Verbal information can be checked against nonverbal cues.

**Disadvantages**:

1. **Interviewer's variability**: The same interviewer may view and record similar responses differently across interviews; becomes a source of variation that reduces dependability.
2. **Inter-interviewer variability**: When multiple interviewers are used in unstructured interviews, they vary considerably in eliciting information, projecting image, and organizing data. Problem compounds as interviewer numbers increase.
3. **Validity of verbal responses**: Serious doubt exists whether people actually behave as they say they do; verbal responses may lack validity and dependability.
4. **Time**: Interviewing individuals one by one and recording each transaction is time-consuming; even with tapes, transcription adds time.
5. **Variability in context**: Cicourel (1964) showed that the interviewing context does not remain constant across interviews; the context itself becomes a variable that must be accounted for.
6. **Recording problems**: No foolproof standard system for recording exists; approaches range from jotting main points, to verbatim notes, to tape recording. Absence of a standard criterion adversely affects dependability.

---

### Important Sources of Errors in Interviews

1. **Attitude of the interviewer**: Preconceived notions, bias, or favouritism toward respondents leads to incorrect evaluation and recording errors.
2. **Incomprehensibility of questions**: Deliberately difficult or incomprehensible questions produce inaccurate answers; data becomes undependable.
3. **Lack of warmth**: When interviewers exhibit indifferent or cold behaviour, interviewees do not cooperate (Gorden 1969). Warm, affectionate rapport is essential.
4. **Lack of motivation in respondents**: Unmotivated respondents give superficial "Yes" or "No" answers; data no longer represents true viewpoints.
5. **Duration of interview**: Excessively long interviews cause nervousness and monotony in interviewees, making their information undependable.

---

### Selection and Training of Interviewers

**Personal characteristics required**:

1. **Honesty**: Scrupulous conduct; performance assessed across multiple interview boards.
2. **Accuracy**: Accurate recording of answers; in formal interviews, must follow question sequence accurately.
3. **Adaptability**: Capacity to adjust to varying interview contexts (rural/urban/slum areas) without showing prejudice.
4. **Interest**: Maintained across lengthy and repetitive interviewing tasks. Prolonged repetitive assignments tend to erode interest.
5. **Temperament**: Should not be emotionally involved; should avoid over-aggressiveness or over-sociability.

**Training approaches**:
- **Formal training**: Six to eight weeks using lecture or discussion methods.
- **On-the-job training**: Potential interviewers sit with experienced interviewers to observe before taking over.

## Content Analysis

**Content analysis** (also: document analysis) is a method of systematic examination of communications, current records, or documents. Instead of questioning respondents or directly observing behaviour, the content-analyzer examines documents or communications produced by respondents and systematically determines the frequency or proportion of specified categories.

**Primary sources** for content analysis: letters, autobiographies, diaries, compositions, records, reports, printed forms, academic work, books, periodicals, bulletins, catalogues, syllabi, court decisions, pictures, films, cartoons. Researchers must establish the **trustworthiness** of such data. Content analysis can also be applied to responses of projective tests and other verbal materials.

---

### Purpose of Content Analysis

1. To explain and describe prevailing practices or conditions.
2. To identify concepts, beliefs, thinking, and literary styles of a writer.
3. To locate and explain possible causal factors related to some outcome or event.
4. To analyze types of errors in students' work.
5. To locate the level of difficulty of presentation in textbooks.
6. To analyze the use of symbols representing institutions, countries, or points of view.
7. To find the relative importance of topics or problems.
8. To evaluate bias, prejudice, or propaganda in textbook presentation.

---

### Methods of Content Analysis

The most widely applied method is **Berelson's (1954) method**, presented under three heads:

#### Step 1: Specification of the Universe (U)

The first step is to **define the universe** (U) of the content, i.e., the dependent variable under investigation. The U is then divided into categorical subparts. For each subcategory, a corresponding hypothesis is formulated.

Example: Universe = "classroom discipline." Subcategories: (1) punctuality, (2) attentiveness, (3) task completion. Three hypotheses: If teacher is strict, students will (1) come on time, (2) be attentive, (3) do homework.

#### Step 2: Unit of Analysis

Berelson (1954) identified **five major units of analysis**:

| Unit | Description | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Words** | Smallest unit; letters may also be used | Used for word-comprehension studies |
| **Themes** | A sentence or proposition; often combined into sets | Realistic but may yield unreliable results if complex; best for self-disclosure (statements using I, My, Me, Mine) |
| **Items** | A whole production toward a given stimulus (e.g., TAT story, autobiography, TV programme) | Most important unit for behavioural research |
| **Characters** | Individual persons in a production | Used in literary research and story analysis; applicable to projective tests |
| **Space-and-time measures** | Physical measurement of content (pages, photographs, discussions, books) | Not important for behavioural research per Berelson |

#### Step 3: Quantification

**Quantification** refers to assigning numerals to the objects of content analysis. Three methods:

1. **Nominal measurement**: Each object is assigned to a category and counted. Used when the number of objects is large.
2. **Ordinal measurement**: Objects are ranked by subjects according to a fixed criterion (e.g., ranking photographs by degree of emotion expressed).
3. **Rating**: The whole reproduction or object is rated on several dimensions (e.g., an essay rated on originality, fluency, organization, spelling).

---

### Evaluation and Limitation of Content Analysis

**Merits**:

1. Applicable to a wide variety of materials: creativity, attitude, ethnocentrism, stereotypes, curriculum, values, interest, religiosity, budgets.
2. Can examine the effect of experimental manipulation upon dependent variables (e.g., effect of practice on handwriting improvement).
3. Can be used to **validate other measures**: e.g., validating a self-disclosure inventory by content-analyzing responses to projective-type questions.

**Limitations**: Involves complexities; must be used with caution; results may be unreliable when themes are complex.

## Observation as a Tool of Data Collection

### Meaning and Nature

**Observation** as a fundamental data-gathering technique refers to watching and listening to others' behaviour over time **without manipulating or controlling it**, and recording findings in ways that allow analytical interpretation and discussion (Weick 1968 defined observation as selecting, recording, and encoding behaviour for empirical aims of description or theory development).

Scientific observation is distinguished from casual observation by:
1. Natural social context in which behaviour is studied.
2. Capture of significant events affecting relations among persons being studied.
3. Identification of regularities and recurrences by comparing across natural settings.

---

### Purpose of Observation

1. **Snapshot comprehension**: To capture and study human behaviour as it actually happens in real or social life.
2. **Graphic description**: To provide detailed descriptions of life events otherwise unavailable (e.g., how a delinquent operates; how a person learns to be a professional).
3. **Exploration**: To explore overlooked variables, examine alternative research directions, and correct methodological errors.

Of these purposes, **description and exploration are more important**.

---

### Important Types of Observation

#### Classification by Ability to Generate Researchable Information (Reiss 1971)

1. **Systematic observation**: Done according to explicit procedures and in accordance with the logic of scientific inference. Example: A psychologist studying children's aggressive play behaviour with predetermined, objective principles.

2. **Unsystematic observation**: Casual observation without specifying explicit, objective principles. Example: A sociologist observing people on a railway platform without any pre-set procedures.

#### Classification by Role of the Investigator

**A. Participant observation**

In **participant observation**, the investigator **actively participates** in the activities of the group being observed. The investigator may already be a group member or may join specifically to observe. Usually unstructured. The identity of the observer may or may not be known.

- **Disguised participant observation**: Identity of the observer is NOT known to group members; treated as an ordinary member. Classic example: Rosenhan (1973) - disguised participant observers posed as patients in a psychiatric hospital and reported on the experience.
- **Undisguised participant observation**: Persons being observed know the observer is collecting information.

Strengths of participant observation:
1. Natural setting yields realistic, meaningful data and convincing conclusions.
2. Extended observation period (days to months) yields broad and meaningful information.

Limitations of participant observation:
1. Unstructured; less precise; less attention to precision, more to discovery (Reiss 1971).
2. Time-consuming; many observers avoid it.
3. Observer may develop emotional involvement (love, sympathy, hatred) with group members, jeopardizing validity and dependability.

**B. Nonparticipant observation**

In **nonparticipant observation**, the investigator observes behaviour in a natural setting **but does not participate** in the group's activities. Usually structured. The observer's identity is typically known to those being observed.

Strengths of nonparticipant observation:
1. Structured design yields more reliable and representative data.
2. Observer can concentrate on specified aspects of social behaviour and probe specific research questions.

Limitations of nonparticipant observation:
1. Awareness of being observed can distort the natural flow of behaviour (though Black & Champion 1976 found little evidence of detrimental effects from nonparticipant observation on the behaviour studied).
2. Fails to capture natural context of social settings to the extent participant observation does.

---

### Difference Between Participant and Nonparticipant Observation

| Feature | Participant Observation | Nonparticipant Observation |
|---|---|---|
| Active participation in group activities | Yes | No |
| Structure | Usually unstructured | Usually structured |
| Identity of observer | Often hidden (disguised) | Usually known to those observed |
| Flexibility | Greater flexibility in deciding what and how to observe | Less flexibility |
| Reliability | Lower (less precise) | Higher |
| Ecological validity | Higher (natural context) | Somewhat lower |

## Rating Scale

### Observation of Actual vs. Remembered Behaviour

**Observation of actual behaviour**: Persons or objects are physically present and interacting (e.g., teacher-pupil interactions, students solving a problem).

**Observation of remembered behaviour** (also called **perceived behaviour**): Persons or objects are not physically present but are symbolically represented; the rater judges on the basis of previous experience. Rating scales assess both actual and remembered behaviour.

---

### Definition

A **rating scale** is a technique through which the observer or rater **categorizes objects, events, or persons on a continuum**, represented by a series of continuous numerals. Its purpose is to capture the impressions that objects or persons have made on raters.

**Components**:
- **Stimulus variable (S)**: Trait names or qualities to be rated.
- **Response options**: Numerical or descriptive categories.

**Ratings** may be:
- **Retrospective**: Summarize impressions gathered over an extended period.
- **Concurrent**: Summarize impressions gathered during an ongoing event (e.g., an interview).

Typical scale: 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, or 11 points on a line with descriptive categories at both ends (and sometimes the middle). Beyond 7 intervals, fine discrimination becomes unreliable even for trained raters; intervals should be kept below 7.

Three precautions in constructing rating scales:
1. Each trait should be clearly defined with specific instances.
2. Each interval or scale point should be clearly defined.
3. Rating scales should focus on **overt, objectively observable traits** (leadership, honesty, punctuality, co-operativeness, industriousness) rather than covert traits (ego-strength, job satisfaction, emotional stability), because overt traits are rated more reliably.

---

### Types of Rating Scales

According to **Guilford (1954)**, rating scales are divided into **six categories**:

1. Numerical scales
2. Graphic scales
3. Percentage rating
4. Standard scales
5. Scales of estimated (cumulated) points
6. Forced-choice scales

---

### Numerical Rating Scale

The easiest to construct and apply. The rater is given a sequence of numbers and rates objects on that sequence. Three subtypes:

**Subtype 1: Scales with numerical anchors** (most common): Numbers (e.g., 1-9) paired with meaning labels printed on the first page; subsequent pages contain statements with blank spaces for the rater to write the appropriate number.

| Number | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Extremely disagree |
| 2 | Strongly disagree |
| 3 | Moderately disagree |
| 4 | Mildly disagree |
| 5 | Indifferent |
| 6 | Mildly agree |
| 7 | Moderately agree |
| 8 | Strongly agree |
| 9 | Extremely agree |

**Subtype 2: Scales without numerical anchors**: All descriptive cues printed beside each statement; rater places a tick mark on the most appropriate descriptor (e.g., Strongly agree / Agree / Indifferent / Disagree / Strongly disagree).

**Subtype 3: Bipolar numerical scales**: Neutral point = 0, positive agreement numbered +1, +2, +3; negative disagreement numbered -1, -2, -3.

Example:
```
+3  Strongly agree
+2  Mildly agree
+1  Agree
 0  Indifferent
-1  Disagree
-2  Mildly disagree
-3  Strongly disagree
```

General demerit of numerical scales: Raters with no statistical background may be confused by abstract numbers; negative numbers and zero points may disrupt perceived continuity. Guilford (1954) cautioned that raters must be informed of the meaning of the numbers; modern scales print anchors with meanings on each page.

---

### Graphic Rating Scale

One of the most popular and widely used rating scales. An improvement over numerical scales because qualities are presented **graphically** with descriptive cues or anchors along a continuous line; the rater puts a tick mark or cross on the chosen descriptor.

Example:
> Mrs Shukla delivers lectures:
> Extremely rapidly | Tolerably rapidly | With slow speed | Sluggishly | Extremely slowly

Scale points may be connected horizontally (continuous line) or arranged vertically (Balanced Rating Scale). A bias exists for raters to cluster toward certain scale points when horizontal; this is overcome by many divisions or the **Felt Bar Technique**.

Advantages: Simple, easily administered, quickly completed.

Limitations:
1. No numerical anchors; rater may experience discontinuity and confusion.
2. Scoring is more time-consuming than for numerical scales.

---

### Percentage Rating

Used for quick rating with maximum uniformity across raters. The rater places the subject at a percentile in the performance group:

- Highest 5%
- Second highest 5%
- Highest 25% (excluding top 10%)
- Top half (excluding top 25%)
- Lower half

Common among teachers rating students' overall classroom performance.

Limitation: Rater must be generous; rating may be influenced by the individual's reputation or personality among raters.

---

### Standard Scales

Not very popular; developed to achieve more uniformity of meaning at scale points and an unbiased rating through comparison with **pre-established standards**.

**Two types**:

#### Man-to-Man Scale

Developed during **World War II**. Uses actual persons (men) as standards instead of abstract descriptions. The rater selects five men known to him who represent very high, high, average, low, and very low levels of the trait being rated. These five key persons define the five scale points. Other persons are then rated by comparing them to the five key persons.

Hypothetical example:

| Scale Value | Name |
|---|---|
| Very high | Lt. China |
| High | Col. Shamsher |
| Average | Brig. Kariappa |
| Low | Lt. Bodha |
| Very low | Lt. Gilani |

Advantages:
1. Avoids confusion of abstract numerical anchors.
2. If all raters use the same key-man, ratings are comparable in both absolute and relative terms.
3. Since scale values of key-men are pre-established and fixed, raters cannot shift ratings from day to day.

Disadvantages:
1. Distance between key-men is not equal (intervals are unequal).
2. In practice no two raters rate known persons alike, undermining comparability.
3. Does not control deliberate over- or underestimation by raters.

#### Portrait Matching

Developed by **Hartshorne & May (1929)** in their study on character. A set of verbal sketches (portraits) for a given trait is prepared from a large pool of statements collected and rank-ordered by judges; portraits with about the same average rank-order are synthesized. Scale values are determined by a second group of judges (mean rank = scale value). The rater is given all portraits and names the person(s) who belong to each.

A person's final rating = the average of all portrait values assigned by all raters.

Merit: Eliminates subjectivity common in the Man-to-Man Scale.

Demerit: Does not provide objective, realistic standard scale points. The method reverses the usual rating practice: instead of finding a description to match a person, the rater finds a person to match the description, which requires raters to have all names before them rather than relying on recall.

---

### Scale of Cumulated Points

The most common form of rating scale. The person's **total score = sum of individual ratings or points** assigned to all items. Points may be weighted or unweighted. Distinguished from psychological tests in that the items are answered by the **rater (another person)**, not the person being assessed.

Two subtypes:

#### Checklist (Behaviour Checklist)

Rater is supplied with a large number of specific behavioural statements and checks those that describe the person being rated. Each checked statement is scored:
- Favourable statement: +1
- Unfavourable statement: -1
- Neutral item: 0

Total score = sum of all checked statement scores.

Purpose: To determine whether specified types of behaviour are present or absent.

Classic example: **Vineland Social Maturity Scale** (items: self-direction, self-help, communication, socialization); filled in by a rater who knows the child.

Two varieties:
- **Two-point response checklist**: Yes/No or True/False items.
- **Multiple-choice response checklist** (also: **adjective checklist**): Options are adjectives only (e.g., smart, intelligent, beautiful, ugly, submissive).

Scoring: +1 for each positive rating, -1 for each negative rating; total = positiveness of rater's attitude.

Limitation: Rating influenced by response set biases; all-or-none (binary) checking is susceptible to bias (Guilford 1954).

#### Guess-Who Technique (Casting Characters)

Developed by **Hartshorne & May (1929)** for use primarily with children. Consists of verbal descriptions (one or two sentences) of roles played by children in a group. Child raters name the peers who match each description, mentioning the same child multiple times if appropriate. Each favourable description matched to a child earns one point; all points are summed.

Sample items:
- "Here is one who is always worried."
- "Here is one who is always happy."
- "Here is one who is always discouraging others."
- "Here is one who is always helping others."

Limitation: Raters must be supplied with names of all individuals to be rated; they should not depend on memory. This makes the technique operationally cumbersome. Like portrait matching, the method deviates from the normal practice by requiring the rater to find a person to match the description rather than a description to match a person.

---

### Forced-Choice Rating Scale

A **major departure** from other rating scales. Rather than rating one attribute at a time, the rater is given a **set of attributes (verbal statements) for a single item** and decides which one(s) most accurately represent the individual being rated.

Items may have two, three, four, or five alternatives. **Two-alternative and four-alternative** forms are most common.

- **Two-alternative form**: Both statements are either favourable or unfavourable, but only one is valid or true. The rater must choose, reducing leniency bias.
- **Four-alternative form** (two varieties):
  - All four statements desirable or all undesirable.
  - Two favourable and two unfavourable statements.

### Errors in Ratings (Chapter Preview)

The following rating errors are listed in the chapter preview (not covered in the supplied OCR text):

1. **Halo effect**: Rating on one trait influences ratings on all other traits.
2. **Error of severity**: Rater consistently rates everyone too low/harshly.
3. **Error of leniency**: Rater consistently rates everyone too high/generously.
4. **Error of central tendency**: Rater avoids extremes; rates everyone near the midpoint.
5. **Contrast error**: Rater rates others in contrast to themselves.
6. **Proximity error**: Adjacent traits on a scale rated more similarly than non-adjacent traits.
7. **Logical error**: Rater assumes certain traits go together and rates them similarly.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Primary data** | Data collected originally and for the first time from the source by the researcher |
| **Secondary data** | Data collected second-hand from pre-existing sources (census records, hospital files, literature) |
| **Questionnaire** | A self-administered form of questions measuring attitudes or beliefs; also called attitude scale or opinionnaire |
| **Schedule** | An investigator-administered form of questions filled in by the researcher in a face-to-face situation |
| **Interview guide** | A set of basic interview topics to be covered by the interviewer; not a verbatim question list |
| **Split-ballot technique** | Using different wordings of the same question for different equivalent respondent groups to assess the effect of wording |
| **Leading question** | A question that, by content or structure, leads the respondent toward a specific answer |
| **Presuming question** | A question that assumes the respondent has knowledge or experience they may not have |
| **Hypothetical question** | A question phrased as "Would you like to...?" that reflects no concrete reality |
| **Funnel sequence** | Recommended question order (Kahn & Cannell 1975): start broad, narrow to specific; standard norm in behavioural research |
| **Fixed-response questionnaire** | Questionnaire with preset response options; also called closed-form or pre-coded questionnaire |
| **Open-end questionnaire** | Questionnaire requiring short or lengthy written answers from respondents |
| **Formal interview** | Structured, patterned interview with predetermined questions asked in a set order |
| **Informal interview** | Unstructured interview with no predetermined questions; interviewer decides what to ask around key themes |
| **Content analysis** | Systematic examination of communications/documents to determine frequency or proportion of specified categories |
| **Universe (U)** | The content domain or dependent variable defined at the outset of content analysis (Berelson 1954) |
| **Unit of analysis** | The unit in which content analysis is carried out (words, themes, items, characters, space-and-time measures) |
| **Quantification** | Assigning numerals to objects of content analysis via nominal measurement, ordinal measurement, or rating |
| **Observation** | Watching and listening to others' behaviour over time without manipulation; selecting, recording, and encoding behaviour for empirical purposes |
| **Systematic observation** | Observation done according to explicit procedures and scientific inference logic |
| **Unsystematic observation** | Casual observation without explicit principles or procedures |
| **Participant observation** | Observation in which the investigator actively participates in the group's activities; usually unstructured |
| **Disguised participant observation** | Participant observation in which the observer's identity is concealed from group members |
| **Undisguised participant observation** | Participant observation in which group members know the observer is collecting information |
| **Nonparticipant observation** | Observation in which the investigator observes without participating; usually structured |
| **Rating scale** | A technique for categorizing objects, events, or persons on a continuum represented by continuous numerals |
| **Stimulus variable (S)** | The trait name or quality to be rated in a rating scale |
| **Retrospective rating** | Ratings that summarize impressions accumulated over an extended period |
| **Concurrent rating** | Ratings that summarize impressions gathered in real time (e.g., during an interview) |
| **Numerical rating scale** | Rating scale using a sequence of numbers with or without descriptive anchors |
| **Graphic rating scale** | Rating scale presenting scale points graphically along a continuous or broken line with descriptive anchors |
| **Percentage rating** | Rating technique placing subjects at percentile positions within a performance group |
| **Standard scales** | Rating scales comparing persons to pre-established standards (Man-to-Man Scale; Portrait Matching) |
| **Man-to-Man Scale** | Standard scale developed in World War II using known persons as anchor points instead of abstract descriptions |
| **Portrait matching** | Standard scale using verbal sketches of trait levels as anchors; rater assigns persons to portraits |
| **Behaviour checklist** | Cumulated-point scale where rater checks statements describing the person; scored +1/-1/0 per item |
| **Adjective checklist** | Behaviour checklist where response options consist solely of adjectives |
| **Guess-who technique** | Cumulated-point rating technique (Hartshorne & May 1929) where raters name peers matching verbal descriptions |
| **Forced-choice rating scale** | Scale presenting sets of attributes per item; rater selects the most accurate descriptor(s) |
| **Halo effect** | Rating error: a positive (or negative) impression on one trait influences ratings on all other traits |
| **Error of central tendency** | Rating error: rater avoids extremes and rates everyone near the midpoint |
| **Error of leniency** | Rating error: rater consistently gives ratings that are too high/generous |
| **Error of severity** | Rating error: rater consistently gives ratings that are too low/harsh |
| **Contrast error** | Rating error: rater rates others in contrast to their own self-perception |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Questionnaire vs. Schedule**: Questionnaire = self-administered; Schedule = investigator-administered face-to-face. Both differ from the interview guide (set of topics).

2. **Split-ballot technique**: Used when no single wording of a question is universally acceptable; different equivalent groups get different wordings.

3. **Marquis (1960) finding**: Longer questions have higher validity than shorter ones (counterintuitive; health questionnaire study).

4. **Funnel sequence** (Kahn & Cannell 1975): Questions ordered broad-to-specific; the current standard norm for behavioural research questionnaires.

5. **Mail questionnaire return rate**: Approximately **70% of mailed questionnaires are NOT returned**.

6. **Formal vs. Informal interview**: Formal = structured, standardized, patterned, higher reliability; Informal = unstructured, flexible, allows deeper probing, higher bias, less reliable. Formal interview validities are still typically below those of biodata analysis and standardized tests (Guilford 1966).

7. **Major functions of an interview**: Description and Exploration. **Exploration is considered more important**.

8. **Content analysis (Berelson 1954)**: Three steps: (1) Specify the Universe, (2) Select Unit of Analysis, (3) Quantification. Five units: words, themes, items, characters, space-and-time measures. **Words are the smallest unit; Items are the most important for behavioural research**.

9. **Participant vs. Nonparticipant observation**: Participant = unstructured, identity often concealed, active involvement, higher ecological validity, lower reliability; Nonparticipant = structured, identity known, no involvement, higher reliability.

10. **Rosenhan (1973)**: Classic disguised participant observation; researchers posed as psychiatric patients.

11. **Guilford's (1954) six types of rating scales**: Numerical, graphic, percentage, standard, cumulated-point (includes checklist and guess-who), forced-choice.

12. **Man-to-Man Scale**: Developed in World War II; uses known individuals as anchor points; key limitation is unequal intervals between anchor persons.

13. **Portrait Matching and Guess-who technique**: Both developed by **Hartshorne & May (1929)**. Portrait matching: verbal sketches used as anchors; rater assigns persons to portraits. Guess-who: child raters name peers matching verbal descriptions.

14. **Vineland Social Maturity Scale**: Best known example of a behaviour checklist; covers self-direction, self-help, communication, socialization.

15. **Rating scale interval rule**: More than **seven intervals** leads to unreliable fine discriminations even for trained raters; intervals should be kept below 7. Overt traits (honesty, punctuality) are rated more reliably than covert traits (ego-strength, emotional stability).
