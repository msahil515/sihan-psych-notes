# Writing a Research Report and a Research Proposal

Chapter 24 covers the conventions, structure, and style for writing a formal research report following APA Publication Manual guidelines, and the nine-step format for preparing a research proposal. Both topics are high-yield for MCQ exams because they test precise recall of format components, word limits, typing rules, and definitional distinctions (e.g., references vs. bibliography, limitations vs. delimitations).

---

## General Purpose of Writing a Research Report

The purpose of a research report is **not to convince** readers but to inform them of:
- What was done
- Why it was done
- What results were obtained
- What the results mean

Key stylistic features:
- Writing must be **clear and unambiguous**
- **Personal pronouns** (I, we, my) should be avoided to ensure **objectivity**
- The **passive voice** is normally used; terms like "investigator" or "researcher" replace first-person references
- Report must be free of grammatical and spelling errors
- Research requires imagination, creativity, and inventiveness; scientists communicate in a **digital (unambiguous) style**

The **American Psychological Association (APA) Publication Manual** is the primary style authority in psychology and the social sciences.

## Structure or Format of a Research Report

### Major Style Manuals

| Manual | Edition | Year | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Publication Manual of the American Psychological Association** | 5th ed. | 2001/2002 | Most widely used in psychology |
| Publication Manual of APA | 6th ed. | 2010 | Bhatt & Ghosh |
| **Handbook for Writers of Research Papers, Reports, Theses** | 11th ed. | 2010 | For papers other than psychology |
| **A Manual for Writers of Term Papers, Theses, and Dissertations** | 5th ed. | 2003 | Turabian |

The APA Publication Manual (5th ed., 2001/2002) divides the research report into **nine parts**.

### The Nine Parts of a Research Report (APA Format)

```
I.   Title Page
      A. Title
      B. Author's Name and Affiliation
      C. Running Head

II.  Abstract  (no heading used)

III. Introduction  (no heading used)
      A. Statement of the Problem
      B. Background / Review of Literature
      C. Purpose, Rationale, and Hypotheses

IV.  Method
      A. Subjects
      B. Apparatus or Instrumentation (if necessary)
      C. Design (if complex)
      D. Procedure

V.   Results
      A. Tables and Figures
      B. Statistical Presentations

VI.  Discussion
      A. Support or Non-support of Hypotheses
      B. Practical and Theoretical Implications
      C. Conclusions

VII. References

VIII. Appendix (if appropriate)

IX.  Author Note
      A. Each author's departmental affiliation
      B. Sources of financial support
      C. Acknowledgements / contributions
      D. Contact information
```

## Part I: Title Page

### Title
- Should be **concise** and clearly indicate the **purpose of the study**
- **Adequate length: 12 to 15 words** approximately
- Avoid introductory phrases such as "A Study of..." or "Investigation of..."
  - Example: "A Study of Unlearning, Spontaneous Recovery, and Learning" should become **"Unlearning, Spontaneous Recovery, and Learning"**
- Words typed in upper and lower case; if two lines are needed, **double-spaced**

### Running Head
- An **abbreviated or short title** printed at the top of the page of a published article to identify it
- **Maximum length: 50 characters** (including letters, punctuation, and spaces between words)
- Appears at the **bottom of the title page**

### Author's Name and Affiliation
- Author's name is **centred below the title**
- Institution name and departmental affiliation are listed below the author's name
- If more than one author from the same institution: affiliation listed **last and only once**

---

## Part II: Abstract

- Written on a **separate sheet of paper (page 2)**
- Length: **100 to 150 words**
- The **abstract** is a summary of the study covering:
  - Problem under study
  - Method (subjects, research design, apparatus)
  - Results (including **statistical significance levels**)
  - Conclusions and implications
- **References must NOT be cited in the abstract**

---

## Part III: Introduction

- Starts on a **fresh page (page 3)**; this is where the text of the report begins
- The page is **NOT labelled "Introduction"** -- the running head and complete title appear at the top instead
- All text sections (Introduction, Method, Results, Discussion) follow **without a break** and do not each start on a new page

### Three Components of a Good Introduction

1. **Statement of the Problem**: Clear and definitive; develops a logic in light of pertinent studies; indicates the need and importance of the research in terms of theory and/or practice
2. **Review of Previous Literature**: Provides an understanding of existing relevant literature; logically connects prior literature with the present study
3. **Hypothesis / Hypotheses**: Clearly formulated; stated so it is clear how each hypothesis would be scientifically tested; variables and terms properly defined

---

## Part IV: Method

Separated from the Introduction by the centred heading **"Method"**. Subsections are labelled at the **left side margin**.

### A. Subjects
- Clearly specify **population characteristics**: age, sex, educational history, SES, race, ethnicity, etc.
- State total number of subjects and number assigned to each condition
- Describe manner of assigning subjects to conditions
- Must conform to **ethical standards of APA**

### B. Apparatus
- Model and model numbers of relevant apparatus mentioned (for replication)
- Provide a diagram or sketch if apparatus is complex or novel
- Minute items (pencil, pen, etc.) need **not** be listed

### C. Design
- Specify the **type of research design**
- Indicate grouping: **between groups** (experimental or control)
- Clearly spell out **Independent Variable (IV)** and **Dependent Variable (DV)**
- State **statistical analyses** to be used (if not mentioned in Introduction)
- Specify the technique of **experimental control**

### D. Procedure
- Describes the **actual steps** carried out in conducting the study
- Includes methods chosen, step-by-step conduct of study, measurement of IV and DV, assignment of subjects to conditions, order of treatments
- Must provide sufficient information to **permit easy replication** by subsequent researchers

---

## Part V: Results

- **Heart of this section**: present **all data** including data that **do not support** hypotheses
- Includes: hypotheses, tables, and figures
- A **figure** = graph, photograph, chart, or other visual presentation of findings (especially useful for learning curves, growth indicators over repeated trials)
- **Data in text and tables/figures should NOT be redundant** -- they are complementary
- Results of statistical analyses stated with the **level of statistical significance**
- Statistical results are presented here but **must NOT be discussed** in the Results section

---

## Part VI: Discussion

- Final section of the main body
- **Interpret and evaluate** the results
- Discuss whether hypotheses are **supported or not supported**
- Discuss **implications** of findings: what major new findings mean
- Advance **new hypotheses** about unexplained results
- **Negative results** must also be discussed (when a hypothesis predicts something but results do not support it); brief speculation about why they occurred, along with alternative hypotheses, is appropriate
- Discuss how findings relate to **future research**
- Researcher includes **conclusions** and addresses whether the original research problem can be considered solved

---

## Part VII: References

- Begins on a **new page** with the label "References" centred
- References arranged **alphabetically** by last name of the author; year follows immediately
- When more than one citation appears within parentheses, references separated by **semicolons**
- If no author is listed: use the name of the book, company, or organization

### References vs. Bibliography

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **References** | Only those works actually **cited in the text** of the report |
| **Bibliography** | Everything on a topic that seemed relevant during research, **whether or not cited** in the text |

**Bibliography is NOT generally included in research reports.**

### APA Reference Formats (from Chapter Examples)

1. **Book, single author**: Siegel, S. (1956). *Nonparametric Statistics for the Behavioural Sciences*. New York: McGraw-Hill.
2. **Book, multiple authors**: McGuigan, F. J. (1960). *Fundamental Statistics in Psychology and Education*. New York: McGraw-Hill.
3. **Edited book**: Mitra, G. (ed.) (2011). *Psychology in India*, vol. 4. Delhi: Pearson.
4. **Corporate/association as author**: American Psychological Association (1983). *Publication Manual* (3rd ed.). Washington, DC.
5. **Journal article**: Edwards, A. L. & Kenny, K. C. (1946). A comparison of Thurstone and Likert techniques of Attitude Scale Construction. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 30, 72-73.
6. **Unpublished doctoral dissertation**: Singh, A. K. (1970). Construction and Standardization of a Verbal Intelligence Scale. Unpublished Doctoral Dissertation, Patna University, Patna.
7. **Chapter in edited book**: Atkinson, R. C. & Shiffrin, R. M. (1968). Human Memory: A proposed system and its control processes. In K. W. Spence & J. T. Spence (Eds.), *The Psychology of Learning and Motivation*, Vol. 2 (pp. 89-195). New York: Academic Press.
8. **Unpublished paper at meeting**: Singh, A. K. & Singh, K. K. (2012, August). Demographic Correlates of Job Stress. Paper presented at the Annual Meeting of Council for Social Sciences, Lucknow.
9. **Unpublished manuscript**: Omer, M. K. (1981). A personal annotation in relation to media among college students. Unpublished Manuscript, Ranchi University, Ranchi.
10. **Manuscript submitted for publication**: Omer, M. K. (2013). A scale to assess Big Five Personality dimensions. Manuscript submitted for Publication.
11. **Internet source**: American Psychological Association (2001). APA style Homepage. Retrieved July 28, 2001 from http://www.apastyle.org/

## Part VIII: Appendix

- Appears on a **new page** labelled "Appendix" along with an **identifying letter** and heading
- Contains items that would be **inappropriate or too long** for the main body
- Typical contents: tests, detailed statistical treatments, computer programmes, stimulus materials, instructions given to subjects
- Each appendix starts on a new page

---

## Part IX: Author Note

Researcher addresses four basic items:
1. Source of **financial support**
2. **Acknowledgements** (contributions to the article)
3. **Special information**
4. Each author's **departmental affiliation** (when multiple authors contributed)

## Style of Writing a Research Report

### General Rules

| Rule | Specification |
|---|---|
| Tense | **Past tense** for research procedures; **present tense** when discussing conclusions from prior literature |
| Personal pronouns | Use "I" or "we" **cautiously**; generally discouraged for objectivity |
| Cited authors | Use **only last names** with year (e.g., Hilgard & Bower, 1986) |
| Author citations (2-6 authors) | **All names** cited at first occurrence; subsequently **first author + "et al."** |
| Author citations (>6 authors) | Use **first author + "et al."** even on the **first citation** |
| Direct quoting | **Avoid** where possible; paraphrase and summarize |
| Referring to own study | Use "**this study**" or "**the present study**" |
| Terminology | Use **approved psychological terminology**; define nonstandard terms on first use |

### Number Usage Rules

| Condition | Format |
|---|---|
| Numbers 0-9 | **Spell out** in words (one, two, three...) |
| Numbers 10 and larger | **Use digits** |
| Sentence beginning | Always **spell out**, regardless of size |
| Statistical results, decimals, measurements | Use **digits** |
| Series containing one number >= 10 | Use **digits** for all in series |
| Fractions / numbers < 10 | Spell out (e.g., "one-half") |
| Percentages | Digits + "per cent" (e.g., "20 per cent"), unless starting a sentence |
| Large numbers (thousands/millions) | Digits with commas (e.g., 23,305,000) |

### Abbreviations

- Justified only if: (a) term appears very frequently, (b) term contains complex words, (c) not using numerous different abbreviations simultaneously
- **Procedure**: Write the full term first, then the abbreviation in parentheses (e.g., Long Term Memory [LTM])
- Subsequently use **only the acronym**, except as the first word of a sentence (where the complete term must be used)

### Subject Terminology

APA style prefers **specific, less inferential, more descriptive** terms over the generic "subjects": men, women, children, pigeons, etc.

## Typing the Research Report

| Specification | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Paper size** | **8.5" x 11"** bond paper; **one side only** |
| **All margins** | **1.5 inches** (top, bottom, left, right) |
| Word division | Do not split words at line end unless necessary |
| **Short quotations** (3 or fewer lines) | In text, enclosed in **quotation marks** |
| **Long quotations** (more than 3 lines) | Set off from text, **double-spaced**, indented **~4 spaces** from left margin, **without quotation marks** |
| Pagination | Page information in parenthesis at end of direct quotation |
| **Italics** | Words to be italicized should be **underlined** in manuscript |

## Evaluating a Research Report

Critical evaluation questions by section:

### 1. The Title
- Is it clear? Is it concise?

### 2. The Problem
- Is the problem clear? Is its significance recognized? Have specific questions been raised? Are testable hypotheses framed? Are assumptions stated? Are limitations recognized? Are important terms defined?

### 3. Review of Related Literature
- Does it cover the area? Are main findings of previous researches noted? Is it well organized and summarized?

### 4. Methods
- Is the research design appropriate and described in detail? Are the subjects described? Are dependent variables well organized and controlled? Are psychometric properties of data-gathering instruments satisfactory? Are statistical procedures appropriate?

### 5. Results and Discussion
- Are tables and figures used appropriately? Is the discussion in the text clear and concise? Is the data analysis logical and perceptive? Is the statistical analysis interpretable?

### 6. Summary and Conclusions
- Is the problem reinstated? Are hypotheses/questions restated? Are procedures well described? Are findings concisely reported? Are findings and conclusions based upon data collected and analyzed?

## Writing a Research Proposal

### Nature and Purpose

A **research proposal** is a **detailed plan of the research to be conducted**. It is comparable to the **blueprint an architect prepares** for building a structure.

- Provides the basis for evaluation of proposed research by submitting professors and agencies
- **Major purpose**: to seek **permission**, often with **financial support**, from government agencies
- Follows the general format of a journal article but developed in sentences and paragraphs

### The Nine Steps of a Research Proposal

#### Step 1: Problem
- Expressed in a **declarative statement** (may also be in question form)
- Must be stated in clear, testable terms

#### Step 2: Definitions, Assumptions, Limitations, and Delimitations

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Operational definitions** | How variables are defined in **operational (measurable)** terms |
| **Assumptions** | Statements the researcher believes to be true **but cannot prove** |
| **Limitations** | Factors or conditions **beyond the researcher's control** that may influence conclusions or findings |
| **Delimitations** | The **boundaries of the research** -- who will be included as the sample and for whom the conclusions will be valid |

Key exam fact: "The plan to randomly select subjects and obtain adequate doses of reliable and valid data-gathering instruments" is cited as an example of a **good limitation**.

#### Step 3: Review of Related Literature
- Should be **selective** (not exhaustive), including only competently executed and clearly related studies
- Ensures the researcher knows what is **already known** and what **remains unknown**
- Helps **eliminate duplication** of what has already been done
- Provides background to see how the current investigation fits into the larger body of research
- Helps identify comparable studies, sampling strategies, procedures, extraneous variables, and recommendations for further research

#### Step 4: Hypotheses
- Include **major hypotheses** to be tested and **minor hypotheses** if any
- Should be formulated **before** starting the research
- A good hypothesis: reasonable, consistent with known facts, current in the area, stated in the **simplest possible terms**

#### Step 5: Methods
Three subsections:
1. **Subjects subsection**: Details of population, total number desired, characteristics, and procedure for selection
2. **Procedure subsection**: Outlines the research plan -- what is to be done, how, what data will be gathered, what data-gathering devices will be used
3. **Data analysis subsection**: Method of analyzing data; must mention the **rationale for selecting statistical techniques**

#### Step 6: Time Schedule
- Divide the entire research into **manageable parts** with **probable completion dates**
- Helps the researcher proceed **systematically and efficiently**

#### Step 7: Expected Results
- Indicates **possible or probable outcomes** (may be theoretical conjecture)
- Includes: brief restatement of anticipated results, **reasonable alternative outcomes**, and **null hypotheses** the researcher will accept if results deviate from research hypotheses

#### Step 8: References
- Same format as in the final report
- Any literature added to the final report's Discussion that was not anticipated in the proposal is an **exception, not the rule**

#### Step 9: Appendix
- All materials to be used in the study: research instruments, stimulus materials and apparatus, copy of instructions to be given to subjects

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Research Report** | Formal written document communicating research findings; structured per APA guidelines |
| **Abstract** | Summary of the study (100-150 words) on page 2; no references cited |
| **Running Head** | Abbreviated title (max 50 characters) printed at the top of each page of a published article |
| **Introduction** | First text section (page 3); NOT labelled; contains problem statement, literature review, and hypotheses |
| **Method Section** | Describes how research was conducted; subsections: Subjects, Apparatus, Design, Procedure |
| **Results Section** | Presents all data including non-supportive data; statistical significance stated but not discussed |
| **Discussion Section** | Interprets results; addresses hypotheses support, implications, negative results, future research, conclusions |
| **References** | Alphabetical list of only those works cited in the text |
| **Bibliography** | All works consulted on a topic, whether cited or not; NOT included in research reports |
| **Appendix** | Supplementary materials too long or inappropriate for the main body |
| **Author Note** | Identifies affiliations, financial support, acknowledgements, and contact information |
| **Research Proposal** | Detailed plan of research to be conducted; analogous to an architect's blueprint |
| **Assumptions** | Statements believed true by the researcher but that cannot be proved |
| **Limitations** | Factors beyond the researcher's control that may influence study conclusions |
| **Delimitations** | Boundaries of the research; specifies who is included and for whom conclusions are valid |
| **Operational Definitions** | Definitions of variables in practical, measurable terms |
| **Time Schedule** | Section of a research proposal assigning probable completion dates to research phases |
| **Expected Results** | States probable outcomes, alternative outcomes, and acceptable null hypotheses |
| **Figure** | Graph, photograph, chart, or other visual presentation in the Results section |

## Likely Exam Points

1. The purpose of a research report is **NOT to convince** but to inform readers what was done, why, what was found, and what it means.

2. The APA Publication Manual divides the research report format into **nine parts**: Title Page, Abstract, Introduction, Method, Results, Discussion, References, Appendix, Author Note.

3. The **Abstract** is written on **page 2** and is **100-150 words** long; **references must NOT be cited** in it.

4. The **Running Head** has a maximum length of **50 characters** (including letters, punctuation, and spaces).

5. The **recommended title length** is approximately **12-15 words**; introductory phrases like "A Study of..." must be avoided.

6. The **Introduction is NOT labelled** "Introduction" on the page; it starts on **page 3**.

7. The **Introduction** has three components: (1) problem statement, (2) review of literature, (3) hypotheses.

8. The **Results section** presents ALL data -- including data that **do not support** hypotheses -- but does **NOT discuss** results.

9. **References** = only cited works; **Bibliography** = all consulted works (bibliography is NOT included in research reports).

10. **Limitations** = factors beyond researcher's control; **Delimitations** = boundaries of the study (who is included); **Assumptions** = statements believed true but unprovable.

11. **Typing rules**: paper size **8.5" x 11"**, all margins **1.5 inches**; quotations of **more than 3 lines** are set off, double-spaced, indented **~4 spaces**, without quotation marks.

12. Numbers **0-9** are spelled out; numbers **10 and larger** are written as digits; a number **beginning a sentence** is always spelled out.

13. For **2-6 authors**: cite all names at first occurrence; use "first author **et al.**" subsequently. For **more than 6 authors**: use "first author + et al." even on the **first citation**.

14. A **research proposal** has **nine steps**: Problem; Definitions/Assumptions/Limitations/Delimitations; Review of Literature; Hypotheses; Methods (subjects, procedure, data analysis); Time Schedule; Expected Results; References; Appendix.

15. A research proposal is analogous to an **architect's blueprint**; its major purpose is to seek **permission and financial support** from government agencies.
