## 1.1 Measurement and Evaluation

**Measurement** is defined as the process of assigning numerals to events, objects, etc., according to certain rules.
- Tyler (1963): "assignment of numerals, according to rules."
- Nunnally (1970): "Measurement consists of rules for assigning numbers to objects in such a way as to represent quantities of attributes."

**Three core properties of measurement (Nunnally):**
1. Numbers are assigned according to fixed, explicit rules (rules transform qualities into numbers).
2. Measurement is always concerned with *attributes* of an object, not the object itself.
3. Numerals represent *quantities* of the attribute (quantification: how much?).

**Evaluation** is different: appraisal or assessment with respect to some standard (social, cultural, scientific).
- Tuckman (1975): "a process wherein the parts, processes or outcomes of a programme are examined to see our own expectations or our own standards of excellence."
- Measurement answers: *How much?* Evaluation answers: *How good/bad relative to a standard?*

> **Key Point:** Measuring a child's height (30") is measurement. Calling that child "short" (comparing to a norm) is evaluation.

### Difference Among Assessment, Testing, and Measurement

| Term | Scope | Key Feature | Question Answered |
|------|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Assessment** | Broadest | Any procedure to obtain info about performance (paper-pencil, performance tasks, observation, self-report); classifies behaviour against normative standards | How well does the individual perform? |
| **Testing** | Intermediate | Systematic procedure for measuring a sample of behaviour using a set of questions in a uniform manner; a test is one form of assessment | How well does the individual perform relative to others or a criterion? |
| **Measurement** | Narrowest | Numerical description of the degree to which an individual possesses a characteristic, done according to rules | How much? |

**Key distinction:** A test is called a test (not merely an assessment device) only when: (1) administration, scoring, and interpretation are standardized; (2) a standardization sample exists; (3) evidence for reliability and validity is available.

## 1.2 History of Psychological Measurement and Mental Testing

### Ancient Origins
- **206 BC, China (Han Dynasty):** Officials examined every 3 years to determine fitness for office; test batteries covered civil law, military, revenue, agriculture.
- **Ming Dynasty (1368-1644 AD):** National multistage testing programme; local, provincial, and national rounds.
- **1832:** British government encouraged East India Company to follow Chinese selection model.
- **1855:** British civil service adopted testing.
- **1883:** US Civil Service Commission established; competitive examinations for government jobs.

### Three Major Influences (1850-1900)
Singh identifies three key streams:
1. **Psychophysics** (Fechner's methods; relationship between physical stimuli and sensations)
2. **Darwinian biology** (individual differences within a species)
3. **Clinical practices** (clinical interest in feeble-minded, insane, misfits)

### Key Figures (Singh's Exact Sequence)

| Figure | Contribution | Year/Work |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| **Wilhelm Wundt** | First psychological laboratory (Leipzig/Karl Marx University); "thought meter" (calibrated pendulum to assess swiftness of thought); began measuring mental processes as early as 1862 | Lab est. **1879** |
| **Hubert Von Grashey** | Antecedent of the memory drum; tested brain-injured patients via moving slot | 1885 |
| **Conrad Rieger** | Test battery for brain-damaged; took >100 hours; went out of use | Late 1800s |
| **Sir Francis Galton** | Half-cousin of Darwin; coined term "psychometrics"; set up psychometric laboratory (International Health Exhibition, London, 1884); measured intelligence by reaction time (RT) and sensory discrimination; **Father of mental testing** (Goodenough 1949); Hereditary Genius (1869); Inquiries into Human Faculty (1883) | 1869-1884 |
| **Karl Pearson** | Product-moment correlation; statistical analysis of individual differences | ~1896 |
| **J.M. Cattell** | Studied with both Wundt and Galton; coined term **"mental test"** (paper: *Mental Tests and Measurement*); research lab at Columbia University | ~1890 |
| **Clark Wissler** | Showed mental test scores did NOT correlate with academic performance (RT, colour naming: r = -0.15); discredited Galtonian brass-instrument approach | 1901 |
| **Alfred Binet** | Gathered data on school children's intelligence in France (clinical interest); developed Binet-Simon Intelligence Scale | **1905** |
| **Lewis Terman** | Stanford; most influential American adopter of Binet; published Stanford-Binet; colleague **Arthur Otis** did group testing | **1916** |
| **William Stern** | Introduced concept of **IQ** in the 1912 revision of Binet scale | 1912 |
| **Charles Spearman** | British; two theories: (1) statistical theory of measurement inconsistency (foundation of reliability theory); (2) g-factor of intelligence | Early 1900s |

### Six Periods of 20th-Century Mental Testing (Thorndike & Thorndikechrist, 2015)

| Period | Dates | Key Features |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| **(i) Early Period** | 1901-1915 | Tentative exploration; Binet-Simon Scale (1905); Stanford-Binet (1916); Porteus Maze Test; Spearman's g-factor |
| **(ii) Boom Period** | ~1916-1930 | WWI; Robert Yerkes led group; **Army Alpha** (verbal) and **Army Beta** (performance) tests; **Woodworth Personal Data Sheet** (1918, first objective personality measure); Strong Vocational Interest Blank (SVIB); Thurstone's attitude scaling (1929) |
| **(iii) First Period of Criticism** | 1930s | Criticism and Consolidation; MMPI developed; Wechsler-Bellevue Intelligence Scale; Kuder Preference Record; Thurstone refined factor analysis; **Oscar Buros** started **Mental Measurement Yearbook (MMY)** in 1935 |
| **(iv) Battery Period** | 1940s-1950s | Widespread test use; Bloom's and Guilford's taxonomies; DAT (Differential Aptitude Test); GATB (General Aptitude Test Battery); APA released test guidelines (1954) |
| **(v) Second Period of Criticism** | 1960s | Gender and minority bias concerns; legal proceedings in USA |
| **(vi) Period of Accountability** | 2000s | NCB (No Child Left Behind) bill, 2002 (USA); standardized tests mandated |

## 1.3 Levels of Measurement (Measurement Scales)

Singh follows **Stevens (1951)**: four levels based on postulates of measurement. Nine postulates (Guilford, 1954) underlie them, grouped in three categories: equalities/identities, rank order, and additivity.

### The Four Scales

| Scale | Level | Properties | Examples | Permissible Statistics |
|-------|-------|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| **Nominal (Classificatory)** | Lowest | Identity only (classification; equivalence within groups) | Religion (Hindu/Muslim/Sikh), sex, political party | Frequency, percentage, proportion, mode, coefficient of contingency; NO addition/subtraction |
| **Ordinal** | Second | Identity + **Magnitude** (rank order; "greater than/less than") | Socio-economic status, class rank | Median, percentiles, rank correlation; PLUS all nominal statistics |
| **Interval (Equal-interval)** | Third | Identity + Magnitude + **Equal intervals** (equal distances mean equal differences); zero is arbitrary | Intelligence test scores, aptitude test scores, Fahrenheit/Celsius thermometers, calendar time | Mean, SD, Pearson r, t-test, F-test; NO coefficient of variation |
| **Ratio** | Highest | Identity + Magnitude + Equal intervals + **Absolute (true) zero** | Weight, height, length, loudness | All statistics including coefficient of variation |

> **Key Point:** Psychological tests are **interval scales** (arbitrary zero, no true ratio). Physical measures (weight, height) are **ratio scales** (true zero).

### Three Properties That Differentiate Scales

1. **Magnitude** ("moreness"): A scale has magnitude if a given instance of the attribute represents more, less, or equal amounts than another instance (McCall, 1994). Ordinal, interval, ratio all have it; nominal does not.

2. **Equal intervals**: A scale has equal intervals if the difference between any two points at any place has the same meaning as an equal numerical difference elsewhere. Example: 4 kg vs. 6 kg = same as 14 kg vs. 16 kg (2 kg either way). Psychological tests rarely have equal intervals (IQ difference of 50-60 does not mean the same as 110-120 difference). Interval and ratio scales have this property.

3. **Absolute zero**: Exists when nothing of the property being measured exists. Physical: heart rate of 0 means no heart rate. Psychological: 0 on a shyness scale does not mean "absolutely no shyness." Only ratio scales have a true absolute zero.

> **Key Point:** The **coefficient of variation** is not applicable to interval measurement because the mean shifts with any change in the arbitrary zero point, making the ratio SD/mean meaningless.

## 1.4 Functions of Measurement

Singh lists six primary functions in psychology and education:

1. **In Selection** - predicts the ability of an individual; basis for personnel selection decisions.
2. **In Classification** - classifies individuals into categories (retarded/average/gifted; types of mental illness; job satisfaction groups).
3. **In Comparison** - measures individual differences; enables comparison between persons or groups (Galton-Darwin tradition).
4. **In Guidance and Counselling** - identifies strengths and weaknesses; helps predict adjustment problems; assists diagnosis.
5. **In Research** - fundamental basis of all psychological and educational research; ensures valid design and statistical calculation.
6. **In Improving Classroom Instruction** - reveals which students benefit from instruction; guides qualitative and quantitative modifications.

## 1.5 Psychological vs. Physical Measurement

| Dimension | Physical Measurement | Psychological Measurement |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Unit of measurement | Fixed and constant throughout | Not fixed; varies during measurement |
| Zero point | True zero (absolute) | Arbitrary zero |
| Accuracy/Predictability | More accurate; ratios meaningful | Less accurate; ratios NOT meaningful |
| Directness | Direct (place ruler against cloth) | Indirect (infer from responses) |
| Completeness | Entire quantity measurable | Only a sample of behaviour measured |

## 1.6 Steps in Measurement Process

Three common steps (applicable to any field):

1. **(i) Identifying and Defining the Quality or Attribute** - select relevant, clearly defined attribute (e.g., the "construct" of intelligence; operationally defined).
2. **(ii) Determining Operations for Isolating and Displaying Attribute** - find a set of operations that isolate and correctly display the attribute (operational definition).
3. **(iii) Quantifying the Attribute** - assign numerical values by a set of rules (a scale). Advantages: facilitates communication; allows mathematical summary operations.

**Problems at each step:**
- (i) Difficulty defining constructs like intelligence, anxiety, adjustment (diversity in definitions).
- (ii) No single accepted procedure for many psychological attributes (unlike physical measurement).
- (iii) No demonstration of equality of units; items are not equal-interval (analogy item ≠ arithmetic series item).

## 1.7 General Problems of Measurement

Four general problems Singh identifies:

1. **Indirectness of Measurement** - most psychological attributes cannot be observed directly; measures are of a sample of behaviour that may be unreliable, invalid, or unrepresentative.

2. **Incompleteness of Measurement** - any psychological measure is a partial, incomplete sample; the total domain (e.g., all possible attitude items) is limitless.

3. **Relativity of Measurement** - scores have meaning only relative to a reference group; zero does not mean absence of the trait; measurement is not absolute.

4. **Errors in Measurement** - both physical and psychological measures contain uncontrolled factors producing errors (see Sources below).

## 1.8 Sources of Errors in Measurement

Singh identifies four main sources:

| Source | Nature of Error |
|--------|----------------|
| **(i) Respondent** | Reluctance to express true feelings; lack of knowledge; response distortion |
| **(ii) Measurer** | Behaviour/style/looks of the measurer encourages certain replies; incorrect coding; faulty tabulation; inappropriate statistical analysis |
| **(iii) Situation** | Unnecessary strain (e.g., interview setting); lack of anonymity introduces error |
| **(iv) Test Instrument** | Poor psychometric qualities; defective measuring instrument; low reliability or validity |

## 2.1 Meaning of a Test

**Psychological/Educational test** (Anastasi & Urbina, 1997): "essentially an objective and standardized measure of a sample of behaviour."

- Bean (1953): "an organized succession of stimuli designed to measure quantitatively or to evaluate qualitatively some mental process, trait or characteristic."
- Cullari (1998): "A test is a standardized procedure for sampling behaviour and describing it with scores or categories."
- Kaplan & Saccuzzo (2001): "a set of items designed to measure characteristics of human beings that pertain to behaviour."

**Key features of a psychological test:**
- Standardized procedure (administration, scoring, interpretation are uniform).
- Quantitative AND qualitative measurement.
- Based on a limited **sample** of behaviour (error is inherent; minimized by design, never eliminated).
- Scores interpreted with reference to a **standardization sample**.

**Norm-referenced vs. Criterion-referenced:**
- **Norm-referenced:** results compared to average performance of standardization sample.
- **Criterion-referenced:** compared to a fixed criterion/standard (e.g., 80% accuracy in arithmetic).

## 2.2 Classification of Tests (Singh's Taxonomy)

Singh classifies tests on **six criteria**:

### 1. Administrative Conditions
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Individual test** | Administered to one person at a time; time-consuming; requires trained examiner | Kohs Block Design Test |
| **Group test** | Administered to more than one person; surveys cognitive skills and achievement | Bell Adjustment Inventory |

### 2. Scoring
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Objective (New-type/Limited-answer)** | No scope for subjective judgement; multiple-choice, true-false, matching items; unambiguous scoring |
| **Subjective (Essay/Free-answer)** | Scope for scorer's judgement; essay-type items |

### 3. Time Limit
| Type | Description | Feature |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Power test** | Generous time limit; items of varying difficulty, in increasing order; measures ability/knowledge | Most intelligence and aptitude tests |
| **Speed test** | Severe time limit; items comparatively easy and of uniform difficulty; measures speed of response | Most clerical aptitude tests |

> **Key Point:** A pure power test or pure speed test is rare; mixtures are common. Whether a test is power or speed depends partly on the examinees' level.

### 4. Nature/Content of Items
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Verbal (Paper-pencil)** | Reading, writing, oral expression; items in language | Jalota Group General Intelligence Test |
| **Nonverbal** | Pictures, figures, symbols; language used in instructions only | Raven Progressive Matrices |
| **Performance** | Examinees perform a task (assemble puzzle, place pictures in sequence); oral instruction or gesture; individually administered | Porteus Maze Test |
| **Nonlanguage** | No written, spoken, or reading communication; gestures/pantomime; for those who cannot communicate in ordinary language | Various cultural-fair tests |

### 5. Purpose/Objective
- **Intelligence tests:** assess intelligence.
- **Aptitude tests:** assess potentials or aptitudes.
- **Personality tests:** assess traits, adjustments, interests, values.
- **Neuropsychological tests:** assessment of brain dysfunction.
- **Achievement tests:** assess acquired knowledge/skill in a given area after training or learning.

### 6. Standardization
| Type | Features |
|------|---------|
| **Standardized tests** | Standard instructions; uniform scoring; item analysis done; reliability and validity established; norms available; constructed by test specialists |
| **Teacher-made tests** | Constructed by teachers; items modifiable; rules for admin/scoring by teacher; class-specific norms (if any) |

**Singh's four conditions for standardization:**
1. Standard manner of giving instructions.
2. Uniformity of scoring; item analysis index of fairness.
3. Reliability and validity established; intended population stated.
4. Norms available (controversial; Cronbach 1970 said norms not strictly required, but majority favour norms).

## 2.3 Characteristics of a Good Test

Singh lists five characteristics:

1. **Objectivity** - free from subjective element; complete interpersonal agreement among experts regarding items and scoring. Two aspects: (a) objectivity of items (uniform interpretation by all examinees); (b) objectivity of scoring (standard scoring method).

2. **Reliability** - self-correlation of the test; consistency of scores across occasions (temporal consistency) and within a single administration (internal consistency). Reliability is the extent to which scores are free from internal defects that produce measurement errors.

3. **Validity** - extent to which the test measures what it intends to measure; correlation with an independent outside criterion. Validity depends on reliability (unreliable test cannot be valid); but high reliability does NOT guarantee high validity.

4. **Norms** - average performance of a representative sample. Four common types: age norms, grade norms, percentile norms, standard score norms. Norms enable meaningful interpretation of scores.

5. **Practicability/Usability** - test must be practical regarding time, length, scoring ease, cost, and whether scoring requires highly specialized expertise.

## 2.4 General Steps of Test Construction (Singh's 7 Steps)

Singh identifies seven ordered steps:

| Step | Name | Key Actions |
|------|------|------------|
| **1** | **Planning of the Test** | Specify broad and specific objectives; decide content/item type; sampling method; length; time limit; statistical methods; number of reproductions; manual preparation |
| **2** | **Writing Items of the Test** | Prepare items (essay or objective types) following planning; creative art requiring thorough subject knowledge, awareness of examinees, familiarity with item types; submit to subject experts for critique and modification; arrange in increasing order of difficulty |
| **3** | **Preliminary Administration (Experimental Try-out)** | Conrad (1951) recommends 3 stages: (a) Pre-try-out (N ≥ 100): detect gross defects, ambiguities; (b) Try-out proper (N ≈ 400): data for item analysis; (c) Final trial administration: detect minor defects after item analysis; acts as a "dress rehearsal" |
| **4** | **Reliability of the Final Test** | Administer final test on fresh sample (N ≥ 100); compute reliability coefficient by test-retest, split-half, equivalent-form, or Kuder-Richardson methods |
| **5** | **Validity of the Final Test** | Validate against outside independent criteria; compute validity coefficients using data from samples OTHER than those used in item analysis (cross-validation); types: content, construct, criterion-related validity |
| **6** | **Norms of the Final Test** | Develop norms from a representative, randomly selected, cross-sectional sample; types: age, grade, percentile, standard score norms |
| **7** | **Preparation of Manual and Reproduction of the Test** | Manual reports psychometric properties, norms, administration/scoring procedures, time limits, arrangement of materials, references; then print and distribute |

> **Key Point:** Conrad's three try-out stages: Pre-try-out (N ≥ 100), Try-out proper (N ≈ 400 for item analysis), Final trial administration (after item selection).

### Purposes of Experimental Try-out (Conrad, 1951)
1. Find weaknesses, omissions, ambiguities, inadequacies (nonfunctioning distractors, very easy/difficult items).
2. Determine difficulty values for even distribution.
3. Determine discriminatory power of each item (item validity).
4. Determine reasonable time limit.
5. Determine appropriate length.
6. Determine intercorrelations to avoid overlap.
7. Identify weaknesses in instructions and sample questions.

## 2.5 Uses and Limitations of Psychological Tests

### Five Uses (Singh)
1. **Classification** - placement, screening, certification, selection.
2. **Diagnosis and planning for treatment** - MMPI for personality pathology; intelligence tests for mental retardation or learning disability.
3. **Self-knowledge** - feedback to test takers about their trait/ability level.
4. **Evaluation of programmes** - educational and social programme evaluation.
5. **Theoretical and applied research** - test hypotheses in both basic and applied research.

> **Key Point:** Information about published psychological tests is available from: Publisher's Catalogues, Reference books, Journals, Databases, Test manuals. The best single reference is **Mental Measurements Yearbook (MMY)** (Oscar Buros, started 1935).

### Five Limitations
1. Invasion of privacy.
2. Permanent categorization of persons (labels like "mentally retarded" may not be revised even when evidence of change exists).
3. Measure only limited aspects of behaviour.
4. Create test anxiety (examinees who are familiar with test type are less anxious).
5. Penalize bright and creative examinees (insensitive to atypical/creative responses).

## 2.6 Ethical Issues in Psychological Testing

APA officially adopted standards in 1953; current version is **Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct (APA, 1992)** - preamble + 6 general principles + 8 ethical standards. APA Committee on Psychological Tests and Assessment (CPTA) oversees testing practices.

Singh identifies five major ethical issues:

1. **Human rights** - right to not be tested; right to know test scores, interpretations, and basis of decisions; right to confidentiality.
2. **Labelling** - labelling (e.g., "chronic schizophrenic") stigmatizes; may be self-fulfilling; lowers tolerance for stress; makes treatment difficult.
3. **Invasion of privacy** - Dahlstrom (1969): tests have limited, pinpointed aims and cannot invade privacy; privacy is invaded when information is used *inappropriately*; APA code requires confidentiality.
4. **Divided loyalties** (first identified by Jackson & Messick, 1967) - psychologist's loyalty is divided between institutional interests (identify accident-prone employees) and individual welfare (protect job seekers' rights).
5. **Responsibility of test constructors and test users** - constructors must provide a clear manual with appropriate uses, reliability, validity, norms, administration/scoring standards (AERA, APA & NCME, 1999); users must know why they use a test, its consequences, and its psychometric qualities.

## 3.1 Meaning and Types of Items

**Item** (Bean, 1953): "a single task or question that usually cannot be broken down into any smaller units."

Nunnally (1959): items are the "lowest common denominator of a test, which is scored."

**Characteristics of a good item:**
1. Not ambiguous in meaning for item writer or examinees.
2. Not too easy or too difficult.
3. Has discriminating power.
4. Measures significant, not trivial, aspects of subject matter.
5. Does not encourage guesswork.
6. Presents no difficulty in reading.
7. Not an interlocking/interdependent item (answering it should not depend on another item).

### Two Broad Types of Items

```
ITEMS
├── Essay (Free-answer)
│   ├── Short-answer essay
│   └── Long-answer (Extended-answer) essay
└── Objective (New-type)
    ├── Supply type
    │   ├── Unstructured short-answer
    │   └── Completion (Fill-in)
    └── Selection type
        ├── Two-alternative (Dichotomous)
        │   ├── Yes-No
        │   ├── True-False
        │   ├── Right-Wrong
        │   └── Correction form
        ├── Multiple-choice (Polytomous/Polychotomous)
        └── Matching
```

**Other formats (Singh):** Likert format, Category format (10-point scale), Checklists, Q-sorts, Visual analogue scale.

## 3.2 Essay Items

**Merit:** Measure higher mental processes (synthesis, analysis, evaluation, criticism, organization, critical thinking, originality).

**Demerits:**
- Marking highly unreliable (varies scorer to scorer; within-scorer variability across time).
- Lack scoring economy (time-consuming).
- Factual knowledge confounded with ability to write quickly/organize.
- Very few questions possible per session, lowering content validity.

### Variants of Essay Testing Environment
- **Open-book examination:** textbooks/class notes permitted; reduces impact of knowledge differences.
- **Take-home examination:** answers prepared outside class.
- **Cheat sheets:** one piece of paper with any info; suits formula-heavy subjects.
- **Study questions:** list of 10-15 questions; 4-5 appear in exam; combines open-book and take-home.

### Scoring Methods for Essay Items
1. **Sorting method:** answer sheets sorted into groups (best, better, poor); scorer assigns weights and sums; quick; reduces erratic marking.
2. **Point-score method:** grading key with correct answers and points prepared beforehand; suited to short-answer essay items; difficult to apply to extended-answer items.

## 3.3 Objective Items

### Completion Items (Fill-in)
- Supply type; recall required.
- Used in mathematics, chemistry, vocabulary tests, date-event association tests.
- **Demerits:** does not measure complex understanding; scoring may be subjective if item is poorly written.
- Writing guidelines: avoid ambiguity (state "year" or "country," not just a blank); avoid clues (opposite-word blanks); avoid too many blanks.

### Two-alternative (True-False) Items
- Probability of guessing = **50%**; not preferred for intelligence/aptitude tests.
- Writing guidelines: statements must be entirely true or entirely false; avoid double negatives; avoid specific determiners (never, always, all, none tend to be false; often, sometimes tend to be true); use authority for controversial statements; paraphrase from books (avoid verbatim); arrange T/F sequence randomly.

### Multiple-choice Items
- **Most popular, flexible, effective** objective item type.
- Structure: **stem** (question/incomplete statement) + **3-5 alternatives** (keyed answer + **distractors/foils**).
- Also called **polytomous/polychotomous format**.
- **Advantages over true-false:** reduced guessing (1/n instead of 1/2); respondent must know correct answer not just recognize falsity; relatively free from response sets; amenable to diagnostic analysis; higher reliability per item.
- **Limitations:** cannot measure ability to organize materials or express; difficult to write; requires thorough knowledge and trained writer; takes more time to answer; difficult to find sufficient plausible distractors.
- Writing guidelines: keyed response should not be shorter/longer than others; randomly vary position of keyed response; stem must present a clear problem; avoid clang association (phonological similarity between stem and keyed answer); ensure only ONE correct/best answer; use negative sparingly (underline or capitalize if used); ensure wrong answers are plausible; use "none of these/none of the above" only when an absolutely correct answer exists.

### Matching Items
- Two columns: **premises** (left, stems) and **responses/options** (right).
- Measures associative learning.
- **Limitations:** does not measure real understanding; process of elimination reveals last pair; not suited to heterogeneous content.
- Writing guidelines: number of response options must **exceed** number of stems (reduces elimination); include clear instructions stating basis of matching; stems and options must be homogeneous; matching sets should be neither too short (more guessing) nor too long (less homogeneous).

### Likert Format
- 5-option scale: Strongly Agree, Agree, Neutral, Disagree, Strongly Disagree.
- Scored 5-4-3-2-1 for positive items; 1-2-3-4-5 for negative items.
- Can be subjected to factor analysis; requires item discriminability analysis.

### Q-sort (Stephenson, 1953)
- Respondents sort statements into **9 piles**; frequency per category resembles a bell-shaped curve.
- Used in verbalized attitudes, preferences, self-description.

## 3.4 Difference Between Essay-type and Objective-type Tests (Ebel, 1965)

| Dimension | Essay-type | Objective-type |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Response format | Writes own answer | Selects from given alternatives |
| Primary skills | Thinking + writing | Reading + thinking |
| Scoring | Difficult; subjective; variable | Easy; objective; accurate |
| Quality depends on | Scorer's skill | Item writer's skill |
| Guessing/bluffing | Bluffing possible | Guessing possible |
| Score assignment | At scorer's discretion | Predetermined scoring key |
| Item construction | Easy (even minimally skilled) | Difficult; requires trained writer |

**Similarities:** Both involve some subjectivity; both emphasize objectivity in score interpretation; both can measure any educational achievement.

## 3.5 General Guidelines for Item Writing (Singh)

1. **Clarity** - items must not be "verbal puzzles"; simple, clear language; not a test of language ability.
2. **Avoid nonfunctional words** - words that make no contribution toward the correct choice lower validity.
3. **Avoid irrelevant accuracies** - mislead examinees into thinking a statement is true.
4. **Appropriate difficulty level** - not too easy or too difficult; match examinees' level; homogeneous alternatives increase difficulty; heterogeneous alternatives decrease it.
5. **Avoid stereotyped words** - everyone knows them; do not discriminate between those who know and those who do not.
6. **Avoid irrelevant clues** - clang association, verbal association, consistent position of correct answer, length differences, keeping a different foil among homogeneous foils, specific determiners (never, always, all, none).
7. **Avoid interlocking/interdependent items** - do not make answering one item dependent on another.

**Devellis (1991) additional guidelines:**
- (i) Define what is to be assessed clearly; use substantive theory; make items specific.
- (ii) Avoid exceptionally long items.
- (iii) Reading difficulty must be appropriate for the target group.
- (iv) Write 3-4 items for every 1 to be retained (develop an item pool).
- (v) Avoid "double-barreled" items (two ideas in one item).
- (vi) Mix negative and positive items to check acquiescence response set.

## 3.6 General Methods of Scoring Objective-test Items

**Factors for selecting scoring method:** not time-consuming; no highly specialized person required; all scorers arrive at identical conclusions; maximum accuracy.

1. **Overlay-key Method** - cut-out key (window) placed over answer sheet; correct answer shows through window; total score = sum of points.
2. **Strip-key Method** - correct answers printed vertically on strip of paper/cardboard; runs parallel to items; marked in adjacent column; total = sum of points.

For short-answer essay items: can be scored like objective items if planning is done (length of answer, factual information required, points per type, model answers).

## 4.1 Item Analysis: Meaning and Purpose

**Item analysis** is a set of procedures applied to determine indices for the validity (truthfulness) of items; selects valid and discriminating items; eliminates or modifies the rest.

**Three objectives:**
1. Provides **difficulty value** (index of difficulty) for each item.
2. Provides **discrimination value** (index of discrimination / item validity) for each item.
3. Indicates **effectiveness of distractors** in multiple-choice items (distractor analysis).
4. Indicates why an item is not functioning and how it might be modified.

---

## 4.2 Item Difficulty (Power Tests)

**Index of difficulty (p):** the proportion or percentage of examinees who answer an item correctly.

### Formula (entire group):

$$p = \frac{R}{N} \quad \text{(Equation 4.1)}$$

where R = number answering correctly; N = total number of examinees.

### Formula (extreme groups):

$$p = \frac{R_u + R_l}{N_u + N_l} \quad \text{(Equation 4.2)}$$

where R_u = correct in upper group; R_l = correct in lower group; N_u = N in upper group; N_l = N in lower group.

**Extreme groups:** upper 27% and lower 27% (Kelley, 1939 demonstrated this ratio maximizes the critical ratio; total N should be 370 so each extreme = 100). Middle 46% set aside.

**Key relationships:**
- Higher p = easier item (inverse relationship between p and difficulty).
- Maximum variance (and maximum discrimination) when p = 0.50 (SD = √(p × q) = √(0.25) = 0.5).
- Maximum comparisons at p = 0.50: 50 × 50 = 2500 discriminations.
- Items with p near 0.5 (0.4-0.6): preferred ("peaked test"); symmetrical score distribution; largest SD.
- Extreme p values (0.1, 0.9): low inter-item correlations, lower internal consistency reliability.

### Correction for Guessing:

$$S = R - \frac{W}{K-1} \quad \text{(Equation 4.3)}$$

where S = corrected score; R = correct responses; W = incorrect responses; K = number of response options.

**Criticism of correction formula:** (1) assumes all errors result from blind guessing (false; partial knowledge exists); (2) examinees may answer wrongly due to misinformation; (3) can undercorrect when examinees can eliminate some options; (4) does NOT change rank order of examinees if no items are omitted (r between raw and corrected = 1.00).

### Two Methods of Determining Difficulty Value
1. **Method of judgement** - experts rank items in increasing difficulty; subjective; unreliable.
2. **Empirical (Statistical) method** - use formula above; objective and scientific.

## 4.3 Optimal Difficulty Value

| Item type | Optimal p (Lord, 1952) |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Two-alternative (True-false) | 0.85 (uncorrected) |
| Three-alternative | 0.77 |
| Four-alternative | 0.74 |
| Five-alternative | 0.69 |

- For short tests (≤30 items): p near 0.5 preferred (maximizes inter-item correlations and reliability).
- For longer tests (>30 items): scattered difficulty values preferred.
- For personality tests (agree-disagree), p near 0.5 = ambiguous items = near-zero inter-item correlation = zero reliability. Recommended: true-false items should have P above 0.70.

---

## 4.4 Index of Discrimination

Also called **item validity index** (Blood & Budd, 1972). Bean (1953): "the degree to which the single item separates the superior from the inferior individuals in the trait or group of traits being measured."

**Three categories of items by discrimination:**
- **Positively discriminating:** proportion correct is higher in upper group. (Retained.)
- **Negatively discriminating:** proportion correct is lower in upper group. (Dropped.)
- **Nondiscriminating:** proportion correct is equal or approximately equal in both groups. (Dropped.)

### Methods of Calculating Discrimination Index

**1. Critical ratio (significance of difference between two proportions):** indicates only WHETHER the item discriminates; does not show HOW MUCH. Uses upper 27% and lower 27%.

**2. Chi-square (Guilford, 1954)** for equal-size extreme groups:

$$\chi^2 = \frac{N(P_u - P_l)^2}{4pq} \quad \text{(Equation 4.4)}$$

where N = total examinees; P_u and P_l = proportions passing in upper and lower groups; P = arithmetic mean of P_u and P_l; q = 1 - P. Limitation: only tells if item discriminates, not how much; only valid for larger samples.

**3. Net D index (Marshall & Hales, 1972):**

$$V = \frac{R_u}{N_u} - \frac{R_l}{N_l} \quad \text{(Equation 4.5)}$$

Or, since N_u = N_l:

$$V = \frac{R_u - R_l}{N}$$

**Interpretation of Net D (V):**
- V above 0.40: good discrimination.
- V between 0.20 and 0.40: poor discrimination.
- V below 0.20: negligible discrimination.
- Negative V: negative discrimination - drop item.

**4. Correlational techniques (item-total correlation):** each item correlated against total score (internal criterion). Called "internal-consistency item discrimination index" (Lindquist, 1951). **Most preferred measure.**

Selection criterion: at least 75% of correlations positive; items with r ≥ 0.20 accepted. Nunnally (1970): selection of items depends directly on desired reliability level; use KR formula 20 to verify.

**Correlation methods for different item formats:**
| Item type | Appropriate statistic |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Multipoint items (e.g., Likert) | Product-moment (PM) correlation |
| Dichotomous items; total score continuous | Biserial r or point biserial r |
| Both item and total score dichotomized | Tetrachoric r or phi-coefficient |
| Equal-size extreme groups (Flanagan's abac table) | Biserial r (read directly from abac) |

**Flanagan's abac table:** requires N ≥ 100 in each extreme group (total N ≥ 370); directly gives biserial r from proportions passing in upper 27% and lower 27%.

## 4.5 Simplified Item Analysis Form (Step-by-step)

1. Score each item (correct = 1).
2. Calculate total score per examinee.
3. Arrange answer sheets from highest to lowest total score.
4. Select upper 27% (100 cases) and lower 27% (100 cases); set aside middle 46%. Total N = 370.
5. Count number responding to each option for upper and lower groups.
6. Subtract lower group correct from upper group correct (difference = column 3).
7. Divide difference by N of extreme group (= discrimination index D or V).
8. Count middle group correct responses.
9. Sum upper + lower + middle correct (total correct).
10. Divide total correct by N (= difficulty value P).

## 4.6 Distractor Analysis (Effectiveness of Distractors/Foils)

A **good distractor** must:
- Be answered by MORE examinees of the lower group than the upper group (negative discrimination index for distractors; positive only for keyed answer).
- Attract examinees who lack knowledge.

Types of problem distractors:
- **Nonfunctional distractors** - chosen by none of the examinees in either group; drop or rewrite.
- **Equally attractive distractors** - chosen equally by upper and lower groups; modify.
- **Distractor chosen more by upper group** - poor form; rewrite.

**Expected frequency formula (if not using extreme group method):**

$$\text{Expected} = \frac{\text{Number answering incorrectly}}{\text{Number of distractors}} \quad \text{(Equation 4.6)}$$

> **Key Point:** A correct response must have a **positive** discrimination index; distractors must have **negative** discrimination indices.

---

## 4.7 Speed Tests: Item Analysis

For speed tests, position in test inflates difficulty value (later items seem harder, not because they are intrinsically harder, but because fewer reach them).

**Index of difficulty for speed tests:**

$$p_s = \frac{R}{N_r} \quad \text{(Equation 4.7)}$$

where N_r = number of examinees who actually reached (answered) that item.

**Corrected index of difficulty for speed tests:**

$$p_c = \frac{R - (W/K-1)}{N - NR} \quad \text{(Equation 4.8)}$$

where NR = examinees who did not reach the item.

**Index of discrimination for speed tests:** Cannot use item-total correlation (items in arbitrary order; items at beginning have high p; items at end have low p; correlation becomes arbitrary). Instead: select items based on **index of difficulty** and **ideal time limit** (experimentally determined). Largest SD across time limits = ideal time limit (Nunnally, 1970).

---

## 4.8 Factors Influencing Difficulty and Discrimination Indices

**Factors affecting index of difficulty:**
- Complexity/ambiguity of the stem (lowers difficulty index).
- Previous/learning experience of examinees.
- Homogeneity of response alternatives (homogeneous alternatives = harder item).

**Additional factors affecting index of discrimination:**
- Heterogeneity of the group of examinees.
- Ability of the stem to frame a clear question.
- Effectiveness of distractors in appealing to those who don't know the answer.
- All factors affecting difficulty (since discrimination is linked to difficulty).

---

## 4.9 Important Interactions Among Item Characteristics

The three characteristics - difficulty, discrimination, and distractor quality - are **empirically related but conceptually distinct.**

| Interaction | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| Distractors affect difficulty | Plausible distractors = harder item; ridiculous distractors = easier item |
| Distractors affect discrimination | Implausible distractors lower difficulty, and very easy/difficult items cannot make valid discrimination |
| Difficulty affects discrimination | Items with p near 1.0 or 0.0 cannot discriminate; p near 0.50 (0.30-0.70) = maximum discrimination potential; BUT p near 0.50 does NOT guarantee a good discriminator |

> **Key Point:** A poor item with p = 0.50 is still a poor item. Extreme p values place a direct statistical *limit* on discriminating power; p near 0.50 removes statistical limits but does not guarantee discrimination.

## 4.10 Item Characteristic Curve (ICC) and Item Response Theory (IRT)

### ICC
**Item Characteristic Curve:** graphic representation of the probability of giving a correct answer to an item as a function of the level of the attribute (total test score on x-axis; probability of correct response on y-axis).

- **Slope (steepness)** = discriminating power:
  - Positive slope (curve A) = positive discrimination.
  - Flat slope (curve B) = near-zero discrimination.
  - Negative slope (curve C) = negative discrimination.
- **Position** on x-axis = difficulty:
  - ICC rising on left (low scores) = easy item.
  - ICC rising on right (high scores) = difficult item.

### Item Response Theory (IRT)
Also called **latent trait theory** or **item characteristics curve theory**.

**Core statement:** the probability of a particular response to an item is a joint function of one or more characteristics of the *respondent* (ability) and one or more characteristics of the *item*.

**Contrast with classical test theory (CTT):**
- CTT: score = sum of item responses; sample-dependent.
- IRT: uses ICC for each item; yields measures that are **sample-invariant** (difficulty, discrimination measures don't depend on the sample used).

**Key advantages of IRT:**
1. **Sample invariance** - item characteristics not confounded with examinee characteristics (a spelling test difficult for Class IV students may be easy for Class X students; IRT distinguishes this).
2. **Computerized adaptive testing (tailored testing)** - computer estimates ability at each step; selects next item matched to ability level; reduces test length by 50%+ (Weiss, 1985; Weiss & Yoes, 1991); reduces embarrassment, wasted effort, cheating.
3. **Information from distractors** - each distractor gets its own ICC; choice C (a near-correct distractor) can reflect partial knowledge; examinees given partial credit.
4. **Handles specialized tests** - screening tests and criterion-keyed tests (e.g., MMPI) where standard item analysis guidelines don't apply.

**Limitations of IRT** (Kaplan & Saccuzzo, 2001): technical difficulties with model fit and parameter estimation; computationally demanding.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Measurement vs. evaluation vs. assessment vs. testing:** Measurement = how much (numerical description by rules). Evaluation = how good (appraisal relative to a standard). Assessment = broadest (any procedure); includes testing, observation, self-report. Testing = systematic, uniform, standardized sample of behaviour.

2. **Four scales of measurement (Stevens):** Nominal (classification only), Ordinal (rank), Interval (equal intervals, arbitrary zero), Ratio (true zero). Psychological tests = interval; physical measures = ratio.

3. **Properties of scales:** Three properties: magnitude, equal intervals, absolute zero. Nominal has none; ordinal has magnitude only; interval has magnitude + equal intervals; ratio has all three.

4. **Permissible statistics:** Nominal: mode, frequency. Ordinal: median, percentiles, rank correlation. Interval: mean, SD, Pearson r, t, F (NOT coefficient of variation). Ratio: all, including coefficient of variation.

5. **Father of mental testing** = Galton (Goodenough, 1949). **"Mental test"** coined by J.M. Cattell. **First IQ concept** = Stern (1912 Binet revision). **First personality inventory** = Woodworth Personal Data Sheet (1918). **Army Alpha** (verbal); **Army Beta** (performance). **MMY** = Oscar Buros (1935).

6. **Six history periods:** Early (1901-1915), Boom (WWI-1930), First Criticism (1930s), Battery (1940s-1950s), Second Criticism (1960s), Accountability (2002 NCB).

7. **Wundt:** First psychological lab, Leipzig, **1879**; also measuring by 1862 (thought meter).

8. **General Steps of Test Construction (7 steps):** Planning, Item writing, Preliminary administration (Conrad: 3 stages; N_1 ≥ 100, N_2 ≈ 400, N_3 final trial), Reliability, Validity, Norms, Manual and reproduction.

9. **Conrad's three try-out stages:** Pre-try-out (N ≥ 100: gross defects), Try-out proper (N ≈ 400: item analysis data), Final trial (after item selection: "dress rehearsal").

10. **Five characteristics of a good test:** Objectivity, Reliability, Validity, Norms, Practicability.

11. **Classification of tests:** Administrative conditions (individual/group); Scoring (objective/subjective); Time limit (power/speed); Nature of items (verbal/nonverbal/performance/nonlanguage); Purpose (intelligence/aptitude/personality/neuropsychological/achievement); Standardization (standardized/teacher-made).

12. **Item difficulty formula:** p = R/N (full group); p = (R_u + R_l)/(N_u + N_l) (extreme groups). Optimal p for ability tests = 0.50. Lord's optimal for two-alternative items = 0.85 (uncorrected).

13. **Item discrimination formula (Net D):** V = R_u/N_u - R_l/N_l. V > 0.40 = good; 0.20-0.40 = poor; < 0.20 = negligible; negative = drop item.

14. **Kelley's 27% rule:** upper and lower 27% of examinees used for item analysis; maximizes critical ratio; total N = 370 for 100 in each extreme group.

15. **Correction for guessing formula:** S = R - W/(K-1). Does NOT change rank order if no items omitted. Two assumptions: all errors due to blind guessing; equal probability of each wrong option being chosen.

16. **ICC slope:** positive slope = positive discrimination; flat = no discrimination; negative = negative discrimination. Position on x-axis = difficulty (leftward rise = easy).

17. **IRT advantages:** sample-invariant measures; computerized adaptive testing (reduces length by 50%+); information from distractors; useful for specialized tests (screening, criterion-keyed).

18. **Essay vs. objective test distinctions:** Essay: writing + thinking; subjective scoring; bluffing; easy to write. Objective: reading + thinking; objective scoring; guessing; difficult to write.

19. **Distractor analysis:** good distractor chosen more by lower group (negative discrimination); nonfunctional distractor chosen by none; drop or rewrite.

20. **Ethical issues (5):** Human rights, Labelling, Invasion of privacy, Divided loyalties (Jackson & Messick, 1967), Responsibility of test constructors and users.
