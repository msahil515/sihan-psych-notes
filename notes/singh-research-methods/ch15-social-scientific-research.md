# Social Scientific Research

Chapter 15 provides a systematic account of the nature, assumptions, and stages of scientific research, the classification of research types (experimental vs. nonexperimental; quantitative vs. qualitative), the threats to internal and external validity, methods for controlling those threats, and the major nonexperimental research designs (field studies, ex post facto, survey). It is a foundational chapter for MCQ questions on definitions, researchers, and methodological distinctions.

## Meaning and Characteristics of Scientific Research

**Etymology:** The word "research" is composed of two syllables: "re" (again) and "search" (to examine carefully or probe). Together they denote a careful, systematic investigation in a field of knowledge for establishing facts or principles.

**Definition (descriptive):** Any scientific research is a systematic and objective attempt to provide answers to certain questions or problems, with the purpose of discovering and developing an organized body of knowledge.

**Formal definition:** Scientific research may be defined as the systematic and empirical analysis and recording of controlled observation, which may lead to the development of theories, concepts, generalizations, and principles, resulting in prediction and control of activities that have a cause-effect relationship.

**Kerlinger (1973):** "Scientific research is a systematic, controlled, empirical and critical investigation of hypothetical propositions about the presumed relationships among natural phenomena."

### Characteristics of Scientific Research (9)

1. **Problem-directed:** Research is always directed towards the solution of a problem; the researcher tries to answer a question or relate two or more variables.
2. **Empirical basis:** Research is based upon empirical or observable evidence; subjective and unverifiable principles are rejected.
3. **Precise observation and accurate description:** Reliable and valid instruments are selected; statistical measures are used for accurate description of the sample.
4. **Theory-building:** Research gives emphasis to the development of theories, principles, and generalizations helpful in accurate prediction; results are generalized from sample to population.
5. **Systematic, objective, logical procedures:** Bias is eliminated; objectivity is ensured in methods, data collection, and conclusions; logical examination of procedures is conducted.
6. **Patience, courage, and unbiased activity:** Researcher must not answer difficult questions intuitively; must think through problems carefully.
7. **Full command of the problem:** The researcher must know all relevant facts, review important literature, and be aware of sophisticated statistical methods.
8. **Replicability:** Designs, procedures, and results must be applicable so that any other person may duplicate the study; results must be communicable.
9. **Skill in report writing:** The researcher must present the report in unambiguous terms, define complex terminology, formulate a clear-cut design, and summarize the research objectively.

## Scientific Approach to the Study of Behaviour

Psychology is a science like physics, chemistry, or biology because it employs the scientific method. The **scientific method** consists of assumptions, attitudes, goals, and procedures for creating and answering questions about nature.

### The Assumptions of Science (3)

**1. Nature is lawful**
- The basic assumption underlying the scientific method is that nature operates in a lawful manner.
- If nature were random, it could never be fully understood.
- Example: Laws of gravity govern falling objects; laws govern the behaviour of living organisms.
- Implication for psychology: Studying the development of language in humans is, in fact, studying the laws of nature.

**2. Behaviour is deterministic**
- Psychologists assume that the behaviour of the organism is determined.
- **Doctrine of determinism:** Behaviour is solely influenced by natural causes; there is no free will in the sense that a controllable natural variable governs all behaviour.
- All behaviour has natural causes; laws of behaviour force persons to exhibit certain attitudes and behave in certain ways.
- Individual differences and variations exist, so persons in the same situation may behave differently.

**3. Nature is understandable**
- Although nature and behaviour seem complex, it is assumed that they can eventually be understood.
- Important implication: No statement can be taken on faith; scientific statements must logically and rationally fit within known facts.

**Conclusion:** Any behaviour, to be studied scientifically, must be assumed to be lawful, determined, and understandable.

### The Flaws in Scientific Research

Despite rules and objective criteria, scientific research does not guarantee complete elimination of bias and error. Flaws arise in two phases: (a) collecting data and (b) interpreting it.

#### Flaws in Scientific Evidence: Four Factors

1. **Nature of behaviour to be studied:** Some implicit behaviours (attitudes, beliefs, personality) cannot be studied in a completely objective way; no single yardstick exists; cognitive flaws may creep in.
2. **Technical limitations:** Lack of necessary technical capabilities may yield limited and potentially misleading information.
3. **Properties of the design of the study:** The research design may limit the study — focusing on only certain behaviours, types of subjects, measurement methods, and data interpretation.
4. **Limited perspective of a study:** A researcher may interpret results in an overly simplistic way, taking one approach and automatically excluding others.

## Validity in Research / Experimental Validity

**Tuckman (1978):** The extent to which the factors or variables manipulated in the study have a systematic effect upon the desired variable in an experiment is called **experimental validity**.

### Internal Validity

**Definition:** Internal validity indicates the extent to which an experiment is methodologically sound and its results are reliable. The aim of the researcher is to ensure that no extraneous variables were allowed to affect the dependent variable.

**Tuckman (1978):** "A study has internal validity if the independent variable is in fact responsible for whatever change is obtained rather than the result of other causes not systematically dealt with in the study."

**Extraneous variables** (also called relevant variables): uncontrolled and unmanipulated variables that may produce a significant effect upon the dependent variables.

### External Validity

**Definition:** External validity of a study refers to the degree to which the results can be generalized beyond the independent variable.

**Tuckman (1978):** "A study has external validity to the extent that the result obtained would apply to the real world, to other similar programmes and approaches."

**Key rule:** Internal validity is a prerequisite for external validity. If a research has little or no internal validity, its external validity is automatically affected.

---

### Threats to Internal Validity (Campbell et al.)

| # | Threat | Description |
|---|--------|-------------|
| 1 | **Maturation** | Maturational changes (becoming wiser, older) confused with changes produced by the IV; problematic in long experiments |
| 2 | **Unusual instrumentation** | Less reliable/valid tests introduce measurement errors; human observers may introduce errors due to fatigue, experience, or mood |
| 3 | **History** | Environmental events occurring at the time the IV is being tested confound the results (e.g., students measured for prejudice the day an enemy country attacks) |
| 4 | **Testing** | Effect of pre-testing upon post-test performance; subjects become more alert or more skilled due to pre-test exposure |
| 5 | **Selection (Selection Bias)** | Non-equivalent experimental and control groups; best controlled by **random assignment (randomization)** |
| 6 | **Experimental mortality** | Loss of subjects during experimentation; subjects who remain differ from those who drop out |
| 7 | **Statistical regression** | In pretest-post-test with extreme scorers: high scorers tend to score lower on post-test; low scorers tend to increase toward the mean; caused by chance factors affecting extreme scores more than average scores |
| 8 | **Diffusion of treatments (Contamination)** | Participants in different groups communicate, causing cross-contamination; prevented by isolating groups or confidentiality agreements |
| 9 | **Compensatory rivalry (John Henry Effect)** | Control group increases efforts to rival the experimental group when aware of the difference; artificially improves control group performance |
| 10 | **Resentful demoralization** | Control group members, denied treatment, become demoralized and perform worse |
| 11 | **Experimenter's expectation (Experimenter bias)** | Experimenter unconsciously communicates expectations to participants through tone of voice, cues, etc.; threatens construct/conceptual validity |

### Threats to External Validity

| Threat | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Population validity** | Subjects selected are not representative of the population to which results are to be generalized |
| **Setting representativeness** | Experimental condition conducted in a setting not representative of the conditions under which results are to be generalized |
| **Hawthorne Effect** | Tendency of people to behave differently when they know they are being observed or receiving special attention |

## Controlling Threats to Reliability and Validity in Research

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **(a) Randomization** | Most powerful technique; random assignment makes groups equivalent at the outset; controls both known and unknown extraneous variables; preferred method when possible |
| **(b) Matching** | Used when randomization is not possible; subjects matched on the extraneous variable then randomly assigned |
| **(c) Holding variables constant** | Extraneous variables kept constant for all subjects; eliminates the variable as source of variance but limits generalizability |
| **(d) Statistical control (ANCOVA)** | Analysis of covariance adjusts DV scores for differences among subjects on the covariate (the extraneous variable) |

### Additional Control Procedures

**Control of confounding variables:** Use elimination, balancing, and randomization in combination.

**Diffusion of treatment — prevention strategies:**
- Explain to subjects why they should not tell other potential subjects
- Complete the experiment within a brief time period
- Test subjects from different locations
- Disguise the treatments if possible

**Adoption of consistent procedures:**
- **Instructions:** Clear, standardized instructions specifying sequence of events, stimuli, and how to respond
- **Automation:** Use electronic timers, slide projectors, video equipment to present stimuli consistently
- **Pilot studies:** Miniature version of a final study to test procedures with a small number of subjects; evaluates clarity of instructions, task demands, time constraints, and equipment bugs
- **Group testing:** More efficient; all subjects receive same instructions; disadvantage: subjects may communicate or create noise
- **Checks on the manipulation:** After the study, directly measure the intended mediating variable to verify the manipulation worked as intended

## Phases or Stages in Research (8 Steps)

### Step 1: Identifying the Problem

**Problem:** An interrogative statement that brings into relationship two or more variables.
Example: "What is the relationship between academic ability and socio-economic status?"

**Purposes of Literature Review (4):**
1. Eliminates duplication of work
2. Conceptually and practically suggestive for further research
3. Gives ideas about variables found to be relevant in the related field
4. Helps in synthesizing what is already known and building upon it

**Sources of literature:** Journals, books, abstracts, indexes, periodicals.

### Step 2: Formulating a Hypothesis

**Hypothesis:** A tentative statement showing a relationship between variables under study; stated in the form of a declarative sentence.

Examples:
1. Persons from upper and middle SES have stronger academic status than persons from lower SES.
2. Reward facilitates learning.

**Characteristics of a good hypothesis:**
- Consistent with known facts and theories
- Testable (can be verified or validated)
- Stated in probabilistic terms
- Reasonable and expressed in the simplest possible words
- Must be formulated **before** data collection

### Step 3: Identifying, Manipulating, and Controlling Variables

| Variable | Definition |
|----------|------------|
| **Dependent variable (DV)** | Characteristic that changes as the experimenter changes the IV; the variable about which prediction is made |
| **Independent variable (IV)** | Characteristic manipulated or selected by the experimenter to find its relationship to some observed phenomenon |
| **Extraneous variable** | Uncontrolled variable that may affect the DV; not of interest to the experimenter; must be controlled as far as practicable |

### Step 4: Formulating a Research Design

**Research design:** The blueprint of procedures adopted for testing the relationship between the DV and IV. Selection depends on purpose of research, types of variables to be controlled/manipulated, and specific conditions.

### Step 5: Constructing Devices for Observation and Measurement

Tools: questionnaires, opinionnaires, interviews.

### Step 6: Summarizing Results

| Method | Advantage | Limitation |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Tabular method** | Facilitates use of statistical tests; handles complex data | Less visual |
| **Graphic method** | Quick understanding; visually clear | Difficult to display complex data |

Best practice: use both methods together.

### Step 7: Carrying out Statistical Analysis

- Two types of statistical tests: **parametric** and **nonparametric**.
- Two levels of significance: **0.05 level (5%)** and **0.01 level (1%)**.
- Either the null hypothesis is accepted or rejected at these levels.

### Step 8: Drawing Conclusions

- The investigator makes a statement about the research problem.
- Conclusions are generalized to the whole population.
- Predictions are made about related events in new situations.

### Key Definitions

**Fundamental/Pure research:** Basic and systematic process aimed at developing theory, generalizations, and principles; uses controlled situations; little concern for practical application.

**Applied research:** Application of theory or model developed through fundamental research; tests theories in actual situations.

**Action research:** Researcher has basic interest in immediate consequences and applications for a particular group (e.g., local classroom students); does not aim for universal principles.

**Experimental research (SR research):** One where the IV can be directly manipulated. Called "SR research" because the researcher manipulates a **stimulus (S)** to establish whether it produces a change in a **response (R)**.

**Nonexperimental research (RR research):** One where IVs cannot be manipulated; subjects cannot be randomly assigned. "RR research" — relative amounts of one **response (R)** are compared with responses on another **R** variable.

**Quantitative research:** Objectives, design, sample, and statistical analysis are predetermined; also known as **structured research**; more appropriate for determining the *extent* of a problem.

**Qualitative research:** Allows flexibility in all aspects of the process; also known as **unstructured research**; more appropriate for exploring the *nature* of a problem.

## Laboratory Experiments

**Definition (Festinger & Katz 1953):** "One in which the investigator creates a situation with the exact conditions he wants to have and in which he controls some and manipulates other variables."

**Two salient features:**
1. Experimenter creates a situation where all possible extraneous variables are controlled.
2. Variables are manipulated (IVs) and the effect upon the DV is examined.

**Three main purposes (Kerlinger 1986):**
1. Obtain clear, clean, and obvious data on DV and IV under pure, uncontaminated, controlled conditions.
2. Help in testing hypotheses and theories.
3. Help in building theoretical systems by refining theories and hypotheses.

**Common method of manipulation:** Differential instruction — subjects given different verbal instructions that automatically manipulate the variable.

### Strengths of Laboratory Experiments

1. Studies variables in a pure, controlled situation — highest **internal validity**.
2. **Replicable** — others can duplicate the design to verify or refute conclusions.
3. **High precision** in manipulation of independent variables.
4. Sufficient degree of **internal validity** due to maximum control.

### Weaknesses of Laboratory Experiments

1. **Lacks external validity** — generalizations to real life are made at some cost.
2. **Artificial/contrived situation** — criticism often comes from misunderstanding its purpose.
3. **Complex designs with multiple simultaneous variables** — strength of each variable is lowered.
4. **Some situations cannot be studied** — e.g., mass riots (Robinson 1976).
5. **Ethical limitations** — some experiments (e.g., coercive treatment of children) cannot be conducted.
6. **Costly and time-consuming** — expensive apparatus; longitudinal studies particularly problematic.

**Key distinction:** Laboratory experiments have high **internal validity** but poor **generalizability (external validity)**.

## Field Experiments

**Definition:** A controlled experiment conducted in a more or less realistic situation or field setting where the experimenter deliberately manipulates one or more independent variables in a natural setting.

**Three defining features:**
1. Carried out in a **realistic situation** (differs from laboratory's artificial setting).
2. Experimenter **deliberately and systematically manipulates** the independent variable.
3. **Less controlled** than laboratory experiment — controls the situation only to the extent permitted by the situation.

**Kerlinger (1973):** "Sometimes it is hard to label a particular study 'laboratory experiment' or 'field experiment'." The boundary between the two is often not clear.

**Experimental realism:** Extent to which the experimental task engages subjects psychologically, such that they become less concerned with **demand characteristics**.

### Strengths of Field Experiments

1. Deals with realistic life situations — more suited for studying social changes, social processes, and social effects.
2. Greater **face validity** — results easily generalized to the real world.
3. Greater **experimental realism** — subjects psychologically engaged; fewer demand characteristics.
4. Allows replication of laboratory studies in the real world.

### Weaknesses of Field Experiments

1. Contamination by uncontrolled environmental variables.
2. Difficult to manipulate independent variables due to non-cooperation of subjects.
3. Less precision/accuracy due to uncontrolled environmental variables.
4. Requires high social skills from the investigator.

## Field Studies (Survey Studies)

**Definition:** Any ex post facto scientific study that systematically discovers relations and similarities among variables in real life situations (school, factory, community, college, etc.).

**Key features:**
- Field study is **ex post facto** — the researcher studies an effect that has already occurred or is not under the researcher's control.
- **No independent variables are manipulated**.

**Distinction from survey studies:**
- **Survey:** Emphasis on selecting a representative sample to infer about a population.
- **Field study:** Emphasis on studying properties of the group; group studied is small; provides a better picture of real interactions.

### Types of Field Studies (Katz 1953)

**(a) Exploratory field studies:**
- Discovers significant variables in the field situation.
- Does **not** formulate hypotheses beforehand.
- Cannot provide concrete proof of existing relationships.
- Seeks what; does not seek to predict.
- Example: correlating factory productivity with age, sex, pay, period of work without prior hypothesis.

**(b) Hypothesis-testing field studies:**
- Investigator formulates hypotheses and proceeds to test them.
- Provides concrete evidence for the relationship; aims at predicting relations.
- **More popular** than exploratory field studies.
- Example: hypothesizing "poor pay for prolonged hours leads to lower productivity" and then testing it.

### Advantages of Field Studies

1. Provides opportunities for **direct observation** of social interaction and relationships.
2. Carried out in realistic situations — avoids the artificial nature of laboratory experiments.
3. **Continued observation** of a group over time is possible; rare phenomena occur with sufficient frequency (Katz 1953).
4. Allows recording of "on-going perceptions and interdependent reactions" — provides a total picture of social structure.

### Weaknesses of Field Studies

1. Large samples make precise measurement difficult; adversely affects internal and external validity.
2. Large number of uncontrolled variables lowers internal validity.
3. Lack of practicality — longer time, high cost, large samples.

## Ex Post Facto Research

**Literal meaning:** "After the fact" — the researcher has conducted the study after the events have occurred.

**Definition:** "Ex post facto research is a systematic empirical inquiry in which the investigator does not have direct control over independent variables because their manifestations have already occurred or because they are inherently not manipulable. Inferences about relations among variables are made, without direct intervention, from concomitant variation of independent and dependent variables."

**Structure:**
- Effect becomes the **dependent variable**
- Probable cause becomes the **independent variable**

**Also known as:** Causal-comparative research; when correlational analyses are used, it is referred to as **correlational research** (Best & Kahn 1986).

**Example 1:** Lung cancer (DV) with cigarette smoking and genetic cough (IVs). The IVs have already occurred; investigator cannot control them.

**Example 2:** Academic achievement (DV) with SES, motivation, intelligence (IVs). Investigator has no direct control.

### Distinctions: Experimental vs. Ex Post Facto Research

| Dimension | Experimental Research | Ex Post Facto Research |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Control over IV | Direct control; can manipulate | No direct control; IVs have already occurred or are inherently not manipulable |
| Random assignment | Possible | Not possible |
| Certainty of causal statement | Higher | Lower |
| Nature of IVs | Can include manipulable variables | Often includes non-manipulable variables (home background, SES, intelligence, aptitude, parental interest) |

### Strengths of Ex Post Facto Research

1. Very important in behavioural research where many variables are either not manipulable or not amenable to experimental enquiry.
2. More useful than experimental research when one wants to investigate causes on the basis of effects.

### Weaknesses of Ex Post Facto Research

1. Cannot manipulate independent variables; exact nature of the IV-DV relationship becomes dubious.
2. Cannot exercise control over IVs through randomization; cannot assign subjects or treatments to groups randomly.
3. Risk of **post hoc fallacy:** Concluding that because two factors co-occur, one causes the other.

**Post hoc fallacy example:** Smoking and cancer are closely related, so one may conclude smoking causes cancer. But glandular imbalance may be the actual cause — imbalance induces tension, smoking is a tension-releasing mechanism, so heavy smokers may develop cancer from glandular imbalance, not from smoking itself.

## Survey Research

**Historical context:** Survey technique largely developed in the 18th century.

**Father of modern social survey:** **Booth** (Moser & Kalton 1971) — provided systematic literature in the second part of the 19th century.

Survey research as a special branch of social science research is a technique developed in the **20th century**.

**Basic principle:** If one wants to find out what people think or feel, one should ask them.

**Definition:** A technique whereby the researcher studies the whole population with respect to certain sociological or psychological variables.

**Sample survey:** When a researcher takes a sample from the population, the survey is termed a sample survey.

**Primary aim:** To draw inferences regarding the characteristics of the whole population.

### Three Important Factors in Survey Research

1. **Characteristics, attitudes, and behaviours of individuals/group:** Direct contact must be established.
2. **Willingness and cooperation of respondents:** The researcher must motivate respondents.
3. **Skill of survey personnel:** Must have manipulative skill, research insight, and social intelligence.

### Types of Survey Research (by data collection method)

**(1) Personal Interview (Survey Interview)**

A direct conversation between interviewer and respondent to elicit information. Interviewer neither persuades nor educates; simply elicits information.

**Three conditions for a successful personal interview (Cannell & Kahn, in Lindsey & Aronson 1968):**

| Condition | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Accessibility** | Information sought must be expressible; content must not be embarrassing to the respondent |
| **Recognition** | Both parties understand what information is required and in what terms it should be expressed |
| **Motivation** | Respondent must be motivated to give accurate answers; interviewer must build motivating factors |

**(2) Mail Questionnaire**
- One of the most common types of survey methods in educational and sociological research.
- Questionnaire with several items designed to elicit required information is sent/mailed to respondents.

**(3) Telephone Survey**
- Data collected via telephone conversations.

**(4) Panel Technique**
- Data collected from the same group at multiple time points.

## Ethical Problems in Research

Key ethical principles for researchers:
- Act according to standard procedures and not harm others.
- Do not conduct research that could be a mental or physical threat to participants.
- Respect the rights and interests of all respondents/subjects.
- Scientists must not commit fraudulent acts (publishing research known to be incorrect).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Kerlinger's (1973) definition** of scientific research: systematic, controlled, empirical, and critical investigation of hypothetical propositions about presumed relationships among natural phenomena.

2. **Three assumptions of science:** (1) Nature is lawful; (2) Behaviour is deterministic; (3) Nature is understandable.

3. **Six attitudes of scientists:** Open-minded, tolerant of uncertainty, fallible, cautious, skeptical, ethical. Cautious scientists never use the terms "proof" or "proven."

4. **Tuckman (1978) on internal validity:** "A study has internal validity if the independent variable is in fact responsible for whatever change is obtained rather than the result of other causes not systematically dealt with in the study."

5. **Internal validity is a prerequisite for external validity** — if a study has low internal validity, its external validity is automatically affected.

6. **John Henry effect / Compensatory rivalry:** Control group increases efforts to match the experimental group; artificially improves control group performance.

7. **Hawthorne effect:** Subjects behave differently because they know they are being observed.

8. **Statistical regression:** Extreme scorers tend toward the mean on retest; particularly threatens internal validity when groups are selected based on extreme scores.

9. **Randomization** is the most powerful technique for controlling threats to internal validity; controls both known and unknown extraneous variables.

10. **Pilot study:** Miniature version of a final study to test procedures; evaluates clarity of instructions, task demands, time constraints, and equipment bugs.

11. **Bird & Kahn (1993):** Three types of educational research — historical, descriptive, experimental.

12. **Ex post facto research (Best & Kahn 1986):** Also called causal-comparative research; the phrase means "after the fact"; investigator has no direct control over IVs; risk of post hoc fallacy.

13. **Father of modern social survey:** Booth (cited by Moser & Kalton 1971); survey research as a special technique developed in the 20th century.

14. **Three conditions for a successful personal interview (Cannell & Kahn):** Accessibility, recognition, motivation.

15. **Laboratory experiment definition (Festinger & Katz 1953):** "One in which the investigator creates a situation with the exact conditions he wants to have and in which he controls some and manipulates other variables." Primary strength: internal validity. Primary weakness: external validity/generalizability.

16. **SR research vs. RR research:** Experimental = Stimulus-Response (researcher manipulates S); Nonexperimental = Response-Response (compares relative amounts of one R with another R).

17. **Katz (1953):** Two types of field studies — exploratory (discovers variables, no prior hypotheses) and hypothesis-testing (formulates and tests specific hypotheses); hypothesis-testing is more popular.

18. **Diffusion of treatment (contamination):** Subjects in different groups communicate; threatens internal validity; prevented by isolation, time compression, different locations, or disguising treatments.

19. **Four factors determining flaws in scientific evidence:** (1) Nature of behaviour to be studied; (2) Technical limitations; (3) Properties of the design; (4) Limited perspective.

20. **Two levels of significance:** 0.05 level (5%) and 0.01 level (1%).
