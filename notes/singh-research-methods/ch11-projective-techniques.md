# Projective Techniques

Projective techniques are indirect, unstructured methods of personality assessment in which
individuals reveal latent, unconscious aspects of their personality by responding to ambiguous
stimuli. They originated in clinical settings, are grounded in the **projective hypothesis**, and
contrast sharply with direct self-report inventories by employing a global rather than
trait-specific approach to personality.

---

## Meaning and Types of Projective Techniques

**Projective techniques** are indirect measures of personality that present an unstructured
situation to which the individual responds according to his own feelings, needs, emotions, and
motives — most of which are latent and unconscious — without being aware of what he is
revealing.

### Historical Origins

- **14th century**: Leonardo da Vinci reportedly selected pupils on the basis of their attempt
  to find shapes and patterns in ambiguous form (Polyson, 1972). This is the earliest documented
  use of projective-type assessment.
- **1879**: **Francis Galton** developed the first formal projective technique — the
  **word association test**. This is considered the first projective technique in modern
  psychometrics.
- **1910**: **Kent and Rosanoff** adapted Galton's word association procedure (without timing
  constraints).
- **Carl Jung** frequently used the word association test in therapy.
- **1939**: **Lawrence Frank** introduced the term **"projective method"** to describe a
  category of tests for studying personality using unstructured stimuli. This is the foundational
  definitional moment for the field.

### Core Definition

A projective test presents the individual with an **unstructured situation** — a situation whose
meaning and interpretation vary from individual to individual, that has no right or wrong answers,
and is capable of evoking **fantasy material** from the testees (Lindzey, 1961).

The most important assumption is the **projective hypothesis**: while responding to an
unstructured situation, an individual projects his own feelings, needs, emotions, and motives —
mostly latent and unconscious — without being aware of doing so. Because the individual is
unaware of these revelations, he does not resort to defensive reactions.

### Projective Tests vs. Other Personality Assessment Methods

| Feature | Self-Report Inventories | Projective Tests | Observational Methods |
|---|---|---|---|
| Situation | Structured, direct | Unstructured, indirect | Direct observation |
| Approach | Trait-specific | Global (whole personality) | Requires observer knowledge |
| Awareness | Individual aware of what is measured | Individual unaware | Observer must be unbiased |
| Content measured | Traits individually | Personality as a whole | Observable behavior |

## Classification of Projective Techniques

Three major classification systems are presented, plus one practical three-way scheme.

---

### Frank's Classification (1939)

**Frank** (1939) proposed the earliest classification based on the **nature of response evoked**
by the test materials. He identified five categories:

#### 1. Constitutive

The examinee **constitutes or frames structures** upon materials that are yet unstructured. The
emphasis is on **free expression** of the examinee's inclination.

- Examples: finger-painting, drawing-completion.
- Per Zubin, Eron and Schumer (1965): the **Rorschach test** also belongs here, as the examinee
  imposes his own structure upon unstructured inkblots.

#### 2. Constructive

The examinee is required to **construct a specific task** in the direction specified by the
examiner. Unlike constitutive, this does NOT allow free expression.

- Example: drawing a figure of a human being or a house.
- Key distinction from constitutive: constitutive = free expression; constructive = directed task.

#### 3. Interpretative

The examinee adds a **completion pertaining to the situation** — interprets the presented
material.

- Examples: **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)**, word association test.

#### 4. Refractive

The examinee is given an opportunity to express personality through **painting, drawing,
handwriting**, etc.

- Frank's best example: **graphology** (handwriting analysis).

#### 5. Cathartic

The examinee is given an opportunity through **manipulative tasks** for the **release of
conflict, wishes**, etc.

- Best example: **play techniques**.

#### Limitations of Frank's Classification

- The categories **overlap extensively**: the same test can fall under more than one category
  (e.g., a figure-drawing test may fit constitutive, constructive, or refractive).
- Not widely accepted.

---

### Lindzey's Classification (1959)

**Lindzey** (1959) provided the more convincing and widely accepted classification, based on the
**responses of the examinees**. Five categories:

#### 1. Association Techniques

The examinee responds with **associations evoked** in his mind after seeing or hearing stimulus
materials. No moulding of associations occurs.

- Examples: **Rorschach test**, **Holtzman Inkblot test**, **word-association test**.
- In the word-association test, reaction time (time between stimulus word and response word) is
  analyzed for personality.

#### 2. Construction Techniques

The examinee **constructs a story** after seeing stimulus materials (usually a picture). The
examinee's themes and mode of responding are considered relevant.

- Examples: **TAT** (Thematic Apperception Test), **CAT** (Children's Apperception Test),
  **Blacky Pictures**, **Object Relations Technique**, **Pickford Projective Picture (PPP)**.

#### 3. Completion Techniques

The examinee is presented with **incomplete sentences** and completes them any way he desires.

- Examples of items:
  - "I feel tense ......"
  - "My ambition in life is ......"
  - "I often get nervous ......"
- Examples of tests: **Madeleine Thomas Completion Stories test**, **Rotter's Sentence
  Completion Test**, **Rosenzweig Picture-Frustration Study** (Rosenzweig, 1949).
- Also: **Rosenzweig (Mills, 1953)** mentioned as a completion technique.
- Limitation: lack a uniform and standard mode of analysis.

#### 4. Expressive Techniques

The examinee **expresses personality through manipulative tasks** involving interaction with
given materials. Critical feature: attention is paid to the **process** (how the examinee
manipulates materials), NOT to the **end product**.

- Examples: play, drawing, role-playing, painting, finger-painting.
- Best example: **Toy-World Test**.
- Contrast with construction techniques: in construction, emphasis is on the end product (content
  or theme of story); in expressive, emphasis is on the process of handling materials.

#### 5. Choice Techniques (also: Ordering Techniques)

Not projective in the strict sense; regarded as a step toward **objectifying** projective
techniques (Kerlinger, 1973). The examinee chooses or ranks pictures/items.

- Example: **Szondi test** — examinees rank sets of pictures on a **like-dislike dimension**.

---

### Best's (Bird's) Classification (1978)

**Best** (1978) proposed a **four-way classification**:

#### 1. Association

Stimulus materials (pictures, inkblots, words, cartoons) are presented; examinee tells what he
sees, thinks, and feels.

- Examples: Rorschach, Holtzman, word-association test.

#### 2. Completion

Incomplete sentences are presented; examinee completes them.

- Example: sentence-completion test.
- Most useful for: anxieties, guilt, hostility, aggression, attitudes toward sexuality, paranoid
  tendencies.
- Sample items for paranoid tendencies:
  - "Somebody is always trying ......."
  - "I worry about ......."
  - "I think that my body ......."

#### 3. Role-Playing

The examinee acts out a specific role in a group of two or more individuals. Reveals underlying
motives, attitudes, fantasies, fears. Most suitable for: hostility, sympathy, frustration,
dominance, authoritarianism.

#### 4. Creative or Constructive

Includes tasks like playing with dolls/toys, painting, finger-painting, drawing figures, and
making imaginative stories about ambiguous situations (TAT is included here by Bird).

Basis for inference: choice of colours, words, forms, order of sentences.

#### Comparison: Lindzey vs. Best

Lindzey's classification is considered more appropriate because:
- Bird's role-playing and constructive categories are covered under Lindzey's single **expressive
  techniques** category.
- Lindzey's system is broader and more comprehensive with less overlap.

---

### Three-Broad-Category Classification

A practical scheme to reduce confusion. All projective techniques fall into one of three types:

#### 1. Pictorial

Unstructured situation consists of vague or ambiguous pictures; response may be a few words or
a series of sentences.

- Examples: Rorschach, Holtzman Inkblot test, TAT, CAT, Rosenzweig Picture-Frustration test.

#### 2. Verbal

Stimulus materials are presented in verbal form (words or incomplete sentences); examinee
responds with the first word that comes to mind or completes the sentences.

- Examples: word association test, sentence-completion test.

#### 3. Expressive

Examinee expresses personality through manipulative and objective tasks; the manner of doing
the task is the basis for appraisal.

- Examples: graphology, painting, drawing, playing with dolls, role-playing.

---

## Common Characteristics of Projective Tests

Despite their variety, all projective tests share the following features:

1. **Projective hypothesis**: a person will project something significant about himself onto a
   vague or ambiguous (unstructured) stimulus.
2. **Disguised procedures**: the examinee is not fully aware of the purpose for which the test
   is being employed.
3. **Relatively unstructured**: the tasks generate an inexhaustible variety of possible
   responses.
4. **General, vague instructions**: common instructions are "Tell me what you see," "There is no
   right or wrong answer," "What might this be?"
5. **Global approach**: the psychologist constructs a comprehensive description of the examinee's
   psychological functioning rather than identifying a single trait.
6. **Idiographic use in clinical settings**: used to develop a model of personality functioning
   of an individual.
7. **Assessment of unconscious elements**: regarded by supporters as the only effective means
   of evaluating unconscious personality elements.
8. **Psychoanalytic influence**: interpretation of responses is strongly influenced by
   psychoanalytic thinking; **psychic determinism** and the importance of unconscious processes
   are foundational.

---

## Pictorial Techniques

Pictorial techniques present vague or ambiguous pictures as the unstructured stimulus. The
examinee's response may be a few words or a series of sentences.

Primary examples:
- **Rorschach Inkblot Test**
- **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)**
- **Holtzman Inkblot Test**

## The Rorschach Test

The most popular projective technique is the **Rorschach Inkblot Test**, developed by
**Hermann Rorschach**, a Swiss psychiatrist, in **1921**, for making a diagnostic investigation
of personality as a whole.

Key features:
- Measures both **intellectual and non-intellectual traits** of personality.
- Rorschach tested a large number of inkblots; only the **10 inkblots** that most differentiated
  between various psychiatric syndromes were retained.
- Consists of **10 cards**, each with a **bilaterally symmetrical** printed inkblot.

### Card Composition

| Cards | Colours |
|---|---|
| I, IV, V, VI, VII | Shades of black and gray only |
| II, III | Black/gray + bright red patches |
| VIII, IX, X | Several pastel shades |

### Administration of the Rorschach Test

The administration is divided into three stages:

#### Stage 1: Performance Proper

The first and primary stage of administration.

Procedure:
1. Examiner establishes rapport with the examinee.
2. First card is handed over with top side up.
3. Basic instruction (Rorschach): **"What might this be?"**
4. Modified instruction (Klopfer & Kelley, 1942): "People see all sorts of things in these
   inkblots; now tell me what you see, what it might be for you, what it makes you think of."
5. All 10 cards are presented in consecutive order; examiner keeps unused cards out of reach.

The examiner records:

| What is recorded | Symbol/Note |
|---|---|
| **Reaction time** (time between card presentation and first scorable response) | Symbol per Semenoff (1976) |
| **Card position** when response is given | ∧ (upright), V (inverted), < (top at left), > (top at right), ⊗ (rotating) |
| **Responses verbatim** | Full transcription |
| **Total time per card** (free association period) | Symbol T per Semenoff (1976) |

Important norms:
- If examinee rejects card, examiner should encourage.
- **Beck (1944) and Hertz (1969)**: minimum of **2 minutes** before rejection is justified.
- **Beck (1945)**: maximum time per card = **10 minutes**.
- **Piotrowski (1957)**: reasonable maximum = **5 minutes**.
- Total number of responses (**R**): normal adults = **15 to 30**.
- R < 10: may indicate rejection, defensiveness, intellectual limitations, depression, or organicity.
- R = 90 or 100: indicates schizophrenic tendencies or obsessional neurosis.

Nondirective answers to examinee questions (examples):
- "How long should I keep the card?" → "As long as you like."
- "Should I turn the card?" → "It depends upon you."
- "Should I report more than one thing?" → "Most people usually report more than one thing."

#### Stage 2: Inquiry

Conducted **after** responses to all ten cards have been obtained during performance proper.
(Rapaport, Gill & Schafer, 1946 advocated conducting inquiry after each card, but most
Rorschachers reject this view.)

Purpose (twofold):
1. Obtain further information about the examinee's responses.
2. Help the examinee clarify responses by elaborating on what had already been said.

The examiner uses a **location sheet** to record which part of the blot was used in each
response.

Principles of good inquiry:
1. Frame appropriate questions only when needed (clear-cut responses need no questions).
2. Questions must be nondirective.
3. Questions must be brief and precise.

#### Stage 3: Testing-of-the-Limits

The third and **final stage** — not needed for all examinees.

Used when: the examinee gives responses uncommon to most protocols (e.g., gives only whole
responses because he believes whole responses are better than part responses). The examiner
encourages the examinee to give other types of responses, testing the limits of their
responsiveness.

### Scoring the Rorschach

Scoring = classification of responses into categories so that the protocol represents the
examinee's personality as a whole.

Historical note: Rorschach died before completing his scoring system. Five American
psychologists independently developed overlapping scoring systems:

1. **S Beck**
2. **M Hertz**
3. **B Klopfer**
4. **Z Piotrowski**
5. **D Rapaport**

**Comprehensive Scoring System (CSS)** was developed by **Exner and colleagues** (Exner, 1993;
Exner & Winer, 1995) by synthesizing the earlier approaches.

The CSS recognizes **four main categories** of response classification:
1. Location
2. Determinants
3. Content
4. Popular and Original Responses

---

### Scoring Category 1: Location

Location = which **part of the blot** produced the response.

#### W Response (Whole Response)

- Scored when the **entire blot** instigates the response.
- Criterion: either-or — whole blot used or not.
- General interpretation: indicates an overall view of the entire situation; a good response.

Subtypes of W:
- **Cut-off W** (symbol: W): response based on whole blot excluding minor details (suggested by
  Klopfer & Kelley, 1942). Beck objected, calling it unreliable.
- **Confabulatory Whole (DW or Ddw)**: examinee attends to only a detail of the blot but
  generalizes from that detail to the entire blot (Exner, 1974). When usual detail is attended to
  = DW; unusual detail = Ddw. Indicates **perceptual-cognitive impairment**.

#### D Response (Common/Usual Detail)

- Scored when response is based on a **commonly or frequently** identified area of the blot.
- Definition (Rapaport, Gill & Schafer, 1946): area "conspicuous by its size, its location, and
  the frequency of the response it draws."
- Low D percentage usually indicates maladjustment.
- Klopfer et al. (1954): capital D = large usual area; small d = small usual area.

#### Dd Response (Unusual/Uncommon Detail)

- Scored when response is based on an **infrequently or rarely** identified area.
- Exner (1974): "one given to a blot area which is not used frequently."
- Logically: Dd is scored when the response is neither W nor D.

Klopfer & Kelley (1942) subcategories of Dd (all later collapsed by Exner into a single Dd):
- **dd**: tiny areas evoke response.
- **de**: edge or contour instigates response.
- **di**: inside areas evoke response.
- **dr**: rare detail — highly idiosyncratic, does not fit other categories.

Exner's critique of Klopfer's subdivision (three grounds):
1. Inter-scorer disagreement on specific Dd types.
2. Limited research base for such fine differentiation.
3. Very low frequency of any single Dd subcategory. Exner recommends: all types scored simply as Dd.

#### S Response (White Space)

- Scored when the **white space area** is used as the basis of response.
- Never scored alone; always used in conjunction with W, D, or Dd (per Exner, 1974).
- Rorschach regarded S as a subtype of Dd; Beck as a subclass of D or Dd (symbols DS or DdS).
- Hertz recommends separate S scoring: capital S = common white space; small s = rare white space.
- When scored in combination: WS, DS, or DdS.

#### DdD Response (Confabulated Detail)

- Scored similarly to DW and DdW.
- Definition (Exner, 1974): "subject interprets an unusual area and then extends the percept from
  that interpretation to a common detail area."
- Criterion: usual detail interpreted secondarily, unusual detail is primary.
- Occurs less frequently than DW/DdW in normal protocol.

#### Normal Adult Averages (Nunnally, 1970)

| Response Type | Average Count |
|---|---|
| W | 6 |
| D | 20 |
| Dd | 4 |

### Scoring Category 2: Determinants

Determinants = **features of the blot** that produced a particular response. The most complex
scoring category.

**Original Rorschach symbols (1921)**: F (form), M (human movement), FC (form-colour),
CF (colour-form), C (pure colour). A sixth symbol — parenthesized (C) — for shading
(**chiaroscuro**) was added posthumously (1921). Note: the original cards contained no shading
variation; shading appeared due to a **printing error** (Ellenberger, 1974), which Rorschach
quickly incorporated.

**Exner's Comprehensive System (1974)**: 9 determinant categories, 24 symbols total.

#### (a) Form Determinant (F)

- Scored when the response occurred purely because of the **form or shape** of the blot.
- F+ = clear, good form. F- = vague form.
- Precaution: when M is scored, F is NOT also scored (M automatically assumes form).
- Spontaneity rule: M (or any determinant) is NOT scored if the response was provoked by the
  examiner's inquiry question rather than volunteered.

#### (b) Movement Determinants (M, FM, m)

Three types of movement, each with its own symbol:

**M (Human Movement)**
- Proposed by Rorschach; reserved for **human movement** responses.
- Rorschach's flexion-extension types:
  - **Flexor**: movement indicating pulling inside the centre axis.
  - **Extensor**: movement indicating pulling away from the centre axis.
- Beck et al. (1961) added a third type: **static movement**.
- Rapaport, Gill & Schafer (1946): M restricted to responses containing human figures (complete
  or incomplete).
- Exner (1974): divides M into active (running, walking, jumping, crying = Mᵃ) and passive
  (sleeping, talking, waiting = Mᵖ).
- Special case: if content is animal but movement is exclusively human, score as M
  (e.g., "Two butterflies talking with each other," "Two pigs playing football").

**FM (Animal Movement)**
- Originally suggested by **Klopfer (1930)**; used by Piotrowski and Hertz.
- Rorschach himself did not attach much significance to animal movement.
- Examples: "Two bears having a tug of war," "Two cats fighting," "The rat is jumping."
- Also divided into active (FMᵃ) and passive (FMᵖ) per Exner.
- Only Piotrowski, Hertz, and Klopfer use this symbol.

**m (Inanimate/Inorganic Movement)**
- Originally suggested by **Piotrowski (1936)**; incorporated by Klopfer and Hertz.
- Represents inanimate or manmade objects producing movement.
- Klopfer and Hertz divided m further: Fm (form dominant), mF (movement dominant), m (pure).
  This subcategorization was not popular (overlapped with M categories).
- Piotrowski's simpler system prevailed: all inanimate movement = m.
- Active: mᵃ (e.g., "Aeroplane flying speedily," "Top spinning fast").
- Passive: mᵖ (e.g., "Ship moving slowly," "Train crossing bridge at slow speed").

#### (c) Chromatic Colour Determinants (C, CF, FC, Cn)

Applied to 5 chromatic cards (Cards II, III, VIII, IX, X).

**C (Pure Colour Response)**
- Response wholly based on **colour alone**; form is irrelevant.
- Examples: blood, fire, water, red, blue.
- Interpretation: indicates **lack of control over emotional responses**.

**CF (Colour-Form Response)**
- Colour = primary determinant; form is vague, undifferentiated, and secondary.
- Examples: fire, meat (where colour is emphasized first).
- Interpretation: comparatively little control over emotional impulses.

**FC (Form-Colour Response)**
- Form = primary determinant; colour is secondary (for elaboration/classification).
- Examples: lungs, heart, a red dog, a red shirt.
- Interpretation: indicates **control over emotional impulses** (controlled use of colour).

**Cn (Colour Naming)**
- Examinee simply **names the colour** without further elaboration.
- Originally scored as CC by Rorschach; symbol **Cn introduced by Piotrowski (1936)**.
- Examples: "Here it is pink," "This is a red colour," "Greenish."
- Exclamations like "Look at the colours!" are NOT scored as Cn.

#### (d) Achromatic Colour Determinants (C', C'F, FC')

Black, white, and gray = achromatic colours.

- Rorschach never suggested a separate scoring for achromatic colours.
- **Klopfer (1938)** introduced separate achromatic scoring based on Binder's studies (1932).
- Rapaport adopted the same symbols and criteria; Hertz and Piotrowski modified the symbols.

**C' (Pure Achromatic Colour Response)**
- Based entirely on achromatic colour; no form reference. Very rare.
- Examples: coal, mud, soil, snow.

**C'F (Achromatic Colour-Form Response)**
- Achromatic colour = primary; form is vague and secondary.
- Examples: black cloud, white water, gray sky.

**FC' (Form-Achromatic Colour Response)**
- Form = primary; achromatic colour is secondary (for elaboration/classification).
- Examples: black dog, white horse, gray cow.

#### (e) Texture (Shading) Determinants (T, TF, FT)

- Scored when light-dark (shading) features represent **tactual stimuli**.
- Common tactual features: cold, hot, rough, hard, soft, smooth, sticky, greasy, furry, silky.
- **Klopfer** was the first to include a separate symbol for texture responses (symbol: (C)).
- **Beck (1944)** modified Klopfer's symbol to **T**.
- **Piotrowski (1957)** does not provide a separate scoring for texture.

**T (Pure Texture Response)**
- Shading features = tactual/textural; no form involvement.
- Examples: Silk, Ice, Hair, Flesh, Wood (if shading is involved). Very uncommon.

**TF (Texture-Form Response)**
- Shading perceived as texture = primary; form = secondary.
- Examples: "A soft piece of ice," "A hard metal," "A rough skin," "Rough sandpaper."

**FT (Form-Texture Response)**
- Form = primary (distinct and clear); shading as texture = secondary.
- Examples: "Fur coat," "A glass made of hard metal," "Smooth chair."
- Research: **three-fourths of all texture responses are FT** (Exner, 1974); FT is most frequent
  across all Rorschach cards except IV and VI.

#### (f) Vista (Shading-Dimensionality) Determinants (V, VF, FV)

- Scored when shading features are perceived as representing **depth and/or dimensionality**.
- **Klopfer (1942)** first provided a separate symbol (FK) for these, but FK was not clearly
  differentiated from KF and K responses.
- **Beck** first used the more distinguishing symbols V, VF, and FV.
- **Exner (1974)** does NOT include reflections under vista; gives reflections a separate category.

**V (Pure Vista Response)**
- Shading = depth/dimensionality; no form. Very rare.
- Examples: "Deepness," "Height" (if shading is involved).

**VF (Vista-Form Response)**
- Shading as depth = primary; form = secondary.
- Examples: "Mountain peak," "Depth of a lake," "Aerial view of the city."

**FV (Form-Vista Response)**
- Form = primary (distinct); shading as depth = secondary.
- Examples: "A deep well," "A tall tree," "A woman behind a curtain."
- Of the three vista responses, **FV occurs in greatest frequency**.
- Beck and Klopfer: scored reflections (e.g., "two women in a mirror") as FV or FK respectively.

#### (g) General-Diffuse Shading Determinants (Y, YF, FY)

- Includes all shading responses that are neither texture nor vista.
- This is the response category that Rorschach originally symbolized as parenthesized (C).
- Used in a more general and nonspecific sense than texture or vista.

**Y (Pure Shading/General-Diffuse Response)**
- Exclusively based on shading; content has no form; shading is neither texture nor vista.
- Examples: Darkness, Ink, Smoke, Fog.

**YF (Shading-Form Response)**
- Shading = primary; form is secondary and vague.
- Examples: "Some sort of X-ray," "Dark clouds."

**FY (Form-Shading Response)**
- Form = primary; light-dark features = secondary.
- Examples: "Dark pen," "Dirty shirt," "Dark ship."

#### (h) Form-Dimensional Response (FD)

- **New category introduced by Exner (1974)**.
- Differs from FV: in FV, dimensionality is based on shading (light-dark); in FD, dimensionality
  is based on form interpreted by size of the blot area.
- Definition (Exner, 1974): "responses which include perspective or dimensionality based
  exclusively on form, interpreted by size or in relation to other blot areas."
- Examples: "A bird on top of a tree," "A woman lying down," "A statue on top of a hill."

#### (i) Reflection Responses (fr, Fr) and Pair Responses (2)

Provided as a separate category by **Exner (1974)**; based on symmetry of the blot.

**fr (Reflection-Form Response)**
- Symmetry = primary; form = secondary and vague.
- Examples: "Sky reflected in a pond," "A reflection of something in a lake."
- Very low frequency.

**Fr (Form-Reflection Response)**
- Form = primary and clear; content perceived as reflected due to symmetry.
- Examples: "A girl in a mirror," "Own face reflected in water."

**Note on Beck/Klopfer**: Beck would score all reflections as FV; Klopfer would score them as FK.

**(2) Pair Response**
- Based on symmetry of the blot evoking the perception of **"twoness" or a pair**.
- Scored as parenthesized (2) by Exner (1974).
- Examples: "A pair of flags," "A pair of shoes," "Two little girls, one on each side."
- Caution: (2) is NEVER used when a pair is perceived as reflected.

### Scoring Category 4: Popular and Original Responses

#### Popular Responses (P)

**Popular responses** are those that occur with high frequency across Rorschach protocols.

**Rorschach's original criterion**: a response should occur at least **once in every 3 protocols**
to be scored as P.

Other criteria:
- **Exner (1974)**: P = occurs at least once in every **3-5 protocols**.
- **Piotrowski (1952)**: P = occurs at least once in every **6 protocols** (same as Rorschach's
  original criterion).

Interpretation: P indicates the ability for **conventional perception**.

**Popular responses by card:**

| Card | Popular Responses |
|---|---|
| I | Bat or butterfly (W); human figure (d) |
| II | Human figures (W); butterfly (d); animal heads (dogs or bears) (d); crab (d) |
| III | Two human figures or single human figures, dolls (W); butterfly (D); fish (d) |
| IV | Animal skin or human figure covered in animal skin (d); shoe or boot (d) |
| V | Moth, vulture, eagle, bat, butterfly (W); rabbit (D); leg (d) |
| VI | Animal skin (W) |
| VII | Head or face of a woman or a child (W or D) |
| VIII | Animal figures — wolf, fox, coyote, dog, bear (D); tree, bush (D) |
| IX | Head or face of a male (D); camel's head (D); tree (D) |
| X | Many-legged creatures — crab, lobster, spider, rabbit's head (D); dogs (D) |

#### Original Responses (O)

**Original responses** are rare and creative responses.

**Rorschach's criterion**: a response occurring **no more than once in 100 protocols** = O.

Subtypes:
- **O+**: original response with clear, distinct form.
- **O-**: original response with vague, indistinct form.

Cautions:
- **Piotrowski (1957)**: scoring O is highly subjective; only trained Rorschachers should attempt
  it.
- **Rapaport et al. (1946)**: scoring O at some point is a difficult task for trained Rorschachers
  only.
- **Klopfer & Kelley (1942)**: scoring O is "a hopeless enterprise" because the number of such
  responses is unlimited.

## Interpretation of the Rorschach Protocol

Rorschach data interpretation requires considerable training, skill, and experience. The method
used throughout this text is **psychogram analysis** (also called document analysis).

### Total Number of Responses (R)

| Authority | Normal Range for R |
|---|---|
| Rorschach | 15 to 30 |
| Klopfer | 20 to 45 |
| Exner (1974) | 17 to 27 |

| R Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| R ≤ 10 | Rejection; may indicate defensiveness, intellectual limitations, depression, organicity |
| R within normal range | Normal |
| R ≈ 90-100 | Schizophrenic tendencies or obsessional neurosis |

### Reaction Time and Colour Shock

**Colour shock** is indicated when the average reaction time to **chromatic cards** (II, III,
VIII, IX, X) is **higher** than the average reaction time to **achromatic cards** (I, IV, V,
VI, VII).

Note: Piotrowski (1957) remarked that Rorschach never used a stopwatch and never recorded
reaction time with any precision, though he noted longer pauses.

### Interpretation of Location Scores

**W responses**
- Direct relationship with intellectual capacity and ability to organize things meaningfully
  (Rorschach, 1942).
- Controversy: Armitage et al. (1955) found very low correlation between W and IQ; Lotsof (1953)
  found W relates only to verbal fluency; Wittenborn (1950) found no relationship.
- Current consensus: correlation between number of W responses and IQ scores is approximately
  **r = .40** (Kaplan & Saccuzzo, 2001); only for specific cards.

**W:D ratio** (Exner, 1974):
- Normal adult: **W:D = 1:2**.
- Elevated D side: examinee selects easier perceptual-cognitive mode when facing ambiguity.
- Elevated W side: examinee resorts to excess in organising ambiguous situations.

**W:M ratio** (both indicative of intellect):
- Normal adult: **W:M = 2:1 to 3:1**.
- W > 3:1 ratio: high aspiration level exceeding ability level.
- W < 2:1 ratio: aspirations below ability level.

**D responses**
- Indicate ability to perceive and react to clear, distinct characteristics of the world.
- Generally easier to give than W responses.
- Low D proportion: usually indicates maladjustment or stress/anxiety.

**Dd responses**
- In normal adults: **5-8%** of total responses (approximately 1 in 20).
- Dd > 30%: high mental disturbance (schizophrenia or obsessive-compulsive neurosis).
- When present in appropriate ratio: indicates initiative, capacity, and ability to withdraw.
- Positively related to internal adjustment; negatively related to external adjustment.

**S responses**
- Rorschach (1942), Beck (1945), Rapaport et al. (1946): S = **negativism or oppositional
  features**.
- Klopfer et al. (1954): S = **constructive self-assertiveness** (if not disproportionate).
- Research: Bandura (1954) confirmed S is positively related to oppositional/negativistic
  tendencies; Murray (1974) found no such relationship.
- Rapaport et al. (1946): **paranoid patients** show the highest frequency of S responses.

**Confabulatory responses (DW, DdW, DdD)**
- Most Rorschachers (excluding Beck): highly pathological; indicate intellectual constriction and
  distorted perception.
- Beck (1945): confabulatory responses are given by normal adults with **superior intellectual
  capacity** and indicate logical cognition (also Exner, 1974).

### Interpretation of Determinant Scores

Determinant scores indicate the **psychological action** underlying a response.

**F (Pure Form Response)**
- Rorschach: indicates thinking/reasoning; adequate affective delay or control.
- Beck (1945) and Klopfer et al. (1954): agree, but caution that the locus of pure form response
  indicates emotional conflict controlled through deliberate thought operations.
- Rapaport et al. (1946): indicates formal reasoning, attention, discriminating judgment, regard
  for environmental standards.

**F% Formula:**

$$F\% = \frac{\Sigma F \times 100}{R}$$

Used to compare pure form responses with nonpure form responses.

**Lambda Index (Beck)**
An alternative method for comparing pure form vs. non-pure form responses. Lambda avoids
problems associated with F%.

## Verbal Techniques

Verbal techniques present stimulus materials in **verbal form** (words or incomplete sentences).

### Word-Association Test

- Developed by **Galton (1879)**: first projective technique.
- Adapted by **Kent and Rosanoff (1910)**.
- Frequently used by **Jung** in therapy.
- Procedure: examinees are presented with a variety of words one at a time; instructed to respond
  with the **very first word** that comes to mind after hearing the stimulus word.
- **Reaction time** (interval between stimulus word and response word) is recorded and analyzed.
- Classified as: Association technique (Lindzey); Association (Best/Bird); Interpretative (Frank);
  Verbal technique (three-way scheme).

### Sentence-Completion Test

- The examinee completes **incomplete sentences** in any way he chooses.
- Examples:
  - "I feel tense ......"
  - "My ambition in life is ......"
  - "I often get nervous ......"
- Most useful for: anxieties, guilt feelings, hostility, aggression, attitudes toward sexuality,
  paranoid tendencies.
- Named tests: **Rotter's Sentence Completion Test**, **Madeleine Thomas Completion Stories
  test**, **Rosenzweig Picture-Frustration Study (1949)**.
- Classified as: Completion technique (Lindzey); Completion (Best/Bird); Verbal technique
  (three-way scheme).
- Limitation: lack of uniform and standard mode of analysis.

## Expressive Techniques

Expressive techniques give the examinee an opportunity to **express personality through
manipulative and objective tasks**. The manner in which the task is performed is the basis for
appraisal — attention is on the **process**, not the end product.

### Figure-Drawing Tests

- Include drawing a human figure, a house, etc.
- Under Frank: constructive or refractive.
- Under Lindzey: expressive technique.

### Toy Tests

- **Toy-World Test**: best example of expressive technique (Lindzey).
- Examiner observes how the examinee selects and handles dolls during play.

### Artistic Productions

- Painting, finger-painting — examples of expressive techniques.

### Graphology

- **Graphology** = handwriting analysis.
- Frank cited graphology as the best example of the **refractive** category (1939).
- Classified as: refractive (Frank); expressive (Lindzey, three-way scheme).

### Rosenzweig Picture-Frustration Study (1949)

- Classified as both a completion technique (Lindzey) and a pictorial technique.
- Involves responses to pictorial frustration situations.

### Szondi Test

- Example of **choice/ordering technique** (Lindzey).
- Examinees rank sets of pictures on a **like-dislike dimension**.
- Kerlinger (1973): choice techniques are a step toward objectifying projective techniques.

## Evaluation of Projective Techniques

The chapter preview lists the following evaluative dimensions (detailed coverage was not included
in the provided text extract, but the framework is established in the chapter):

- **Falsifiability**: whether projective technique interpretations can be refuted empirically.
- **Objectivity**: degree of examiner-independence in scoring and interpretation.
- **Standardization**: whether uniform procedures, norms, and scoring rules exist.
- **Reliability**: consistency of scores (inter-rater, test-retest). The Rorschach is notably
  criticized for low inter-scorer reliability, particularly for determinant categories.
- **Validity**: whether the technique measures what it claims to measure. Criterion validity
  studies for projective tests have shown mixed results.
- **Situational Variables**: examiner effects, test-taking attitudes, and context can distort
  responses.
- **General Applicability**: cross-cultural, age, and clinical versus non-clinical applicability
  concerns.

General critique established in the chapter: projective tests are characterized by a global
approach to personality and are best suited for idiographic (individual-level) clinical
assessment; their psychometric properties are weaker than objective tests.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **First projective technique** = word association test, developed by **Galton (1879)**.
   Adapted by Kent & Rosanoff (1910). First classification term ("projective method") =
   **Frank (1939)**.

2. **Rorschach test**: developed by **Hermann Rorschach, Swiss psychiatrist, 1921**.
   Published in **Psychodiagnostik**. 10 bilaterally symmetrical inkblot cards.
   Cards I/IV/V/VI/VII = black/gray; II/III = black/gray/red; VIII/IX/X = pastel shades.

3. **Three stages of Rorschach administration**: Performance Proper (stage 1), Inquiry (stage 2),
   Testing-of-the-Limits (stage 3).

4. **Rorschach's four scoring categories**: Location, Determinants, Content, Popular and Original
   Responses.

5. **Exner's Comprehensive Scoring System (CSS)**: 9 determinant categories, **24 symbols total**
   (Exner, 1993; Exner & Winer, 1995). Synthesizes the five American systems (Beck, Hertz,
   Klopfer, Piotrowski, Rapaport).

6. **Popular response (P) criteria**: Rorschach = once in every 3 protocols; Exner = once in
   3-5; Piotrowski = once in 6. Original response (O) = no more than once in 100 protocols.

7. **Normal total R**: 15-30 (Rorschach); 20-45 (Klopfer); 17-27 (Exner). Low R (≤10) =
   defensiveness/depression/organicity. High R (90-100) = schizophrenia/obsessional neurosis.

8. **W:D ratio** for normal adult = 1:2; **W:M ratio** = 2:1 to 3:1 (Exner).
   **Dd percentage** in normal adult = 5-8%; if Dd > 30% = schizophrenia or OCD.
   Correlation between W responses and IQ ≈ **0.40** (Kaplan & Saccuzzo, 2001).

9. **Lindzey's five categories (1959)** (most accepted classification): Association, Construction,
   Completion, Expressive, Choice/Ordering. Choice/Ordering techniques not truly projective
   (Kerlinger, 1973).

10. **Frank's five categories (1939)**: Constitutive, Constructive, Interpretative, Refractive,
    Cathartic. Limitation: excessive overlap. Best's (Bird's) four categories (1978):
    Association, Completion, Role-playing, Creative/Constructive.

11. **FM** (animal movement) first proposed by **Klopfer (1930)**; **m** (inanimate movement)
    first proposed by **Piotrowski (1936)**; achromatic colour (C') scoring introduced by
    **Klopfer (1938)** based on Binder's studies (1932); Cn (colour naming) introduced by
    **Piotrowski (1936)**.

12. **S response** interpretations: negativism = Rorschach, Beck, Rapaport; constructive
    self-assertiveness = Klopfer. Highest S response frequency = **paranoid patients**
    (Rapaport et al., 1946).

13. **Colour shock** = average reaction time to chromatic cards (II, III, VIII, IX, X) is higher
    than to achromatic cards (I, IV, V, VI, VII).

14. **F% formula**: F% = (ΣF × 100) / R. Used to compare pure form vs. non-pure form responses.
    Beck proposed the **Lambda index** as an alternative.

15. **Content categories**: Beck = 35 (longest); Klopfer & Davidson = 22 (shortest); Exner = 26
    (22 basic + 4 supplementary). Original Rorschach (1921) had only 6: H, Hd, A, Ad, Obj, (2).

16. **FD** (form-dimensional response): new category introduced by **Exner (1974)**; differs
    from FV in that dimensionality is based on form/size, not on shading.

17. **Three-fourths of all texture responses are FT** (most frequent texture response), occurring
    with great frequency in all Rorschach cards except IV and VI (Exner, 1974).
