# Historical Research

Chapter 17 covers the nature and necessity of historical research, its step-by-step procedure, the classification of data sources into primary and secondary, the two-pronged process of historical criticism (external and internal), and the inherent limitations of the historical method as applied to behavioural and social sciences.

---

## Meaning of Historical Research and Its Necessity

**Historical research** is the application of scientific method to the description and analysis of past events. It is distinct from most behavioural science research because it cannot manipulate variables or observe events directly; instead, it critically evaluates existing records and artifacts.

### Definition (Kerlinger, 1986)

**Kerlinger (1986, p. 25)** defined historical research as:

> "The critical investigation of events, developments and experiences of the past, the careful weighing of evidence of the validity of the sources of information on the past, and the interpretation of the weighed evidences."

Key elements of this definition:
- Critical investigation (not passive recording)
- Covers events, developments, and experiences
- Weighing evidence for validity
- Interpretation of weighed evidence

### Nature of History

- History is not merely a **chronological list** of events.
- It is a **broad integrated account** of relationships among persons, times, and events.
- **Historical analysis** can be directed at: an individual, an idea, a movement, or an institution.
- None of these objects can be treated in isolation because of intense interrelationship among institutions, movements, and people.
- The method used by a historical investigator is called **historiography** (the historical method).

### Why Historical Research Is Necessary

Historical research is needed not only for knowing the past but for understanding the present and predicting the future.

Supporting quotations:
- **Arthur Schlesinger** (historian): "No individual, let alone a social scientist, can ignore the long arm of past."
- **George Bernard Shaw** (thinker): "The past is not behind the group; it is within the group."

### Key Social Scientists Who Used Historical Data

| Scholar | Contribution Relevant to Historical Research |
|---|---|
| **W.I. Thomas** | Concept of "the processing of becoming"; linked past experiences to present attitudes and values |
| **A.T. Adorno et al. (1950)** | Authors of *Authoritarian Personality*; demonstrated roots of prejudice lie in early family experiences; examined techniques for controlling prejudice at source |
| **Committee on Historiography (Social Science Research Council)** | Insisted that understanding dynamics of change and development is required for understanding the relationship of man, institution, and movement |
| **Anthropologists** | Used historical data for studying culture groups |

### Three Situations Where Historical Data Are Particularly Relevant

1. When they are presented as a complex phenomenon of **social forces or social dynamics**.
2. When social phenomena reflect **complex social processes** in a meaningful way.
3. When various dimensions of interrelationships (psychological, economic, educational, religious, and political) make significant contribution toward a **unified complex pattern**.

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative Nature

Historical research may be **qualitative**, **quantitative**, or **both**, depending on the nature of the problem and the type of data processed (Ber & Khan, 2006).

## Steps in Historical Research

A historical researcher follows steps sequentially, similar to a behavioural researcher. The four major steps are:

### Step 1: Selection of a Problem

- Not all problems are suited for historical research.
- Problems selected must:
  - Require scanning of **historical records**
  - Have **social utility**
- No current problem can be meaningfully studied with historical research alone.

### Step 2: Formulation of Hypotheses

- **Hypotheses** are explicitly formulated in historical investigation.
- The historian collects evidence and carefully evaluates the trustworthiness of hypotheses.
- If evidence is **compatible** with the hypothesis (or its consequences), the hypothesis is **confirmed**.
- **Historical generalizations** are ultimately established through the synthesis of confirmed hypotheses.

### Step 3: Collection of Data

- Historical data are collected through two types of sources:
  - **Primary sources**: Eyewitness accounts by actual observers or participants in the event.
  - **Secondary sources**: Accounts gathered after the reporter has talked with the actual observer or has seen the primary document/source.

### Step 4: Analysis and Generalization

- All data must be verified for completeness; nothing should be left out.
- The historian applies **principles of probability** (similar to a physical scientist).
- Evidence is rigorously subjected to **critical analysis** to establish authenticity and correctness.
- A final **generalization** is arrived at.

## Sources of Historical Data

There are two major types of data sources in historical research.

---

### (1) Primary Sources

**Primary sources** are eyewitness accounts of events reported by actual observers or participants in an event. They are first-hand records.

#### (a) Documents

**Documents** are records kept and written by actual participants or direct witnesses of an event. They are produced for the purpose of transmitting information to be used in the future.

Examples of documents included in primary sources:
- Official minutes or records
- Constitutions
- Certificates
- Declarations
- Licenses
- Deeds
- Wills
- Affidavits
- Books
- Diagrams
- Paintings
- Pictures
- Newspapers
- Catalogues
- Films
- Advertisements
- Maps
- Handbills
- Recordings of research
- Reports

**Situations where direct use of documents is most useful:**

1. When events depicted have **not yet been analyzed** by researchers.
2. When such events have **not yet been incorporated** into cultural-historical interpretive writings.
3. When the purpose is to **verify certain events directly**.
4. When certain aspects of life the investigator is interested in have **not been addressed** as a separate source of historical information.
5. When there is a **missing link** in knowledge that needs to be connected so the whole event may be meaningfully understood.
6. When **controversial issues** need to be resolved.
7. When **controversial points** need to be settled.

**Precaution in using documents:** Documents should not preferably be used until they are the only source of information available. The closer a person remains to the time, spirit, and scene of action, the more able he or she will be in understanding the events.

#### (b) Remains or Relics

**Remains or relics** are objects associated with some persons, groups, or periods. Unlike documents, they were **not directly intended** for transmitting information, but they provide clear evidence of past life and happenings.

Examples:
- Foods
- Skeletons
- Tools
- Utensils
- Weapons
- Clothing
- Buildings
- Coins
- Furniture
- Pictures
- Art work

#### (c) Oral Testimony

**Oral testimony** is the spoken account of a witness or participant in an event.

- Can be obtained in a **personal interview**.
- Can be **recorded or transcribed** as the witness relates experiences.

---

### (2) Secondary Sources

**Secondary sources** are accounts of an event that were **not actually witnessed** by the participant or the reporter. The writer is not present at the actual scene; he or she reports what a direct witness said or wrote.

Key characteristics:
- Many books on history and encyclopedias are secondary sources.
- Encyclopedias are often far from the original contents.
- Secondary sources are used **less** than primary sources because of errors that may result when information is passed from one person to another.

### Primary vs. Secondary: Context-Dependent Classification

The same material may function as a primary or secondary source depending on the purpose of the investigation.

**Example:** A high school textbook on Indian history is:
- Ordinarily a **secondary source** (of historical events).
- A **primary source** if the investigator is studying the gradually changing emphasis on nationalism in high school textbooks on Indian history (then the textbook itself is the document being studied).

## Historical Criticism

Historians face difficulty in obtaining trustworthy and authenticated data because they ordinarily **do not use direct observation**. Since past events cannot be repeated at the will of the investigator, historians must depend on witnesses and participants.

**Historical evidence** is data that are trustworthy, authentic, and worth using. It is derived from historical data through the process of **criticism**, which has two types: external and internal.

---

### (1) External Criticism

**External criticism** establishes the **authenticity or genuineness** of historical data. It answers the question: *Is this document/relic what it claims to be? Is it a forgery?*

Methods historians use in external criticism:
- Recognizing **external marks**: handwriting, script, language, documentation, knowledge available at that period.
- **Physical and chemical tests** of ink, paper, and writing.
- Checking whether elements are consistent with known facts about the person, knowledge available, and technology of the period.

External criticism determines **genuineness** only, not accuracy.

---

### (2) Internal Criticism

**Internal criticism** evaluates the **accuracy or worth** of the data. Even if a document is genuine (established by external criticism), internal criticism asks: *Does it reveal a true picture of the past?*

Questions addressed in internal criticism:
- Were the authors or discoverers of the data **honest and unbiased**?
- Were they actually acquainted with the facts?
- Were they making **hasty generalizations** or presenting a true picture?
- Were they in **agreement with other competent authority** of that period?
- Did they write about events firsthand, or were they subject to **pressure, fear, or vanity**?

Although these questions are difficult to answer, historians strive to establish that data are not only genuine but also **accurate**. Without this, they cannot introduce historical evidence worth consideration.

### Summary: External vs. Internal Criticism

| Criterion | External Criticism | Internal Criticism |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Genuineness / Authenticity | Accuracy / Worth |
| Question asked | Is this source real/original? | Does this source tell the truth? |
| Methods used | Handwriting analysis, physical/chemical tests, language | Evaluating author's honesty, bias, corroboration with other authorities |
| Outcome | Establishes if data are genuine | Establishes if data are accurate |

## Limitations of Historical Research

Historians themselves accept certain limitations of the historical method. These are:

**(a) Inappropriate or Unavailable Data**
Data being made available may be inappropriate, creating an inaccurate picture of the past. Modern historians agree that few narrators report historical events without injecting their own impressions, introducing an **impressionistic and propagandistic element**.

**(b) Selectivity and Bias in Recording**
No life-size writing of historical events is possible. Each historian records what he considers relevant and ignores what he considers irrelevant. This introduces an element of **bias** in historical research.

**(c) Incomplete Records**
Not all happenings in time and space can be known at the time of writing. Events in distant villages or remote places may not come to the notice of historians, leaving records **incomplete and partial**.

**(d) Unconscious Personal Biases**
Personal biases sometimes creep almost unconsciously into the interpretation of historical events. Historians may omit details and interpret retained information toward their own conclusions rather than in a detailed and unbiased manner.

**(e) Problem of Defining the Research Problem**
Difficulties arise in recognizing and properly defining the research problem. Historians often state a **too broad a problem** that is not specific enough. Historians must select a penetrating analysis of a limited problem rather than a superficial sketch of a broad area.

**(f) Vague Generalizations**
Historical research has limitations that tend to make **generalizations vague** and conclusions inappropriate.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Kerlinger's definition (1986)** of historical research has three components: critical investigation, weighing evidence for validity, and interpretation of weighed evidence. This definition is a direct MCQ target.

2. **Historiography** is the term for the method used by a historical investigator (historical method). Do not confuse with historiography as the broader academic discipline.

3. Historical research has **four steps**: (i) selection of problem, (ii) formulation of hypotheses, (iii) collection of data, (iv) analysis and generalization.

4. **Primary sources** are first-hand (eyewitness) accounts; **secondary sources** are second-hand accounts. Secondary sources are used less due to transmission errors.

5. The three types of primary sources are: **(a) documents, (b) remains/relics, (c) oral testimony.** This list is frequently tested.

6. **Remains/relics** differ from documents in that they were **not originally intended** to transmit information, yet still provide historical evidence.

7. **External criticism** = establishes **genuineness/authenticity** (is the source real?). **Internal criticism** = establishes **accuracy/worth** (does the source tell the truth?). The distinction between external and internal criticism is a high-yield MCQ target.

8. External criticism uses **physical and chemical tests** of ink, paper, and writing, as well as analysis of handwriting, script, and language.

9. **Adorno et al. (1950)** and the *Authoritarian Personality* are cited as a prime example of using historical data to trace the roots of prejudice to early family experiences.

10. **W.I. Thomas** is associated with the concept of **"the processing of becoming"** — linking past experiences with present attitudes.

11. A high school textbook can be a **primary source** (if studying changes in textbook content) or a **secondary source** (if studying the historical events it describes). Context determines classification.

12. Historical research can be **qualitative, quantitative, or both** (Ber & Khan, 2006) -- not exclusively qualitative.

13. The key **limitation** most tested: Each historian selects what is relevant and discards what is irrelevant, making a **life-size account impossible** and introducing bias.

14. The **Committee on Historiography** of the Social Science Research Council is associated with the argument that understanding dynamics of change is essential for understanding human-institution-movement relationships.

15. Arthur Schlesinger's quote ("long arm of past") and Shaw's quote ("the past is not behind the group; it is within the group") are signature quotations associated with justifying the **necessity** of historical research -- both are MCQ-worthy attribution questions.
