# Qualitative Research

Chapter 22 provides a comprehensive treatment of qualitative research: its meaning, historical development, theoretical frameworks, design strategies, sampling techniques, data-collection methods, and analytic approaches. It draws on multiple traditions (ethnography, grounded theory, phenomenology, hermeneutics, etc.) and contrasts them systematically with quantitative approaches.

---

## Meaning and Essential Features of Qualitative Research

### Two Major Research Paradigms

| Paradigm | Basis | Methods |
|---|---|---|
| **Logical positivism / post-positivist paradigm** | Assumptions of natural sciences | Experimental; description, hypothesis testing, cause-effect relationships |
| **Phenomenological paradigm** | Phenomenological philosophy | Interpretive research methods and designs |

- **Qualitative research** is sometimes defined negatively: it is what quantitative research is *not* (a "negative connotation"), rather than being defined positively on its own terms.
- Other researchers view it as a variety of *alternative approaches* to traditional, positivist research.

### Ethnographic Origins of the Definition

The definitional roots trace to **ethnographers**: **Malinowski (1926)** and **Spradley (1979)**. They defined **ethnographic research** as:

> A methodology to study culture by analyzing the subjective meaning of social production of music, events or practices by collecting non-standardized data and analyzing texts and images rather than numbers and statistics.

### When Qualitative Research Is Appropriate

Qualitative research is especially appropriate when:
1. The researcher wants to collect data on the *perception* that individuals hold about a subject.
2. The researcher wants to develop (not test) theory about the subject — building understanding from the ground up.
3. The result is intended to develop concepts/ideas that emerge and evolve throughout the study.
4. The researcher can interact with participants directly.
5. The research requires incorporating *assessments* of the subject from the participants' own viewpoint.

## Relevance of Qualitative Research

### Pluralization of Life-Worlds

**Flick (2002)** argued that qualitative research is especially relevant for studying social relations due to the **pluralization of life-worlds** — the diversification occurring in modern society. Traditional class models (working class, middle class, upper class) are no longer sufficient; local and subcultural differences must now be factored in.

### Postmodernism and Social Change

- **Postmodernism** as a social theory (**Flick, 2005**) heightened the relevance of qualitative research. Locally, temporally and situationally limited narratives now dominate social science.
- Classical deductive methodologies (hypothesis formulation from theoretical models, then empirical testing) are failing due to differentiation of social conditions.
- Researchers now "start from scratch and from the bottom up" — requiring sensitive methods for varying social contexts (**Bruner, 1991**).

## Brief History of Qualitative Research

### Roots

- **William Wundt** used description and introspection alongside experimental methods in early psychology — an early instance of qualitative methods within an experimental programme.
- German-speaking sociology and philosophy debated the distinction between explanation and understanding, giving birth to biographical methods and case studies.
- **Glaser and Strauss (1967)** sparked a resurgence of qualitative research through their critique of standardized, quantifying social research.
- **Willg and Stainton-Rogers (2007)** noted the resulting resurgence in psychology.
- Important methodological insights — including work of **Piaget** and **Vygotsky** — have roots largely in qualitative research.

### Seven (Later Eight) Phases: Denzin and Lincoln (2005)

**Denzin and Lincoln (2005)** identified the following phases of qualitative research development in the United States:

| Phase | Period | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Traditional** | 1900–1945 | Malinowski and Chicago School of Sociology; studying "the other" — foreign or distant peoples; documenting native lives |
| 2. **Modernist** | 1945–1970s | Formalizing qualitative research; textbook publication increased; kept alive by Glaser and Strauss (1967), Strauss and Corbin (1990), Miles and Huberman (1994) |
| 3. **Blurred genres** | Until mid-1980s | Researchers used multiple models side by side: ethnomethodology, phenomenology, symbolic interactionism, semiotics (Guba 1990) |
| 4. **Crisis of representation** | Mid-1980s onward | Anthropological critique (Clifford and others); validity/reliability connected to ways of displaying reality; interview accounts are partial, one-version representations |
| 5. **Narratives replace theories** | 1990s | Research shifted toward narratives about specific, local, historical problems rather than grand theoretical models |
| 6. **Writing and democracy** | 1990s onward | Writing linked to democratic politics (Flick 2009) |
| 7. **New journals** | ~2000–2004 | Qualitative research consolidated through new, successful journals |
| 8. **Evidence-based practice** | Post-2004 | Evidence-based practice emerged as the new criterion of relevance for social science |

### Development in Germany

- **Habermas (1967)** first recognized the emerging qualitative tradition in American sociology (Goffman, Garfinkel).
- **Glaser and Strauss (1967)** model: the researcher is attached to the material; no fixed perspective; the research object comes to its form only at the end.
- The **1970s** saw discussion of openness and critique of traditional psychology in Germany.
- **Flick (2002)** described discussions around analyzing and understanding interviews in depth.
- The **early 1980s in Germany** saw two crucial original methods developed:
  1. **Narrative interview** — a specific form of interview based on one extensive main narration; the interviewer asks participants to tell the story of their lives as a whole, without interruptions.
  2. **Objective hermeneutics** — methodology for doing extensive research by analyzing texts for identifying their *latent meaning* (e.g., a transcript of family interaction analyzed for implicit conflicts underlying the communication). Became influential in biographical research (**Rosenthal, 2004**).
- **Mid-1980s Germany**: concept of **triangulation** proposed for validating and verifying results.
- **Richards and Richards (1994); Guba (2007)**: computers introduced into qualitative analysis due to the unstructured nature of qualitative data.

### Development in India

Qualitative research has been practiced in India since the late 19th century (**Bhawuk 2008; Dhanda 1992; Misra 2001**). Methods used in Indian psychology include:

- Action research (Rao 1998; Sharma and Bhatarya 2003)
- Observation (Bala et al. 2000)
- Ethnography (Priya 2009; Kiran 2003; Bhattacharyya 2002; Tripathi 2003)
- Case study (Dosajh 2003; Dalal et al. 1993, 1994; Sinha 2000; Yadava 2002)
- Textual analysis (Bhawuk 2008)
- Narrative inquiry (Misra 2005)
- Diary method (Sinha 2005)
- Sociometry (Thomas and Bhawuk 1992; Shenoy 1996; Sharma 2002)
- Conversational analysis (Sandy 1995; Mall 1987; Raina 1997; Channabasavanna and Bhatti 2011)

## Themes of Qualitative Research (Patton, 2002)

**Patton (2002)** identified **12 themes** characterizing qualitative research, grouped into **three major categories**:

### Category 1: Design Strategies (3 themes)

1. **Emergent design flexibility**: The inquiry design is subject to change and modification as data are collected and analyzed. The researcher is not locked into any rigid design. This contrasts sharply with quantitative research, where the design is fully spelled out before data collection and no changes are made during the study. A qualitative researcher can enter the field with a small set of research questions, ready to adapt.

2. **Purposive sampling** (purposeful sampling): Qualitative researchers prefer purposive sampling over probability sampling. Aimed and directed toward "information-rich cases" selected for illumination and perspective. The researcher samples for *information-rich cases*.

3. **Naturalistic inquiry** (ethnographic inquiry): Researchers study real-world situations as they unfold naturally; no manipulation or stimulation. Openness to whatever emerges is the key stance.

### Category 2: Data Collection and Fieldwork Strategies (4 themes)

4. **Qualitative data**: Rich, detailed, and in-depth data permit deeper understanding than quantitative data.

5. **Personal experience and engagement**: The researcher has direct, personal, immediate, sustained, and close contact with the field (real-world settings: programmes, organizations, neighbourhoods, street corners). Such direct contact enables insights not obtainable in a detached manner.

6. **Empathic neutrality and mindfulness**: The researcher is empathetic toward participants while maintaining analytical neutrality and a non-judgmental attitude. The researcher reports sources of bias and uses multiple sources to produce trustworthy results.

7. **Dynamic systems perspective**: Individual and community must be understood across time and as part of a larger system. Single data snapshots are insufficient and often misleading.

### Category 3: Analysis Strategies (4 themes)

8. **Holistic perspective**: Each case is treated as unique. The researcher's training (psychology, sociology, anthropology) adds unique quality to the analysis. Encompasses complex interdependencies that cannot be reduced to a few discrete variables.

9. **Inductive analysis**: Data exploration without prior hypotheses. Allows discovery of reality without fitting it into a predetermined theoretical perspective. Distinct from quantitative deductive testing.

10. **Context sensitivity**: Qualitative data are sensitive to the social, cultural, temporal, and historical context in which gathered. Findings are not easily generalized to other contexts spatially or temporally.

11. **Reflexivity**: The researcher's background, perspective, and role are acknowledged as part of the analysis.

## Theoretical Perspectives of Qualitative Research (Patton, 2002)

**Patton (2002)** provided **16 theoretical perspectives** — the most comprehensive framework:

| # | Perspective | Disciplinary Root | Central Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ethnography** | Anthropology | What are the physical and social environments in which persons under study live and act? |
| 2 | **Autobiography / Autoethnography** | Literary arts | How does the researcher's own experience of this culture illuminate the feelings, events, and way of life of its inhabitants? |
| 3 | **Reality testing (Feminist/Realist approach)** | Philosophy | What is real? What is the truth, as near as we can get? |
| 4 | **Constructivism / Constructionism** | Sociology | Why do people have the individual explanations they do? What are the consequences of those explanations (actions, behaviours)? |
| 5 | **Phenomenology** | Philosophy | What is the meaning, structure, and essence of the lived experience of a phenomenon for a person or group? |
| 6 | **Heuristic inquiry** | Humanistic psychology | What is the experience of others who have also experienced the same phenomenon intensely? |
| 7 | **Ethnomethodology** | Sociology | In what ways do people make sense of their social life? |
| 8 | **Symbolic interactionism** | Sociology | What is the universe of symbols and understandings persons use to give meaning to individual actions? Note: Assumes people act based on meanings assigned via social interaction; meanings are modified through an interpretive process (**Blumer, 1969**) |
| 9 | **Ecological psychology** | Ecology and Psychology | How do persons fit in or adapt to their environments? Note: methodology is qualitative but data analysis may remain quantitative (behavioural mapping, ecological analysis) |
| 10 | **Semiotics** | Linguistics | How do various signs (words, symbols) convey meaning in a particular context? |
| 11 | **Hermeneutics** | Philosophy | What are the conditions under which human actions take place that help in interpreting meaning? |
| 12 | **Narrative analysis / Narratology** | Social sciences, literature, folklore | What does the story or narrative reveal about life and culture, and how can we interpret it for understanding? |
| 13 | **System theory** | Interdisciplinary | How does this system function as a whole? |
| 14 | **Chaos / Nonlinear dynamics** | Theoretical physics and natural sciences | What is the underlying order, if any, of disorderly phenomena? |
| 15 | **Grounded theory** | Sociology (Glaser and Strauss, 1967) | What theory emerges from systematic comparative analysis of the obtained data? |
| 16 | **Orientational theories (Feminist theory, Critical theory)** | Social sciences | How do feminist/critical perspectives manifest in the phenomenon under study? |

### Key Notes on Selected Perspectives

**Symbolic interactionism (Blumer, 1969)** — two key assumptions:
1. People act and interact on the basis of *meanings* that things are assigned through social interaction.
2. Meanings are modified through the *interpretive process* used by the person in dealing with things/objects encountered.

**Feminist theory**: Has made important contributions since the 1960s. Critiques ignorance of women's life situations and male dominance. Quantitative research seen as normalizing experience; transforming actors into objects studied in a value-neutral manner (Olesen 1994; Roman 1992; Allen 1988). Qualitative research allows critical analysis of gender relationships.

Three most common perspectives: **ethnography**, **symbolic interactionism**, and **action psychology**.

## Research Design Strategies of Qualitative Research

Five major research design strategies:

1. Ethnographic / ethnographic studies
2. Case studies
3. Document or content analysis
4. Grounded theory
5. Retrospective studies

### 1. Ethnographic Studies

**Ethnography** from Greek *ethnos* (people/group). Definition: the study of people or groups as they naturally go about their daily lives, using methods that capture their ordinary activities and social meanings, with the investigator participating directly in the setting without anything imposed externally.

**Hammersley and Atkinson (1995)** described ethnography as "overt" participation: the researcher participates, overtly or covertly, in people's daily lives for an extended period of time, watching what happens, listening to what is said, asking questions, and collecting relevant data. They emphasized ethnography as **participant observation** — a way of doing social research developed by ethnographers in the face of difficulties created by positivism.

#### Six Characteristics of Ethnographic Study

(i) Ethnographers assume shared **cultural meanings** of a group are crucial to understanding its behaviour. Primary task: uncover such meanings (**Goffman, 1961**).

(ii) The ethnographer studies the setting sensitive to the **emic perspective** — the meanings that behaviour, actions, and events have in the eyes of the people being studied (**Sturtevant 1964; Pike 1967**).

(iii) The ethnographer studies the group in its **natural settings** — becoming part of that natural setting.

(iv) Ethnography is a **data-intensive process**. Not normally clear what to study in depth until progress has been made; specific questions emerge over time.

(v) Ethnographic data collection requires a **comprehensive and detailed record** of events because the ethnographer captures cultural and personal meanings across their entire range. Takes time.

(vi) Ethnography is **eclectic and not restrictive** in data collection — ranging from direct nonparticipant observation to full participant involvement.

#### Indian Ethnographic Studies (Examples)

- **Sarangpani (2003)**: Ethnographic study of schooling in North Delhi; found children perceived schooling as making them good citizens; memorization/recalling were key criteria of intelligence; teachers' voice and memorization considered important.
- **Priya (2000)**: Ethnographic study of suicide and the Gujarat earthquake (20 January 2001). Villagers believed karma brings harmony and eliminates suffering; urban victims were more materialistic-oriented.
- **Bhattacharya (2001)**: Ethnographic study of women's education in Delhi; used participant observation, interviews, informal interactions, and questionnaires over 3 months in a women's college. Found a kaleidoscopic view of curriculum organization, teaching styles, disciplinary techniques, and grading patterns.
- **Jha (1999)**: Ethnographic research in Mumbai.

### 2. Case Studies

Used to study complex problems in depth. Indian examples: Gandhi (1975) — physical assault; Sinha (1995) — effectiveness of compromise between complainant (woman victim) and offender (husband/in-laws).

### 4. Grounded Theory

**Grounded theory** developed by **Glaser and Strauss (1967)**. Simultaneously a method, approach, strategy, and theory. Basic purpose: **generate theory from data** — "grounded" = the theory should be grounded in the data. Theory is developed **inductively** from data.

#### Historical Background of Grounded Theory

| Publication | Year | Authors | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Awareness of Dying* | 1965 | Glaser and Strauss | First major grounded theory study; medical sociology; dying in hospitals |
| *Time for Dying* | 1968 | Glaser and Strauss | Second study; hospitals |
| ***The Discovery of Grounded Theory*** | **1967** | Glaser and Strauss | Key publication: offered rationale; suggested specific characteristics; legitimated qualitative research generally |
| [Theoretical memos book] | 1978 | Glaser (solo) | Focused on theoretical memos; developing theoretical sensitivity |
| *Basics of Analysis for Qualitative Research* | 1987 | Based on Glaser and Strauss | Handbook for understanding social behaviour through qualitative data collection |
| ***Basics of Qualitative Research: Grounded Theory Procedures and Techniques*** | **1990** | Strauss and Corbin | Clear guide to grounded theory; especially data analysis; very useful across disciplines |
| *Basics of Grounded Theory Analysis: Emerging versus Forcing* | 1992 | Glaser | Corrected misconceptions in Strauss and Corbin's 1990 book |
| *Examples of Grounded Theory: A Reader* | 1993 | Glaser (ed.) | Further examples |
| *More Grounded Theory Methodology* | 1994 | Glaser | Additional methodology |
| [Chapter in *Handbook of Qualitative Research*] | 1994 | Strauss and Corbin | Edited by Denzin and Lincoln |

Other contributors: **Charmaz (1983)**, **Chernitz and Swanson (1986)**.

#### Key Principles of Grounded Theory

- Theory developed **inductively** from data; not deductively tested as pre-formed hypotheses.
- Theoretical propositions are proposed, not tested as hypotheses.
- Researcher starts with a **broad general question** or inquiry.
- **Use of literature**: Different from other approaches. If a satisfactory theory already exists, a grounded theory study is unnecessary. Literature reviewed *after* conceptual categories within data become clear — treated as further data.
- Equally applicable to **quantitative data** (stressed by Glaser, 1992).
- Does not depend on any particular disciplinary perspective.

### 5. Retrospective (Biographical) Research

**Biographical research** / **retrospective research**: Studies in which, retrospectively from the point in time when research is carried out, individuals are asked verbally or in writing to reconstruct their biography or reflective life histories (**Mick, 2000**).

Key features:
- Informants selected for their relevance to the process being researched.
- Data collected via **narrative methods**.
- Analyzed with **narrative or hermeneutic approaches**.
- Purpose: understand social phenomena in their totality.

**Danger**: Current situation can selectively influence what the informant recalls or describes; perspective may be distorted from the interviewer's viewpoint or from documents produced earlier.

## Sampling Techniques of Qualitative Research

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative Sampling

| Feature | Quantitative | Qualitative |
|---|---|---|
| Type | Probability sampling | **Purposive / deliberate sampling** |
| Goal | Representativeness (for generalization) | Information-richness (for answering research questions) |

**Purposive sampling**: Sampling done deliberately with some purpose in mind; selects cases that can provide desired information. The basic aim: the sample is useful in answering the researcher's questions.

**Miles and Huberman (1994)** identified a typology of qualitative sampling strategies. **Patton (1990)** and **Lincoln and Guba (1994)** also contributed significantly.

### 12 Qualitative Sampling Strategies

**(i) Complete collection**: Structure of groups defined before data collection; all cases meeting specified criteria are included. Example: **Gerhardt (1986)** — studied patients' careers in chronic renal failure in south-east Britain; included all male, married patients aged 30-50 at treatment start from five major hospitals in a region. Common in rural studies.

**(ii) Quota sampling**: Group defined before data collection; restricts range of variation in the sample; no real new findings at this level.

**(iii) Theoretical sampling**: Introduced by **Glaser and Strauss (1967)** in medical sociology. Persons, groups, or materials selected according to their **relevance for the theory being developed** and what has already been found. Process: jointly collect, code, and analyze data; each step guides what data to collect next. **Theoretical saturation** = the point at which more data about a theoretical category produces no further theoretical insight — "nothing new is emerging." Sampling stops at theoretical saturation.

**(iv) Extreme case sampling** (deviant case sampling): Used to learn from unusual or negative manifestations of the phenomenon. Selects cases that differ from the dominant pattern. Goal: collect unusual, different, or problematic cases. Example: High-school dropouts from stable, two-parent, upper-middle-income families (opposite of the typical low-income, single-parent profile).

**(v) Typical case sampling**: Selects cases considered representative or illustrative of the field. Example: For the secondary labour market, selecting workers who represent the overall average view.

**(vi) Maximum variation sampling**: Researcher intentionally selects as wide a variation as possible; only a few cases but as different as possible, so the full range of variation in the field is disclosed.

**(vii) Homogeneous sampling**: Selects cases according to *similarity* of features, processes, and experiences; greatest intensity or systematically important intensities.

**(viii) Stratified purposeful sampling**: Selects important or sensitive issues across a range of cases. Example: A few top leaders from Hindu and Muslim communities to assess views on constructing a mosque in a designated place.

**(ix) Critical case sampling**: Selects critical cases where relations to be studied become clear, or which are particularly important for the programme under study.

**(x) Convenience sampling** (opportunistic sampling): Selects cases easiest to access. Cheap and quick. Example: person-on-the-road television interviews.

**(xi) Purposeful random sampling**: **Patton's** distinction between **primary selection** (participants' knowledge, capability to reflect/articulate, adequate time, willingness to share) and **secondary selection** (criteria for choosing from among those willing). **Morse** cautions against investing many resources in such cases.

**(xii)** [Text references twelve total; Miles and Huberman (1994) provide the formal typology.]

### Six (Seven) Evaluative Questions for a Qualitative Sampling Plan (Miles and Huberman, 1994)

1. Within the conceptual framework, what are the research questions?
2. Is the sampling relevant for the conceptual framework and research questions?
3. Will the phenomenon in which the researcher is interested appear in principle?
4. Does the sampling plan enhance the generalizability of the findings?
5. Is the sampling plan feasible in terms of time, money, and access to people?
6. Is the sampling plan ethical (informed consent, potential benefits/risks, relationship with respondents)?
7. Can believable and trustworthy explanations be produced that are true to real life?

## Data Collection Techniques in Qualitative Research

Three most popular data collection techniques (**Fielding, 1993**):

1. Interview
2. Observation
3. Document review

### 1. Interview

**Interview**: Used to gather information regarding an interviewee's experience and knowledge, values, background, beliefs, feelings, and demographic data. Can determine past/current information and future predictions.

- Group interview typically involves **6 to 8 participants** in qualitative research.
- Forms: face-to-face individual, face-to-face group, mailed questionnaire, telephone survey.
- Can be structured, semi-structured, or unstructured.
- Duration: 5-10 minutes (brief telephone) to multiple lengthy sessions over days (life history interviewing).

#### Classification of Interview Types

**Patton (1980)**: informal conversational interviews; general interview guide approach; standardized open-ended interviews.

**Fielding (1993)**: standardized, semi-standardized, unstandardized.

**Fontana and Frey (1994)**: structured, semi-structured, unstructured (applying to individual and group).

##### (i) Structured Interview

- Pre-stated questions with **preset response categories**.
- The series of questions = **interview schedule**.
- Questions standardized; same questions in same order to all respondents.
- Flexibility minimized; interviewer plays a **neutral role**.
- Stresses rational rather than emotional interaction; easily quantified; enables comparison.
- Limitation: little room for unanticipated discoveries; respondents feel constrained.

##### (ii) Unstructured Interview

- Precise questions and order **not fixed**; develop through interaction.
- Open-ended; interviewees may say as little or as much as they choose.
- Also known as **informal interview** or **ethnographic interview**.
- **Fontana and Frey (1994)** identified **seven important aspects** for planning unstructured interviews:
  1. Accessing the setting
  2. Understanding the language and culture of respondents
  3. Deciding how to present oneself
  4. Locating an informant
  5. Gaining trust
  6. Establishing good rapport
  7. Collecting empirical materials
- Analysis is time-consuming and difficult.
- More natural than structured interaction.
- Different questions with different people make data organization very difficult.

##### (iii) Semi-structured Interview

- Mixture of structured and unstructured elements.
- Especially useful for life history projects.
- Design is a creative process.

##### Focus Group Interview

- Researcher works with **several people simultaneously**.
- Originally used in **market research**; now increasingly used in social science research.
- **Fontana and Frey (1994)**: strength lies in **group dynamics** — interactions produce data and insights less accessible without group interaction.
- Researcher's role: **moderator or facilitator** rather than interviewer; participants address questions to one another, not just to the researcher.
- Data transcribed, analyzed, and summarized like other interview forms.

#### Practical Aspects of Interviewing

Three practical aspects:

**(a) Interview respondents** — key issues:
1. Who is to be interviewed and why?
2. How many times will each respondent be interviewed?
3. When and for what period?
4. At what place?
5. How will access be organized?

**(b) Managing the interview** — five key points:
1. Preparation of the interview schedule
2. Establishing rapport at the beginning
3. Communication and listening skills
4. Sequence and types of questions
5. Closing the interview

- **Minichello et al. (1990)**: reported **16 subtleties of listening** recommended for social science students to practice.
- **Patton (1990)** classified questions by topics: experiences/behaviours, knowledge, feeling, sensory, and demographic background. Also classified **probe questions** as critically important.

**(c) Recording**: Options — checkmarks on response sheet (structured), tape recording, video recording, or note-taking. Situation dictates recording method.

#### Two Approaches to Interviewing

1. **Emic approach**: Uncovers data within the respondent's experiential world; uses structured or semi-structured schedule; categories covered during interview to delineate concepts, interpretations, inferences.
2. **Traveller's approach**: More open-ended and explorative.

### 2. Observation

Detailed notation of events, behaviours, and contexts surrounding them in natural context. Also referred to as **fieldwork** in social sciences.

Used by psychologists (Irwin 1980; Brandt 1981; Liebert 1993), sociologists (Adler and Adler 1994), anthropologists (Tedlock 1991).

#### Quantitative vs. Qualitative Approach

| Feature | Quantitative | Qualitative |
|---|---|---|
| Structure | Highly structured; predetermined observation schedule | Much more unstructured; no predetermined categories |
| Recording | Structured coding | Natural; behaviours observed as they unfold |

#### Five Dimensions of Observation (Patton, 1980)

1. Observer can play from **full participant to complete outsider**.
2. Observation may be conducted **covertly** (e.g., behind one-way mirror) or **overtly** (with knowledge of participants).
3. **Full explanation, partial explanation, or false explanation** may be given to those being observed.
4. Sometimes **no explanation** can be provided.
5. **Breadth of focus**: ranges from very broad to quite narrow; duration from one hour to one year.

#### Five Stages of Unstructured Observation (Silverman, 1993)

1. Descriptive observation
2. Noting field notes
3. Initial analytical planning
4. Testing hypotheses
5. Making broader links

#### Sequential Phases of Participant Observation (Spradley, 1980)

1. **Descriptive observation**: Beginning phase; broad information gathering; grasps field complexity; develops more concrete research questions.
2. **Focused observation**: Second phase; narrows perspective to processes and problems identified as relevant in descriptive phase.
3. **Selective observation**: Hints toward the end of data collection; finding further evidence for types of practices found in focused phase.

#### Limitations of Participant Observation

- Not all phenomena can be observed directly (e.g., biographical processes).
- Rare/crucial events can only be captured via key informants.
- Cannot be standardized beyond a general research strategy (**Lüders, 2004**).

### 3. Document Review

**Documents**: Notes, case reports, diaries, annual reports, certificates, judgements, letters, expert opinions, biographies, official statistical data, government records, pronouncements, proceedings (**Atkinson 1996; Wolff 2004**).

#### Scott's (1990) Classification of Documents

**Scott (1990)** distinguished **12 types of documents** on two axes:

| Axis | Categories |
|---|---|
| **Authorship** (who produced it) | Personal / Official-Public / Official-Private (3 categories) |
| **Access** (availability to others) | Closed / Restricted / Open-archival / Open-published (4 categories) |

3 authorship categories x 4 access categories = **12 types of documents**.

#### Scott's Four Criteria for Assessing Document Quality

(a) **Authenticity**: Is the document genuine and of unquestionable origin?
(b) **Credibility**: Is the document free from error? Has it not been distorted?
(c) **Representativeness**: Is the document typical of its kind? If not, is the extent of its uniqueness known?
(d) **Meaning**: Is the document clear and comprehensible?

#### Practical Notes on Document Use

- Documents are a way of communicating information, *not* "information containers."
- No part of a document should be taken as arbitrary; start from **ethnomethodological assumptions of order at all points** (Atkinson 1996).
- Use triangulation: compare documents with other sources.

#### Constraints in Document Use

- Documents may be unavailable, inaccessible, or lost.
- People may block access.
- Language difficulties (jargon, abbreviations, archaic language).

### Other Data Collection Techniques

- **Psychological tests and scales**: TAT (Thematic Apperception Test), word association tests, projective techniques — can be used in qualitative or quantitative data collection.
- **Psycho-physiological measures**: heart rate, galvanic skin response (GSR), body movements.
- **Narratives**: Used to study individual life stories. Structure (**Hermans, 1991**): initial situation outlined; events/roles selected; development described; final situation presented.
- **Episodic interviews**: Combine narrative experiences with semantic knowledge in episodic form; designed to make interviewing as natural as possible.

### Triangulation

**Triangulation**: The use of multiple data collection techniques. Permits **corroboration and completeness** of qualitative data. Covers the weakness of one method with the strengths of another. A good qualitative research plan includes multiple methods of data collection.

## Data Analysis and Interpretation

**Data analysis** (general): Examining, coding, categorizing, and recombining evidence to address the initial purpose of a study.

- **Miles and Huberman (1994)**: Identified **six common moves** across different types of analysis; labelled their approach **"transcendental realism."**

### Seven Approaches to Qualitative Data Analysis

1. Miles and Huberman's technique
2. Grounded theory analysis
3. Narrative and hermeneutic analysis
4. Ethnomethodology and conversation analysis
5. Discourse analysis
6. Documentary and textual analysis
7. Use of computers in analysis

### 1. Miles and Huberman's Technique

A comprehensive system directed at finding basic and stable relationships among social phenomena. **Three main components**:

#### (a) Data Reduction

Occurs throughout the analysis in three stages:
- **Beginning stage**: Editing, segmenting, and summarizing the data.
- **Middle stage**: Coding and memoing; finding themes, clusters, and patterns.
- **Concluding stage**: Conceptualizing and explaining; developing abstract concepts. Developing abstract concepts requires reduction of data into its basic constituent parts.

Purpose: Reduce raw data without significant loss of information; shorten or simplify data; help researchers not to stray data from context.

#### (b) Data Display

Common modes: graphs, charts, networks, diagrams (class diagrams, causal models). Miles and Huberman made data display a *central and explicit* part of analysis. Displays organize and summarize data and show how far analysis has progressed.

#### (c) Drawing and Verifying Conclusions

- Both inductive and deductive logic used.
- Conclusions early in analysis held **tentative and provisional** until enough data analyzed.
- Once formed, stated as propositions and **verified**.
- All three components are **interwoven and interactive** throughout.

---

### 2. Grounded Theory Analysis

Central theme: find a **core category** at a high level of abstraction that is grounded in data. Build theory from the bottom up from fact-based grounded data.

#### Three Objectives and Corresponding Coding Types

| Objective | Process / Coding |
|---|---|
| Find **substantive codes** (categories naming empirical data at more abstract level) | **Open coding** |
| Find **relationships among substantive codes** using theoretical codes | **Axial coding** |
| Find a high-order, more abstract construct (**core category**) integrating hypotheses into theory | **Selective coding** |

Note: These codings are not necessarily done sequentially; they may overlap and occur concurrently.

#### Three Types of Theoretical Codes in Grounded Theory

1. Codes *within* categories
2. Codes *between* categories
3. **Core codes** — most abstract, integrating all categories

#### Open Coding

**First process of conceptual analysis**. Analysis begins by "fracturing or breaking open" the data. The researcher examines data to condense into preliminary analytic categories or codes through close examination.

## Comparison of Qualitative and Quantitative Data Analysis

| Feature | Qualitative | Quantitative |
|---|---|---|
| Logic | Inductive (primarily) | Deductive (hypothesis testing) |
| Design | Emergent, flexible | Fixed in advance |
| Sampling | Purposive | Probability |
| Data type | Words, images, texts | Numbers |
| Generalizability | Limited; context-sensitive | Broad; representative |
| Starting point | Research questions, broad inquiry | Hypotheses from theory |
| Role of theory | Generated from data | Pre-specified; tested |
| Analysis | Coding, themes, narrative | Statistics |

---

## Ethical Dimensions of Qualitative Research

Ethical concerns specific to qualitative research:
- **Informed consent**: Participants must be aware they are being studied (where relevant).
- **Potential benefits and risks**: Must be weighed carefully.
- **Relationship with respondents**: Must be managed ethically throughout.
- **Anonymity and confidentiality**: Must be maintained where required.
- **Covert observation**: Conducting observation without the knowledge of those being observed raises ethical issues that must be considered carefully.
- Evaluative questions for any sampling plan (Miles and Huberman, 1994) explicitly include ethical dimensions.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Two paradigms**: Logical positivist (experimental, hypothesis testing) vs. Phenomenological (interpretive). Qualitative research belongs to the **phenomenological paradigm**.

2. **Ethnographic research definition** traces to **Malinowski (1926)** and **Spradley (1979)**: studying culture by analyzing subjective meaning; non-standardized data; texts and images rather than numbers.

3. **Patton (2002): 12 themes** in three categories: Design strategies (emergent design flexibility, purposive sampling, naturalistic inquiry); Data collection strategies (qualitative data, personal experience, empathic neutrality, dynamic systems); Analysis strategies (holistic perspective, inductive analysis, context sensitivity, reflexivity).

4. **Patton (2002): 16 theoretical perspectives**. Key discipline pairings: Ethnography = Anthropology; Phenomenology = Philosophy; Ethnomethodology = Sociology; Heuristic inquiry = Humanistic psychology; Symbolic interactionism = Sociology (Blumer 1969); Grounded theory = Glaser and Strauss (1967); Semiotics = Linguistics; Hermeneutics = Philosophy; Ecological psychology = Ecology and Psychology.

5. **Denzin and Lincoln (2005): Eight phases** in USA: Traditional (1900-1945), Modernist (1945-1970s), Blurred genres (until mid-1980s), Crisis of representation (mid-1980s), Narratives (1990s), Writing and democracy, New journals (2000-2004), Evidence-based practice.

6. **Grounded theory** = Glaser and Strauss (1967); *The Discovery of Grounded Theory* published **1967**. Theory generated inductively. Three coding types in order: **open coding** (substantive codes, first level) → **axial coding** (relationships among codes) → **selective coding** (core category). Stopping criterion = **theoretical saturation** (nothing new emerging).

7. **Narrative interview** (Germany, early 1980s) = one extensive main narration, no interruptions. **Objective hermeneutics** (Germany, early 1980s) = analyzing texts for latent meaning (Rosenthal 2004). **Triangulation** = proposed in mid-1980s Germany for validating results.

8. **Theoretical sampling** (Glaser and Strauss, 1967): cases selected based on relevance to emerging theory; stops at **theoretical saturation**.

9. **Miles and Huberman's three-component analysis**: Data reduction → Data display → Drawing and verifying conclusions. Labelled **"transcendental realism."** **Tesch (1990)** identified **26** approaches to qualitative data analysis.

10. **Scott (1990)** classified documents on two axes: authorship (3: personal/official-public/official-private) x access (4: closed/restricted/open-archival/open-published) = **12 types**. Four quality criteria: **authenticity, credibility, representativeness, meaning**.

11. **Unstructured interview** = also called **informal interview** or **ethnographic interview**. **Fontana and Frey (1994)** gave **seven aspects** for planning: accessing setting, understanding language/culture, deciding how to present oneself, locating informant, gaining trust, establishing rapport, collecting empirical materials.

12. **Focus group interview**: typically **6 to 8 participants**; researcher = **moderator/facilitator**; strength = **group dynamics** (Fontana and Frey, 1994). Originally from **market research**.

13. **Participant observation phases (Spradley, 1980)**: Descriptive observation → Focused observation → Selective observation.

14. **Triangulation** = using multiple data collection methods for corroboration and completeness of qualitative data.

15. **Pluralization of life-worlds** (**Flick, 2002**) = diversification of modern society; traditional class models no longer adequate; key rationale for qualitative research. **Bruner (1991)** = bottom-up, sensitive methods in varying social contexts.
