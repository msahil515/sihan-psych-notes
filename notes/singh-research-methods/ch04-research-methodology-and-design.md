## 1. Meaning and Characteristics of Scientific Research

**Scientific research** (Kerlinger, 1973): "A systematic, controlled, empirical and critical investigation of hypothetical propositions about the presumed relations among natural phenomena."

### Key Characteristics (Singh's list)

1. Always directed towards the **solution of a problem** (relationship between variables).
2. Based upon **empirical or observable evidence** (rejects subjective revelation).
3. Involves **precise observation and accurate description** (valid/reliable instruments).
4. Aims at developing **theories, principles, generalizations** enabling accurate prediction.
5. Characterized by **systematic, objective and logical procedures** (eliminates bias).
6. Marked by **patience and courage** (avoids hurried conclusions).
7. Requires **expertise** of the researcher and knowledge of relevant literature.
8. **Replicable**: designs, procedures and results reproducible by others.
9. Requires skill in **writing the research report** clearly.

### Assumptions of Science

| Assumption | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Nature is lawful** | Behaviour governed by natural laws; not random |
| **Behaviour is deterministic** | Caused by natural, identifiable causes; not free will |
| **Nature is understandable** | Can be studied and eventually fully comprehended |

### Attitudes of Scientists

- **Open-minded**: no preconceptions; consider all directions
- **Uncertain**: recognize limits of current knowledge
- **Fallible**: accept that current descriptions may be wrong
- **Cautious**: never use the terms "proof" or "proved"
- **Skeptical**: critically evaluate all evidence
- **Ethical**: avoid harm to subjects; no fraudulent acts

## 2. Phases/Stages in Scientific Research

Singh outlines eight sequential stages:

1. **Identifying the problem** (operationally defined; interrogative; two or more variables)
2. **Formulating a hypothesis** (testable; declarative)
3. **Identifying, manipulating and controlling variables** (IV, DV, extraneous)
4. **Formulating a research design** (blueprint for testing hypothesis)
5. **Constructing devices for observation and measurement** (tests, questionnaires)
6. **Summarizing results** (tabular or graphic methods)
7. **Carrying out statistical analysis** (parametric or non-parametric)
8. **Drawing conclusions** (generalization to population)

## 3. Types of Research

### A. By Application

| Type | Description | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Basic/Pure/Fundamental** | Develops theory or model; discovers broad generalizations | No concern with immediate application |
| **Applied** | Tests/applies theory to actual problems | Generalizes from sample; practical outcomes |
| **Action** | Immediate, local problem; term coined by **Kurt Lewin (1944)** | Emphasizes local applicability; not universal theory |

**Types of Applied Research** (Singh): (1) Evaluation research (formative vs summative), (2) Action research, (3) Social impact assessment research.

### B. By Objectives (Experimental vs Non-experimental)

| Type | Definition | Synonyms |
|---|---|---|
| **Experimental** | IV directly manipulated; subjects randomly assigned to conditions; S/R research | Lab experiment; field experiment |
| **Non-experimental** | IV cannot be manipulated; subjects not randomly assigned; R/R research | Descriptive; post facto research |

**Non-experimental subtypes** (Singh's six):

| Subtype | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| **Field studies** | Study in natural settings; no manipulation |
| **Ex post facto** | "After the fact"; IV already occurred; no manipulation by researcher |
| **Survey research** | Systematic collection of data from a defined population |
| **Content/Document analysis** | Analysis of recorded communication |
| **Case study** | Intensive study of a single individual/group |
| **Ethnographic study** | Cultural description; participant observation |

### C. By Inquiry Mode

| Type | Features |
|---|---|
| **Quantitative** | Objectives, design, sample, statistics predetermined; structured |
| **Qualitative** | Flexibility in process; unstructured; inductive; meaning-focused |

### D. Types of Experiment (Singh)

1. **Exploratory**: Little prior knowledge; vague hypotheses; "I wonder what would happen if..."
2. **Confirmatory**: Clear hypothesis; findings test hypothesis; "I'll bet this would happen if..."
3. **Crucial**: Tests all possible hypotheses simultaneously; ideal but rarely achieved
4. **Pilot**: Miniature version of final study; "dress rehearsal"; identifies procedural bugs

## 4. Experimental Validity: Internal and External

### Internal Validity

**Internal validity**: the extent to which the IV alone affected the DV; the study is free from confounding. Tuckman (1978): "A study has internal validity if the outcome is a function of the programme being tested rather than other causes."

### Threats to Internal Validity (Campbell & Stanley, 1966)

| Threat | Description |
|---|---|
| **History** | Events external to experiment occurring during experimentation (e.g., news event affects scores) |
| **Maturation** | Biological/psychological changes in subjects over time (especially long experiments) |
| **Testing** | Pretest experience improves post-test performance (practice effect); raises subject awareness |
| **Instrumentation** | Changes in measuring instruments, observer fatigue, or rater drift |
| **Statistical regression** | Extreme scorers regress toward the mean on re-testing (chance factors affect extreme scores most) |
| **Selection bias** | Non-equivalent groups; control and experimental groups differ at start |
| **Experimental mortality** | Subject dropout; those who remain are more motivated, creating imbalance |
| **Diffusion of treatment** | Subjects communicate across conditions; treatment leaks to control group |
| **Compensatory behaviour** | Control group rivalry or resentful demoralization when they know they lack treatment |
| **Experimenter's expectation** | Researcher unconsciously communicates desired outcomes to subjects |

> **Key Point:** Statistical regression occurs when groups are selected on extreme scores. High scorers regress down; low scorers regress up toward the mean on post-test, even without treatment.

### External Validity

**External validity**: generalizability of results to other subjects, settings, and times. Tuckman: "A study has external validity if the result would apply to the real world."

| External Threat | Description |
|---|---|
| **Artificial situation** | Lab control makes situation unlike real life; limits generalization |
| **Interaction of selection bias** | Non-representative sample; results may not generalize to population |
| **Prior knowledge of subjects** | Experimenter bias or cue-giving |
| **Carry-over effects of prior treatment** | Within-subjects designs; first treatment effects carry into second |
| **Hawthorne effect** | Awareness of participation increases performance (Mayo, Roethlisberger & Dickson, 1920) |
| **Temporal validity** | Generalizability across different time periods |
| **Ecological validity** | Extent to which experimental situation represents natural settings |

> **Key Point:** Internal validity is MORE important than external validity. If internal validity is absent, external validity is automatically compromised.

### Controlling Threats

Methods: (i) **Reliable manipulation** of IV; (ii) **Control group** use; (iii) **Randomization**; (iv) **Blind/double-blind** procedures; (v) **Pilot studies**; (vi) **Automation** of stimulus presentation.

## 5. Sampling

### Meaning

A **population/universe**: any identifiable, well-specified group. A **sample**: a smaller representation of the population selected by some rule. A measure from sample = **statistic**; from population = **parameter**.

**Sampling error**: difference between parameter and statistic. Greater with smaller samples and more heterogeneous populations.

### A. Probability Sampling Methods

Each element has a known, equal probability of inclusion. Requires: (i) known population size, (ii) equal chance for each element, (iii) specified sample size.

| Method | Description | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple random sampling** | Each individual has equal, independent chance | Fishbowl draw, table of random numbers, computer; foundation of all probability sampling |
| **Stratified random sampling (proportionate)** | Population divided into strata; random sample drawn from each in same proportion as population | Increases precision; minimizes sampling error |
| **Stratified random sampling (disproportionate)** | Equal or varied numbers drawn from each stratum regardless of proportion | Overrepresents some strata; useful for underrepresented groups |
| **Area/Cluster sampling** | Geographic areas drawn randomly; all elements in chosen areas interviewed | Multi-stage sampling; useful for large geographic surveys |
| **Multistage sampling** | Cluster sampling with multiple stages of subdivision | Area first, then sub-areas, then individuals |
| **Systematic sampling** | Every nth person from a list | First element random; then fixed interval; mixed probability/non-probability traits |

### B. Non-probability Sampling Methods

No basis for estimating probability of inclusion; samples not necessarily representative.

| Method | Description | Key Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Quota sampling** | Strata identified; individuals selected arbitrarily (not randomly) from each; "poor man's proportionate stratified sample" | Quick surveys; crude results |
| **Purposive/Judgemental** | Handpicked as typical of population; based on investigator's judgment | Elections forecasting; expert panels |
| **Accidental/Incidental** | Selected by convenience/availability; also called **convenience sampling** (Heiman, 1995) or **opportunity sampling** (Pennington, 2002) | Most convenient; least generalizable |
| **Snowball** | Sociometric chain: initial subject leads to others; useful for "hidden populations" (addicts, criminals) | Small N; social network studies |
| **Saturation sampling** | All individuals with the characteristic of interest | Small communities; N not exceeding 1,000 |
| **Dense sampling** | 50% or more from population with specified characteristics (between random and saturation) | Majority selected |

### C. Mixed Sampling

**Systematic sampling** has characteristics of both probability (first element random) and non-probability (fixed interval excludes intervening elements).

### Sampling Distribution and Sampling Error

- **Sample distribution**: data from the drawn sample
- **Population distribution**: data from entire population (usually unknown)
- **Sampling distribution**: theoretical distribution of a statistic computed from all possible samples of fixed size from the population

**Principles of Sampling** (Singh):
1. There will almost always be a difference between sample statistic and population parameter (sampling error).
2. The greater the sample size, the more accurate the estimate of the population parameter.

### How Large Should a Sample Be?

Depends on: (a) degree of accuracy required, (b) variability/heterogeneity of population, (c) number of variables to be analyzed simultaneously.

| Population Size | Sampling Ratio Needed |
|---|---|
| Small (under 1,000) | At least 30% (sample ~300) |
| Moderately large (10,000) | ~10% (sample ~1,000) |
| Large (160,000) | ~1% (sample ~1,600) |

> **Key Point:** Samples of 30+ are considered large; Student's t (Gosset, 1915) enables valid inference from small samples. Samples larger than necessary increase cost and logistic problems without proportionate gain in accuracy.

## 6. Variables

### Types of Variables

**From causation:**
- **Independent variable (IV)**: manipulated/selected by experimenter; stimulus variable (Underwood); cause
- **Dependent variable (DV)**: measured outcome; response variable; effect; what experimenter predicts
- **Extraneous variable**: uncontrolled; may affect DV; also called **relevant variable**; must be controlled to avoid **confounding**
- **Intervening variable**: internal mediating state linking IV to DV (e.g., anger intervenes between frustration and aggression)

**Note on confounding**: when an extraneous variable co-varies with IV, experiment is confounded; impossible to attribute changes in DV to IV alone.

### Techniques of Controlling Extraneous Variables

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Elimination** | Remove the extraneous variable from the situation entirely (e.g., soundproofing) |
| **Constancy of conditions** | Hold extraneous variables constant for all subjects (same instructions, time, apparatus) |
| **Balancing** | Give equal treatment to all groups; extraneous variables affect all equally; use control group |
| **Counterbalancing** | Distribute practice and fatigue (order effects) equally across conditions; each condition precedes and follows all others equally |
| **Randomization** | Random assignment controls unknown extraneous variables; "insurance"; makes observations independent |
| **Statistical control** | Use ANCOVA or partial correlation to statistically remove the effect of covariates |

**Counterbalancing** assumes **asymmetrical transfer** needs control. Technique: each condition must appear equally often and in each position.

## 7. The Problem and the Hypothesis

### Research Problem: Characteristics

A **problem statement** has three essential characteristics (Singh):
1. Written **clearly and unambiguously**, usually in **question form**
2. Expresses **relationship between two or more variables** (testable)
3. **Testable by empirical methods** (data can be collected)

Additional: avoid moral/ethical judgements; sufficient importance; not too broad nor too specific.

**Steps in formulating a research problem** (Singh's seven):
1. Identify broad field of interest
2. Dissect into subareas
3. Select the subarea of most interest (elimination process)
4. Raise research questions
5. Formulate objectives and subobjectives
6. Assess feasibility of objectives
7. Do a double-check

### Hypothesis: Meaning and Characteristics

A **hypothesis** (McGuigan, 1990): "A testable statement of a potential relationship between two or more variables, advanced as a potential solution to the problem." Stated in **declarative sentence** form (unlike problem which is interrogative).

**Characteristics of a good hypothesis** (Singh's eight):
1. **Conceptually clear**: operationally defined concepts
2. **Testable**: can be found probably true or probably false
3. **Economical and parsimonious**: fewer variables; less expensive
4. **Related to existing theory and fact**
5. **Logical unity and comprehensiveness**
6. **General in scope**: permits several deductions
7. **Related to available tools and techniques**
8. **In accord with other hypotheses** in the field

### Types of Hypotheses

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Universal** | Relationship holds for all levels, all times, all places | "Adequate light increases reading efficiency" |
| **Existential** | Holds for at least one case | "At least one schizophrenic has no delusion or hallucination" |
| **Causal** | Postulates a causal influence | "Boring ads cause channel changing" |
| **Descriptive** | Describes characteristics without claiming causality | "Channel changing occurs more when alone" |
| **Simple** | Two variables only | "Reward improves learning" |
| **Complex** | More than two variables | "Children from upper/lower SES have more adult adjustment problems than middle SES" |
| **Research/Working** | Derived from theory; what researcher believes | "IQ is positively related to classroom achievement" |
| **Alternative (H₁)** | Operational form of research hypothesis; asserts IV has effect | Accepted when H₀ rejected |
| **Null (H₀)** | No-effect/no-difference hypothesis; formulated to be rejected | "Mean IQ of two groups is equal" |

> **Key Point:** Null hypothesis is stated for the express purpose of being rejected. If H₀ rejected, H₁ is accepted, which strengthens the research hypothesis.

**Directional vs Non-directional:**
- **Directional (one-tailed)**: specifies direction of difference (Group A > Group B)
- **Non-directional (two-tailed)**: specifies only that groups differ, not direction

### Ways of Stating a Hypothesis

Russell's form: **"If...then..."** (general implication): "If reward is given, then learning is improved." Modern publications often use direct declarative form: "Reward improves learning" (equivalent; can be restated in If-then form).

### Functions of Hypotheses

1. Test various theories
2. Provide direction to research
3. Help identify relevant and irrelevant variables
4. Enable prediction and explanation

### Sources of Hypotheses

1. Observations and experiences
2. Review of existing research
3. Existing research itself (generates additional hypotheses)
4. Theory (logically organized propositions; e.g., Freudian theory)
5. Models (flow charts/diagrams of psychological processes; e.g., Information Processing Model)

## 8. Research Design

### Meaning and Purpose

**Research design**: the detailed blueprint of procedures adopted for testing hypotheses and analyzing data. Ensures relevant data collected in a way permitting objective analysis.

**Three criteria of a good research design** (Singh):
1. **Capability to answer research questions adequately** (appropriate design for the hypothesis)
2. **Control of variables** (especially extraneous variables; randomization is best)
3. **Generalizability** (external validity; can results be extended beyond the sample?)

### Basic Principles of Experimental Design (Fisher, per Ostle & Mensing, 1975)

| Principle | Description |
|---|---|
| **Replication** | Deliberate repetition with different subjects, settings, and times; provides accurate estimate of experimental error; not the same as multiple measurements |
| **Randomization** | Ensures independence of observations; makes statistical tests valid; controls extraneous variables automatically; "insurance" |
| **Local control** | Balancing, blocking, and grouping of subjects to make design statistically sound |

**Local control** involves: **Grouping** (homogeneous subjects assigned to groups), **Blocking** (homogeneous units within blocks), **Balancing** (grouping and blocking done symmetrically).

### Between-Subjects vs Within-Subjects Designs

| Feature | Between-Subjects | Within-Subjects (Repeated measures) |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Separate group for each condition | Same group tested under all conditions |
| **Economy of subjects** | Requires more subjects | Fewer subjects needed |
| **Individual differences** | Not controlled (different subjects) | Automatically controlled |
| **Error variance** | Higher (individual differences) | Lower |
| **Order effects** | Not a problem | Problem (practice, fatigue); need counterbalancing |
| **Appropriate when** | Asymmetric transfer expected; carryover effects serious | Small N available; no good matching tasks; psychophysics/scaling |

**Controlling sequence effects in within-subjects**: counterbalancing (ABBA, complete counterbalancing, random orders); assumes asymmetric transfer is present and must be distributed equally.

**Types of between-subjects designs:**
- **Two-randomized-groups design** (t test/Mann-Whitney U)
- **More-than-two-randomized-groups design / Multi-groups design** (ANOVA/F ratio)
- **Matched-groups design** (groups matched on mean, SD, pairs)
- **Factorial design** (two or more IVs; independent and interactive effects)

## 9. Campbell & Stanley Classification of Research Designs

**Symbols used:**
- **R** = Random selection or assignment
- **X** = Treatment/experimental manipulation
- **O** = Observation/measurement/test
- Left-to-right = temporal order; vertical rows (no dashed line) = randomized; **dashed line** = groups NOT equated by randomization

### Pre-Experimental Designs (Nondesigns)

| Design | Notation | Threats Controlled | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **One-shot case study** | `X O` | None | No control group; no baseline; cannot infer causation |
| **One-group pretest-posttest** | `O₁ X O₂` | Selection, mortality (partially) | History, maturation, testing, instrumentation NOT controlled |
| **Static-group comparison** | `X O₁` / `--- O₂` | History, maturation, testing, regression | Selection NOT controlled (no random assignment; groups not equivalent) |

### True Experimental Designs

| Design | Notation | Controls |
|---|---|---|
| **Posttest-only, equivalent-group** | `R X O₁` / `R O₂` | History, maturation, selection, mortality; no pretest needed; strongest design |
| **Pretest-posttest control group** | `R O₁ X O₂` / `R O₃ O₄` | History, maturation, regression, selection, mortality; BUT testing effect and interaction of testing x treatment NOT controlled |
| **Solomon four-group** | `R O₁ X O₂` / `R O₃ O₄` / `R X O₅` / `R O₆` | All above; also controls reactive effects of testing; replication in four ways; developed by Solomon (1949) |

> **Key Point:** Solomon four-group design = Pretest-posttest control group + Posttest-only control group simultaneously. Controls BOTH internal and external validity threats. Four replications of X effect.

### Quasi-Experimental Designs

| Design | Notation | Controls | Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Time-series** | `O₁O₂O₃O₄ X O₅O₆O₇O₈` | Maturation, testing, selection, mortality | History NOT controlled |
| **Equivalent time-samples** | `X₁O₁ X₀O₂ X₁O₃ X₀O₄` | History (controls by repeated introduction of X) | Multiple interference lowers generalizability |
| **Non-equivalent control group** | `O₁ X O₂` / `--- O₃ O₄` | Maturation, history | Selection NOT controlled (intact groups; no random assignment) |
| **Counterbalanced design** | Multiple groups receive all treatments in different orders (Latin square) | Order effects | Multiple interference |

**Additional quasi-designs**: Separate-sample pretest-posttest; Patched-up design (combines inadequate designs to overcome mutual weaknesses); Longitudinal; Cross-sectional; Cohort.

### Ex Post Facto Design

In **ex post facto research**: experimenter evaluates effects of a naturally occurring treatment after it has occurred. IV handled by selection, not manipulation. Difficult to establish cause-effect.

| Type | Description | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **Correlational design** | Two sets of data from same group; correlation computed (O₁ → O₂) | Cannot establish causation; three possible interpretations for any correlation |
| **Criterion-group design** | Group with criterion behavior vs. without (C O₁ / O₂) | Selection not controlled; cannot establish causation |

## 10. Single-Subject / Small-N Research Designs

### Nature and Rationale

Single-subject (N=1) research: pioneered by Ebbinghaus (memory), Watson & Raynor (Little Albert), Skinner ("study one rat for 1,000 hours"). **Sidman (1960)**: group averages can characterize no single individual; obscure individual performance. Historical roots: Wundt's Leipzig lab; small N studies common before Fisher's ANOVA (1930s).

**Justification for small N**: (i) group data obscures individual differences, (ii) clinical populations are small, (iii) intensive study reveals functional relationships better.

### Withdrawal Designs

| Design | Phase Sequence | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **A-B design** | Baseline → Intervention | Simplest; baseline then treatment; weak (no return to baseline) |
| **A-B-A design** | Baseline → Intervention → Baseline | Withdraws treatment to confirm effect; stronger evidence |
| **A-B-A-B design (reversal)** | Baseline → Intervention → Baseline → Intervention | Strongest withdrawal design; intervention effect replicated; behavior should return to baseline when treatment withdrawn and improve again when reinstated |

> **Key Point:** A-B-A-B is called the **reversal design** because treatment is removed (reversed) in the second A stage. If behavior reverses with removal and improves again with reinstatement, functional relationship is demonstrated (Kazdin, 1982).

**Alternating-treatment design**: Two different treatments alternated randomly; evaluates relative effectiveness of treatments without a baseline withdrawal.

### Multiple-Baseline Designs

No need to withdraw treatment (advantage over withdrawal designs). Replication across:

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Across behaviours** | Multiple compatible behaviours; treatment applied to one at a time; others serve as controls |
| **Across subjects** | Same behaviour in different subjects; time-staggered intervention (also: **time-lagged control design**) |
| **Across conditions/environments** | Same behaviour in different settings; different baseline periods |

**Reversal designs**: Two incompatible behaviours; treatments reversed between them.

## 11. Historical Research

**Historical research** (Kerlinger, 1986): "The critical investigation of events, developments and experiences of the past, the careful weighing of evidence of the validity of the sources of information on the past, and the interpretation of the weighted evidences."

Not merely chronological events; integrated account of relationships among persons, times, and events. Can be qualitative or quantitative.

### Steps in Historical Research

1. Selection of a problem (suitable for historical investigation; social utility)
2. Formulation of hypotheses (evaluated against gathered evidence)
3. Collection of data (primary and secondary sources)
4. Analysis of data and generalization (critical evaluation of evidence; principles of probability)

### Sources of Historical Data

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary sources** | Eyewitness accounts; actual observers/participants | Documents (official records, diaries, newspapers, maps, films), Remains/relics (fossils, tools, art), Oral testimony |
| **Secondary sources** | Not directly witnessed; reporter narrates what another said or wrote | History textbooks, encyclopedias; less reliable due to information transfer errors |

> **Key Point:** Same material can be primary for one purpose and secondary for another. A history textbook is ordinarily secondary; but if studying changes in textbook content over time, it becomes primary.

### Historical Criticism

| Type | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **External criticism** | Establishes **authenticity/genuineness** of data (signature, handwriting, physical/chemical tests, consistency with known technology/knowledge of the period) |
| **Internal criticism** | Establishes **accuracy/worth** of data (were authors honest? Unbiased? Under pressure? In agreement with other authorities?) |

### Limitations of Historical Research

(i) Dependability of data uncertain, especially for distant past. (ii) No life-size history possible; historian selects what is "relevant." (iii) Not all happenings are known. (iv) Personal biases and private interpretations enter unconsciously. (v) Problem may be too broad; historians should choose "penetrating analysis of a limited problem."

## 12. Qualitative Research

### Meaning and Features

**Qualitative research**: flexible, unstructured; inductive; naturalistic; studies participants in natural settings; researcher is primary instrument; analysis done with words.

**Key features** (Miles & Huberman, 1994; Lesch, 1990):

1. Conducted through **prolonged contact** with a field/life situation in natural settings
2. Researcher seeks a **holistic overview** of the unit studied
3. Data gathered from the **inside perspective** (empathic understanding; suspends preconceptions)
4. Identifies **themes and expressions** in their original forms
5. Explicates how individuals **understand, take action, and manage** daily situations
6. Researcher uses **little standardized instruments**; is the main measurement device
7. Most analysis done **with words** (assembled, sub-clustered, organized into semiotic segments)

### Major Qualitative Research Design Strategies (Singh's five)

| Strategy | Description | Key Figures |
|---|---|---|
| **Ethnography** | Cultural description; prolonged participant observation; studying people in natural settings; "describing a culture from inside" | Malinowski; Chicago School; Hammersley & Atkinson |
| **Case study** | Intensive study of single individual, group, or phenomenon; clinical psychology applications | Used in India for women's problems (Gandhi, 1995) |
| **Document/Content analysis** | Analysis of recorded communication and texts | Singh Ch. 12 |
| **Grounded theory** | Theory generated inductively from data; developed by Glaser & Strauss (1967); no up-front theory or hypothesis; "Discovery of Grounded Theory" | Glaser & Strauss; Strauss & Corbin (1990) |
| **Retrospective studies** | Past events/processes analyzed from present viewpoint; biographical research | Narrative and hermeneutic analysis |

### Qualitative vs Quantitative Research

| Feature | Quantitative | Qualitative |
|---|---|---|
| **Design** | Predetermined; structured | Flexible; emergent |
| **Hypothesis** | Formulated before data collection | May develop during study |
| **Sampling** | Probability sampling (representativeness) | Purposive sampling (information-rich) |
| **Data** | Numerical; statistics | Words, narratives, themes |
| **Goal** | Test theory; determine extent of problem | Generate theory; explore nature of problem |
| **Generalization** | Statistical; to population | Theoretical; transferability |
| **Researcher role** | Detached; objective | Primary instrument; reflexive |

### Theoretical Perspectives of Qualitative Research (Patton, 2002: 16 perspectives)

Key ones for exam:
- **Phenomenology**: meaning, structure, essence of lived experience; "What is the meaning of this experience for these persons?"
- **Ethnography**: cultural phenomena; physical and social environment
- **Grounded theory**: theory generation from comparative data analysis
- **Case study**: intensive single-unit analysis
- **Narrative analysis/Narratology**: what does the story reveal about individual and culture?
- **Symbolic interactionism**: people act on meaning of words/things derived from social interaction (Blumer, 1969)

### Sampling in Qualitative Research

Primarily **purposive sampling** (deliberate; selects information-rich cases). Miles & Huberman (1994) list 16 qualitative sampling strategies. Key types: complete collection, theoretical sampling (Glaser & Strauss; stops at **theoretical saturation**), extreme/deviant case, typical case, maximum variation, intensity, convenience sampling.

| **Extraneous/Confounding variable** | Uncontrolled variable co-varying with IV |
| **Counterbalancing** | Technique to distribute order effects equally across conditions |
| **Null hypothesis (H₀)** | No-effect hypothesis; formulated to be rejected |
| **Alternative hypothesis (H₁)** | Operational statement of research hypothesis; accepted if H₀ rejected |
| **Research/Working hypothesis** | Derived from theory; what researcher believes to be true |
| **Replication** | Deliberate repetition of experiment under nearly identical conditions |
| **Randomization** | Principle ensuring independence of observations; controls extraneous variables |
| **Local control** | Balancing, blocking, grouping of experimental units |
| **Pre-experimental design** | Lacks control group or equivalence; fails to control internal validity threats |
| **True experimental design** | Randomized control and experimental groups; controls all internal validity threats |
| **Quasi-experimental design** | Some control over IV but not equivalent groups; between pre- and true experimental |
| **Solomon four-group design** | Combines posttest-only and pretest-posttest designs; controls reactive effects of testing |
| **A-B-A-B (reversal) design** | Single-subject design with two baseline and two treatment phases |
| **Multiple-baseline design** | Replication design; treatment applied sequentially; no withdrawal needed |
| **Theoretical saturation** | Point in grounded theory where new data yield no new theoretical insights |
| **Grounded theory** | Research strategy to generate theory inductively from data (Glaser & Strauss, 1967) |
| **External criticism** | Establishes authenticity/genuineness of historical data |
| **Internal criticism** | Establishes accuracy/worth of historical data |
| **Ethnography** | Describing culture from inside; prolonged participant observation |
| **Action research** | Focus on immediate local problems; term coined by Kurt Lewin (1944) |
| **Ex post facto research** | IV already occurred; researcher evaluates existing conditions; no manipulation |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Kerlinger's definition of scientific research** (systematic, controlled, empirical, critical): memorize verbatim for definitional questions.

2. **Three assumptions of science** (nature is lawful, behaviour is deterministic, nature is understandable): all three are required; "free will" is rejected by determinism.

3. **Basic vs Applied vs Action research**: Basic = theory development; Applied = testing theory; Action = local, immediate (Lewin, 1944); not about universal validity.

4. **Six types of non-experimental research** (Singh): field studies, ex post facto, survey, content/document analysis, case study, ethnographic study.

5. **Eight threats to internal validity** (Campbell & Stanley): History, Maturation, Testing, Instrumentation, Statistical Regression, Selection, Mortality, + Diffusion of treatment. Statistical regression specifically applies to groups selected on extreme scores.

6. **Hawthorne effect**: increases performance; Mayo et al. (1920); threat to external validity specifically.

7. **Internal vs external validity**: Internal is more important; lack of internal validity automatically affects external validity.

8. **Sampling probability conditions**: (i) population size known, (ii) each element has equal chance, (iii) sample size specified.

9. **Quota sampling**: stratified but NOT random in final selection = "poor man's proportionate stratified sample."

10. **Snowball sampling**: for hidden populations (addicts, criminals); sociometric; chain referral.

11. **Fisher's three principles**: Replication, Randomization, Local Control. Replication ≠ multiple measurements of same subject.

12. **Campbell & Stanley design notation (X/O)**: memorize the three pre-experimental, three true, and two key quasi-experimental designs with their notation and which threats each controls/fails to control.

13. **Solomon four-group design**: combines posttest-only + pretest-posttest; four replications of treatment effect; controls reactive effects of testing; Solomon (1949).

14. **A-B-A-B = reversal design**: because treatment is reversed/removed in second A; reinstated in second B; demonstrates functional relationship.

15. **Multiple-baseline advantage** over withdrawal designs: treatment never needs to be withdrawn.

16. **Historical criticism**: External = genuineness; Internal = accuracy/worth.

17. **Primary vs secondary sources**: Primary = eyewitness; Secondary = reported by someone who was not there. Same document can be primary for one purpose, secondary for another.

18. **Grounded theory** (Glaser & Strauss, 1967): theory generated from data (inductive); no prior hypothesis; stops at theoretical saturation.

19. **Qualitative sampling**: purposive/deliberate (not probability); theoretical sampling stops at theoretical saturation.

20. **Null hypothesis**: always stated for express purpose of being rejected; no-difference hypothesis; if rejected, H₁ accepted.

21. **Types of hypotheses**: Universal > Existential (greater predictive power); directional (one-tailed) vs non-directional (two-tailed).

22. **Characteristics of a good hypothesis** (Singh's 8): clear, testable, parsimonious, related to theory/fact, logically unified, general, related to available tools, in accord with other hypotheses.

23. **Steps in research problem formulation** (7 steps): identify field → dissect to subareas → select subarea → raise questions → formulate objectives → assess feasibility → double-check.
