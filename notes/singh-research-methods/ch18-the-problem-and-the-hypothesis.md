# The Problem and The Hypothesis

Chapter 18 covers the full lifecycle of scientific inquiry from the initial sense of unease through to formal hypothesis testing. It defines what constitutes a research problem, how to select and formulate one, and then moves into the nature, types, sources, and functions of hypotheses. Both topics are foundational for understanding how any behavioural science investigation is structured.

---

## Meaning and Characteristics of a Research Problem

### From Unease to Problem Statement

Scientific investigation begins with an accumulation of facts that still leave something unexplained. The progression has three stages:

1. **Diffuse feeling of unease** — a vague, general discomfort that something requires closer study.
2. **Research problem** — when the diffuse feeling is sharpened and better defined.
3. **Problem statement** — when the problem is cast in a researchable, formal form.

A **research problem** is defined as some difficulty a researcher experiences in a theoretical or practical situation for which a solution is sought. Formulating the problem statement is often the hardest part of the research process.

### Essential Characteristics of a Problem Statement

A problem statement must satisfy two mandatory criteria to be considered acceptable:

1. It must **generate hypotheses**.
2. It must be **verifiable**, usually by empirical means.

### Three Basic Issues Every Researchable Problem Must Address

- There is a **relationship between two or more variables**.
- The problem must be **researchable** (data can be gathered).
- There must be **no moral or ethical conflict** in investigating the problem.

## Research Questions

### Characteristics of a Good Research Question

A **good research question** has four core properties:

| Property | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Clear** | Unambiguous and well understood by the research community |
| **Answerable** | Has specific answers derivable from collectable data |
| **Connected** | Linked to existing research and literature |
| **Substantively relevant** | Interesting and makes a significant contribution to the field |

### Characteristics of a Bad Research Question

A **bad research question** fails one or more of the above conditions. Two common failures:

- **Too general**: e.g., "What is the meaning of life?" — no two or more variables, too vague to answer definitively.
- **Confounded**: e.g., a question that attempts to address multiple issues simultaneously, making it impossible to isolate variables.

A bad question may also be one that calls for ethical judgements (objective research cannot answer normative questions) or one that is too broad to be operationally tested.

## Sources of Stating a Research Problem

Locating a suitable research problem is a difficult task. Multiple sources have been identified in the literature.

### Researcher's Own Experiences

The researcher's personal academic and professional experiences are a primary source of research ideas. Consulting research professors, colleagues, and field experts is also advised.

### Baker and Keller (1998) Sources

**Baker and Keller (1998)** outlined several sources for locating a research problem, including:

1. Programmed activities
2. Research proposals
3. Study of government policies
4. Framework policies and practices
5. Social values
6. Extracurricular activities
7. Educational activities
8. Employment, political systems, and occupation

Additional sources they listed include: meta-analysis, self-image analysis, and vocational objectives of students.

### Young (1956) — Three Major General Sources

**Young (1956)** proposed three broad source categories for locating a research problem in behavioural sciences:

| Source | Description |
|---|---|
| **(i) Documentary sources** | Official and unofficial statistics, local newspaper accounts, census publications — yield descriptive material |
| **(ii) Key informants** | Professionals with relevant knowledge and insight; persons directly responsible for or involved in the behaviour under study |
| **(iii) Library sources** | Textbooks, journals, monographs, newspaper articles — supply practical and theoretical knowledge |

## Important Considerations in Selecting a Research Problem

Before starting an investigation, six evaluative questions must be answered affirmatively:

1. **Is the problem significant?** Will the solution make a meaningful difference to psychological, educational, or social theory or practice?
2. **Can the problem be solved by research?** Can relevant data actually be gathered?
3. **Is the problem new/original?** Has it already been fully investigated? Replication is permissible only when verification or generalization is the goal.
4. **Does the problem have theoretical value?** Will it fill a gap in the literature or advance a theory?
5. **Is the problem workable?** Researcher must confirm:
   - Competence to carry it out
   - Sufficient time
   - Adequate financial resources
   - Availability of subjects' cooperation
6. **Are pertinent data and instruments available?** Are valid and reliable data-collection tools accessible?

## Ways in Which a Research Problem Is Manifested

A problem is said to exist when we know there is something we do not know. There are three ways a problem is manifested:

### (1) Remarkable Gap in the Results of Investigations

A **noticeable gap or absence of information** signals a problem. Example: a community wants to provide psychotherapy but cannot determine which form is most effective for which neurosis — no existing knowledge fills that gap.

### (2) Contradictory Results of Investigations

When several studies in the same area yield **inconsistent or contradictory findings**, a problem exists. Classic examples:

- **Reinforcement and learning**: some studies confirm learning cannot occur without reinforcement; others confirm it can occur without reinforcement.
- **Rest pause and learning**: one set of experiments shows progressively increasing rest periods are better; a second shows progressively decreasing rest periods are better; a third shows no difference. The hidden extraneous variables must be identified and controlled.

### (3) Isolated Fact in the Form of Unexplained Information

When a researcher has a **fact that cannot be explained or placed within existing knowledge**, a problem is manifested. The classic illustration is **Max Wertheimer's discovery of the phi phenomenon**.

> **Phi phenomenon**: Wertheimer observed apparent movement between two stationary stimuli (e.g., perceived movement from one telephone pole to another while on a train, even though both poles were stationary). This isolated observation could not be explained by existing psychological theories.

Wertheimer, along with **Wolfgang Kohler** and **Kurt Koffka** (who later became major psychologists), systematically studied the phenomenon and advanced a new theoretical framework — the **Gestalt theory** — based on the principle that phenomena must be understood as wholes, not by dissecting them into isolated parts.

## Types of Research Problems

Research problems are broadly categorized into two types:

### (i) Solvable Problem

A **solvable problem** raises questions that can be answered using normal empirical capacities. Two defining features:

- Can be solved by **empirical methods** (observation of natural events).
- A **suitable hypothesis** can be advanced as a tentative solution.

Science addresses itself exclusively to solvable problems.

*Example*: "Does reincarnation facilitate learning?" — though unusual, it is technically a solvable problem because it implies an observable, testable relationship.

### (ii) Unsolvable Problem

An **unsolvable problem** raises questions that cannot be answered by normal empirical capacities. Such problems are generally metaphysical.

*Examples*: "How does our mind work?" / "Is there a divinity that shapes our ends?"

Three characteristics of an unsolvable problem:

| Characteristic | Description |
|---|---|
| **Unstructuredness** | Subject is unclear; area is so vague that relevant observations cannot be specified or related to the formulation |
| **Inadequately defined terms** | Operational definition is not possible; variables cannot be operationalized (e.g., "human nature") |
| **Data cannot be collected** | Even if structured, relevant empirical data cannot be gathered |

---

## Importance of Formulating a Research Problem

- Formulating a research problem is the **first and most significant step** in the research process — analogous to identifying a destination before a journey.
- It determines the **variables** to be studied, the **research design**, the **sampling strategy**, the **procedures**, and the **type of analysis**.
- **Kerlinger (1986)** stressed: the researcher must know clearly what the problem is — not just what he thinks he should find, but what he actually wants to find out.
- The principle of **GIGO (Garbage In, Garbage Out)** from computer science applies: vague or poor problem input yields questionable, unbelievable output.

---

## Steps in Formulating a Research Problem

Seven sequential steps are involved:

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1. Identify broad area** | Select a broad field of interest (e.g., clinical psychology, social psychology, industrial/organizational psychology) |
| **2. Dissect into subareas** | Break the broad area into smaller subareas (e.g., clinical psychology → mental health of the elderly, causes of depression, treatment of phobia) |
| **3. Select a subarea** | Choose the subarea of highest interest, manageable scope, and matching expertise |
| **4. Raise research questions** | Pose all questions the researcher wants answered within the subarea; eliminate unmanageable ones |
| **5. Formulate objectives** | Transform research questions into declarative, action-oriented objectives ("to determine", "to examine", "to explore") |
| **6. Assess objectives** | Evaluate feasibility in terms of financial resources, human resources, and technical expertise |
| **7. Double-check** | Reflect on genuine interest and readiness; reassess objectives if any answer is "no" |

**Key distinction**: Research questions are in **interrogative form**; objectives are in **declarative/transformational form** with action-oriented verbs.

## Meaning and Characteristics of a Good Hypothesis

### Definition

A **hypothesis** is a testable statement of a potential relationship between two or more variables, offered as a tentative solution to a research problem.

Key definitions:

- **McGuigan (1994, p. 159)**: "A testable statement of a potential relationship between two (or more) variables; that is advanced as potential solution to the problem."
- **Kerlinger (1973, p. 16)**: "A conjectural statement of the relation between two or more variables. Hypotheses are always in declarative sentence form, and they relate, either generally or specifically, variables to variables."

### Two General Characteristics of a Hypothesis

1. It is a **testable statement** — variables are measurable or potentially measurable.
2. It exhibits either a **general or specific relationship** between variables.

### Problem vs. Hypothesis: Key Distinctions

| Dimension | Research Problem | Hypothesis |
|---|---|---|
| Form | Interrogative sentence | Declarative sentence |
| Testability | Not directly testable | Directly testable |
| Direction | Raises the question | Proposes the answer |

---

## Criteria of a Good Hypothesis

Eight criteria define a good hypothesis:

1. **Conceptually clear**: Uses well-defined, operationally acceptable, and communicable concepts.
2. **Testable**: Can be tested directly and found probably true or probably false; not too broad.
3. **Economical and parsimonious**: The simplest hypothesis that accounts for the phenomena is preferred (principle of parsimony).
4. **Related to existing theory and fact**: Must connect with the established body of knowledge; not purely speculative.
5. **Logically unified and comprehensive**: The most logical and comprehensive hypothesis is preferred when alternatives exist.
6. **General in scope**: A general hypothesis explains several facts at once and permits multiple deductions; however, excessively broad hypotheses are not testable.
7. **Related to available scientific tools and techniques**: Data must be collectable using existing instruments and methods.
8. **In accord with other hypotheses in the same field**: Consistency with established hypotheses in the domain is desirable (though not absolutely essential if strong scientific rationale and experimental support exist).

## Formulating a Hypothesis

### Difficulties in Formulation (Goode and Hatt, 1952)

**Goode and Hatt (1952)** identified three major difficulties in formulating a good research hypothesis:

1. **Absence or inadequacy of a theoretical framework**: Without theoretical grounding, hypothesis formulation is impossible.
2. **Lack of knowledge about the current state of theoretical evidence**: The investigator may be unaware of available theoretical support.
3. **Unfamiliarity with scientific research techniques**: Without knowledge of methodology, a testable hypothesis cannot be framed.

### The Two Contexts of Hypothesis Formulation

- **Context of discovery**: The process through which a scientist actually arrives at a hypothesis (intuitive, exploratory, often not systematic).
- **Context of justification**: The presentation of evidence in support of the hypothesis (rational, systematic, communicable).

Scientists report their work primarily through the **context of justification** — they do not typically reveal the idiosyncratic process of discovery.

### Procedure

1. Hypothesis is typically **derived from the problem statement**.
2. Should be formulated **before data collection**.
3. If data suggests a hypothesis, that hypothesis must be **tested on a new dataset**, not on the data that generated it.
4. The scientist surveys data, abstracts similarities, classifies, and generalizes into a hypothesis.

## Ways of Stating a Hypothesis

**I. Russell** (cited in Ruckmick, 1947) proposed that a hypothesis should be stated as a **conditional or causal association** in the "If..., then..." form (also expressed as "p then q" or "if p, then q"):

- **Antecedent condition**: The "if" clause (the condition that holds).
- **Consequent condition**: The "then" clause (what follows when the antecedent is true).

*Example*: "If a reward is given to a rat for learning, then learning is faster."

**Important clarification**: The general implication does not assert that all true antecedent conditions guarantee a true consequent — it states only that *when* the antecedent is true, the consequent is also true.

In practice, many hypotheses are stated as **direct declarative sentences** without the explicit "if...then..." form. This is acceptable provided the hypothesis can be restated in that form:

- "Boys are more aggressive than girls." → "If the subject is male, then aggression is higher than in females."
- "The onset of fatigue reduces the efficiency of workers." → "If fatigue is present, then worker efficiency is reduced."

## Types of Hypotheses

### Classification 1: By Degree of Generality

#### (a) Universal Hypothesis

A **universal hypothesis** states that the stated relationship holds for **all levels/values** of a variable, **at all times and in all places**.

*Example*: "Adequate level of light increases reading efficiency."

Universal hypotheses are **preferred** because they have greater **predictive power**.

#### (b) Existential Hypothesis

An **existential hypothesis** states that a relationship holds for **at least one particular case**.

*Example*: "There is at least one biosphere that does not have either mercury or [a specified element]."

### Classification 2: By Goals of Explanation and Control

Four types based on the goals of explaining and controlling behaviour:

#### 1. Causal Hypothesis

A **causal hypothesis** postulates a specific causal influence on behaviour — it tentatively identifies a cause for a particular behaviour.

*Example*: "Boring content is a common cause of channel changing by TV viewers." (Note: it does not claim to be the only cause; it is the probable cause under investigation.)

#### 2. Descriptive Hypothesis

A **descriptive hypothesis** postulates particular characteristics of a behaviour or predicts what types of behaviour will be shown. It describes behaviour in terms of situational characteristics without identifying causes.

*Example*: "Channel changing during TV viewing occurs more frequently when the person is alone than when watching with others." (This describes situational conditions, not causal mechanisms.)

### Classification 3: By Structure and Statistical Use

#### 1. Simple Hypothesis

A **simple hypothesis** contains only **one independent variable and one dependent variable**.

*Examples*:
- "Children from broken homes tend to become delinquent."
- "Reward improves learning."
- "Aggression is associated with frustration."

#### 2. Complex Hypothesis

A **complex hypothesis** contains **more than two variables**. The interrelatedness of multiple variables makes assessment more difficult both quantitatively and theoretically.

*Example*: "Children from upper and lower socio-economic status have different self-adjustment than children from middle socio-economic status."

#### 3. Research Hypothesis

A **research hypothesis** is derived from a researcher's theory about some aspect of behaviour. The researcher believes it to be accurate and that its validity depends on the correctness of the underlying theory.

**Kerlinger and Lee (2000)** and **Castellan (1988)** defined research hypothesis as: "The prediction derived from the theory under test."

#### 4. Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)

The **alternative hypothesis (H₁)** is the operational statement of the investigator's theory or expectation. It is an alternative to the null hypothesis and typically asserts that the independent variable has an influence on the dependent variable.

- When H₀ is rejected, H₁ is accepted and the research hypothesis is supported.
- Also called the **experimental hypothesis** or **statistical hypothesis**.
- If the research hypothesis predicts direction (one group > other), H₁ states: Mean₁ > Mean₂.
- If the research hypothesis predicts only difference (no direction), H₁ states: Mean₁ ≠ Mean₂.

#### 5. Null Hypothesis (H₀)

The **null hypothesis (H₀)** is the reverse of the research hypothesis — a "no-effect," "no-difference," or "negative hypothesis" that denies what the research hypothesis asserts.

- Typically stated as: "The mean scores of the two groups are the same."
- The experimenter's aim is usually to **reject H₀** so that H₁ (and by extension the research hypothesis) can be accepted.

**Interrelation example**:
- Research hypothesis (theory): Group 1 and Group 2 differ on intelligence.
- H₁ (alternative): Mean intelligence of Group 1 > Mean intelligence of Group 2.
- H₀ (null): Mean intelligence of Group 1 = Mean intelligence of Group 2.
- If data allows rejection of H₀ → H₁ is accepted → research hypothesis is confirmed.

## Sources of Hypotheses

Five major sources from which a researcher generates hypotheses:

| Source | Description |
|---|---|
| **1. Researcher's own opinions, observations, and experiences** | Personal theoretical beliefs and prior observations; tested through experiments |
| **2. Hypotheses from other researchers** | Previously raised hypotheses by other investigators that are re-examined |
| **3. Existing research studies** | Reading reported research results generates new hypotheses and identifies factors that may modify findings |
| **4. Theory** | A logically organized set of proposals that defines, explains, and interrelates concepts; the most systematic source. *Example*: Freudian theory of anxiety generates specific testable hypotheses |
| **5. Models** | Analogical descriptions of processes underlying observed behaviours. *Example*: Information processing model of memory (Atkinson and Shiffrin, 1968) — describes short-term and long-term memory structures, guiding hypotheses about storage and retrieval |

## Functions of Hypotheses

### Primary Functions

1. **Testing theories**: Hypotheses make it possible to test theories indirectly. Because theories are rarely tested directly, hypotheses derived from them are tested instead. When a hypothesis is supported, the theory is partially supported.

2. **Serving in the absence of formal theory**: Some hypotheses are not associated with any particular theory. A researcher may form a hypothesis and theorizing follows later. Hypotheses function as **explanatory statements of relations among variables**.

3. **Facilitating theory development from observation**: Occasionally the reverse process occurs — a researcher observes a recurring relationship, hypothesizes causal agents or prominence ordering, and then constructs a logical system of propositions (a theory) to explain the relationship.

4. **Describing and empirically linking social phenomena**: A hypothesis after development permits empirical study of abstract constructs or phenomena. When supported, it provides information about the phenomenon; when not supported, it reduces the researcher's ignorance by revealing what is not true.

### Secondary Functions

- **Unifying common-sense notions** about human and non-human behaviour.
- **Informing improvements**: Testing hypotheses can revise conceptual and observational knowledge, improve teaching methods, suggest solutions to problems, and redesign practices.
- **Focusing the inquiry**: Hypotheses tell the researcher **what data to collect and what not to collect**, thereby providing direction and focus.
