# Research Design

Chapter 21 provides a systematic treatment of research design in behavioural science, covering the three-variance framework (experimental, extraneous, error), the criteria and principles of sound experimental design, the terminology of factorial experiments, and the major classes of between-subjects and within-subjects designs with their statistical implications. The chapter is strongly applied, working through worked examples of randomization procedures, matching techniques, and the logic of factorial interaction.

---

## Meaning and Purpose of Research Design

**Research design** is defined as the detailed plan or blueprint of an investigation: the sequence of steps taken ahead of time to ensure that relevant data are collected in a way that permits objective analysis of hypotheses formulated with respect to research problems.

Key definitional elements:
- It is a *sequence of steps taken in advance*.
- It ensures data collection is structured to allow objective, valid testing of hypotheses.
- It guides the researcher in testing relationships between **independent variables** (IVs) and **dependent variables** (DVs).
- Selection of a design is not arbitrary; it depends on the purpose of the investigation, the nature of the problem, and the methodology to be used.

### Two Core Functions of Research Design

1. **Answering research questions** as objectively, validly, and economically as possible. The design specifies which variables serve as controls, which methods of data collection are most adequate, and how to analyze collected data so as to answer research problems.
2. **Acting as a control mechanism**: enabling the researcher to control relevant variances. Every scientific investigation involves three types of variance that the design must manage.

---

## Experimental Variance

**Experimental variance** is the variance produced in the dependent variable by the systematic manipulation of the independent variable by the experimenter.

- The experimenter varies the independent variable as systematically and as widely as possible to *maximize* experimental variance, thereby obtaining a valid and distinct experimental effect.
- Example: In a learning experiment, three practice conditions are used (5 min, 10 min, 20 min). The differences in performance across conditions represent experimental variance.
- **Rule:** The higher the difference between experimental conditions, the higher the experimental variance. The experimenter always tries to *maximize* experimental variance.
- Experimental variance is at a maximum when conditions are as different from each other as possible (e.g., high reward vs. low reward producing very different learning rates). It is at a minimum when conditions are nearly equal.

---

## Extraneous Variance (Control Variance)

**Extraneous variance** (also called **control variance**) is the variance produced by extraneous variables or relevant variables that the experimenter does not intend to manipulate but that can affect the dependent variable.

- The researcher's goal is to *reduce* (control or eliminate) variance due to extraneous variables.

---

## Error Variance

**Error variance** is defined as the variability in measures that occurs as a function of factors not directly related to the independent variables manipulated by the researcher.

Sources of error variance include:
- Subject characteristics: attitude, motivation, need, status, etc.
- Experimental conditions: fluctuation in experimental trials, differences in conditions, temporary emotional state of subjects, emotional fatigue.

### Three Key Properties of Error Variance

1. **Self-compensating**: Variability is sometimes positive and sometimes negative, tending to cancel out across measurements.
2. **Diminishes with more measurements**: The more observations in a design, the less the error variance.
3. **Follows predictable probability**: Based on random errors; distinct from systematic variance (which is non-random and predictable).

### Two Purposes Served by Minimizing Error Variance

1. Improves **reliability** of measures.
2. Reduces **within-group variance**, raising the power (statistical validity) of the experiment. If a significant difference exists but error variance is large, the ratio of systematic variance to between-subject variance may not reach significance.

**Note:** Consistent, controlled experimental conditions minimize error variance. Non-identical conditions allow error variance to accumulate.

---

## Control of Research Design: Confounding

The fourth function of a good research design is to provide **unambiguous results** and to avoid **confounding**.

**Confounding** (technical definition): "Mixing up" or "confusion of variables" — a situation in which the independent variable changes concomitantly with another variable, so that changes in the dependent variable cannot be attributed to the independent variable alone.

- Confounding is a major threat to establishing cause-effect (IV-DV) relationships.
- A good research design always attempts to avoid confounded results.

---

## Criteria of a Good Research Design

Three criteria distinguish a strong design from a weak one:

### 1. Capability to Answer Research Questions Adequately

- A design that does not adequately test the hypothesis is a **weak design**.
- Common errors: matching subjects on variables uncorrelated with the DV; using a two-group design when a factorial design is required.
- Example: If four groups are needed but only two are used, the design cannot answer the question adequately.

### 2. Control of Variables

- A good design controls the effects of **extraneous variables**.
- Best method: **Randomization**, which operates at three levels:
  1. Random selection of subjects from the population.
  2. Random assignment of subjects into control and experimental groups.
  3. Random assignment of experimental treatments to experimental groups.
- Adequate control of extraneous variables increases the **internal validity** of the research.

### 3. Generalizability

**Generalizability** = the **external validity** of the research. The extent to which results apply to subjects, groups, or conditions not included in the sample.

- **Basic research**: Generalizability is not the primary focus.
- **Applied research**: Generalizability is central; results are intended for application elsewhere.

## Basic Principles of Experimental Design

According to **Underwood and Shaughnessy (2005)**, a sound experimental design rests on three basic principles: **replication**, **randomization**, and **local control**.

### Replication

**Replication** (fusion of "duplication" and "repetition"): The deliberate repetition of an experiment using a nearly identical procedure with a different set of subjects, in a different setting, or at a different time (**Hagan, 1997**).

- Purpose: Provides an accurate estimate of **experimental error** and serves as quality control (estimates the significance of observed differences).
- **Experimental error**: Errors arising from faulty measurement, faulty observation, uncontrolled variables, and use of inappropriate variables.
- **Critical distinction:** Replication must not be confused with **multiple measurements** (measuring the same response repeatedly under the same condition is NOT replication).

### Randomization

**Randomization** is the second basic principle.

- Makes statistical tests valid; maintains independence of observations.
- Methods: Randomly assigning subjects to treatments, or randomly assigning treatments to subjects.
- Primary advantage: **absence of bias** (Gaito, 1958).
- Note (attributed to unnamed author, 1972): "There are situations in which complete randomization is not desirable. Some intermediate position is the best choice."

### Local Control

**Local control**: The amount of balancing, blocking, and grouping of subjects employed in an experimental design.

Three components:
- **Grouping**: Assigning homogeneous subjects to groups so different groups are available for different experimental conditions.
- **Blocking**: Assigning experimental units to blocks such that units within a block are homogeneous.
- **Balancing**: Arranging grouping, blocking, and assignment of units so that the design is balanced.

A statistically and experimentally sound design must possess the property of local control.

## Basic Terms Used in Experimental Design

### (i) Factor

A **factor** is an independent variable manipulated by the experimenter. Also called a **treatment** or **experimental variable**.

Two types:
1. **Directly manipulated (experimental variable)**: Experimenter changes the variable directly (e.g., intensity of sound).
2. **Classification factor (manipulated by selection)**: Subject-related variables that cannot be directly manipulated (e.g., age). Experimenter selects subjects at different levels.

Factors are represented with capital letters: A, B, C, D...

In factor analysis, a "factor" is a cluster (group) of correlated variables.

### (ii) Levels

A **level** is each specific variation within a factor (e.g., 40 dB, 60 dB, 80 dB for a sound factor).

| Model | Selection of Levels | Inference |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed effect model** | Systematic, non-random — chosen levels are specifically of interest | Conclusions restricted to those specific levels only |
| **Random effect model** (variance component model) | Random — levels are a random sample from a population | Generalizes to the population of levels; rarely used in practice |

### (iii) Treatment Combinations

A **treatment** is a particular experimental manipulation or procedure under which participants are run.
- Single-factor experiment: levels of the factor are the treatments.
- Multi-factorial (e.g., 2 × 3): 6 treatment combinations; each participant randomly assigned to one.

### (iv) Dimensions

The **dimensions** of a factorial experiment are indexed by the number of levels of each factor and the number of factors.
- Example: Factor A (3 levels), Factor B (4 levels) = a 3 × 4 factorial.
- Three-factor design with 2, 3, and 4 levels = a 2 × 3 × 4 factorial.

### (v) Main Effects, Simple Effects, and Interaction Effects

**Simple effect**: The effect of one factor at a *specific level* of another factor; the mean performance of one factor for a particular level of another factor.

**Main effect**: The effect of a factor averaged across *all levels* of every other factor (the marginal mean difference). It is the average of all simple effects for that factor.

**Interaction effect**: Exists when the effect of one IV depends on the level of another IV.

- Graphically: interaction = **non-parallel lines** (converging or crossing).
- No interaction = parallel lines (constant distance between groups across conditions).
- **Disordinal interaction**: Lines cross; the direction of one factor's effect reverses across levels of the other.
- **Ordinal interaction**: Lines converge but do not cross; magnitude differs, direction does not.

**Critical rule:** When an interaction is present, main effects may be misleading. Each factor's effects must be interpreted in light of the interaction.

#### Illustrated Example: Drug × Age (Three Patterns)

| Pattern | Graph | Interaction? | Main Effect of Drug |
|---|---|---|---|
| A: Constant drug effect at all ages | Parallel lines | No | 20-point effect (accurate) |
| B: Drug effect only in older animals | Converging/separated lines | Yes (ordinal) | 20-point main effect (misleading) |
| C: Drug increases in young, decreases in old | Crossing lines | Yes (disordinal) | 0-point main effect (completely misleading) |

Primary classification: one group (within-subjects) vs. separate groups (between-subjects).

## Between-Subjects Design

A **between-subjects design** uses a separate group of subjects for each value/condition of the IV (Kantowitz & Roediger, 1984).

---

### Two-Randomized-Groups Design

Subjects randomly assigned to exactly two groups.

**Procedure:**
1. Define IV and DV; select two values/conditions of the IV.
2. Define population; randomly select a sample.
3. Randomly assign subjects to two equal groups.
4. Determine by coin toss which group is experimental vs. control.
5. Administer treatment to experimental group; control receives no treatment.
6. Collect and statistically analyze DV scores.

**Statistical tests:** t-test (parametric) or Mann-Whitney U-test (non-parametric).

#### Methods of Random Assignment (Underwood, 1966)

**A. Captive Assignment** (all subjects known before experiment begins):
1. **Table of Random Numbers** (most common and most preferred).
2. Numbered slips in a box; draw half to each group.
3. Count by seating order (least preferred — may introduce systematic bias).
4. Alphabetical alternation (first to Group 1, second to Group 2, etc.).

**B. Sequential Assignment** (subjects arrive one at a time):

| Method | Description | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **Complete randomization** | Slips with condition labels shuffled; each arriving subject assigned to next drawn slip | By chance, one condition may fill up early, creating order-condition correlation |
| **Block randomization** | Each condition appears once per successive block; order within blocks is random | Prevents order-condition confound; preferred when N is small |

**Block randomization defined:** Each condition of the IV occurs exactly once in each successive block of trials; the order within a block is random and differs from every other block. All conditions must occur an equal number of times; block size = number of conditions.

#### Two Limitations of the Two-Randomized-Groups Design

1. **Subject attrition**: Dropout of subjects disrupts group equivalence, threatening generalizability of conclusions.
2. **Restricted inference**: Only two values of the IV studied; impossible to establish precise functional relationships between IV and DV.

---

### More-Than-Two-Randomized-Groups Design (Multi-Group Design)

Three or more conditions of the IV; three or more randomly assigned groups.

**Three advantages over two-groups design:**

1. **Efficiency**: All IV levels tested simultaneously (e.g., comparing 4 teaching methods requires only 1 multi-group experiment vs. 6 separate two-group experiments).
2. **Fuller sampling of IV levels**: More values of the IV yield a fuller picture of the IV's influence and allow better description of the functional relationship.
3. **Establishes adequate IV-DV relationships**: Two groups yield only two data points; the relationship (linear, curvilinear, etc.) cannot be determined. Multiple groups allow the shape of the IV-DV function to be identified.

**Statistical tests:** ANOVA (parametric) and Kruskal-Wallis test (non-parametric); post-hoc: Duncan Range Test.

---

### Matched-Groups Design (Randomized Block Design)

Also called **randomized block design** (term borrowed from agriculture, where adjacent plots are assumed homogeneous in fertility).

**Logic:** Subjects are first measured on a **matching variable** (highly correlated with DV), then matched and randomly assigned to experimental conditions. Creates statistically equivalent groups before treatment.

**Inference:** If groups are equivalent before treatment and differ significantly after, the difference is attributable to the experimental treatment.

#### Selection of the Matching Variable

Most critical criterion: the matching variable must yield a **high positive correlation** with the DV.

- Best: DV itself as matching variable (correlation = 1.00).
- Practical alternatives: initial trial performance scores, or any other measure with high established correlation with DV.
- **Rule:** Do NOT use matched-groups design if the matching variable is not expected to correlate highly with the DV.

#### Methods of Matching

**Method 1: Matching by Rank (Matching by Pairs)**
- Subjects ranked on matching variable scores; each subject paired with nearest score equivalent.
- Each pair = one block; members randomly assigned to conditions (coin toss).
- **Limitation:** Subjects with deviant (unmatched) scores must be dropped, reducing N and threatening generalizability.

**Method 2: Matching in Terms of Mean and Standard Deviation**

Three sub-methods:

| Sub-method | Procedure | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| **Random-blocks method** | Form blocks in decreasing score order; randomly assign block members to groups using random sequence | Most preferred — smallest mean differences between groups |
| **Counterbalancing order method** | Formula sequence (ABCCBA...) assigns subjects to groups | Intermediate |
| **Block-repetition method** | Natural sequence (ABCABC...) repeated in blocks | Least preferred — largest mean differences between groups |

#### Matched-Groups vs. Randomized-Groups: Decision Rules

| Criterion | Prefer Matched-Groups | Prefer Randomized-Groups |
|---|---|---|
| Correlation between matching variable and DV | High positive correlation | Low or unknown |
| N | Large (df loss is tolerable) | Small (df loss is costly) |
| Matching variable available? | Yes, easily | No, or requires extra lab visit |
| Statistical complexity | Lower | Higher techniques more easily applied |

**Degrees of freedom (df) comparison (N = 20 subjects):**
- Matched-groups: df = 10 pairs - 1 = **9**
- Randomized-groups: df = 20 - 2 = **18**

With the same obtained t (e.g., 2.10), the randomized-groups design may reach significance (more df, smaller critical value needed) while the matched-groups design may not.

**Statistical test:** t-test, or **Sandler's A test** (computationally simpler substitute for paired t-test; Sandler, 1955).

---

### Factorial Design (Multivariate Design)

A **factorial design** is an experimental design in which selected values of two or more IVs are manipulated in all possible combinations so that both the individual (main) effects and the interactive effects may be studied.

**Three defining characteristics:**
1. Two or more IVs are manipulated.
2. Different subgroups of subjects serve under every possible combination of IV levels.
3. Equal number of subjects per subgroup preferred (balanced design; simpler computation).

**Types of variables in factorial designs:**
- **Active variable**: IV directly manipulated (e.g., noise level).
- **Organismic/attribute variable**: Subject characteristic used as IV; manipulated by selection (e.g., sex, age).

#### Example: 2 × 2 Factorial Design

IVs: A (Noise: A₁ high, A₂ low) × B (Illumination: B₁ high, B₂ low)

| | A₁ (High Noise) | A₂ (Low Noise) |
|---|---|---|
| **B₁ (High Illumination)** | A₁B₁ | A₂B₁ |
| **B₂ (Low Illumination)** | A₁B₂ | A₂B₂ |

Four treatment combinations; subjects randomly assigned (e.g., 40 subjects, 10 per cell) using Table of Random Numbers.

#### Notation for Factorial Designs

| Notation | IVs | Levels | Total Conditions |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 × 2 | 2 | 2 each | 4 |
| 2 × 3 | 2 | 2 and 3 | 6 |
| 3 × 3 | 2 | 3 each | 9 |
| 2 × 3 × 4 | 3 | 2, 3, 4 | 24 |
| p × q | 2 | p and q | p × q |

**General form:** A × B design.

**Key advantage over separate single-IV experiments:** Allows simultaneous study of main effects AND interaction — impossible with one-IV-at-a-time designs.

---

## Within-Subjects Design (Repeated Measures Design)

A **within-subjects design** uses the same subjects under all conditions of the IV.

### Complete Within-Subjects Design

Conditions administered *multiple times* to each subject in different orders; practice effects are balanced individually.

Balancing techniques:
1. **Block randomization**
2. **ABBA counterbalancing**

### Incomplete Within-Subjects Design

Each condition administered to each subject *only once*; order varied across subjects so that practice effects cancel when results are combined.

Common technique: Use all possible orders of conditions; randomly assign subjects to orders.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Research design is a blueprint** of procedures for testing hypotheses; selection is governed by purpose, problem nature, and methodology — NOT by researcher preference.
2. **Three types of variance** a researcher manages: experimental (maximize), extraneous/control (minimize), error (minimize). The fourth function is avoiding confounding.
3. **Error variance** is self-compensating (positive and negative), decreases with more observations, and follows predictable probability based on random errors — unlike systematic variance which is non-random.
4. **Confounding** = variables mixed up so DV changes cannot be attributed solely to the IV; major threat to internal validity.
5. **Three criteria of a good design:** (1) capability to answer research questions, (2) control of variables (internal validity), (3) generalizability (external validity).
6. **Three basic principles (Underwood & Shaughnessy, 2005):** Replication, Randomization, Local Control.
7. **Replication is NOT multiple measurements.** Replication = independent repetition with different subjects, setting, or time.
8. **Primary advantage of randomization (Gaito, 1958):** Absence of bias.
9. **Fixed effect model** restricts conclusions to chosen levels. **Random effect model** generalizes to population; rarely used.
10. **Interaction effect = non-parallel lines.** When interaction is present, main effects alone are misleading.
11. **Statistical tests by design:** Two-randomized-groups: t-test or Mann-Whitney U. Multi-group: ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis (+ Duncan Range Test). Matched-groups: t-test or Sandler's A test.
12. **Degrees of freedom:** Matched-groups df = n_pairs - 1 (fewer); Randomized-groups df = N - 2 (more). More df = smaller critical t = greater power. Prefer matched-groups ONLY when matching variable highly correlates with DV.
13. **Three methods for matching on mean and SD** (best to worst): Random-blocks method > Counterbalancing order method > Block-repetition method.
14. **Factorial design** is multivariate by definition — always two or more IVs; uniquely allows study of interaction effects not detectable in single-IV designs.
15. **Block randomization for sequential assignment:** Each condition appears exactly once per block, preventing chance order-condition correlation when N is small — preferred over complete randomization in such cases.
