# Test Construction

This chapter covers the full lifecycle of psychological and educational test development: the meaning and classification of tests, the five hallmark characteristics of a sound test (objectivity, reliability, validity, norms, practicability), the seven sequential steps from initial planning through manual preparation, and the practical, ethical, and selection considerations that govern responsible test use. It is a foundational chapter for MCQs on test terminology, researcher attributions, classification criteria, and procedural sequence.

---

## Meaning of a Test in Psychology and Education

A **psychological (or educational) test** is defined as a **standardized procedure to measure quantitatively or qualitatively one or more than one aspect of a trait by means of a sample of verbal or non-verbal responses** (A.K. Singh, following the textbook definition).

The twofold purpose of a psychological test:
1. Compare the **same individual** on two or more aspects of a trait.
2. Compare **two or more persons** on the same trait.

### Key Definitions by Scholars

| Scholar | Year | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **Beare** | 1953 | "An organized succession of stimuli designed to measure quantitatively or to evaluate qualitatively some mental process." |
| **Russ** (as quoted in Anastasi & Urbina) | 1951 / 1997 | "Essentially an objective and standardized measure or sample of behaviour." |
| **Cultan** | 1996 | "A standardized procedure for sampling behaviour and describing it with categories or ratings." |
| **Kaplan and Saccuzzo** | 2001 | "A psychological test or educational test is a set of items designed to measure characteristics of human beings that pertain to behaviour." |

**Key implication:** A psychological test is a *sample* of behaviour, not an exhaustive census. It is *standardized*, meaning uniform conditions apply to all test-takers.

## Classification of Tests

Tests have been classified on the basis of **six major criteria**. Each criterion and its subtypes are high-yield MCQ targets.

---

### 1. On the Basis of Administration Conditions

**Individual tests** — administered to **one person at a time**.
- Example: **Binet-Simon test**.
- Used by school psychologists and counsellors for individual evaluation.
- Characteristics: time-consuming, require full examiner attention, given orally, demand trained/experienced examiners, require strict adherence to standardized instructions.
- Advantage: more **dependable and flexible** than group tests.
- Disadvantage: costly, limited practical utility.

**Group tests** — administered to **more than one person at a time**.
- Developed to meet large-scale assessment needs.
- Adequate for measuring **aptitude, achievement, strengths and weaknesses** in classroom settings.
- Less examiner-intensive than individual tests.

---

### 2. On the Basis of Scoring

**Objective tests** — scored by competent examiners with **no scope for subjective judgment** entering into scoring.
- Item types: **multiple-choice**, **true-false**, **matching**.
- Also called: **new-type tests** or **limited-answer tests**.
- The problem (question) in an item is called the **stem**.
- An incorrect but plausible answer choice is called a **distractor**.
- Note from text: "A distractor answer is one which is similar to the correct answer" — distractors are designed to attract examinees who lack full knowledge.

**Subjective tests** — scoring involves **some scope for subjective judgment**.
- Examples: **essay tests**, **interviews**.
- Characteristics: elements of vagueness and ambiguity in scoring.
- Also called: **essay tests** or **free-answer tests**.
- Advantages of essay tests:
  - Can assess organization of a comprehensive answer.
  - Assesses recall and selection of important information.
  - Assesses logical presentation.
  - The examinee is free to write and organize answers in their own manner.

---

### 3. On the Basis of Time Limit (Speed vs. Power)

**Power tests** — have a **generous time limit** so most examinees can attempt every item.
- Items are usually arranged in **increasing order of difficulty**.
- Demonstrate **how much knowledge or information** the examinee possesses.
- Example: intelligence tests (though difficult to purely categorize).

**Speed tests** — have **severe time limits**; items are comparatively easy and of roughly equal difficulty.
- Very few examinees make errors on individual items.
- Reveal **how rapidly** the examinee can respond within a given time.
- Example: **most clerical aptitude tests**.

---

### 4. On the Basis of Nature or Contents of Items

Four subtypes:

#### (i) Verbal Tests
- Items emphasize **reading, writing, and oral expression** as the primary mode of communication.
- Instructions are printed or written; examinee reads and responds.
- Also called **paper-pencil tests** (examinee works on paper without handling physical test materials).
- Examples: **Jalota Group General Intelligence Test**, **Mehta Group Test of Intelligence**.

#### (ii) Nonverbal Tests
- Attempt to **reduce (but not eliminate)** the use of language by using **symbolic materials** such as pictures and figures.
- Language is used in *instructions* but NOT in the items themselves.
- Commonly used with **young children** to assess nonverbal aspects of intelligence such as **spatial perception**.
- Example: **Raven Progressive Matrices**.

#### (iii) Performance Tests
- Require the examinee to **perform a task** rather than answer questions.
- Language is **prohibited** in items; instructions may be given orally, through **gesture** or **pantomime**.
- Tasks include: assembling puzzles, placing pictures in correct sequence, placing pegs in boards, pointing to correct patterns.
- Limitation: usually **administered individually** (examiner must count errors and time).
- Common feature: emphasis on the examinee's **ability to perform** a task.

#### (iv) Nonlanguage Tests
- **Completely independent** of any form of written, spoken, or reading communication.
- Instructions given through **gestures, pantomime, or demonstration**; responses by pointing or manipulating objects (pictures, blocks, puzzles).
- Used for persons who **cannot communicate in any form of ordinary language**.
- Distinction from nonverbal: nonverbal tests still use language in instructions; nonlanguage tests eliminate language entirely.

---

### 5. On the Basis of Purpose or Objective

| Test Type | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Intelligence tests** | Assess intelligence of examinees |
| **Aptitude tests** | Assess potentials or aptitudes |
| **Personality tests** | Assess traits, adjustments, interests, values |
| **Neuropsychological tests** | Assessment of persons with known or suspected brain dysfunction |
| **Achievement tests** | Assess knowledge/skills acquired in a knowledge/social domain |

---

### 6. On the Basis of Standardization

**Standardized tests** — have been subjected to the **procedure of standardization**. Four conditions for standardization:

1. **Standard set of instructions** with uniformity of administration for all test-takers.
2. **Uniformity of scoring** with an index of fairness of correct answers through **item analysis**.
3. **Reliability and validity** must be established; individual performance must be explicitly mentioned.
4. **Norms** should be available (controversial condition — Cronbach, 1970 noted a test *without* norms may still be called standardized; majority of psychologists favour norms).

Key features:
- Constructed by **test specialists**.
- Administered to a **large sample under standard testing conditions**.
- Results from different samples may legitimately be **compared**.
- Items are **fixed and not modifiable**.

**Teacher-made tests** — constructed by teachers for use largely within their own classrooms.
- Effectiveness depends on the teacher's **pedagogical knowledge** of test construction.
- Items may come from any area of curriculum and may be **modified** at the teacher's will.
- Rules for administration and scoring are **determined by the teacher**.
- Particular norms are not provided standardly (though a teacher may develop class-level norms).

## Characteristics of a Good Test

A scientifically sound test must possess five essential characteristics:

---

### (i) Objectivity

**Objectivity** — the test must be **free from subjective elements** so that there is complete **interpersonal agreement** among experts regarding the meaning of items and scoring.

Two aspects:
- **Objectivity of items**: items must be phrased so they are interpreted in **exactly the same way** by all test-takers. Items must have a standardized set of information for answering.
- **Objectivity in scoring**: the scoring method must be **standard** so complete uniformity can be maintained when scored by different experts at different times.

---

### (ii) Reliability

**Reliability** — refers to the **self-correlation** of the test; the extent to which results are **consistent** across administrations on the same group with a reasonable time gap.

Two components:
- **Internal consistency** — consistency in results from a **single administration**.
- **Temporal consistency** — consistency in results across **testing and retesting**.

A reliable test:
- Consistently ranks scores.
- Scores are free from internal defects of standardization that produce **errors of measurement**.

Methods for calculating reliability coefficient:
1. **Test-retest method**
2. **Split-half method**
3. **Equivalent-form method**
4. **Kuder-Richardson formulas**
5. **Rulon formula**

(Detailed discussion in Chapter 5 of the textbook.)

---

### (iii) Validity

**Validity** — indicates the extent to which the test **measures what it intends to measure**, when compared with some outside independent criterion.

- Validity = **correlation of the test with some outside criterion**.
- The criterion must be **independent** and the **true measure** of the trait being assessed.
- Generally, validity is **dependent upon reliability** — a test with poor reliability (inconsistent results) is unlikely to correlate well with an outside criterion.

Three main types:
1. **Content validity**
2. **Construct validity**
3. **Criterion-related validity**

Statistical techniques for validity coefficient:
- **Pearson's product-moment correlation**
- **Rank difference correlation**
- **Abac tables** prepared by **Flanagan** — for directly reading validity coefficients of point-biserial or phi-coefficient when proportions passing in upper and lower groups are known.

(Detailed discussion in Chapter 6 of the textbook.)

---

### (iv) Norms

**Norms** — the **average performance of a representative sample** on a given test.

Four common types of norms:
1. **Age norms**
2. **Grade norms**
3. **Percentile norms**
4. **Standard score norms**

Purpose: norms enable **meaningful interpretation** of obtained scores. Without norms, a raw score on a test conveys no meaning about the ability or trait being measured.

General rule for obtaining norms:
- Sample must be **representative** of the target population.
- Must be **randomly selected**.
- Should represent a **cross-section** of the population.

(Detailed discussion in Chapter 7 of the textbook.)

---

### (v) Practicability / Usability

A test must be easily practicable from the standpoint of:
- **Time** taken to complete.
- **Length** of the test.
- **Scoring** — must not be overly difficult or require highly specialized scorers.
- **Economy** — must be affordable (cost-effective).

## General Steps of Test Construction

Seven sequential steps:

---

### Step 1: Planning of the Test

The **first step** involves careful planning before any items are written.

The test constructor specifies:
- **Broad and specific objectives** of the test.
- **Nature of content or scope** to be included.
- **Method of sampling** to be used.
- Detailed arrangement for **preliminary and final administration**.
- **Probable length and time limit** for completion.
- **Probable statistical methods** to be adopted.
- **Total number of reproductions** of the test.
- **Preparation of a manual**.

---

### Step 2: Writing Items of the Test

**Item** — defined by **Brown (1953: 15)** as "A single question or task that is not often broken down into any smaller units."

If an objective test: items written include:
- **Alternative response items** (true-false)
- **Matching items**
- **Controlled completion items**
- **Multiple choice items**
- **True-false items**

**Item writing is essentially a creative art** — no set rules guarantee good items. It depends on the writer's **initiative, imagination, experience, practice, and ingenuity**.

Five prerequisites for effective item writing:

1. **Thorough knowledge and complete mastery** of the subject matter — must know all facts, principles, misconceptions, fallacies, and peculiarities.
2. **Full awareness of the target population** — know the intelligence level of persons for whom the test is meant; manipulate difficulty level accordingly; supply sufficient relevant clues to correct responses.
3. **Familiarity with different types of items** — their advantages, disadvantages, characteristics of good items, and common errors in item writing.
4. **Large vocabulary** — know different meanings of words to avoid confusion; convey meaning in simplest possible language.
5. **Submission to subject experts** — written items must be submitted to a group of subject experts for criticism and suggestions, then modified accordingly.

**Sources of ideas for items**: textbooks, journals, discussions, interview questions, course outlines, other instructional materials.

**Arrangement of items**: generally arranged in **increasing order of difficulty**; items with the same format and dealing with the same content are placed together.

---

### Step 3: Preliminary Administration (Experimental Try-Out) of the Test

The experimental try-out serves multiple purposes. According to **Conrad (1951)**, the main purposes are:

1. Finding out **major weaknesses**: omissions, ambiguities, inadequacies; identifying ambiguous/undiscriminating items, nonfunctioning distractors, very difficult or very easy items.
2. Determining **difficulty values** of each item for proper distribution in the final form.
3. Determining **validity of each individual item** — specifically the **discriminating power (index)** of each item (extent to which an item discriminates between those who possess the trait in larger vs. least amounts).
4. Determining the **desirable time limit** of the test.
5. Determining the **appropriate length** (number of items) for the final form.
6. Determining the **arrangement of items** to avoid overlapping.
7. Identifying **weakness and vagueness in directions/instructions** and in fore-exercises or sample questions.

#### Conrad's Three-Administration Framework

**Conrad (1951)** recommended at least **three preliminary administrations**:

| Administration | Conrad's Name | Purpose | Minimum Sample |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First** | "Pre-try-out" | Detect gross ambiguities, inadequacies, omissions in items and directions | Not less than **100** |
| **Second** | "Try-out proper" | Provide data for **item analysis** | Not less than **200** (similar to target population) |
| **Third** | "Final trial administration" / "Dress rehearsal" | Detect minor defects missed by first two; final check on administration procedure and time limit | Not specified (but fresh sample) |

**Item analysis** — a technique of selecting discriminating items for the final composition of the test. It provides three pieces of information per item:
1. **Difficulty value** of the item
2. **Discrimination index** of the item
3. **Effectiveness of distractors**

(Detailed discussion in Chapter 4 of the textbook.)

After the **final trial administration**, **no material changes** are made to items in the final form.

Note: Conrad's prescribed number of administrations and sample sizes have been widely appreciated but are not followed as a fixed rule — actual practice varies by nature and purpose of the test.

---

### Step 4: Reliability of the Final Test

After item-based selection, the final test is administered on a **fresh sample** to compute the **reliability coefficient**.

- Minimum sample size: **not less than 100**.
- Reliability = **consistency**; a reliable test consistently ranks scores.

Methods for computing reliability coefficient:
1. **Test-retest method**
2. **Split-half method**
3. **Equivalent-form method**
4. **Kuder-Richardson formulas**
5. **Rulon formula**

---

### Step 5: Validity of the Final Test

After estimating reliability, the test is **validated against some outside independent criterion**.

- Validity = **correlation of the test with some outside independent criterion**.
- Validation data are obtained from a **fresh sample** (not those used in item analysis).
- The process is called **validation**.

Three main types of validity:
1. **Content validity**
2. **Construct validity**
3. **Criterion-related validity**

Statistical techniques:
- **Pearson's product-moment correlation**
- **Rank difference correlation**
- **Abac tables** (Flanagan) — for point-biserial or phi-coefficient

---

### Step 6: Norms of the Final Test

**Norms** — defined as the **average performance or score of a large sample representative of a specified population**.

Commonly used norms:
1. **Percentile ranks**
2. **Percentile scores**
3. **Standard scores**
4. **Standard score norms**

Not all norms suit all types of tests — the test constructor selects appropriate norms based on purpose and type of test.

Normative sample requirements:
- Must be **representative** of the target population.
- Must be **randomly selected**.
- Should represent a **cross-section** of the population.

---

### Step 7: Preparation of Manual and Reproduction of the Test

The **test manual** must include:
- **Psychometric properties** of the test.
- **Norms and references**.
- **Procedures of test administration**.
- **Scoring methods**.
- **Purpose** for which the test is intended.
- Details about **test materials** and item arrangement.
- **Normative data**.
- Information about the **test constructor**.

After the manual is prepared, the test constructor orders **printing of the test and the manual**.

## Uses of Psychological Tests

Five major uses:

### 1. In Classification
Tests are used to **assign individuals to categories**. Sub-types of classification:

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Placement** | Setting persons into appropriate programmes according to needs or skills (e.g., placing students into science, history, or political science programmes) |
| **Screening** | Identifying and categorizing persons having exceptional talent or specific characteristics |
| **Certification** | Awarding certificates/licenses to persons who pass a test (e.g., driving licence) |
| **Selection** | Choosing persons from a pool on the basis of test scores for admission or employment |
| **Diagnosis** | Determining the nature of abnormal behaviour within an accepted classification system |

### 2. In Diagnosis and Planning for Treatment
- Intelligence tests: important for **diagnosis of mental retardation**.
- Other tests: diagnosis of **learning disability**.
- Tests not only provide a label but also create **opportunity for planning treatment**.

### 3. In Self-Knowledge
- Tests give **feedback to test-takers** regarding their trait levels.
- Help individuals **select proper career paths** and make plans for self-improvement.

### 4. In Evaluation of Programmes
- Used to assess the **impact of educational and social programmes**.
- Achievement and intelligence tests evaluate programme effectiveness in schools and colleges.

### 5. In Theoretical and Applied Branches of Behavioural Research
- Psychologists investigate **theoretical matters** with no immediate practical application.
- Example: **Witkin (1949)** used the **tilting-room-tilting-chair test** to analyze **cognitive field dependence**.
- Example: Testing the hypothesis that **low lead absorption** affects classroom behaviour, validated using psychological tests.

### Sources of Information about Psychological Tests
- **Publisher's Catalogues**
- **Reference Books**
- **Journals**
- **Databases**
- **Test Manuals**
- **Mental Measurements Yearbook (MMY)** — the most important reference source; reviewed periodically to incorporate new tests.

## Limitations of Psychological Tests

Five major limitations:

### (i) Invasion of Privacy
Tests may constitute an **invasion of privacy** if used without the permission of testees to obtain personal and sensitive information.

### (ii) Permanent Categorization
Tests may **permanently categorize** persons (e.g., mentally retarded, gifted, schizophrenic) and authorities may behave accordingly, **disregarding evidence of further change**. This has serious implications because examinees can and do change.

### (iii) Measurement of Only Limited Aspects of Behaviour
Tests **cannot measure the most important human traits**; they force evaluation on **limited and relatively unimportant criteria**.

### (iv) Creation of Anxiety
Examinees feel **anxious** when assessed through psychological tests, which **affects their performance**. Note: examinees familiar with specific test types are **less anxious** than those unfamiliar (Anastasi, 1976).

### (v) Penalization of Bright and Creative Examinees
Tests are **insensitive to atypical and creative responses**. Such responses receive little credit, **discriminating against talented examinees**.

## Ethical Issues in Psychological Testing

The **American Psychological Association (APA)** officially adopted a set of standards governing proper and improper uses of psychological tests.

- Current APA document: **Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct** (APA, 1992).
- Consists of a **preamble and six general principles** guiding psychologists toward the highest ideals.
- The **APA Committee on Psychological Tests and Assessment (CPTA)** considers problems regarding sound testing/assessment practices and provides technical advice.

**AERA** = American Educational Research Association  
**NCME** = National Council on Measurement in Education  
These three bodies (AERA, APA, NCME) jointly set standards for test constructors and users.

### Five Major Ethical Issues

#### 1. Issues of Human Rights
- The right **not to be tested** — persons who don't wish to be tested cannot be ethically forced.
- The right to **know their test scores**, their interpretations, and the basis of any decisions affecting their lives.
- The right to **know the purpose** behind psychological testing data.
- The right to **confidentiality** of test results.
- Test interpreters have an ethical obligation to **protect these human rights**.

#### 2. Issues of Labelling
- Assigning psychiatric diagnoses (e.g., "chronic schizophrenia") can become a **self-fulfilling prophecy**.
- Labelling **stigmatizes** a person and shifts focus to deficits.
- Labelling makes the person **passive**, leaving no incentive to alter negative conditions.
- Labelling can bring **tolerance for crime and negative consequences**.
- Persons have the **right to resist labelling**.

#### 3. Issues of Invasion of Privacy
- Responding to test items requires revealing **personal information**.
- **Dahlstrom (1969)** investigated this issue in detail and pointed to two aspects:
  - Much fear of invasion of privacy is based on **serious misunderstandings**; psychological tests have limited aims and limited invasion compared to many other devices.
  - Tests are sometimes **misused or abused**.
- APA (1992): Psychologists maintain **confidentiality** — personal information is communicated to others only as required by law or ethics. Exceptions exist only when withholding information may cause **danger** or in the case of **court-ordered records**.

#### 4. Issues of Divided Loyalties
- First identified by **Jackson and Messick (1967)**; remains a central problem today.
- Dilemma: loyalty to the **institution** (e.g., an industrial firm wanting to identify accident-prone persons) vs. responsibility to **protect the rights and welfare** of persons being tested.
- Example: A psychologist in an industrial firm identifying accident-prone individuals must balance institutional responsibility with individual rights protection.

#### 5. Responsibility of Test Constructors and Test Users
- **Test constructors** must provide a test manual that clearly states:
  - Appropriate uses of the test.
  - Psychometric evidence and norms.
  - Scoring and administration standards.
- **Test users** (per APA, 1974) must:
  - Maintain security of the test.
  - Know the test well (reason for use, consequences of use).
  - Know qualifications needed to interpret the test.
  - Possess adequate knowledge of basic principles, interpretations, and standardization underlying any test they administer.
  - Be aware of the **psychometric qualities** of measures and relevant literature.
  - Not plead ignorance; find relevant and pertinent information from available resources.

## Steps in Selecting Appropriate Published Tests

Three sequential steps:

### Step 1: Defining the Need Precisely
- Specify the **purpose of testing** and the **type of information** sought.
- **Objectives of the study** must match the **specific purpose of the test**.
- Necessary whether selecting a **single test or a battery** of tests.

### Step 2: Narrowing the Choice
- Consider the test in relation to the **total measurement programme**.
- Match the test type with specific objectives (e.g., scholastic aptitude test matched with a diagnostic reading test).
- Consider **adequacy of the test user/researcher** to administer and interpret results.
- Narrow to a **reasonable number** of tests that can be properly evaluated.

Key information to obtain from a **good test manual**:
1. Appropriate uses for which the test is recommended.
2. Qualifications essential for administering and interpreting the test.
3. Evidence of **reliability** of recommended uses.
4. Evidence of **validity** of recommended uses.
5. Directions for administering and scoring the test.
6. Adequate **norms** for interpreting the scores.

Also recommended: study individual test items and scoring methods carefully.

### Step 3: Using a Test Evaluation Form to Compare Tests
- A **test evaluation form** allows comprehensive, systematic evaluation.
- Provides a **summary comparison** of each test's strengths and weaknesses.

**Test Evaluation Form covers:**

*Identification:*
- Title of Test, Author(s), Publisher, Purpose of Test, Date of Publication, Forms, Types of Scoring, Administration Time

*Technical Features:*
- **Reliability**: nature of evidence (internal consistency, external consistency, standard error of measurement)
- **Validity**: nature of evidence
- **Norms**: type and appropriateness to local situation

*Practical Features:*
- Ease of scoring and interpretation
- Ease of administration (procedure and timing)
- Adequacy of test manual and related materials

*General Evaluation:*
- Summary of strengths
- Summary of weaknesses
- Comments of reviewers
- Recommendation regarding local use

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Psychological test** | A standardized procedure to measure quantitatively or qualitatively one or more aspects of a trait by means of a sample of verbal or non-verbal responses |
| **Individual test** | A test administered to one person at a time (e.g., Binet-Simon) |
| **Group test** | A test administered to more than one person simultaneously |
| **Objective test** | A test scored with no scope for subjective judgment; uses multiple-choice, true-false, or matching items |
| **Subjective test** | A test in which scoring involves subjective judgment (e.g., essay tests) |
| **Stem** | The problem or question part of an objective test item |
| **Distractor** | An incorrect but plausible answer option in a multiple-choice item |
| **Power test** | A test with generous time limit; items arranged in increasing difficulty; measures depth of knowledge |
| **Speed test** | A test with severe time limit; easy, equal-difficulty items; measures response rate |
| **Verbal test** | A test using reading, writing, and oral expression as primary mode; also called paper-pencil test |
| **Nonverbal test** | A test using symbolic materials (pictures, figures) to reduce (not eliminate) language use |
| **Performance test** | A test requiring execution of a task rather than answering questions; prohibits language in items |
| **Nonlanguage test** | A test completely independent of any written, spoken, or reading language; instructions via gesture/pantomime |
| **Standardized test** | A test constructed under standard conditions, administered uniformly, with established reliability, validity, and norms |
| **Teacher-made test** | A test constructed by a teacher for classroom use; modifiable; no standard norms |
| **Objectivity** | Property of a test ensuring complete interpersonal agreement on item meaning and scoring |
| **Reliability** | Self-correlation of a test; extent of consistency in results across administrations (includes internal consistency and temporal consistency) |
| **Internal consistency** | Consistency of results within a single test administration |
| **Temporal consistency** | Consistency of results across testing and retesting |
| **Validity** | Correlation of a test with an outside independent criterion; extent to which a test measures what it intends to measure |
| **Norms** | Average performance of a representative sample on a given test; used to interpret individual scores |
| **Age norms** | Norms based on age group averages |
| **Grade norms** | Norms based on grade level averages |
| **Percentile norms** | Norms expressed as percentage of persons scoring below a given point |
| **Standard score norms** | Norms expressed in standard deviation units from the mean |
| **Item** | A single question or task not broken down into smaller units (Brown, 1953) |
| **Item analysis** | Technique for selecting discriminating items; determines difficulty value, discrimination index, and distractor effectiveness |
| **Difficulty value** | Index of how easy or hard a test item is |
| **Discrimination index** | Extent to which an item differentiates between high- and low-trait scorers |
| **Pre-try-out** | Conrad's first preliminary administration; detects gross ambiguities; minimum n = 100 |
| **Try-out proper** | Conrad's second preliminary administration; provides item analysis data; minimum n = 200 |
| **Final trial administration** | Conrad's third preliminary administration; "dress rehearsal"; checks administration procedure and time limit |
| **Validation** | Procedure of comparing a test with an outside criterion using data from a fresh sample |
| **Test manual** | Document reporting psychometric properties, norms, administration procedures, and scoring methods for a test |
| **Mental Measurements Yearbook (MMY)** | The most important reference source for information about psychological tests |
| **CPTA** | APA Committee on Psychological Tests and Assessment |
| **Divided loyalties** | Ethical dilemma where a psychologist owes responsibility to both an employing institution and the individual being tested |
| **Labelling** | Assigning a diagnostic category to a person; can lead to stigmatization and self-fulfilling prophecy |
| **Certification** | Award of a certificate or license based on passing a test |
| **Placement** | Assignment of persons to appropriate programmes based on needs or skills |
| **Screening** | Identifying persons with exceptional talents or characteristics for categorization |
| **Selection** | Choosing persons from a pool based on test scores for admission or employment |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Russ (1951)** defined a psychological test as "essentially an objective and standardized measure or sample of behaviour" — most frequently quoted in MCQs on definitions.

2. **Brown (1953)** defined an item as "a single question or task that is not often broken down into any smaller units."

3. The **stem** is the problem/question part of an objective test item; the **distractor** is a plausible but incorrect answer option.

4. **Conrad's three preliminary administrations**: Pre-try-out (n ≥ 100), Try-out proper (n ≥ 200), Final trial administration ("dress rehearsal"). Item analysis data come from the **second** administration.

5. **Item analysis** provides three outputs: (i) difficulty value, (ii) discrimination index, (iii) effectiveness of distractors.

6. The final test reliability computation requires a fresh sample of **not less than 100**.

7. **Nonverbal tests** (e.g., Raven Progressive Matrices) use language in instructions but NOT in items; **nonlanguage tests** eliminate all language including instructions (given by gesture/pantomime).

8. **Speed tests** = severe time limit, easy equal-difficulty items, few errors (clerical aptitude tests). **Power tests** = generous time limit, items in increasing difficulty, measures depth of knowledge.

9. **Cronbach (1970)** stated a test even without norms may be called standardized — the fourth condition (norms) is the controversial one.

10. **Validity is generally dependent upon reliability** — a test with poor reliability ordinarily cannot correlate with an outside criterion.

11. **Flanagan** prepared abac tables for reading point-biserial or phi-coefficient validity values from upper/lower group proportions.

12. **Dahlstrom (1969)** investigated invasion of privacy in psychological testing and argued much fear is based on misunderstandings; the APA mandates confidentiality with exceptions only for danger or court orders.

13. **Jackson and Messick (1967)** first identified the ethical issue of **divided loyalties** in psychological testing.

14. The APA document governing ethical use of tests is **Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct** (APA, 1992), consisting of a preamble and **six general principles**.

15. **AERA** = American Educational Research Association; **NCME** = National Council on Measurement in Education — along with APA, they jointly set standards for test constructors and users.

16. **Witkin (1949)** used the **tilting-room-tilting-chair test** to study **cognitive field dependence** — classic example of tests in theoretical behavioural research.

17. The **Mental Measurements Yearbook (MMY)** is the most important reference source for information on published psychological tests.

18. The **five characteristics** of a good test in order: **Objectivity, Reliability, Validity, Norms, Practicability**.

19. The **seven steps** of test construction in order: Planning, Writing Items, Preliminary Administration, Reliability, Validity, Norms, Manual Preparation.

20. Labelling as an ethical issue: a diagnosis can become a **self-fulfilling prophecy**, stigmatizes the person, shifts focus to deficits, makes the person passive, and reduces incentive to change — all reasons a person has the **right to resist labelling**.
