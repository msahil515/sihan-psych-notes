# Single-Subject Experimental Research and Small N Research

This chapter covers the meaning, origin, and general procedures of single-subject experimental research, which evolved from B.F. Skinner's operant conditioning work and is most widely used in behaviour modification and clinical research. It details the core designs (withdrawal, reversal, multiple-baseline), data collection strategies, and evaluation methods, and contrasts this approach with large N research. The chapter concludes with the historical background and rationale for small N designs.

---

## Meaning and Origin of Single-Subject Experimental Research

**Single-subject research** (also called **single-case research** or **N of 1 research**) is a detailed experimental research in which usually one subject, or a very small number of subjects, participates in an experiment, and the effect of an intervention is studied on the same individual over time.

- When more than one subject participates but the N is still small, the situation is referred to as **Small N research** (Kazdin, 1984).
- Single-subject research is NOT primarily a method of testing hypotheses in the traditional sense; rather, it is used to test the hypothesis that a particular treatment will have an effect on one or more specific behaviours.
- These designs are most useful in **clinical research**, particularly **behaviour modification** and **developmental research**.
- The designs are structurally similar to three quasi-experimental designs: the **time-series design**, the **equivalent samples design**, and the **equivalent materials pre-test post-test design**. The key difference is that quasi-experimental designs are used with groups, while single-subject research is applied to individuals.

### Origin and Historical Development

- Single-subject research developed from the **case study method**.
- According to **Kazdin (1982)**, the development of single-subject research as currently practised is largely the outgrowth of the work of **B.F. Skinner**.
- Skinner's animal laboratory research elaborated **operant conditioning**. His research methodology is popularly called the **experimental analysis of behaviour**, and contains many features characteristic of modern single-subject research design.
- In the **1950s**, several investigators adopted Skinner's methodology to human participants and demonstrated the clinical utility of experimental analysis of behaviour for modifying adult and child behaviour.
- This gave birth to the field of **Applied Behaviour Analysis (ABA)**, with its own journal, the **Journal of Applied Behaviour Analysis**, first published in **1968**.

## General Procedures of Single-Subject Experimental Research

There are four core procedural aspects:

1. Repeated Measurement
2. Baselines (Operant Level)
3. Manipulating Variables
4. Length of Phases

---

### 1. Repeated Measurement

**Repeated measurement** is the systematic, repeated observation of the same individual over time. It is one of the most important procedural aspects of single-subject research.

- **Purpose:** To determine whether changes introduced in the experimental condition (the **intervention** or **treatment**) produce changes in the dependent variable.
- The more systematic and repeated the observations, the more valid and reliable the data.
- Behaviours to be observed must be clearly defined, objectively selected, and exhibited by the subject with a reasonable degree of frequency.
- Measurement procedures may include: tests, surveys, questionnaires, observations, and attitude scales.
- Instruments must be usable repeatedly without **contamination** or **test interaction effects**.
- Measurements must be conducted under completely **standardized conditions**: uniform time of day, location, presence of others, and surrounding circumstances.

---

### 2. Baselines or Operant Level

The **baseline** (also called **operant level**) is a record of the subject's behaviour prior to any intervention by the researcher. It establishes the pre-treatment status.

- Baseline data is gathered by observing different aspects of the individual's behaviour several times before intervention begins.
- The baseline must be long enough to determine the **trend of data**.
- Three types of trends a baseline may reveal:
  - **Stable rate** (flat trend)
  - **Increasing rate** (upward trend)
  - **Decreasing rate** (downward trend)
- For best evaluation of intervention effectiveness, the baseline must demonstrate a **stable rate**.
- A baseline showing an **increasing rate** creates a serious problem: the behaviour is already trending in the desired direction before any treatment.
- A good baseline requires a minimum of **three separate observations**; **five to eight or more** observations are preferable.

---

### 3. Manipulating Variables

Like all experimental research, single-subject research requires manipulation of independent variables.

- The ideal condition: **only one variable should be manipulated at any given time**.
- If two variables are manipulated simultaneously, their individual effects cannot be separated, making the outcome **uninterpretable**.
- Variables should be introduced **sequentially** rather than simultaneously.
- **Example:** To study effects of systematic desensitization and medication on an aggressive child:
  - Establish baseline (A)
  - Introduce first intervention, e.g., systematic desensitization (B1)
  - Re-establish baseline (A)
  - Introduce second intervention, e.g., medication (B2)
  - Return to baseline (A)

---

### 4. Length of Phases

In single-subject experimental research, there are ordinarily three phases: **baseline (A) - intervention (B) - baseline (A)**.

- **Barlow and Hersen (1984)** recommended that the relative length of each phase should be as equal as possible.
- A second baseline should always be of the same duration as the initial baseline.
- **Carry-over effect**: A potential danger when the intervention phase is longer than necessary. The positive effect of the intervention continues into the next (withdrawal/baseline) phase.
- **Bijou et al. (1968)** recommended short intervention periods to prevent carry-over effects.

## Basic Designs of Single-Subject Experimental Research

Three basic design types are used:

1. Withdrawal Designs
2. Reversal Designs
3. Multiple-Baseline Designs

---

### I. Withdrawal Designs

**Withdrawal designs** are those in which the intervention is withdrawn after producing behavioural change following the baseline period.

#### A-B Design

The **A-B design** is the simplest research design for N=1 research.

- **A** = Baseline (control condition)
- **B** = Intervention (treatment condition)
- The researcher predicts that if no intervention is introduced, behaviour will continue along the same course as during baseline.
- After establishing the baseline, the intervention is introduced and the researcher examines whether the intervention produces change.
- **Limitations of A-B design:**
  - The researcher cannot know what the response rate would have been without treatment.
  - The researcher cannot determine with certainty whether the response change was produced by the specific intervention or by other extraneous factors (including **placebo effect**).
  - Conclusions are therefore **tentative**.

#### A-B-A Design

The **A-B-A design** (also called the **withdrawal design** or **reversal design**) adds a second baseline phase:

- Sequence: Baseline (A) --> Intervention (B) --> Baseline withdrawal (A)
- The researcher studies whether behaviour:
  - Changes from A to B (increases during treatment)
  - Returns to baseline level after withdrawal of intervention (decreases after B is removed)
- If this pattern is observed, a **functional relationship** (cause and effect) between the intervention and the behavioural change is established.
- More informative and powerful than A-B design.
- **Limitation:** The study ends with the withdrawal phase (A), meaning the subject is left without treatment at the end.

#### A-B-A-B Design

The **A-B-A-B design** is produced when intervention is reintroduced after the withdrawal phase.

- Sequence: Baseline (A) --> Intervention (B) --> Withdrawal baseline (A) --> Reintroduction of Intervention (B)
- Analogous to the **between-group pre-test post-test quasi-experimental design**.
- Pattern expected:
  - Behaviour changes from A to B (improves with treatment)
  - Behaviour decreases when treatment is withdrawn (second A)
  - Behaviour improves again when treatment is reintroduced (second B)
- The convergence of these changes establishes the **functional relationship** between intervention and behavioural measures.
- **Kazdin (1982)** labels baseline condition as **A** and intervention condition as **B**, and suggests that a minimum of four phases (A-B-A-B) are required.
- Preferred over A-B-A design because the study ends with intervention, meaning the subject receives treatment at the study's conclusion.
- **Classic Example (Harris, Wolf and Baer, 1964):**
  - Subject: A 4-year-old boy who cried approximately 8 times each school morning.
  - Phase 1 (A - Baseline, 10 days): Crying rate recorded; teacher ignored the child.
  - Phase 2 (B - Intervention, 10 days): Teacher withdrew attention (reinforcement) from crying and rewarded non-crying behaviour. Crying markedly diminished.
  - Phase 3 (A - Withdrawal, 10 days): Teacher reinstated attention to crying. Crying increased again.
  - This confirmed that teacher attention was the reinforcer maintaining crying behaviour.

#### A-B-A-C-A Design

A common variation of A-B-A-B, used to assess effects of **two different treatments (B and C)** relative to baseline (A).

- The researcher can confirm that both B and C have an effect on behaviour.
- **Limitation:** Cannot assess the **relative efficiency** of B vs. C (i.e., which treatment is more effective).

#### Alternating Treatments Design (ATD)

The **Alternating Treatments Design** is a subclass of A-B-A-B design.

- Two different treatments (A and B) are **alternated randomly** within a single subject.
- Treatment A is withdrawn and replaced by treatment B (not by a baseline).
- Used when a researcher wants to evaluate the **relative effectiveness of two or more treatments**.
- **Example (Ollendick, Shapiro and Barrett, 1981):**
  - Studied relative effectiveness of treatments for **hair-twirling behaviour**.
  - Three conditions: (a) no-treatment (A), (b) practice (first intervention), (c) physical restraint (second intervention).
  - All three conditions were simultaneously alternated and administered.
  - Result: Greater decrease in hair-twirling with "practice" intervention compared to "physical restraint."
- **Advantage:** Treatment is used without a withdrawal and return to baseline, which is more ethically acceptable.
- This design is gaining increasing popularity.

---

### II. Reversal Designs

The **Reversal Design** is another single-subject design in which two **alternative incompatible behaviours** are chosen.

- Separate baselines are established for each incompatible behaviour.
- One behaviour receives Treatment Type 1; the incompatible behaviour receives Treatment Type 2.
- After sufficient data, **treatment conditions are reversed**: the first behaviour now receives the treatment initially given to the second behaviour, and vice versa.
- The researcher compares the behavioural outcomes across sessions to evaluate the differential effects of treatment.
- **Example (McGuigan, 1990):** Two incompatible behaviours: **talking** and **crying**:
  - Phase 1: Reinforce talking (talking increases); do not reinforce crying (crying decreases).
  - Phase 2 (Reversal): Reinforce crying (crying increases); do not reinforce talking (talking decreases).
  - The reversal of treatment conditions produces a reversal of the behavioural outcomes, confirming the functional role of the treatments.

---

### III. Multiple-Baseline Designs

**Multiple-Baseline Designs** involve the simultaneous establishment of **several baselines** prior to the administration of treatments. These designs are essentially **replication designs**.

There are three types:

#### (i) Multiple-Baseline Design Across Behaviours

- Evaluates the effect of the independent variable (treatment) across several different **behaviours** of the **same individual**.
- Steps:
  1. Identify several **compatible behaviours** (behaviours that occur simultaneously in the individual).
  2. Establish separate baselines for each behaviour.
  3. Introduce treatment sequentially to one target behaviour at a time.
  4. If one behaviour changes following treatment while others remain unchanged, treatment is concluded to be effective.
  5. Treatment is then applied to the next behaviour, and so on.

#### (ii) Multiple-Baseline Design Across Subjects

- Applies treatment sequentially to the **same class of behaviour** in **different participants** in the **same environment**.
- Multiple baselines are established to represent the same behaviour in different individuals.
- Treatment is applied one individual at a time (time-staggered intervention).
- Also called a **delayed inter-control design**.
- **Example (Singh and Millichamp, 1987):**
  - Eight adult females with intellectual disabilities (mental retardation) in an institution.
  - Taught to enhance **independent play** using verbal and physical prompts.
  - Baselines established for both independent play and inappropriate play (both were low).
  - Treatment: Verbal instructions plus physical guidance (placing hands on toys).
  - Result: Independent play increased in all participants; inappropriate play remained low throughout; gains maintained at follow-up.

#### (iii) Multiple-Baseline Design Across Conditions or Environments

- Treatment is applied sequentially to the same behaviour across **several different environments or conditions**.
- Example: Four patients in four different rooms, each with different baseline durations (e.g., 20, 30, 40, and 50 minutes). After each respective baseline, the treatment (e.g., reinforcement) is introduced. If response increment is observed in all patients following intervention, treatment is deemed effective.

#### Major Advantage of Multiple-Baseline Design

- The researcher does NOT need to **withdraw the treatment** once applied, unlike withdrawal designs.
- This is particularly important when withdrawal would be unethical (e.g., removing an effective therapy).

## Data Collection Strategies in Single-Subject Experimental Research

Two types of behaviour are studied: **overt behaviour** (directly observable) and **covert behaviour** (internal experiences).

### Methods for Overt Behaviour

#### 1. Frequency Measure

The most popular method of data collection in single-subject research.

- A simple **count of the number of instances** of a target behaviour observed during a given period of time.
- Example: Counting the number of times a child crosses the boundary of a classroom, or the number of unauthorized talkings in class.
- Sometimes more than one behaviour is counted.

#### 2. Duration

A **time-based method** that determines the **actual amount of time** during which an individual performs a behaviour.

- Example: Measuring the total time a hyperactive child sits still on a chair during an instructional programme.

#### 3. Method of Interval Recording (Time-Sampling Method)

Also known as the **time-sampling method**.

- The total observation period is divided into equal, small **observational intervals** (e.g., 2 minutes each).
- The researcher observes the child's behaviour during each interval and records it during a non-observation period.
- Example: 2-minute observation period followed by a 2-minute non-observation recording period.
- **Limitation:** Has many obvious flaws (Barlow and Hersen, 1984).

#### 4. Real-Time Observation Method

An excellent but expensive method.

- Target behaviours are recorded at their **actual frequency, duration, and order**.
- Requires sophisticated and expensive apparatus and equipment.
- Not widely used due to cost.

### Methods for Covert Behaviour and Special Cases

#### 5. Psychophysiological Measures

- Measures of **skin temperature, skin resistance (GSR), pulse rate, blood pressure**, etc.
- Taken using sophisticated instruments.

#### 6. Self-Report Measures

- Subjects are asked to report their feelings and experiences via **questionnaires**.
- Used for studying covert behaviour and subjective experiences.

#### 7. Behavioural Measures

- Data on specific **indirect indices** of target behaviour.
- Example: In a weight reduction study, data might include count of calories consumed and distance walked each day.

## Evaluating Data in Single-Subject Experimental Research

Data in single-subject research are most commonly evaluated through **visual inspection** (graphical analysis). Statistical analysis is seldom used.

### What the Researcher Examines (Kazdin, 1982)

Through visual inspection, the researcher examines:

1. **Level**: The average rate of performance (number of occurrences divided by number of sessions). A change in level refers to a shift in performance between the end of one phase and the start of the next.
2. **Trend**: Systematic increases or decreases in the rate of behaviour over time (measured by the slope of the data lines).
3. **Latency**: The speed at which a change in response occurs after beginning or withdrawing treatment. Quick latency after treatment change confirms effectiveness.
4. **Magnitude**: The size of the change in behaviour.

- The examiner checks whether: (a) the average performance level changes between phases, (b) the rate of behaviour changes between phases, (c) the slope (trend) of data lines changes, and (d) there is a change in response latency when treatment or withdrawal is introduced.
- Some researchers use parametric and non-parametric statistical analyses, but visual/graphical analysis remains the primary method.

## Strengths and Weaknesses of Single-Subject Experimental Research

### Strengths (Advantages)

| # | Strength |
|---|----------|
| 1 | Can conduct scientific investigation with only **one or two subjects**, saving time and resources. |
| 2 | Allows **more precise experimental control** because the researcher focuses entirely on one subject throughout. |
| 3 | Does **not require advanced statistical tests** for data evaluation; visual inspection suffices. |
| 4 | Both **exploratory** and **descriptive** research can easily be carried out. |
| 5 | Better control of **extraneous variables** than intersubject comparison designs. |

### Weaknesses (Limitations)

| # | Weakness | Details |
|---|----------|---------|
| 1 | **Inappropriateness** | Not suitable for survey or ex post facto studies. |
| 2 | **Time-consuming** | Takes several fortnights or months to complete; many large N designs can be done in one session. |
| 3 | **Confounding from order effects** | Effects of one treatment may carry over to subsequent treatment phases; fatigue effects possible. Cannot always separate carry-over from genuine treatment effects. |
| 4 | **Baseline problems** | A-B design cannot demonstrate causality. Variable baselines make conclusions impossible. (Barlow and Hersen, 1984) |
| 5 | **Irreversibility of treatment** | Some treatments (e.g., drug effects, learned behaviours) cannot be reversed. Once a phobia is eliminated, ethical constraints prevent reintroducing it to re-establish a baseline. |
| 6 | **Lack of internal validity** | Changes in behaviour may be due to extraneous variables rather than the treatment. A-B-A-B designs partially address this, but threats remain. |
| 7 | **Researcher bias** | During phase changes, the researcher may inadvertently alter behaviour toward the subject. Strict pre-specification of phase lengths before data collection is required. |
| 8 | **Multiple-baseline specific problems** | Requires selecting behaviours or subjects that are sufficiently independent; if treatment affects untreated behaviours (**response generalisation**), the design logic is compromised. |
| 9 | **Lack of external validity** | Results based on one or very few subjects are difficult to generalise to other participants, settings, or conditions. |
| 10 | **Replication difficulties** | Because subjects learn or change during the study, returning to baseline is often impossible; replication in new individuals who have not experienced prior treatment is difficult. |

## Comparison Between Single-Subject Research and Large N Research

| Dimension | Large N Research | Single-Subject Research |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Control technique** | Uses **randomisation** and statistical methods to control extraneous/confounding variables | Subject serves as **his own control** in all phases; no randomisation of conditions |
| **Subject variables** | Equated or held constant at the beginning by group assignment | Controlled continuously throughout the experiment by the researcher |
| **Number of subjects** | Many (large N) | One or a very small number |
| **Data analysis** | Statistical analysis (means, tests of significance) at the end | **Graphic/visual analysis** of dependent variable values across experimental stages for each subject |
| **Within-condition monitoring** | Researcher generally does not examine data during the experiment | In ABAB designs, data must be collected, evaluated, and analysed **during the experiment** to determine when to change phases |
| **External validity** | Relatively higher; findings can potentially be generalised more broadly | Lower; results from a single or very few subjects are difficult to generalise |
| **Generalisation method** | Statistical generalisation from sample to population | **Inductive reasoning** from specific cases; depends on comparing conditions and differences across situations |

## Small N Design: Nature and Historical Perspectives

### Historical Context

- When psychology emerged as a discipline in the second half of the **19th century**, statistical analysis was still in its infancy.
- **Galton** was beginning to conceptualise correlation; higher inferential statistics were decades away.
- **Fisher's work on ANOVA** appeared in the **1920s**.
- Before that time, **small N studies** prevailed in psychology.
- **Small N designs** are those in which more than one, but still a small number of, subjects participate.

### Early Pioneers Using Small N and Single N Designs

| Pioneer | Contribution |
|---------|-------------|
| **Hermann Ebbinghaus** | Conducted exhaustive study of his own ability to learn and recall lists of nonsense syllables. |
| **Charles Darwin** | Studied child development by keeping a detailed account of his son's early behaviour. |
| **Watson and Rayner** | Conducted their classic study on Little Albert (conditioning of fear in a single infant). |
| **Wundt (Leipzig)** | Laboratory experiments at Leipzig University routinely used very small N (typically doctorate students). |
| **Cattell and Gustav Berger** | Much original work on perception and reaction time at Leipzig included data from just **two persons**. |
| **Dodge (1913)** | Famous study of focal vision using only **three participants**. |

- Large N studies were not completely absent even early on; they appeared in educational psychology and child development (Townsend, 1952).

---

### Reasons for Using Small N Design

#### 1. Moderating Effect / Individual Differences Problem

- **Sidman (1960)** argued, in his book *Tactics of Scientific Research* (1960), that grouping data can **obscure individual performance** and produce **misleading results**.
- Group averages may produce results that characterise no single individual who participated in the study.
- As cited in Solso and Johnson: "Group data may often describe a process or a functional relation that **has no validity for an individual**." (Sidman)
- Individual differences contaminate group means.

#### 2. Practical Problems with Large N

- In **abnormal and clinical psychology**, obtaining large enough samples of individuals with specific disorders is extremely difficult.
- Example: Testing the effectiveness of play therapy for a specific boundary disorder requires at least 60+ matched participants; obtaining a matched sample of 100+ individuals with that specific disorder may be impractical.
- A related problem occurs in **animal research**: when surgical procedures are time-consuming and expensive, or when animal training requires hundreds of hours per animal, a large N design becomes infeasible.
  - **Example:** Patterson and Linden (1981) demonstrated that a **lowland gorilla** can learn more than **400 different signs** by age 10, starting training at age 1 -- such research requires an enormous investment per animal.

#### 3. Skinner's Rationale: Intensive Individual Study

- **Skinner** believed that the best way to **understand, predict, and control behaviour** is to study single individuals intensively.
- General principles should be derived only after exhaustive study of individual performance; inference should be **inductive** (from specific cases to general laws), not deductive.
- Skinner (1956, p. 21): "The rat is always right."
- Skinner preferred "**a few rats for a hundred hours each**" over "**a thousand rats for a single hour**."

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Single-subject research is also called N of 1 research or single-case research.** When more than one but still few subjects are used, it is called Small N research (Kazdin, 1984).

2. **The origin of single-subject research is attributed to B.F. Skinner's experimental analysis of behaviour.** Skinner conducted animal laboratory research on operant conditioning and studied one or a few subjects intensively.

3. **The Journal of Applied Behaviour Analysis was first published in 1968** and is the primary journal for single-subject research.

4. **A good baseline requires a minimum of 3 separate observations** (5-8 or more is better). The baseline must show a stable rate for intervention effects to be clearly interpretable.

5. **A-B-A-B design is the gold standard** for establishing functional (causal) relationships in single-subject research. The classic example is Harris, Wolf and Baer (1964) -- the crying boy experiment.

6. **Carry-over effect** is the continuation of a treatment effect into the next phase; Bijou et al. (1968) recommended short intervention periods to prevent it.

7. **Alternating Treatments Design** (ATD) is a subclass of A-B-A-B design in which two treatments are randomly alternated without returning to baseline. Classic study: Ollendick, Shapiro and Barrett (1981) -- hair-twirling behaviour.

8. **The major advantage of multiple-baseline designs is that the treatment does not need to be withdrawn**, making them more ethical than A-B-A and A-B-A-B designs when treatment withdrawal is harmful.

9. **Three types of multiple-baseline designs: across behaviours, across subjects, and across environments.** Singh and Millichamp (1987) is the cited example for across subjects.

10. **Primary method for evaluating data in single-subject research is visual inspection**, not statistical analysis. Four criteria examined: level, trend, latency, and magnitude (Kazdin, 1982).

11. **Frequency measure is the most popular method of data collection** in single-subject research. The four overt behaviour methods are: frequency, duration, interval recording (time-sampling), and real-time observation.

12. **Sidman (1960), in *Tactics of Scientific Research*,** argued that group data may describe a functional relation with no validity for any single individual -- the core rationale for small N designs.

13. **Ebbinghaus, Darwin, and Watson and Rayner** are the classic early pioneers of single N design. Dodge (1913) studied focal vision with only 3 participants; Cattell and Berger supplied data for much of Wundt's perceptual research.

14. **A-B-A-C-A design can confirm that both Treatment B and Treatment C are effective** relative to baseline, but CANNOT determine which treatment is more effective (no relative comparison possible).

15. **The biggest limitation of single-subject research is poor external validity**: results from one or very few subjects cannot be readily generalised. Replication across many subjects improves this. **The biggest advantage** is the ability to conduct rigorous scientific investigation with only a single subject.
