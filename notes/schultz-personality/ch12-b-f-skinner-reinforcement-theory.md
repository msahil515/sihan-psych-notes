# Ch 12: B. F. Skinner: Reinforcement Theory

Skinner's radical behaviorism accounts for all behavior through observable consequences — specifically reinforcement. He rejected internal states, traits, and the unconscious, defining personality as a collection of operant behaviors shaped by external reinforcers. His work founds behavior modification, token economies, and applied behavior analysis.

---

## Rats, Pigeons, and an Empty Organism

- Skinner offered **no personality theory**; he aimed to account for **all behavior** in descriptive terms, studying only what could be **seen, manipulated, measured**. The **antithesis** of psychoanalytic, trait, cognitive, and humanistic approaches.
- **Empty organism**: nothing inside us explains behavior scientifically. Skinner did **not deny** internal forces, only their **usefulness for science** ("no more value than...the soul").
- Subjects were **rats and pigeons** — animal behavior is simpler; human/animal differences are in **degree, not kind**. His techniques (behavior therapy) apply to humans nonetheless.

---

## The Life of Skinner (1904–1990)

- Born in **Susquehanna, Pennsylvania**. Strict childhood: "I was taught to fear God, the police, and what people will think." Mother's control = saying "tut tut." These experiences showed him adult behavior is **determined by childhood rewards/punishments**; saw people as "complex systems behaving in lawful ways."
- Built mechanical devices and kept animals as a child; fascinated by **performing pigeons**; later trained pigeons to play Ping-Pong and guide missiles.
- Majored in **English** at **Hamilton College**; aspired to be a novelist. His failed writing period (age 22) = his **"dark year"** (compared to Erikson's identity crisis).
- Turned to behavioral science; read **Pavlov** and **Watson**; entered **Harvard in 1928**; **Ph.D. in three years**.
- In his 40s, a depression resolved by writing **Walden Two (1948)** — a society run by **positive reinforcement** (2+ million copies; possible precursor to positive psychology).
- His Rorschach/TAT profile (Gronerod et al., 2013): high **conscientiousness** and **openness**, somewhat extraverted, **neurotic**, quirky, driven. Cited **more than Freud**.

## Reinforcement: The Basis of Behavior

- **Core idea**: behavior is controlled by its **consequences**. Whoever controls the reinforcers **controls behavior**.

### Respondent Behavior
- **Respondent behavior**: responses **elicited** by specific stimuli (e.g., knee jerk); unlearned, automatic, involuntary; or learned through **conditioning**.

#### Conditioning (Classical/Pavlovian)
- **Conditioning**: substituting one stimulus for another; from **Pavlov**, adopted by **Watson**.

> **KEY STUDY: Pavlov's Dog** — pairing a bell with food made the dog salivate to the bell alone. Fundamental law: "A conditioned response cannot be established in the absence of reinforcement."

- **Reinforcement**: strengthening a response by adding a reward, increasing repetition.
- **Extinction**: eliminating a behavior by **withholding reinforcement**; greater training reinforcement → **more resistant** to extinction (Shull & Grimes, 2006).

### Operant Behavior
- **Operant behavior**: behavior **emitted** spontaneously/voluntarily that **operates on the environment** to change it. More important than respondent behavior; its frequency is modified by the **reinforcement that follows**.

---

## Operant Conditioning and the Skinner Box

- **Operant conditioning**: changing the consequences of a response affects how often it occurs.
- **Skinner box**: a box with a lever/bar, food trough, and recording equipment.
- **Process**: a food-deprived rat behaves randomly (emitted, not elicited) → accidentally presses the lever → food drops → presses more → behavior is now **under the control of reinforcers**; withholding food **extinguishes** it. **The person who controls the reinforcers controls behavior.**
- Real-world: drug-sniffing dogs, mine-detecting rats (Poling et al., 2010), trained whales/dolphins.
- **Personality** = a **collection of operant behaviors** strengthened from infancy; "neurotic/abnormal" behaviors were somehow reinforced. **Continuous reinforcement** = reinforcing every response.

---

## Schedules of Reinforcement

- Behavior is rarely reinforced every time. **Reinforcement schedules** = patterns of providing/withholding reinforcers.

| Schedule | Basis | Response Rate | Resistance to Extinction | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Fixed Interval (FI)** | First response after a set time | Moderate; drops after reinforcement | Lower (if previously continuous) | Weekly salary |
| **Fixed Ratio (FR)** | After a set number of responses | High | Moderate | Piece-rate pay, commission |
| **Variable Interval (VI)** | After varying time intervals | Moderate and stable | Higher | Fishing |
| **Variable Ratio (VR)** | After varying number of responses | **Highest and most stable** | **Highest** | Slot machines, gambling |

- **FI**: timing unrelated to number of responses; shorter intervals → higher response rate.
- **FR**: depends on how often the subject responds; faster than FI.
- **VR**: most effective for high, stable rates; most resistant to extinction; most everyday learning is VI/VR.

## The Shaping of Behavior

- **Successive approximation (shaping)** (Skinner, 1953): complex behavior is acquired by reinforcing **successive stages** that approximate the final behavior.

> **KEY STUDY: Pigeon Pecking** — reinforced first for turning toward a spot, then moving toward it, then thrusting its head, finally touching it with its beak; conditioned in **under 3 minutes**.

- **Language acquisition**: parents reinforce sounds that increasingly approximate real words.

> **KEY STUDY: Shaping Erich Fromm** — Skinner shaped Fromm's hand into a chopping motion during a lecture by smiling/nodding when the hand came down; within **five minutes** Fromm was "chopping the air...vigorously." Shows shaping works on humans naturally.

---

## Superstitious Behavior

- **Superstitious behavior**: persistent behavior with a **coincidental, not functional**, relationship to reinforcement (accidental pairing).

> **KEY STUDY: Pigeon Superstition** — a hungry pigeon reinforced every **15 seconds** regardless of behavior repeated whatever it was doing at the moment of reward; short intervals → learned quickly. The connection is unintentional and can persist for life.

- **MLB players**: **74%** admitted superstitious behavior; **Americans more superstitious** than Japanese (Burger & Lynn, 2005).

---

## The Self-Control of Behavior

- Behavior is controlled by **external variables**, but we can use **self-control** — altering the impact of external events (not via a mysterious "self," but by controlling external variables).

**Four techniques:**
1. **Stimulus avoidance** — remove yourself from the stimulus (alcoholics keep no liquor at home).
2. **Self-administered satiation** — overdo a habit until disgusted (chain-smoking to quit).
3. **Aversive stimulation** — declare intentions publicly so failure brings embarrassment.
4. **Self-reinforcement** — reward yourself for desirable behavior.

> **KEY STUDY: Tangney, Baumeister, & Boone (2004)** — high self-control → better grades, adjustment, self-esteem, relationships; lower anger.
> **Wills et al. (2007)**: more nurturing, involved parents → higher self-control in African-American children.

- High self-control also relates to life satisfaction and happiness (Hofmann et al., 2014). Baumeister: self-control failures underlie addiction, overeating, crime, debt, etc.

## Applications of Operant Conditioning

### Token Economy
- **Token economy**: tokens (exchangeable for objects/privileges) awarded for desirable behaviors.

> **KEY STUDY: Ayllon & Azrin (1968)** — pioneering study; a ward of female psychotic patients earned **tokens** for grooming and work (1–10 tokens), exchangeable for candy, cigarettes, outings. **Escorted town trip / social-worker meeting = 100 tokens; psychologist meeting = only 20**. Patients began grooming, working, socializing; self-esteem improved, dependency decreased.

- Other applications: reduced aggression by **79%** in impaired patients (DePhilippis et al., 2008); effective for **autistic children** in Japan; online token economy for **smokers** using video + carbon monoxide monitoring (Dallery, Glenn, & Raiff, 2007).
- **When tokens stop**: behaviors usually **revert** (Kazdin & Bootzin, 1972) unless caregivers continue rewarding with smiles/praise (Kazdin, 1989).

### Behavior Modification
- **Behavior modification**: applying reinforcement principles to produce behavioral change — in education, business (reduced absenteeism, improved performance/safety), and job-skills training. Focus: **overt behavior**, not unconscious anxieties.

### Punishment and Negative Reinforcement
- **Punishment**: applying an aversive stimulus to **decrease** a response. Skinner found it **ineffective** ("give no long-term results...escape, counterattack, or apathy"); **positive reinforcement** is much more effective.
- **Negative reinforcement**: **strengthening** a response by **removing** an aversive stimulus — **NOT** punishment (critical distinction). E.g., stopping smoking to end a nagging spouse. Skinner preferred positive reinforcement (more consistently effective).

## Questions about Human Nature

- **Nature vs. nurture**: people are products of **learning**, shaped more by external variables than genetics. Childhood matters (basic behaviors form there), but behavior can **change at any age**.
- **Uniqueness**: different experiences → no two people behave identically.
- **No ultimate goal** (no inferiority, anxiety, or self-actualization — those assume internal states). **Societal goal**: in *Walden Two*, direct behavior toward a society most likely to survive.
- **Free will vs. determinism**: people function like **machines** in lawful, predetermined ways; rejected an autonomous self. Yet we are responsible for **designing our environment**: "I am not trying to change people...I want to change the world in which they live."

## Assessment in Skinner's Theory

- No free association, dream analysis, or projective tests — interested in **behavior**, not personality.
- **Functional analysis**: assess (1) **frequency** of behavior, (2) **situation** in which it occurs, (3) **reinforcement** associated with it.

**Three approaches:**
1. **Direct observation** (two+ observers for reliability).

> **KEY STUDY: Hawkins et al. (1966)** — psychologists observed an unruly 4-year-old at **home (16 hours)**; identified **9 undesirable behaviors**; found the mother was **reinforcing** misbehavior with toys/food; instructed her to reward positive behavior only. Baseline established for comparison.

2. **Self-reports** (interviews/questionnaires; e.g., fear in driving, dentistry, public speaking).
3. **Physiological measurements** (heart rate, muscle tension, brain waves) to confirm other data.

- **Ultimate goal**: modify behavior, not change personality.

---

## Research on Skinner's Theory

- Preferred **intensive study of a single subject** over group statistics (averages don't reflect individual response behavior). Believed valid, replicable results come from sufficient single-subject data under controlled conditions. Results were **highly supportive**.

## Reflections on Skinner's Theory

### Criticisms
1. **Determinism**.
2. **Humanistic objections**: ignores free will and uniquely human qualities.
3. **Type of subject**: extrapolating from pigeons to people.
4. **Oversimplification** of social/cultural issues from simple experiments.
5. **All behavior is learned** — challenged by **instinctive drift**.

### Instinctive Drift
> **KEY STUDY: Breland & Breland (1961), "The Misbehavior of Organisms"** — two of Skinner's students conditioned **6,000+ animals** of **38 species**. Animals showed **instinctive drift** (substituting instinctive for reinforced behaviors, even delaying food). Pigs buried/rooted coins; raccoons "washed" them instead of depositing. The title parodied Skinner's **"The Behavior of Organisms" (1938)**.

- **Instinctive drift**: substituting instinctive behaviors for reinforced ones.

### Current Status
- A potent force in 20th-century psychology. Journals: *Journal of the Experimental Analysis of Behavior* (**1958**), *Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis* (**1968**). Awards: APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award (**1958**), Gold Medal, U.S. National Medal of Science; *Time* cover. Key works: *The Behavior of Organisms* (1938), *Walden Two* (1948), *Beyond Freedom and Dignity* (1971, controversial bestseller).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Skinner offered no personality theory**; personality = a collection of operant behaviors shaped by reinforcers.
2. **"Empty organism"**: nothing inside explains behavior scientifically.
3. **Respondent (elicited, reflexive) vs. operant (emitted, operates on environment)**.
4. **Resistance to extinction**: VR (highest) > VI > FR > FI (lowest when previously continuous).
5. **VR produces the highest, most stable response rate** — the gambling principle.
6. **Negative reinforcement ≠ punishment**: NR removes an aversive stimulus to strengthen; punishment applies one to decrease.
7. **Successive approximation (shaping)**: pigeon pecking a spot in <3 minutes.
8. **Superstitious behavior**: coincidental, not functional; pigeons reinforced every 15s; 74% of MLB players (Burger & Lynn, 2005).
9. **Token economy (Ayllon & Azrin, 1968)**: social-worker meeting = 100 tokens; psychologist = only 20.
10. **Instinctive drift (Breland & Breland, 1961)**: instinctive behaviors override reinforced ones (pigs/raccoons with coins); title parodied "The Behavior of Organisms" (1938).
11. **Functional analysis**: frequency, situation, reinforcement.
12. **Four self-control techniques**: stimulus avoidance, self-administered satiation, aversive stimulation, self-reinforcement.
13. **Single-subject intensive study** (not group statistics).
14. **Skinner's profile** (Gronerod et al., 2013): high conscientiousness/openness, neurotic, quirky.
15. **Key works**: *The Behavior of Organisms* (1938), *Walden Two* (1948), *Beyond Freedom and Dignity* (1971); APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award (1958).
