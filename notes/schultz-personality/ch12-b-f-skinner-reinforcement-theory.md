# Ch 12: B. F. Skinner: Reinforcement Theory

B. F. Skinner's approach is a radical behavioral theory that accounts for all behavior through observable, measurable consequences — specifically reinforcement. Unlike other personality theorists, Skinner rejected internal states, traits, and unconscious processes entirely, defining personality as nothing more than a collection of operant behaviors shaped by external reinforcers. His work forms the foundation of behavior modification, token economies, and applied behavior analysis.

---

## Rats, Pigeons, and an Empty Organism

### Core Stance
- Skinner did **not** offer a personality theory per se; his work aimed to account for **all behavior** in factual, descriptive terms.
- He restricted investigation to only what could be **seen, manipulated, and measured** in the laboratory.
- **Psychology**, in Skinner's view, is the science of behavior — of what an organism **does**.
- His approach is the **antithesis** of psychoanalytic, trait, life-span, cognitive, and humanistic approaches — different in subject matter, methodology, and aims.

### The "Empty Organism"
- Most theorists look **inside** the person for causes of behavior (unconscious motives, drives, traits).
- Skinner made **no reference to internal, subjective states** to account for behavior.
- He argued that unconscious influences, defense mechanisms, and traits cannot be seen and therefore have **no place in scientific psychology** — "no more value for science than the old theological concept of the soul."
- Skinner did **not deny** the existence of internal forces, only their **usefulness for science**.
- He applied the same logic to physiological processes: "The inside of the organism is irrelevant either as the site of physiological processes or as the locus of mentalistic activities" (Evans, 1968, p. 22).
- **Empty organism**: Skinner's term indicating there is nothing inside us that can explain behavior in scientific terms.

### Where Are the People?
- Skinner's research subjects were **rats and pigeons**, not humans.
- Rationale: animal behavior is **simpler** than human behavior; science must proceed from simple to complex.
- Differences between human and animal behavior are in **degree, not kind** — fundamental processes are similar.
- Despite animal focus, his techniques (behavior therapy) have been applied to humans: psychoses, mental retardation, autism, schools, businesses, correctional institutions, hospitals.

## The Life of Skinner (1904–1990)

### A Strict Childhood
- Born in **Susquehanna, Pennsylvania**; older of two sons (brother died at age 16).
- Parents constantly dictated clear rules of proper behavior.
- Quote: "I was taught to fear God, the police, and what people will think" (Skinner, 1967, p. 407).
- Mother's control method: saying **"tut tut"** (never deviated from strict standards).
- Grandmother used the red-hot coals of the parlor stove to illustrate the punishments of Hell.
- Father showed him the county jail and took him to a lecture about prison life.
- Key insight: these experiences demonstrated to Skinner that his adult behaviors were **determined by rewards and punishments (reinforcements) received as a child**.
- He came to view people as **"complex system[s] behaving in lawful ways"** (Skinner, 1971, p. 202).

### Machines and Animals
- Spent hours as a child constructing mechanical devices: wagons, seesaws, carousels, slingshots, model airplanes, a steam cannon.
- Also attempted (and failed) a perpetual-motion machine.
- Made pets of turtles, snakes, toads, lizards, and chipmunks.
- Was fascinated by a flock of **performing pigeons** at a county fair (pulling fire engines, climbing ladders, wearing fireman's hats).
- Later trained pigeons to play **Ping-Pong** and guide a missile to its target; taught a cat to play piano; taught a Beagle to play hide and seek.

### A Dark Year
- Majored in **English** at **Hamilton College** (upstate New York); aspired to be a novelist.
- Built a study in his parents' attic in Scranton, Pennsylvania — waited for inspiration that never came.
- Called this period his **"dark year"** (age 22); Schultz compares it to **Erikson's identity crisis**.
- His occupational identity as a writer had collapsed, taking with it his sense of self-worth.
- Moved to **Greenwich Village** in New York City but still could not write.
- Several women rejected him; he branded one woman's initial on his arm.

### A New Identity
- Decided to study human behavior by **scientific methods** rather than methods of fiction.
- Read **Pavlov** and **Watson**; committed himself to **behaviorism**.
- Entered **Harvard in 1928** to study psychology (had never taken a course before); earned **Ph.D. in three years**.
- Chose behaviorism, leading him to reject all feelings and emotions he had tried to draw on as a writer.
- Psychic processes appeared in his work only as "objects of derision."

### A Period of Depression
- Held postdoctoral fellowships; stayed at Harvard until 1936.
- Taught at **University of Minnesota** and **Indiana University**; returned to Harvard in 1947.
- In his **40s**, experienced a period of depression.
- Resolved it by returning to writing: projected his emotional and intellectual discontent onto the protagonist of the novel **Walden Two** (1948).
- *Walden Two*: describes a society where all aspects of life are controlled by **positive reinforcement**.
- Has sold more than **2 million copies**; still in print.
- Has been suggested as a precursor to **positive psychology** (Adams, 2012).

### Quirky, Conscientious, and Somewhat Neurotic
- Early in career, Skinner took the **Rorschach** and the **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)**.
- In 2012, psychologists in Norway (Gronerod, Overskeid, & Hartmann, 2013; Overskeid et al., 2012) examined his results.
- Personality profile: **high in conscientiousness** and **openness to new experiences**; somewhat **extraverted**; **neurotic**; **quirky and driven**; strained social relationships; lack of liveliness.
- Well into his 80s, he continued working, recording daily work output and average time spent per published word (**2 minutes per published word**).
- Cited in psychology literature **more frequently than Freud**.

## Reinforcement: The Basis of Behavior

- **Fundamental idea**: behavior can be controlled by its **consequences** (what follows the behavior).
- An animal or human can be trained to perform virtually any act; the **type of reinforcement** following the behavior determines it.
- Whoever controls the reinforcers **controls behavior**.

### Respondent Behavior
- **Respondent behavior**: responses made to or elicited by specific environmental stimuli.
- Example: a knee jerk (tap stimulus → leg jerks).
- Characteristics: **unlearned, automatic, involuntary**; no training required.
- At a higher level: respondent behavior that is **learned** through **conditioning**.

#### Conditioning (Classical/Pavlovian)
- **Conditioning**: learning that involves the substitution of one stimulus for another.
- Originated in the work of **Ivan Pavlov** (Russian physiologist, early 1900s).
- Adopted by **John B. Watson** as basic research method for behaviorism.

> **KEY STUDY: Pavlov's Dog Experiment**
> - **Researcher**: Ivan Pavlov
> - **Method**: Sounded a bell shortly before feeding a dog; repeated pairings of bell + food.
> - **Finding**: Dog initially salivated only to food; after repeated pairings, began salivating to the bell alone. Conditioned to respond to what was previously a neutral stimulus.
> - **Fundamental law of learning**: "A conditioned response cannot be established in the absence of reinforcement."

#### Reinforcement
- **Reinforcement**: the act of strengthening a response by adding a reward, thus increasing the likelihood that the response will be repeated.
- The act of reinforcing a response **strengthens it** and increases the likelihood of repetition.

#### Extinction
- **Extinction**: the process of eliminating a behavior by withholding reinforcement.
- If the bell is rung repeatedly without food, the dog's salivary response decreases in frequency and intensity until no response occurs.
- **Greater reinforcement during training** = **more resistant** the conditioned response is to extinction (Shull & Grimes, 2006).
- Extinction will eventually occur regardless.

### Operant Behavior
- **Operant behavior**: behavior emitted spontaneously or voluntarily that operates on the environment to change it.
- To Skinner, operant behavior was **more important** than respondent behavior.
- Much human behavior appears spontaneous and cannot be traced to a specific stimulus.
- Behavior is **emitted** (not elicited); appears voluntary, not involuntary.
- **Nature and frequency** of operant behavior will be determined or modified by the **reinforcement that follows it**.
- Key contrast: respondent behavior has **no effect** on the environment; operant behavior **operates on the environment and changes it**.

## Operant Conditioning and the Skinner Box

- **Operant conditioning**: the procedure by which a change in the consequences of a response will affect the rate at which the response occurs.
- **Skinner box** (operant-conditioning apparatus): a box containing a lever/bar on one wall, a trough for food pellets, and recording equipment.

### The Rat in the Skinner Box (Process)
1. Food-deprived rat is placed in the box.
2. Rat's initial behavior is **spontaneous and random** (sniffing, poking, exploring) — behavior is emitted, not elicited.
3. At some point, rat **depresses the lever** — food pellet drops into the trough.
4. Bar-pressing has **operated on the environment** (environment now includes a food pellet).
5. Rat presses bar **more often** → gets more food → presses even more frequently.
6. Rat's behavior is now **under the control of the reinforcers**.
7. Next day: experimenter can **predict** behavior and **control** bar-pressing by presenting or withholding reinforcers.
8. Withholding food → **extinguishes** operant behavior (same as respondent behavior).
9. Key: **the person who controls the reinforcers controls the subject's behavior**.

### From the Skinner Box to the Real World
- Real-world examples of operant conditioning:
  - Dogs conditioned to sniff out drugs or explosives.
  - Giant African rats in Mozambique trained to detect buried land mines (Poling et al., 2010).
  - Lobsters taught to grasp a bar with their claws for food (Tomina & Takahata, 2010).
  - Whales and dolphins trained to perform tricks at SeaWorld.
  - Calves taught to urinate only in designated places (Vaughan et al., 2014).

### Operating on the Environment (Human Example)
- Infants display random, spontaneous behaviors; parents reinforce some (with food, hugs, toys) and extinguish others.
- **Positively reinforced behaviors persist; disapproved behaviors are extinguished**.
- Skinner quote: "Operant conditioning shapes behavior as a sculptor shapes a lump of clay" (1953, p. 91).
- Reinforcers: experimenter with food pellet, puppy owner with dog biscuit, mother with a smile, boss with a pat on the back, government with a promise.

### Personality: A Collection of Operant Behaviors
- **Personality** in Skinner's framework: a **pattern or collection of operant behaviors**.
- Reinforced behaviors from infancy onward **strengthen and form patterns**.
- What other psychologists called **neurotic or abnormal behavior** = behaviors that somehow were reinforced.
- **Continuous reinforcement**: presenting a reinforcer after every response.

## Schedules of Reinforcement

- In real life, behavior is **rarely reinforced every time** it occurs.
- **Reinforcement schedules**: patterns or rates of providing or withholding reinforcers.
- Skinner tested four main schedules:

### Fixed Interval (FI)
- **Fixed-interval schedule**: reinforcer is presented following the **first response after a fixed time interval** has elapsed.
- The timing of reinforcement has **nothing to do with the number of responses**.
- Whether the rat responds 3 or 20 times per minute, reinforcer only arrives after the fixed time period **plus** the correct response.
- Real-world example: **weekly or monthly salary** (paid by time elapsed, not by output).
- Shorter intervals → **greater frequency of response**.
- Response rate **declines** as interval between reinforcements lengthens.
- **Effective in real-world situations**: e.g., reducing disruptive behavior in special education classrooms (Tomlin & Reed, 2012).
- Responses stop **sooner** if the rat was previously reinforced continuously (vs. intermittently).

### Fixed Ratio (FR)
- **Fixed-ratio schedule**: reinforcers are given only after a **specified number of responses**.
- Unlike FI, presentation of reinforcers **depends on how often the subject responds**.
- Rat must perform a required number of responses to get food.
- Brings about a **faster rate of responding** than fixed-interval.
- Real-world examples: **piece-rate pay** (earn more by producing more); **commission-based sales** (income depends on number of products sold).

### Variable Interval (VI)
- **Variable-interval schedule**: reinforcer appears after **varying amounts of time** (not fixed).
- Example: 2 hours, then 1 hour 30 minutes, then 2 hours 15 minutes.
- Real-world example: **fishing** — reward determined by random appearance of fish.

### Variable Ratio (VR)
- **Variable-ratio schedule**: based on an **average number of responses between reinforcers**, with great variability around that average.
- Skinner found VR most effective in bringing about **high and stable response rates**.
- Most resistant to extinction.
- Real-world examples: **slot machines, roulette wheels, horse races, state lottery games** — all pay on VR schedule.
- **Most everyday learning** occurs as a result of variable-interval or variable-ratio schedules.

### Summary Table: Schedules of Reinforcement

| Schedule | Basis for Reinforcement | Response Rate | Resistance to Extinction | Real-World Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fixed Interval (FI) | First response after a set time period | Moderate; drops after reinforcement | Lower (if previously continuous) | Weekly salary |
| Fixed Ratio (FR) | After set number of responses | High | Moderate | Piece-rate pay, commission |
| Variable Interval (VI) | After varying time intervals | Moderate and stable | Higher | Fishing |
| Variable Ratio (VR) | After varying number of responses | Highest and most stable | Highest | Slot machines, gambling |

## The Shaping of Behavior

### Successive Approximation
- **Successive approximation** (also called **shaping**): the method by which complex behavior is acquired.
- Organism is reinforced as its behavior comes in **successive, consecutive stages** to approximate the final desired behavior.
- Proposed by **Skinner (1953)**.

> **KEY STUDY: Pigeon Pecking Experiment**
> - **Researcher**: Skinner
> - **Method**: Trained pigeon to peck at a specific spot in its cage using successive approximation.
> - **Process**:
>   1. First, pigeon reinforced for merely **turning toward** the designated spot.
>   2. Then reinforcement withheld until pigeon made any **movement toward** the spot.
>   3. Then only movements **closer** to the spot.
>   4. Then only when pigeon **thrust its head** toward the spot.
>   5. Finally, only when **beak touched** the spot.
> - **Finding**: Pigeons conditioned in **less than 3 minutes**.

### Application to Language Acquisition
- Children learn complex behavior of **speaking** through successive approximation.
- Infants spontaneously emit meaningless sounds → parents reinforce by smiling, laughing, talking.
- Parents provide stronger reinforcers for sounds that **approximate real words**.
- Parental reinforcement becomes more restricted over time — only for appropriate usage and proper pronunciation.
- Acquiring language skills = shaped by **differential reinforcement in stages**.

> **KEY STUDY: Shaping Erich Fromm's Behavior**
> - **Researcher**: Skinner
> - **Subject**: Psychoanalyst Erich Fromm (during a lecture, annoyed Skinner with excessive talking).
> - **Method**: Skinner wrote a colleague to "Watch Fromm's left hand. I am going to shape a chopping motion." Whenever Fromm's left hand came up, Skinner looked straight at him; when the hand came down, Skinner nodded and smiled.
> - **Finding**: Within **five minutes**, Fromm was "chopping the air so vigorously that his wristwatch kept slipping out over his hand" (Skinner, 1983, pp. 150-151).
> - **Significance**: Demonstrates shaping works on humans in naturalistic settings.

## Superstitious Behavior

- **Superstitious behavior**: persistent behavior that has a **coincidental and not a functional** relationship to the reinforcement received.
- Occurs when we are reinforced **accidentally** after displaying some behavior — the behavior is then repeated because it was accidentally paired with the reinforcer.
- Real-world examples from football:
  - Tampa Bay Buccaneers offensive lineman: switched beds to be closer to bathroom → playing improved → insisted on same bed arrangement for rest of career.
  - NFL kicker: hugged goal posts before each game (had done it once before a successful kick).

### How to Shape Superstitious Behavior

> **KEY STUDY: Pigeon Superstitious Behavior**
> - **Researcher**: Skinner
> - **Method**: Hungry pigeon placed in operant-conditioning apparatus; reinforced every **15 seconds** on a fixed-interval schedule regardless of behavior.
> - **Finding**: A single reinforcement was powerful enough to lead the pigeon to repeat the accidentally reinforced behavior more frequently. With short intervals between reinforcers, superstitious behaviors are **learned quickly**.
> - Whatever behavior was being emitted **at the moment of reward** would be reinforced.
> - **Significance**: Connection between behavior and reinforcement is **unintentional**; in humans, such behaviors may persist throughout life and require only occasional reinforcement.

- A study of **major league baseball players** in the US and Japan:
  - **74 percent** admitted engaging in superstitious behavior (Burger & Lynn, 2005).
  - **American players** were more superstitious than **Japanese players** — cultural differences influence superstitious behavior.

## The Self-Control of Behavior

- According to Skinner, behavior is controlled by **external variables**.
- However, we have the ability to use **self-control** — acting to alter the impact of external events.
- **Self-control**: the ability to exert control over the variables that determine our behavior.
- Skinner did **not** mean acting under a mysterious "self" — rather, we can control the **external variables** that determine behavior.

### Four Self-Control Techniques

#### 1. Stimulus Avoidance
- **Stimulus avoidance**: removing yourself from an external variable (stimulus) that is affecting your behavior.
- Example: if roommate is too noisy, go to the library.
- Example: alcoholics avoid having liquor in their homes.

#### 2. Self-Administered Satiation
- **Self-administered satiation**: curing a bad habit by overdoing the behavior until one becomes disgusted or sick of it.
- Example: chain-smoking until so disgusted that one quits.
- Has been successful in therapeutic programs to eliminate smoking.

#### 3. Aversive Stimulation
- **Aversive stimulation**: declaring an intention publicly so that failure results in unpleasant consequences (embarrassment, criticism, personal failure).
- Example: obese people declare their intention to lose weight to friends (in person or via Facebook/social networking) — failure = embarrassment.

#### 4. Self-Reinforcement
- **Self-reinforcement**: rewarding oneself for displaying good or desirable behaviors.
- Example: a teenager who agrees to maintain a certain GPA might reward themselves with concert tickets or new clothes.

### Benefits of Self-Control

> **KEY STUDY: Self-Control and College Students**
> - **Researchers**: Tangney, Baumeister, & Boone (2004)
> - **Method**: Large-scale study of American college students scored on a measure of self-control.
> - **Finding**: High self-control → **better grades, higher psychological adjustment, greater self-acceptance and self-esteem, better interpersonal skills and family relationships, lower levels of anger**.

> **KEY STUDY: Self-Control and African-American Children**
> - **Researchers**: Wills et al. (2007)
> - **Sample**: African-American children (average age 11.2 years).
> - **Finding**: Children whose parents were **more nurturing and involved** had **higher levels of self-control**.

- High self-control also related to **satisfaction with life, happiness, and avoidance of frequent conflict** (Hofmann et al., 2014).
- Baumeister (quoted in Weir, 2012): self-control failures are central to "most problems that plague modern individuals" — addiction, overeating, crime, domestic violence, STDs, prejudice, debt, unwanted pregnancy, underperformance at school and work, failure to exercise.

## Applications of Operant Conditioning

- Operant-conditioning techniques applied in clinical, business, and educational settings.
- Used with children and adults, mentally healthy and mentally disturbed, individuals and groups.

### Token Economy Programs

- **Token economy**: a behavior-modification technique in which tokens (which can be exchanged for valued objects or privileges) are awarded for desirable behaviors.

> **KEY STUDY: Ayllon & Azrin (1968) — Token Economy with Psychotic Patients**
> - **Researchers**: Ayllon & Azrin (1968) — pioneering study.
> - **Setting**: A ward of female psychotic patients in a state mental institution (treated as a "giant Skinner box").
> - **Method**: Patients offered opportunities to work at jobs normally performed by paid hospital attendants, in exchange for **tokens** (functioning like money).
> - **Tokens could purchase**: candy, cigarettes, lipstick, gloves, newspapers, movies on the ward, walks around the hospital grounds, room upgrades.
> - **Most expensive privileges (100 tokens)**: escorted trip into town, private meeting with a social worker.
> - **Private meeting with a psychologist**: worth only **20 tokens**.
> - **Behaviors to earn tokens**: taking baths at designated time, brushing teeth, making beds, combing hair, dressing properly (1 token each); periods of work in hospital kitchen or laundry, cleaning the ward, running errands, taking other patients for walks (up to 10 tokens per period).
> - **Findings**: Patients began grooming themselves, cleaning surroundings, busying themselves at tasks, interacting socially, assuming some responsibility for patient care. Self-esteem improved markedly; became less dependent.

- Additional token economy applications:
  - Aggressive behaviors among cognitively impaired patients in a psychiatric hospital reduced by **79 percent** after a nine-month program (DePhilippis et al., 2008).
  - Highly effective for changing behavior of **autistic children** in Japan (Ogasahara et al., 2013).

#### The Token Economy Online
> **KEY STUDY: Online Token Economy for Smokers**
> - **Researchers**: Dallery, Glenn, & Raiff (2007)
> - **Method**: Heavy smokers made **video recordings of themselves** at home **twice a day** over four weeks; used web camera to provide a carbon monoxide sample sent electronically to smoking clinic.
> - **Vouchers** earned by reducing carbon monoxide level over four days and by maintaining level consistent with non-smoker; exchanged for items purchased over Internet.
> - **Finding**: Significant decreases in carbon monoxide levels; sustained abstinence throughout the study period.

#### What Happens When the Tokens Stop?
- **Reinforcement must be continued** if desired behavior changes are to persist.
- When tokens are no longer provided, reinforced behaviors **usually revert to original state** (Kazdin & Bootzin, 1972; Repucci & Saunders, 1974).
- **Exception**: if caregivers trained to reward desirable behaviors with **smiles, praise, hugs**, etc., behaviors conditioned in the token economy are **more likely to continue** in the home setting (Kazdin, 1989).

### Behavior Modification Programs
- **Behavior modification**: a form of therapy that applies the principles of reinforcement to bring about desired behavioral changes.
- Applications:
  - **Education**: improved academic performance and social behavior; reduced behavioral, emotional, and developmental problems (Lang & Rispoli, 2015).
  - **Business and industry**: reduced absenteeism, lateness, abuse of sick-leave privileges; improved job performance and safety.
  - **Job skills training**: teaching low-level job skills.
- Business reinforcers: pay, job security, recognition from supervisors, perks and status, opportunity for personal growth.
- Focus: changing **overt behavior**, not addressing unconscious anxieties or repressed traumas.

### Punishment and Negative Reinforcement

- **Punishment**: the application of an aversive stimulus following a response in an effort to **decrease the likelihood** that the response will recur.
- Skinner said punishment was **ineffective** in changing behavior from undesirable to desirable.
- Quote: "What's wrong with punishments is that they work immediately, but give no long-term results. The responses to punishment are either the urge to escape, to counterattack, or a stubborn apathy. These are the bad effects you get in prisons or schools, or wherever punishments are used." (Skinner, quoted in Goleman, 1987)
- **Positive reinforcement** for desirable behaviors is **much more effective** than punishment.

- **Negative reinforcement**: the **strengthening of a response** by the **removal** of an aversive stimulus.
- **Negative reinforcement ≠ punishment** (critical distinction).
- A **negative reinforcer** = an aversive or noxious stimulus whose **removal** is rewarding.
- Example in lab: loud noise or electric shock continues until subject emits desired response — the noxious stimulus disappears when the correct response is made.
- Real-world example: a person stops smoking to avoid the aversive stimulus of a nagging spouse.
- Skinner opposed using noxious stimuli to modify behavior — consequences not as predictable as with positive reinforcement; positive reinforcement is **more consistently effective**.

## Questions about Human Nature

- **Nature vs. nurture**: people are primarily **products of learning**, shaped more by **external variables than genetic factors**.
- Childhood experiences are more important because **basic behaviors are formed in childhood**.
- However, behavior can **change in adulthood**: what is learned in childhood can be modified; new behavior patterns can be acquired at any age — verified by success of behavior modification programs.
- **Uniqueness**: because we are shaped by experience and we all have **different experiences**, no two people will behave in precisely the same way.
- **No reference to ultimate goal**: no overcoming inferiority, reducing anxiety, or striving for self-actualization (such motives assume internal subjective states, which Skinner did not accept).
- **Societal goal**: in *Walden Two* and other writings, he discussed the ideal human society; individual behavior must be directed toward the society with the greatest chance of survival.
- **Free will vs. determinism**: Skinner believed people function like **machines**, in lawful, orderly, predetermined ways.
- Rejected all suggestions of an inner being or autonomous self that determines action or chooses to act freely.
- Deterministic view: a dictator who orders mass killing can no more be held responsible than a driverless car plunging down a hill — both operate in lawful, predictable ways controlled by external variables.
- **Not pessimistic fully**: although behavior is controlled by external stimuli and reinforcers, we are responsible for **designing our environment**.
- Quote: "We may not be free agents, but we can do something about our lives, if we would only rearrange the controls that influence our behavior…. I am not trying to change people. All I want to do is change the world in which they live" (quoted in Bjork, 1993, pp. 16, 233).

## Assessment in Skinner's Theory

- Skinner did **not use** typical assessment techniques: no free association, dream analysis, or projective techniques.
- No interest in assessing **personality** per se — interested in assessing **behavior**.

### Functional Analysis
- **Functional analysis**: an approach to the study of behavior that involves assessing three aspects:
  1. The **frequency** of the behavior
  2. The **situation** in which the behavior occurs
  3. The **reinforcement** associated with the behavior
- Without these evaluations, it is not possible to plan and implement a behavior modification program.
- Example (smoking cessation): smokers keep accurate records of cigarettes smoked per day and situations in which they smoke (place, time, presence of others, after meals, while driving); identify reinforcers.

### Three Approaches to Assessing Behavior

#### 1. Direct Observation of Behavior
- **Direct observation**: observing behavior directly.
- Usually **two or more observers** to assure accuracy and reliability.

> **KEY STUDY: Direct Observation — Unruly Child**
> - **Researchers**: Hawkins, Peterson, Schweid, & Bijou (1966)
> - **Setting**: A woman sought treatment for her 4-year-old son whose behavior was unruly.
> - **Method**: Two psychologists observed mother and child **in their home** to evaluate nature and frequency of undesirable behaviors, when and where they occurred, and reinforcers the child received.
> - **Finding**: Nine undesirable behaviors identified (kicking, throwing things, biting, pushing a sibling). The mother was reinforcing the child by giving him **toys or food** when he behaved badly (intending to stop misbehavior but actually rewarding it).
> - **Duration**: Direct observation lasted **16 hours**.
> - **Intervention**: Psychologists instructed the mother to use **attention and approval** as reinforcers for positive behaviors and **never reward** him during undesirable behaviors.
> - **Significance**: Frequency of undesirable behaviors in direct observation = **baseline** against which to compare behavior during and after treatment.

#### 2. Self-Reports of Behavior
- **Self-report technique**: carried out through interviews and questionnaires.
- Person observes his or her own behavior and reports on it.
- Example: questionnaire assessing extent of fear in situations such as driving, going to the dentist, or speaking in public.
- Similar in format to self-report inventories that assess personality.

#### 3. Physiological Measurements of Behavior
- **Physiological assessments**: heart rate, muscle tension, brain waves.
- Used to evaluate physiological effects of various stimuli.
- Can confirm accuracy of information obtained by other methods.
- Example: person too embarrassed to reveal a fear of elevators in an interview might exhibit change in heart rate or muscle tension when asked about elevators.

- **Ultimate goal**: to modify behavior, not to change personality.

## Research on Skinner's Theory

- Research methods diverged from mainstream experimental psychology.
- Standard procedure: study **large groups** of subjects, **statistically compare** average responses.
- Skinner preferred: **intensive study of a single subject**.
- Rationale: data on average performance of groups is of little value in dealing with a particular case; averages do not reflect **individual response behavior and individual differences**.
- Skinner believed: valid and replicable results could be obtained **without statistical analysis** as long as sufficient data were collected from a single subject under well-controlled conditions.
- Topics studied: reinforcement schedules, language acquisition, behavior shaping, superstitious behavior, behavior modification.
- Results: **highly supportive** of Skinner's ideas.

## Reflections on Skinner's Theory

### Criticisms
1. **Determinism**: opponents of determinism find much to dislike.
2. **Humanistic objections**: humanistic psychologists argue that exclusive emphasis on overt behavior ignores uniquely human qualities such as **conscious free will**; people are more complex than machines, rats, or pigeons.
3. **Type of subject**: critics question whether one can extrapolate from a pigeon pecking a disc to a person functioning in the real world — the gap is too vast.
4. **Simplicity of situations**: Skinner made broad assertions about social, economic, religious, and cultural issues based on simple lab experiments.
5. **All behavior is learned**: challenged by the phenomenon of **instinctive drift**.

### Instinctive Drift

> **KEY STUDY: Breland & Breland (1961) — "The Misbehavior of Organisms"**
> - **Researchers**: Keller Breland & Marian Breland — two of Skinner's former students who conditioned **more than 6,000 animals** of **38 different species** to perform for television commercials and tourist attractions.
> - **Animals included**: pigs, raccoons, chickens, hamsters, porpoises, whales, cows.
> - **Key observation**: animals displayed a tendency toward **instinctive drift** — substituting **instinctive behaviors for the behaviors that had been reinforced**, even when instinctive behaviors interfered with receiving food.
> - **Specific example (Piggy Bank task)**:
>   - Pigs and raccoons conditioned to pick up a coin, carry it some distance, and deposit it in a toy bank; reinforced with food upon depositing a certain number of coins.
>   - Learned behaviors quickly, but then began engaging in undesirable behaviors.
>   - **Pigs**: would stop on their way to the bank, bury the coin in the sand, and take it out with their snout.
>   - **Raccoons**: spent a lot of time handling the coin with their well-known washing-like movements.
>   - Instinctive behavior took precedence over learned behavior — even at the cost of delayed reinforcement.
>   - "Commercially, it was a disaster."
> - **Term**: **Instinctive drift** — the substitution of instinctive behaviors for behaviors that had been reinforced.
> - **Title significance**: "The Misbehavior of Organisms" was a parody of Skinner's groundbreaking book **"The Behavior of Organisms" (1938)** — reportedly left Skinner displeased.

- **Instinctive drift**: the substitution of instinctive behaviors for behaviors that had been reinforced.

### Response to Criticism
- Skinner largely **ignored** criticisms.
- Quote about a critic's book review: "I read a bit of it and saw that he missed the point…. There are better things to do with my time than clear up their misunderstandings."
- When asked how he dealt with being misunderstood: "I find that I need to be understood only three or four times a year."

### Current Status
- Skinner was a **potent force in 20th-century American psychology**; shaped the field more than almost any other individual.
- **Journal of the Experimental Analysis of Behavior** (begun **1958**): publishes research on the behavior of individual subjects.
- **Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis** (established **1968**): outlet for work on behavior-modification techniques.
- Awards:
  - **American Psychological Foundation Gold Medal**
  - **American Psychological Association Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award (1958)**: citation reads "Few American psychologists have had so profound an impact on the development of psychology and on promising younger psychologists."
  - **U.S. National Medal of Science**
- Appeared on the cover of **Time** magazine; headlined as world's most famous American psychologist.
- **The Behavior of Organisms: An Experimental Analysis (1938)**: described as one of the few books to truly change the nature of the field (Thompson, 1988).
- **Beyond Freedom and Dignity (1971)**: controversial book; became a bestseller.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Respondent behavior** | Responses made to or elicited by specific environmental stimuli; unlearned, automatic, involuntary |
| **Conditioning** | Learning that involves the substitution of one stimulus for another; originated with Pavlov |
| **Reinforcement** | The act of strengthening a response by adding a reward, increasing the likelihood that the response will be repeated |
| **Extinction** | The process of eliminating a behavior by withholding reinforcement |
| **Operant behavior** | Behavior emitted spontaneously or voluntarily that operates on the environment to change it |
| **Operant conditioning** | The procedure by which a change in the consequences of a response will affect the rate at which the response occurs |
| **Skinner box** | Operant-conditioning apparatus; contains lever/bar, food trough; used to study operant conditioning |
| **Reinforcement schedules** | Patterns or rates of providing or withholding reinforcers |
| **Fixed-interval schedule** | Reinforcer presented following the first response after a fixed time interval elapses |
| **Fixed-ratio schedule** | Reinforcers given only after a specified number of responses have been made |
| **Variable-interval schedule** | Reinforcer appears after varying amounts of time |
| **Variable-ratio schedule** | Reinforcer based on an average number of responses, with great variability around that average |
| **Successive approximation** | Method (shaping) by which complex behavior is acquired through reinforcing behavior that comes in successive stages to approximate the final desired behavior |
| **Shaping** | Another term for successive approximation; reinforcing progressively closer approximations to desired behavior |
| **Superstitious behavior** | Persistent behavior that has a coincidental and not a functional relationship to the reinforcement received |
| **Self-control** | The ability to exert control over the variables that determine our behavior |
| **Stimulus avoidance** | Self-control technique of removing oneself from a stimulus that is affecting one's behavior |
| **Self-administered satiation** | Self-control technique of overdoing a behavior until one becomes disgusted and stops |
| **Aversive stimulation** | Self-control technique of declaring intentions publicly so that failure results in embarrassment or criticism |
| **Self-reinforcement** | Self-control technique of rewarding oneself for displaying desirable behaviors |
| **Behavior modification** | A form of therapy that applies principles of reinforcement to bring about desired behavioral changes |
| **Token economy** | Behavior-modification technique in which tokens exchangeable for valued objects or privileges are awarded for desirable behaviors |
| **Punishment** | Application of an aversive stimulus following a response to decrease the likelihood that the response will recur |
| **Negative reinforcement** | Strengthening a response by the removal of an aversive stimulus (not the same as punishment) |
| **Functional analysis** | Approach to studying behavior involving assessment of frequency of behavior, situation in which it occurs, and reinforcement associated with it |
| **Instinctive drift** | The substitution of instinctive behaviors for behaviors that had been reinforced |
| **Empty organism** | Skinner's term for his view that there is nothing inside humans that can explain behavior in scientific terms |
| **Continuous reinforcement** | Presenting a reinforcer after every response |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Skinner did NOT offer a personality theory** — he aimed to account for all behavior; personality = a collection of operant behaviors shaped by external reinforcers.
2. **"Empty organism"** = Skinner's term indicating nothing inside humans can explain behavior scientifically; internal states (traits, unconscious drives) have no place in scientific psychology.
3. **Respondent vs. Operant behavior**: respondent behavior is elicited by a specific stimulus (reflexive, involuntary); operant behavior is emitted spontaneously/voluntarily and operates on the environment.
4. **Reinforcement schedules in order of resistance to extinction**: Variable Ratio (highest) > Variable Interval > Fixed Ratio > Fixed Interval (lowest when previously continuously reinforced).
5. **Variable-ratio schedule produces the highest and most stable response rate** — this is the principle behind gambling (slot machines, roulette).
6. **Negative reinforcement ≠ punishment**: negative reinforcement = removing an aversive stimulus to strengthen a response; punishment = applying an aversive stimulus to decrease a response.
7. **Successive approximation (shaping)**: reinforcing progressively closer approximations to the final desired behavior; Skinner trained a pigeon in less than 3 minutes to peck a specific spot.
8. **Superstitious behavior**: behavior has coincidental, not functional, relationship to reinforcement; demonstrated with pigeons receiving food on fixed 15-second intervals; 74% of MLB players admit superstitious behaviors (Burger & Lynn, 2005).
9. **Token economy (Ayllon & Azrin, 1968)**: pioneering study with female psychotic patients; tokens exchanged for goods/privileges; private meeting with a social worker = 100 tokens; private meeting with a psychologist = only 20 tokens.
10. **Instinctive drift (Breland & Breland, 1961)**: instinctive behaviors substitute for reinforced behaviors even at the cost of delayed reinforcement; pigs rooting and raccoons washing coins instead of depositing them; "The Misbehavior of Organisms" was a direct parody of Skinner's "The Behavior of Organisms" (1938).
11. **Functional analysis** involves assessing three things: frequency of behavior, situation in which it occurs, reinforcement associated with it.
12. **Skinner's four self-control techniques**: stimulus avoidance, self-administered satiation, aversive stimulation, self-reinforcement.
13. **Skinner used single-subject intensive study** (not group statistics) — argued data on group averages cannot reflect individual response behavior.
14. **Skinner's personality description** (Gronerod et al., 2013): high conscientiousness and openness, somewhat extraverted, neurotic, quirky, driven; strained social relationships.
15. **Key publications**: *The Behavior of Organisms* (1938, first major book), *Walden Two* (1948, novel about society run by positive reinforcement, 2+ million copies sold), *Beyond Freedom and Dignity* (1971, controversial bestseller); APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award (1958).
