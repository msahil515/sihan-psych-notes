# Ch 15: Personality in Perspective

This final chapter synthesizes the major themes running across nearly two dozen personality theories covered in the book. Rather than declaring one theory correct, it identifies seven cross-cutting factors — genetic, environmental, learning, parental, developmental, consciousness, and unconscious — that together explain what shapes personality, noting where theorists converge and diverge on each.

---

## The Genetic Factor

### Core Claim
There is increasingly strong evidence that many personality traits or dimensions are **inherited**. Genetics supplies predispositions, not destinies; tendencies, not certainties.

### Inherited Traits Identified by Major Theorists

| Theorist(s) | Inherited Dimensions / Traits |
|---|---|
| **Eysenck** | Psychoticism, Neuroticism, Extraversion (extraversion derived from Jung) |
| **McCrae & Costa** | Big Five: Neuroticism, Extraversion, Openness to Experience, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness |
| **Buss & Plomin** | Three temperaments: Emotionality, Activity, Sociability |
| **Zuckerman** | Sensation seeking (primarily genetic) |

### Open Question
How many inherited factors exist? Candidates:
- Cattell's **16** personality factors
- Eysenck's **3** dimensions
- McCrae & Costa's **5** (Big Five)
- Buss & Plomin's **3** temperaments
- Some as-yet-undiscovered number

### Key Studies: Genetic Basis

> **Yamagata et al. (2006)** — Twin study (Canada, Germany, Japan): confirmed genetic basis of the five-factor model; suggested it may "represent the common heritage of the human species."

> **Shiner (2014)** — Big Five traits increasingly stable over 20 years from childhood into adulthood.

> **DeFruyt et al. (2006); Kandler et al. (2010)** — Germany and Belgium twin/non-twin siblings: high stability in traits during childhood, adolescence, and into young adulthood.

> **Allik, Realo & McCrae (2013); Heine & Buchtel (2009); Ispas et al. (2014)** — Large-scale research across 50+ cultures (Israel, Korea, Turkey, etc.): Big Five displayed consistently in majority of nations; less consistently in developing countries (Ethiopia, Lebanon, Malaysia, Uganda).

> **Larsson, Andershed & Lichtenstein (2006)** — Swedish adolescent twins: strong genetic component in psychopathic personality prone to violent antisocial behavior.

> **Garcia et al. (2013)** — Heritability of anxiety trait increased between ages 14 and 18.

### Everyday Behaviors with Genetic Influence
Studies from US, Finland, French-speaking Canada, Sweden found genetic influences on:
- Sexual attitudes
- Eating behaviors in 2-year-olds
- Depression in bullying victims
- Attitudes toward practicing musical instruments
- Gun ownership (identical twins more likely to own guns than fraternal twins) — Barnes, Boutwell & Beaver (2014); Butkovic, Ullen & Mosing (2015); Dubois et al. (2013); Iyer, Dougall & Jensen-Campbell (2013); Westerlund et al. (2012)

### Key Principle
> "What we inherit are **predispositions**, not destinies; **tendencies**, not certainties."

## The Environmental Factor

### Core Claim
Genetic effects are most important in infancy and early years; **environmental effects on personality become increasingly important as we advance from childhood to adulthood** and continue throughout life (Briley & Tucker-Drob, 2014; Kandler, 2012).

### Theorists' Environmental Emphasis

- **Adler**: **Birth order** — position in family relative to siblings exposes children to different parental/social challenges, producing different personalities.
- **Horney**: Culture shapes neurosis patterns (German vs. American patients differed); different social environments for boys vs. girls; **female inferiority** develops from treatment in male-dominated cultures; hypothesized that women in a matriarchal culture would have higher self-esteem.
- **Allport**: Genetics supplies raw material; **social environment shapes the finished product**.
- **Cattell**: Heredity more important for some of his 16 factors than others, but environmental influences affect every factor to some extent.
- **Erikson**: Eight stages are innate but environment determines how they are realized; social and historical forces shape **ego identity**.
- **Maslow & Rogers**: Self-actualization is innate, but environmental factors can inhibit or promote it.

### Types of Environmental Influence

#### Large-Scale Societal Events
Wars, economic recessions restrict life choices and influence self-identity formation.

> **Twenge (2000)** — Compared 50,000+ college students/young people: 1950s cohort vs. 1980s cohort. The 1980s group showed significantly higher anxiety and neuroticism, attributed to decreased social connectedness (higher divorce rate, lower birth rate, later age at first marriage, more people living alone).

#### Parenthood
> **Jokela et al. (2009)** — Finland: Becoming a parent increases stress, anxiety, and emotionality; lowers marital and life satisfaction. More children = greater increase in emotionality.

> **Wendorf et al. (2011)** — US, England, Turkey: Parenthood lowers marital satisfaction; more children = lower marital satisfaction.

#### Occupation / Job Status
> **Roberts, Caspi & Moffitt (2003)** — New Zealand, ages 18-26: Those with satisfying, high-status jobs at 26 showed increased positive emotionality (well-being, social closeness, achievement) and decreased negative emotionality (aggressiveness, alienation, stress) compared to age 18. Conclusion: workplace can affect innate personality traits.

#### Stressful Life Events
> **Sutin, Costa, Wethington & Eaton (2010)** — US adults studied in mid-40s and 10 years later: High-neuroticism subjects saw stressful events as turning points; high-extraversion subjects saw them as learning opportunities. Those who saw events as negative turning points became more neurotic; those who saw them as learning opportunities became even more extraverted.

#### Ethnic/Cultural Background
- Members of minority groups develop both **ethnic identity** and **ego identity**.
- **Usborne & Taylor (2010)** — Canada: Among minority-group members, those with strongest ethnic identities were highest in self-esteem and subjective well-being.
- Western cultures: more individualistic; higher extraversion, sensation-seeking, subjective well-being.
- Eastern cultures: lower on these dimensions.

#### Geographic Region
> **Rentfrow (2010)** — US regional differences: Neuroticism higher in northeastern and southeastern states; agreeableness higher in southern states; extraversion higher in northeast, lower in west.

#### Neighborhood
> **Hart, Atkins & Matsuba (2008)** — Economically disadvantaged neighborhoods: children more likely to show maladaptive tendencies (low resiliency, problem behaviors).

> **Jokela et al. (2015)** — London: Life satisfaction, agreeableness, conscientiousness, and openness varied by neighborhood.

## The Learning Factor

### Core Claim
Learning plays a major role in influencing virtually every aspect of behavior, including personality. All social and environmental forces that shape personality do so through **learning** (Bleidorn et al., 2010).

### Key Contributors

- **Skinner**: Demonstrated **positive reinforcement**, **successive approximation**, **superstitious behavior**, and other learning variables in shaping behavior. Personality = accumulation of learned responses.
- **Bandura**: Introduced **observational learning** (learning by watching models) and **vicarious reinforcement**. Agreed with Skinner that most behaviors are learned; genetics plays only a limited role.

### Learned Personality Constructs

| Construct | Originator | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-efficacy** | Bandura | Feeling of adequacy and competence in coping with life |
| **Locus of control** | Rotter | Internal = reinforcement controlled by own behavior; External = by others/fate/luck |
| **Learned helplessness** | Seligman | Perception of no control over environment |
| **Optimism vs. pessimism** | Seligman | Related to explanatory style; optimistic style prevents learned helplessness |

### The "Control" Cluster
These concepts form a unified theme around **level of control**:
- High self-efficacy + internal locus of control + absence of learned helplessness = **optimism**
- **Benefits of high control**: better coping, fewer stress effects, greater mental and physical health, perseverance, higher aspirations and self-esteem, lower anxiety, higher grades, greater social skills and popularity.
- Control is learned in infancy and childhood; parental behavior is paramount in fostering it.

## The Parental Factor

### Core Claim
**Freud** was the first to emphasize parental influence on personality formation; virtually every theorist thereafter echoed his views to some degree.

### Theorist-by-Theorist Parental Emphasis

| Theorist | Parental Emphasis |
|---|---|
| **Freud** | Origin of the emphasis; early parent-child experiences form the foundation |
| **Adler** | Parental rejection leads to insecurity, anger, deficient self-esteem |
| **Horney** | Lack of parental warmth/affection undermines security, causes helplessness |
| **Allport** | Infant's relationship with mother is primary source of affection and security |
| **Cattell** | Infancy is the major formative period; parents and siblings shape character |
| **Erikson** | Mother relationship in Year 1 vital for promoting trust |
| **Maslow** | Parents must satisfy physiological and safety needs in first two years; prerequisite for higher needs |
| **Rogers** | Parents must supply **unconditional positive regard** |

### Parenting Styles and Outcomes

**Authoritative parenting** (warm but firm):
- Children are more competent and mature than those of permissive, harsh, or indifferent parents.
- Quoted finding: "Combination of parental responsiveness and demandingness is consistently related to adolescent adjustment, school performance, and psychosocial maturity" (Steinberg & Morris, 2001).
- **Ang (2006)** — Singapore adolescents with authoritative parents had greater confidence and better social adjustment than those with **authoritarian** parents (strict, harsh, demanding obedience).

**Parental Big Five and Behavior:**
> **Prinzie et al. (2009)** — Parents higher in extraversion, agreeableness, conscientiousness, and openness behaved in warmer, more consistent ways. Parents higher in agreeableness and lower in neuroticism were more supportive of children's independence.

**Cultural Differences in Parenting Style:**
- Arab cultures tend to be more **authoritarian** than authoritative.
- **Rudy & Grusec (2006)** — Mothers who immigrated to Canada from collectivist cultures (Egypt, Iran, India, Pakistan) were more authoritarian than those from individualistic Western European countries.

### Specific Effects of Parental Behaviors

**Negative parenting outcomes:**
> **Brewin, Andrews & Gotlib (1993)** — Childhoods of depressed and anxious adults: parents were more rejecting, abusive, and less caring than parents of less troubled adults.

> **Kochanska, Clark & Goldman (1997)** — Mothers with negative emotions and disagreeableness had children with higher defiance, anger, and disobedience.

> **Raikkonen et al. (2000)** — Finland, 12-year study: mothers with hostile child-rearing attitudes at child ages 3 and 6 produced children who were highly hostile by age 15. Hostile mothers raised hostile children.

> **Kasser, Koestner & Lekes (2002)** — US longitudinal (ages 5 to 31): restrictive, cold, strict parenting at age 5 produced adults high in conformity and low in self-direction.

**Positive parenting outcomes:**
- Praise promotes autonomy, realistic standards, competence, self-efficacy, and intrinsic motivation.
- **Fortuna & Knafo (2014)** — Parents play major role in teaching children kindness, caring, and helping others.

### What Determines How Parents Behave?
> **Klahr & Burt (2014)** — Study of 20,000+ families (Australia to Japan to US): genetic factors accounted for up to **40%** of parental behavior; parental behavior is also influenced by how the child behaves (bidirectional influence).

### Surrogate Caregivers
> **Harvey (1999)** — National longitudinal survey, children ages 3-12: No significant behavioral or self-esteem problems when mothers worked outside the home; care by non-mothers had no negative impact.

**Kibbutz studies (Israel):**
> **Aviezer, Van Ijzendoorn, Sagi & Schuegel (1994)** — Kibbutz children function and adapt well if they bonded securely with parents in infancy (strongest predictor of dominance, independence, achievement orientation). BUT: barracks-style sleeping in early childhood led to more anxious, restrained, emotionally flat personalities. Adults who failed to bond showed introversion, diminished capacity for friendship, reduced emotional intensity.

### The Peer vs. Parent Controversy
A major controversy (late 1990s): parental behaviors have **no long-term effects** on personality outside the home; **peers** influence personality more.
- Children adopt behaviors/attitudes/values of classmates and friends to win acceptance.
- Quote (Harris): "When children go out, they leave behind the behavior they acquired at home. They cast it off like the dorky sweater their mother made them wear."
- **Loehlin (1997)** — Twins in late adolescence: twins with more friends in common were more alike in personality, suggesting peer influence > home environment.

## The Developmental Factor

### Core Claim
Freud believed personality was fixed by **age 5**. Research shows personality continues to develop **well beyond childhood, perhaps throughout the entire life span**.

### Theorist Positions on Developmental Timing

| Theorist | Position |
|---|---|
| **Freud** | Fixed by age 5; difficult to change after |
| **Cattell, Allport, Erikson** | Childhood important but personality can be modified in later years |
| Some theorists | Ongoing development through adolescence |
| **Jung, Maslow, Erikson, Cattell** | Middle age is a time of major personality change |

### Stability vs. Change Findings

**Basic foundation stable from age 30:**
- McCrae & Costa's Big Five traits and other personality aspects are enduring from age 30 on (Nave et al., 2010; Terracciano, McCrae & Costa, 2010).

**Age-related directional changes (cross-cultural consistency: US, Germany, Italy, Portugal, Croatia, Netherlands, Australia, South Korea):**
- **Declining with age** (college age to middle age): Neuroticism, Extraversion, Openness
- **Increasing with age**: Agreeableness, Conscientiousness
- (Allemand et al., 2008; McCrae & Costa, 1997; Lucas & Donnellan, 2009; McCrae et al., 1999)

**Key longitudinal studies:**

> **Helson, Jones & Kwan (2002)** — 40-year study, several hundred people: Dominance and independence peaked in middle age; personality did not stop evolving after age 20.

> **Roberts & Delvecchio (2000)** — Meta-analysis of 152 longitudinal studies, 55,000+ people: High consistency of personality traits at all ages; highest consistency in adulthood and after age 50.

> **Pullmann, Raudsepp & Allik (2006)** — Estonian teenagers ages 12-18: Big Five remained stable over 2-year study.

> **Vaidya, Gray, Haig & Watson (2002)** — American college students over 30 months: Became more open, agreeable, and conscientious.

> **Ferguson (2010)** — Both normal and abnormal personality factors peak in stability around age 30, remain fairly stable for remainder of life span.

> **Allemand, Zimprich & Martin (2008)** — Switzerland: People in their 60s become less neurotic, less extraverted, less open to new experiences.

> **Berg & Johansson (2014); Kandler et al. (2014)** — Twins ages 64-98: Decreases in extraversion, conscientiousness, and perceived control with advancing age; some increase in neuroticism; no significant change in well-being.

> **Srivastava, John, Gosling & Potter (2003)** — 32,000+ people ages 21-60 (Internet study): Conscientiousness and agreeableness increased through early and middle adulthood. Conscientiousness increased most in 20s; agreeableness increased most in 30s.

> **Roberts, Walton & Viechtbauer (2006)** — People become more dominant in social situations and more conscientious and emotionally stable from young adulthood to middle age.

> **Roberts, Caspi & Moffitt (2001)** — New Zealand, ages 18-26: Personality changes showed increasing psychological maturity: more self-controlled, confident socially, less angry and alienated. Women showed higher psychological maturity overall than men.

> **Shiner, Masten & Tellegen (2002)** — Children ages 8-12 surveyed again 10 years later: Personality changes between ages 18-22 could be significantly predicted by childhood personality characteristics.

### What Causes Personality Change in Adulthood?
Social and environmental influences and adaptations to them:
- Economic changes, leaving college, marriage, parenthood, divorce, job loss/advancement, midlife crises, aging parents (McAdams & Olson, 2010)

> **Van Aken et al. (2006)** — Netherlands, 3-year study, men and women in 40s: Those who adapted to expected social roles (career success, family life) scored higher on Big Five. Personality change associated with successful adaptation to midlife concerns.

> **Costa, Herbst, McCrae & Siegler (2000)** — Job loss: significant increases in neuroticism, decreases in conscientiousness and extraversion.

> **Neyer & Asendorpf (2001)** — Active daters/social relationships: lower neuroticism, higher extraversion, conscientiousness, and self-esteem.

### McAdams' Three-Level Model of Personality Development
**(McAdams, 1994)** — Personality continues to develop on three levels:

1. **Dispositional traits**: Inherited traits (like McCrae & Costa's Big Five); remain stable and relatively unchanging from age 30 on.
2. **Personal concerns**: Conscious feelings, plans, and goals; what we want, how we achieve it, feelings about people in our lives. Change often over the life span due to diverse situations.
3. **Life narrative**: Shaping the self, attaining identity, finding unified purpose. Like personal concerns, changes in response to social/environmental situations.

**Summary**: Underlying dispositional traits remain largely constant; conscious judgments about who we are and who we want to be are subject to change.

## The Consciousness Factor

### Core Claim
Almost every personality theory deals explicitly or implicitly with **conscious (cognitive) processes**. Even unconscious-focused theorists (Freud, Jung) acknowledged the conscious ego.

### Theorist Positions on Consciousness

| Theorist | Conscious Role |
|---|---|
| **Freud** | Ego perceives, thinks, feels, remembers; enables interaction with real world |
| **Jung** | Ego = conscious aspect of personality; rational functioning involves conscious judgments and evaluations |
| **Adler** | Humans are conscious, rational beings capable of planning and directing their lives; formulate hopes, plans, dreams; delay gratification; anticipate future events |
| **Allport** | Non-neurotic people function consciously and rationally; aware of and in control of motivating forces |
| **Rogers** | People primarily rational, governed by conscious perception of self and experiential world |
| **Maslow** | Cognitive needs to know and to understand |
| **Kelly** | Most complete cognitive theory: form **constructs** about environment/people; make **predictions (anticipations)**; formulate and test hypotheses about social world |
| **Bandura** | People learn through vicarious reinforcement by anticipating consequences of others' actions; visualize/imagine reinforcement results without personally experiencing them |

### Kelly's Personal Construct Theory (Key Cognitive Theory)
- **Constructs**: Bipolar intellectual hypotheses used to interpret life events (e.g., tall vs. short; honest vs. dishonest)
- **Constructive alternativism**: Free to revise or replace constructs with alternatives as needed
- **Predictions**: Based on constructs, we anticipate how others will behave and adapt accordingly

## The Unconscious Factor

### Core Claim
Freud introduced the unconscious; modern research confirms it is powerful, perhaps more pervasive than Freud suggested. But the contemporary view differs significantly from Freud's.

### Freud's Unconscious
- Murky repository of darkest fears, conflicts, and forces affecting conscious thoughts and behaviors
- Operates via **repression** and other **defense mechanisms**
- Psychologists found evidence that thoughts/memories are repressed unconsciously

### Modern/Contemporary View: The "Non-Conscious"
- **Rational unconscious** vs. Freud's "dark cauldron of repressed wishes and desires"
- Contemporary researchers describe unconscious cognitive processes as more **rational** than emotional
- Term preferred: **non-conscious** (to distinguish from Freud's emotional unconscious)
- **Subliminal activation**: stimuli presented below conscious awareness still activate conscious processes and behaviors

### Key Study: "Mommy and I Are One"
> **Silverman & Weinberger (1985)** — Subliminal presentation of specific stimuli influenced both cognitive and emotional responses. Stimuli had therapeutic value even though subjects had no conscious awareness of the messages.
- Conclusion: The unconscious may have both a **rational** and an **emotional** component.

### Post-Freudian Neglect
- Many theorists who followed Freud ignored the unconscious.
- The **emotional unconscious** as Freud conceived it remains the "least understood factor" — still mysterious and inaccessible.

## Final Comment

- The field has moved from Freud's emphasis on anxiety, unconscious, and repression to **positive psychology** and characteristics of the happy personality.
- Most aspects of personality remain mysterious.
- Each theory has contributed part of the answer.
- Basic traits are enduring; personality is shaped by the interaction of all seven factors.
- The question "What is personality?" remains the most important question in psychology.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Eysenck's three inherited dimensions** are psychoticism, neuroticism, and extraversion (extraversion derived from Jung).

2. **McCrae & Costa's Big Five** — Neuroticism, Extraversion, Openness, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness — confirmed genetically across Canada, Germany, and Japan (Yamagata et al., 2006); stable from age 30.

3. **Buss & Plomin's three temperaments** are emotionality, activity, and sociability — all primarily inherited.

4. **Genetics accounts for approximately 40% of parental behavior** (Klahr & Burt, 2014); remaining parental behavior is also influenced by how the child behaves.

5. **Twenge (2000)** birth cohort study: 1980s students had significantly higher anxiety and neuroticism than 1950s students, attributed to decreased social connectedness.

6. **Freud** argued personality is fixed by age 5; contrast with **Jung, Maslow, Erikson, and Cattell** who viewed middle age as a time of major personality change.

7. **McAdams' three levels**: (a) dispositional traits — stable from 30+; (b) personal concerns — frequently changing conscious goals; (c) life narrative — identity formation, changes with life stages.

8. **Learned constructs** forming a "control cluster": self-efficacy (Bandura), internal locus of control (Rotter), absence of learned helplessness (Seligman), and optimism (Seligman) are all related and beneficial to health, grades, social skills, and coping.

9. **Authoritative parenting** (warm but firm) produces more competent, mature adolescents than authoritarian, permissive, or indifferent parenting; especially documented in Singapore (Ang, 2006).

10. **Silverman & Weinberger (1985)** — "Mommy and I are one" subliminal study: non-conscious has both rational and emotional components; stimuli below awareness still influence conscious processes.

11. **Non-conscious vs. unconscious**: Modern cognitive researchers prefer "non-conscious" for the rational unconscious, distinguished from Freud's emotional "dark cauldron." The emotional unconscious remains the least understood factor.

12. **Age-related trait changes**: Neuroticism, extraversion, and openness DECLINE from college age to middle age; agreeableness and conscientiousness INCREASE. Conscientiousness rises most strongly in 20s; agreeableness most strongly in 30s.

13. **Kibbutz research** (Aviezer et al., 1994): Secure parental bonding in infancy is the strongest predictor of dominance, independence, and achievement orientation in kibbutz-raised children; barracks-style early sleeping leads to anxious, restrained, emotionally flat adults.

14. **Big Five across 50+ cultures** (Allik et al., 2013; Heine & Buchtel, 2009): Consistent in majority of nations; less so in developing countries (Ethiopia, Lebanon, Malaysia, Uganda).

15. **The peer controversy** (Harris): Peers may influence personality more than parents outside the home; supported by Loehlin (1997) — twins with more friends in common were more alike in personality than twins with fewer common friends.
