# Ch 15: Personality in Perspective

The final chapter synthesizes themes across all theories. Rather than declaring one correct, it identifies **seven cross-cutting factors** — genetic, environmental, learning, parental, developmental, consciousness, unconscious — that shape personality, noting where theorists converge and diverge.

---

## The Genetic Factor

Many traits are **inherited** — genetics supplies predispositions, not destinies.

| Theorist(s) | Inherited Dimensions |
|---|---|
| **Eysenck** | Psychoticism, Neuroticism, Extraversion |
| **McCrae & Costa** | Big Five (N, E, O, A, C) |
| **Buss & Plomin** | Emotionality, Activity, Sociability |
| **Zuckerman** | Sensation seeking |

- **Open question**: how many factors? Cattell's 16, Eysenck's 3, Big Five's 5, Buss & Plomin's 3, or some undiscovered number.

> **Yamagata et al. (2006)** — twin study (Canada, Germany, Japan): confirmed the genetic basis of the Big Five; may "represent the common heritage of the human species."
> **DeFruyt et al. (2006); Kandler et al. (2010)** — high trait stability from childhood into young adulthood.
> **Allik, Realo & McCrae (2013)** — Big Five consistent across 50+ cultures, less so in developing countries (Ethiopia, Lebanon, Malaysia, Uganda).
> **Larsson et al. (2006)** — strong genetic component in psychopathic personality (Swedish twins).

- Genetic influences found on sexual attitudes, eating, even **gun ownership** (identical > fraternal twins).

> **Key principle**: "What we inherit are **predispositions**, not destinies; **tendencies**, not certainties."

---

## The Environmental Factor

- Genetic effects dominate in infancy; **environmental effects grow from childhood through adulthood** (Briley & Tucker-Drob, 2014).
- **Adler**: birth order. **Horney**: culture shapes neurosis; female inferiority from male-dominated cultures. **Allport**: environment shapes the finished product. **Cattell**: environment affects every factor. **Erikson**: social/historical forces shape ego identity. **Maslow & Rogers**: environment can inhibit/promote self-actualization.

**Influences:**
- **Societal events** — **Twenge (2000)**: 1980s students higher in anxiety/neuroticism than 1950s (decreased social connectedness).
- **Parenthood** — increases stress/emotionality, lowers marital satisfaction (Jokela et al., 2009; Wendorf et al., 2011).
- **Occupation** — **Roberts, Caspi & Moffitt (2003)**: satisfying high-status jobs at 26 increased positive emotionality — workplace affects innate traits.
- **Stressful events** — **Sutin et al. (2010)**: high-neuroticism people saw events as negative turning points (became more neurotic); high-extraversion people saw learning opportunities (became more extraverted).
- **Ethnic/cultural** — strongest ethnic identity ↔ highest self-esteem/well-being in minorities (Usborne & Taylor, 2010).
- **Region/neighborhood** — US regional trait differences (Rentfrow, 2010); disadvantaged neighborhoods → more maladaptive tendencies (Hart et al., 2008).

---

## The Learning Factor

- All social/environmental forces shape personality **through learning**.
- **Skinner**: positive reinforcement, successive approximation, superstitious behavior — personality = accumulated learned responses. **Bandura**: observational learning, vicarious reinforcement.

| Construct | Originator |
|---|---|
| **Self-efficacy** | Bandura |
| **Locus of control** | Rotter |
| **Learned helplessness** | Seligman |
| **Optimism vs. pessimism** | Seligman |

- **"Control" cluster**: high self-efficacy + internal LOC + no learned helplessness = **optimism** → better coping, health, perseverance, higher self-esteem/grades, lower anxiety. Control is learned in childhood; parental behavior is paramount.

---

## The Parental Factor

**Freud** first emphasized parental influence; nearly all later theorists agreed.

| Theorist | Parental Emphasis |
|---|---|
| **Freud** | Early parent-child experiences form the foundation |
| **Adler** | Rejection → insecurity, anger, deficient self-esteem |
| **Horney** | Lack of warmth undermines security |
| **Allport** | Mother is the primary source of affection/security |
| **Cattell** | Infancy is the major formative period |
| **Erikson** | Year-1 mother relationship vital for trust |
| **Maslow** | Parents must meet needs in the first two years |
| **Rogers** | Parents must supply **unconditional positive regard** |

- **Authoritative parenting** (warm but firm) → more competent, mature children (Steinberg & Morris, 2001; Ang, 2006 [Singapore]).
- **Prinzie et al. (2009)**: parents higher in E/A/C/O are warmer and more consistent.
- **Cultural differences**: collectivist-culture immigrant mothers more **authoritarian** (Rudy & Grusec, 2006).
- **Negative outcomes**: rejecting/abusive parents → depressed/anxious adults (Brewin et al., 1993); hostile mothers raise hostile children (Raikkonen et al., 2000); cold strict parenting at 5 → conforming, low-self-direction adults (Kasser et al., 2002).
- **Positive**: praise promotes autonomy, competence, self-efficacy; parents teach kindness (Fortuna & Knafo, 2014).
- **What shapes parents** — **Klahr & Burt (2014)** (20,000+ families): genetics accounts for up to **40%** of parental behavior; also bidirectional (child affects parent).
- **Surrogate caregivers**: maternal employment had no negative impact (Harvey, 1999). **Kibbutz (Aviezer et al., 1994)**: secure infant bonding predicts dominance/independence/achievement; barracks-style sleeping → anxious, emotionally flat adults.
- **Peer vs. parent controversy** (Harris): peers may influence personality more outside the home; twins with more shared friends were more alike (Loehlin, 1997).

## The Developmental Factor

- **Freud**: personality fixed by **age 5**. Research: it develops **throughout the life span**.

| Theorist | Position |
|---|---|
| **Freud** | Fixed by age 5 |
| **Cattell, Allport, Erikson** | Childhood important but later modification possible |
| **Jung, Maslow, Erikson, Cattell** | Middle age = major change |

- Big Five enduring from **age 30** (Nave et al., 2010). Cross-cultural directional change: **N, E, O decline** with age; **A and C increase** (conscientiousness rises most in 20s, agreeableness in 30s).

> **Helson, Jones & Kwan (2002)** — 40-year study: dominance/independence peaked in middle age.
> **Roberts & Delvecchio (2000)** — meta-analysis (152 studies, 55,000+): high consistency, highest in adulthood and after 50.
> **Roberts, Walton & Viechtbauer (2006)** — more dominant, conscientious, emotionally stable from young adulthood to midlife.
> **Roberts, Caspi & Moffitt (2001)** — increasing psychological maturity (NZ); women higher overall than men.
> **Allemand et al. (2008)** — 60s: less neurotic, extraverted, open.

- **Causes of adult change**: marriage, parenthood, divorce, job loss/advancement (McAdams & Olson, 2010); job loss → higher neuroticism, lower C/E (Costa et al., 2000); active social relationships → lower neuroticism, higher E/C/self-esteem (Neyer & Asendorpf, 2001).

### McAdams' Three-Level Model (1994)
1. **Dispositional traits** — inherited (Big Five); stable from age 30.
2. **Personal concerns** — conscious feelings, plans, goals; change over the life span.
3. **Life narrative** — shaping self, identity, purpose; changes with situations.
- Underlying traits stay constant; conscious self-judgments change.

## The Consciousness Factor

Nearly every theory addresses **conscious (cognitive) processes**; even Freud and Jung acknowledged the conscious ego.

| Theorist | Conscious Role |
|---|---|
| **Freud** | Ego perceives, thinks, remembers |
| **Jung** | Ego = conscious aspect; rational functions |
| **Adler** | Conscious, rational; plans, anticipates the future |
| **Allport** | Healthy people function consciously/rationally |
| **Rogers** | Governed by conscious perception of self |
| **Maslow** | Cognitive needs to know and understand |
| **Kelly** | Most complete cognitive theory: **constructs** and **predictions** |
| **Bandura** | Anticipating consequences of others' actions (vicarious) |

- **Kelly**: bipolar **constructs** interpret events; **constructive alternativism** allows revision; predictions anticipate others' behavior.

---

## The Unconscious Factor

- **Freud's unconscious**: a murky repository of fears/conflicts operating via **repression** and **defense mechanisms**.
- **Modern view ("non-conscious")**: more **rational** than emotional; term **non-conscious** distinguishes it from Freud's emotional unconscious; **subliminal activation** shows below-awareness stimuli still affect conscious processes.

> **Silverman & Weinberger (1985)** — "Mommy and I are one" subliminal study: stimuli below awareness influenced cognitive and emotional responses (therapeutic value). The unconscious may have **both rational and emotional** components.

- Many post-Freudian theorists ignored the unconscious; the **emotional unconscious** remains the "least understood factor."

---

## Final Comment

- The field moved from Freud's anxiety/unconscious/repression to **positive psychology** and the happy personality. Basic traits are enduring; personality is shaped by the interaction of all seven factors. "What is personality?" remains psychology's most important question.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Eysenck's three inherited dimensions**: psychoticism, neuroticism, extraversion.
2. **Big Five** confirmed genetically across Canada/Germany/Japan (Yamagata et al., 2006); stable from age 30.
3. **Buss & Plomin's three temperaments**: emotionality, activity, sociability.
4. **~40% of parental behavior is genetic** (Klahr & Burt, 2014); bidirectional with the child.
5. **Twenge (2000)**: 1980s students higher in anxiety/neuroticism than 1950s.
6. **Freud** (fixed by age 5) vs. **Jung/Maslow/Erikson/Cattell** (midlife major change).
7. **McAdams' three levels**: dispositional traits (stable from 30), personal concerns, life narrative.
8. **Control cluster**: self-efficacy + internal LOC + no learned helplessness = optimism; beneficial across health, grades, coping.
9. **Authoritative parenting** → competent, mature children (Ang, 2006, Singapore).
10. **Silverman & Weinberger (1985)**: non-conscious has both rational and emotional components.
11. **Non-conscious vs. unconscious**: modern researchers prefer "non-conscious" (rational); the emotional unconscious is least understood.
12. **Age-related change**: N, E, O **decline**; A and C **increase** (C most in 20s, A most in 30s).
13. **Kibbutz (Aviezer et al., 1994)**: secure infant bonding predicts dominance/independence; barracks sleeping → flat personalities.
14. **Big Five across 50+ cultures**: consistent in most, less so in developing countries.
15. **Peer controversy** (Harris; Loehlin, 1997): peers may matter more than parents outside the home.
