# Ch 4: Alfred Adler: Individual Psychology

Alfred Adler's **individual psychology** holds that humans are primarily social beings whose personalities are shaped by unique social environments rather than biological instincts. Unlike Freud, Adler placed consciousness (not the unconscious) at the core of personality, emphasized free will and future-oriented striving, and argued that inferiority feelings are the universal source of all human motivation. Each person creates a unique **style of life** in the effort to overcome inferiority and achieve perfection.

---

## The Life of Adler (1870–1937)

### Childhood and Adolescence

- Born 1870 in Vienna; died 1937 in Aberdeen, Scotland (heart attack during a 56-lecture European tour).
- Suffered from **rickets** (vitamin D deficiency, softening of bones) as a child, limiting physical activity and producing feelings of inferiority relative to healthier peers.
- At age 3: younger brother died in the bed next to him.
- At age 4: nearly died from pneumonia; overheard doctor say "Your boy is lost" and resolved to become a physician.
- **Pampered initially by his mother**, then dethroned at age 2 by a new sibling; afterward described as his father's favorite.
- Had no use for the Oedipus complex — his childhood relationship with parents was opposite to Freud's (closer to father, not mother).
- Always jealous of his **older brother** (named Sigmund), who was vigorous and athletic.
- Was initially a mediocre student; a teacher advised his father to apprentice him to a shoemaker; he persisted and rose to top of class through hard work — a personal demonstration of compensation.
- Developed sociability and fondness for peer company; his theory later reflected this by emphasizing peer group over parental influence.

### Adulthood

- Studied medicine at the **University of Vienna**; graduated with a mediocre record.
- Began as an **ophthalmologist**, shifted to general medicine, then specialized in **neurology and psychiatry** after becoming distressed by deaths in younger patients.

### Relations with Freud

- Adler's association with Freud: **1902–1911** (approximately 9 years).
- Freud invited Adler and three others to weekly meetings at his home to discuss psychoanalysis.
- Key distinction: Adler was **never a student or disciple** of Freud; was never psychoanalyzed by him.
- By 1910: Adler was **president of the Vienna Psychoanalytical Society** and co-editor of its journal, yet increasingly a vocal critic of Freudian theory.
- Severed all connection with psychoanalysis and developed his own approach (individual psychology).
- Freud reacted with intense hostility: called Adler "loathsome," "abnormal," "driven mad by ambition," and his theory "worthless."
- Adler called Freud a "swindler" and denounced psychoanalysis as "filth."
- Adler became irate when referred to as "a student of Freud's."

### Becoming a Celebrity in America

- **1912**: Founded the **Society for Individual Psychology**.
- Served in the Austrian army during World War I (1914–1918).
- After the war: organized government-sponsored **child-counseling clinics** in Vienna; introduced **group training and guidance procedures** (forerunners of modern group therapy).
- **1926**: First visit to the United States; extremely popular lecture tours.
- **1929**: Moved to New York City.
- Became "America's first popular psychologist" — a national celebrity.

## Inferiority Feelings: The Source of All Human Striving

- Core Adlerian premise: **"To be a human being means to feel oneself inferior"** (Adler, 1933/1939, p. 96).
- **Inferiority feelings** are the normal condition of all people; they are not a sign of weakness or abnormality.
- **Source of all human striving**: Individual growth results from **compensation** — our attempts to overcome real or imagined inferiorities.
- Process begins in **infancy**: infants are small, helpless, dependent on adults; they are aware of adult power and their own helplessness, producing inferiority feelings.
- Not genetically determined; a function of the universal environment of helplessness and dependence.
- Inferiority feelings are **inescapable and necessary** because they provide the motivation to strive and grow.

### The Inferiority Complex

- When a child cannot compensate for inferiority feelings, those feelings intensify into an **inferiority complex**.
- Persons with an inferiority complex have a poor self-opinion, feel helpless, and are unable to cope with life's demands.
- Adler found this complex in the childhoods of many adult patients.

### Causes of Inferiority Complexes

Three sources in childhood:

#### 1. Organic Inferiority
- Defective body parts or organs shape personality through the person's efforts to compensate.
- Classic examples:
  - **Demosthenes** (ancient Greek statesman): overcame a stutter to become a great orator.
  - **Theodore Roosevelt** (26th US President): sickly child who became a model of physical fitness.
  - Adler's own office was near an amusement park; patients included circus performers and gymnasts who had developed extraordinary skills by compensating for childhood disabilities.
- Failure to compensate for organic inferiority can lead to an inferiority complex.

#### 2. Spoiling (Pampering)
- Spoiled children are the center of home attention; every need is satisfied.
- They develop the expectation that others should always defer to them.
- First experience at school (where they are no longer the focus) comes as a shock.
- Spoiled children lack social feeling and patience; when confronted with obstacles, they attribute the problem to a personal deficiency — leading to an inferiority complex.

#### 3. Neglect
- Neglected, unwanted, or rejected children lack love and security.
- Parents are indifferent or hostile.
- These children develop feelings of worthlessness or anger and view others with distrust.
- Neglect is now recognized as a major form of child abuse.

### The Superiority Complex

- A person may attempt to **overcompensate** for inferiority and develop a **superiority complex**: an exaggerated opinion of one's abilities and accomplishments.
- Features: boasting, vanity, self-centeredness, tendency to denigrate others.
- Two sub-types:
  1. Feels inwardly superior with no need to demonstrate it through actual accomplishments.
  2. Works obsessively hard to become extremely successful.
- Distinct from the healthy concept of "striving for superiority" (see below).

## Striving for Superiority, or Perfection

- Adler's view of the ultimate motivating goal changed over time:
  - **Early view**: identified inferiority with femininity (reflecting women's inferior social standing); compensation was called the **masculine protest** — a drive toward power in which aggression (a supposedly masculine trait) played a large part.
  - **Later view**: rejected the femininity equation; broadened to the drive for **superiority or perfection**.
- **Striving for superiority** = the fundamental fact of life (Adler, 1930).
- NOT about being better than others, NOT about arrogance, NOT related to the superiority complex.
- Meaning: a drive for **perfection** (from Latin: to complete or finish) — to make oneself whole or complete.
- This innate goal is **future-oriented**, contrasting with Freud's past-determined model.
- Only the ultimate, final goal of superiority/perfection can explain personality and behavior.
- Key characteristics:
  - **Increases tension** rather than reducing it (contrast with Freud's tension-reduction model).
  - Manifested **both individually and collectively** (society also strives for perfection/culture).
  - Individuals and society are **interrelated and interdependent**.

### Fictional Finalism

- **Finalism**: the idea that we have an ultimate goal, a final state of being, and a need to move toward it.
- Goals we strive for are **potentialities, not actualities** — they are subjective ideals.
- **Fictional finalism**: fictional (imagined) ideals guide our behavior as we strive toward a complete or whole state of being.
  - Example ideals: "all people are created equal," belief in an afterlife, belief in God.
  - The concept of God is, to Adler, the best formulation humans have developed for the ideal of perfection.
- These beliefs influence how we perceive and interact with others.
- Adler's own fictional goal: to conquer the death he faced at age 4 from pneumonia; his vehicle was becoming a physician.
- Adler preferred the terms **"subjective final goal"** or **"guiding self-ideal"**, but "fictional finalism" has persisted (Watts & Holden, 1994).
- Term originally influenced by philosopher **Hans Vaihinger's** "philosophy of as-if" (implicit in Adler's thinking).

## The Style of Life

- Each person expresses the striving for superiority differently by developing a unique pattern of characteristics, behaviors, and habits — the **style of life** (also called **distinctive character**).
- Development: infants experience inferiority feelings, attempt to compensate, and in doing so acquire a set of behaviors that become the style of life.
- **Crystallized by age 4 or 5**; difficult to change thereafter.
- Determines which aspects of the environment we attend to or ignore and what attitudes we hold.
- Shaped by social interactions, especially **order of birth** and the **parent-child relationship**.

### The Creative Power of the Self

- Apparent contradiction: if style of life is determined by early social relationships and is fixed by age 4-5, how is Adler's theory less deterministic than Freud's?
- Resolution: the **creative power of the self** — the ability to create an appropriate style of life.
- We are **not passively shaped** by childhood experiences; our **conscious attitude** toward those experiences is more important than the experiences themselves.
- Neither heredity nor environment alone determines personality; we creatively construct our attitude toward life from both.
- **Individual free will** allows each person to create a style of life from their genetic endowment and social environment.
- Once created, the style of life **remains constant** throughout life.

### Four Basic Styles of Life

Adler grouped universal problems into three categories:
1. Problems involving behavior toward others
2. Problems of occupation
3. Problems of love

And proposed **four basic styles of life** for dealing with them:

| Style | Description | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Dominant type** | Ruling/dominant attitude with little social awareness; attacks others or attacks self (suicide, addiction) | Sadists, delinquents, sociopaths, alcoholics, drug addicts |
| **Getting type** | Expects satisfaction from others; becomes dependent | Most common human type per Adler |
| **Avoiding type** | Makes no attempt to face life's problems; avoids difficulties and possible failure | Abnormal behavior; neuroses and psychoses |
| **Socially useful type** | Cooperates with others; acts in accordance with their needs | Healthy; copes within a framework of social interest |

- The first three types lack **social interest**; their lifestyles clash with reality, resulting in neuroses and psychoses.
- The socially useful type is the healthy ideal.

## Social Interest

- **Social interest** (German: *Gemeinschaftsgefuhl* = "community feeling"): the individual's innate potential to cooperate with other people in order to achieve personal and societal goals.
  - Term translation: "Gemeinschaftsgefuhl" is best translated as "community feeling" (Stepansky, 1983, p. xiii), but "social interest" is the accepted English term.
- Although we are influenced more strongly by social than biological forces, the **potential for social interest is innate** — a biological element in Adler's otherwise social theory.
- The extent to which social interest is developed depends on **early social experiences**.
- Communities are indispensable for protection and survival; cooperation is necessary for personal and communal goals.
- People have a fundamental need to **belong** in order to be healthy, well-functioning individuals (Ferguson, 2010).
- Lack of social interest is the root cause of: neuroses, criminality, war, racial hatred, public drunkenness.

### The Role of the Mother in Developing Social Interest

- The mother is the **first social contact** of the baby.
- Through her behavior, she can either **foster or thwart** social interest and all other aspects of personality.
- "Every tendency which might be inherited has been adapted, trained, educated and made over again by the mother" (Adler, quoted in Grey, 1998, p. 71).
- The mother must teach: **cooperation, companionship, and courage**.
- Children (and later adults) who view others with suspicion and hostility will approach life with the same attitude.
- Those with no social interest may become neurotics or criminals.

### The Role of Adler's Life Experiences in Developing Social Interest

- Early in his career (within Freud's circle), Adler emphasized **power and dominance** as motivators.
- After breaking with Freud and achieving recognition, he shifted to emphasizing **social interest** as the core motivation.
- His personal mellowing from cantankerous ambition to warmer sociability mirrored the theoretical shift — another example of autobiographical influence on theory.

## Birth Order

- **Order of birth** is a major social influence in childhood; from it we create our style of life.
- Siblings share the same parents and home but NOT the same social environment; different birth positions create different personality-shaping conditions.
- Adler claimed to correctly guess people's birth order from their behavior at lectures and dinner parties.
- Four positions described: first-born, second-born, youngest, and only child.

### The First-Born Child

- Initially receives **undivided parental attention**; secure and happy existence.
- **Dethronement**: when the second child arrives, the first-born is displaced — no longer the center of attention.
- First-borns may become **stubborn, ill-behaved, destructive**, refusing to eat or sleep; ultimately the battle to regain supremacy is lost.
- Degree of loss depends on:
  - Extent of prior pampering (more pampered = greater sense of loss).
  - Age when sibling arrives (older first-born = less dethronement felt; e.g., 8-year-old suffers less than a 2-year-old).

#### Characteristics of First-Borns
- Oriented toward the **past**; nostalgic; pessimistic about the future.
- Concerned with **power and authority** throughout life.
- Intellectually advantaged: forced to tutor younger siblings, gaining verbal fluency and higher levels of intellectual maturity (Zajonc, 2001).
- **Good organizers**; conscientious; scrupulous about detail; **authoritarian and conservative**.
- May grow up feeling insecure and hostile.
- Adler noted that **neurotics, perverts, and criminals** were often first-borns.
- Sigmund Freud was a first-born; Adler described him as "a typical eldest son."

### The Second-Born Child

- Never experiences the powerful position of first-borns; not affected by dethronement.
- Parents typically adopt a **more relaxed child-rearing approach** by the second child.
- Always has the **older sibling as a pacesetter** — model, threat, or competitor.
- Adler himself was a second-born and had a lifelong competitive relationship with his older brother (also named Sigmund).

#### Characteristics of Second-Borns
- Motivated to **catch up and surpass** the older sibling; competition spurs language and motor development.
- More **optimistic about the future**; likely to be competitive and ambitious.
- If the older sibling excels in sports or scholarship, the second-born may give up trying and become an **underachiever**.

### The Youngest Child

- Never faces dethronement (no younger sibling arrives).
- Often becomes the **pet of the family**, especially if siblings are several years older.
- Driven by the need to surpass older siblings; often develops at a **remarkably fast rate**.
- Often **high achievers** in adult work.
- Risk: if excessively pampered, may retain childlike **helplessness and dependency** into adulthood, finding adjustment to adulthood difficult.

### The Only Child

- Never loses primacy and power; remains the focus and center of attention.
- Spends more time with adults; often **matures early** and manifests adult behaviors.
- May experience problems outside the home (e.g., school) where they are not the center of attention.
- Has learned **neither to share nor to compete**; keenly disappointed if abilities don't bring sufficient recognition.

> **Note**: Adler was NOT proposing firm developmental rules. Birth order combines with early social interactions and the creative self to produce probable (not certain) style-of-life tendencies.

## Questions about Human Nature

- Adler's view is the **antithesis of Freud's pessimistic, deterministic model**.
- **Optimistic**: people are not driven by unconscious forces; possess free will to shape social influences and create a unique style of life.
- **Emphasis on uniqueness**: each person is unique (contrast with Freud's universal, deterministic human nature).
- **Innate elements**: potential for social interest and striving for perfection are innate; but experience determines how these are realized.
- **Childhood influences important** (birth order, parent-child interactions) but we are NOT victims of them.
- Believes in individual creative power and is optimistic about social progress.

## Assessment in Adler's Theory

- Adler developed his theory through **case study analysis** of patients.
- Therapy style: **more relaxed and informal** than Freud's.
  - Freud: patient on couch, analyst behind them (asymmetric, formal).
  - Adler: both sat in **comfortable chairs facing each other**; sessions were "like chats between friends."
- Used **humor in therapy**; sometimes teased patients in a lighthearted, friendly way; believed humor could reveal the "ridiculousness" of one's neurosis.
- Assessed personality by observing everything: walking, sitting, **manner of shaking hands**, choice of chair.
- Even **sleep position** was revealing:
  - Restless sleepers / flat on back: want to seem more important than they are.
  - Stomach sleeper: stubborn and negative personality.
  - Fetal position: fearful of interacting with others.
  - Arms outstretched: need to be nurtured and supported.

- Adler's primary assessment methods (called the **"entrance gates to mental life"**):
  1. Order of birth
  2. Early recollections
  3. Dream analysis

### Early Recollections

- **Early recollections**: our earliest memories (real or fantasy) from the first 4–5 years of life indicate the style of life that continues into adulthood.
- It makes **little difference** whether memories are of real events or fantasies — both reveal the person's primary interests.
- "The most satisfactory single indicators of lifestyle" (Manaster & Mays, 2004, p. 114).
- Many early memories contained references to actual **physical objects**.
- Common patterns Adler found:
  - Memories of danger/punishment → tendency toward **hostility**.
  - Memories of sibling birth → continued sense of **dethronement**.
  - Focus on one parent → **preference** for that parent.
  - Memories of improper behavior → warning against repeating it.
- Key contrast with Freud:
  - Adler emphasized **what is remembered** (selective memory supporting current style of life and striving for significance).
  - Freud emphasized **what is forgotten** (repression).

#### Adler's Strange Early Memory (Personal Example)
- As an adult, Adler recalled a vivid early memory (age 5): terror of a cemetery on the path to school.
- He reportedly overcame his fear by running through the cemetery a dozen times.
- Thirty years later, he learned from a former schoolmate and others that **there had never been a cemetery** near their school.
- The memory was entirely fabricated — yet it symbolized his fear, inferiority, and efforts to overcome them: a perfect revelation of his style of life.

### Dream Analysis

- Adler agreed with Freud that dreams have value in understanding personality, but disagreed sharply on interpretation.
- Adler's view: dreams **do NOT** fulfill wishes or reveal hidden conflicts.
- Instead, dreams involve **feelings about a current problem** and what the dreamer intends to do about it.
- Dreams are oriented toward the **present and future**, NOT toward past conflicts.
- Dreams are a **manifestation of style of life** and unique to the individual; should never be interpreted without knowledge of the person and situation.
- Adler's own pre-America dream: ship capsized and sank with all possessions; he swam alone and reached land safely — symbolized his anxiety about the American reception of his theory and his determination to succeed.

**Common dream interpretations (Adlerian):**

| Dream Event | Adlerian Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Falling | Demotion or loss; fear of losing self-esteem or prestige |
| Flying | Ambitious style of life; desire to be above/better than others |
| Flying + falling | Fear of being too ambitious and thus failing |
| Being chased | Feeling of weakness in relation to others |
| Dreaming one is naked | Fear of giving oneself away |
| Being paralyzed | Facing insoluble problems |
| School exams | Being unprepared for life situations |
| Wearing wrong clothes | Being disturbed by one's faults |
| Sexual themes | Retreating from sex or inadequate information about sex |
| Rage | Angry or hostile style of life |
| Death | Unresolved issues about the dead person |

*(Source: Adapted from Grey, 1998, p. 93)*

### Measures of Social Interest

- Adler himself opposed psychological tests for personality assessment; argued tests create **artificial situations with ambiguous results**.
- He supported tests of **memory and intelligence** only.
- He believed therapists should develop their **intuition**.
- Later psychologists developed formal measures:

#### Social Interest Scale (SIS)
- Developer: **Crandall (1981)**
- Format: pairs of adjectives; subjects choose the word in each pair that best describes an attribute they would like to possess.
- High-social-interest words: helpful, sympathetic, considerate.

#### Social Interest Index (SII)
- Developer: **Greever, Tseng, & Friedland (1973)**
- Format: self-report inventory; subjects judge how well statements represent them.
- Example item: "I don't mind helping out friends."
- High SII scorers: high in friendliness, empathy, cooperation, tolerance, independence; lower in anxiety, hostility, depression, and neuroticism (Leak, 2006a, 2006b).

#### Basic Adlerian Scales for Interpersonal Success (BASIS-A)
- A **65-item self-report inventory** assessing lifestyle and degree of social interest.
- Five personality dimensions measured:
  1. Social interest
  2. Going along
  3. Taking charge
  4. Wanting recognition
  5. Being cautious
- (Peluso, Peluso, Buckner, Curlette, & Kern, 2004)

## Research on Adler's Theory

- Adler's primary research method: **case study**.
- Little of Adler's data survived; published only two case fragments (neither written by him — one by a patient, one by a patient's physician; Adler analyzed both remotely).
- Same methodological criticisms as Freud: observations cannot be repeated, not systematic or controlled, patient reports unverified, no experimental method.
- "Adler wanted his psychology to be a science, but it has not been a psychology easily verified by the scientific method" (Manaster, 2006, p. 6).

### Key Studies / Research Findings

> **CALLOUT: KEY STUDIES**

#### Dreams

**Study 1 (Grieser, Greenberg, & Harrison, 1972)**
- Method: Subjects exposed to unsolvable-puzzle situation (threat to personality) then allowed to sleep; one group awakened only during NREM sleep (could dream); another awakened during REM sleep (prevented from dreaming).
- Finding: Those who dreamed recalled significantly more of the uncompleted puzzle. Dreaming enabled subjects to deal with the current threatening situation.
- Supports: Adler's idea that dreams help solve current problems.

**Study 2 (Breger, Hunter, & Lane, 1971)**
- Method: Dreams of (a) college students anticipating a stressful psychotherapy session and (b) patients about to undergo major surgery.
- Finding: Both groups dreamed about their conscious worries, fears, and hopes — current problems.
- Supports: Dreams focus on present/future problems, not past conflicts.

#### Inferiority Feelings

- Adults who scored **low on inferiority feelings** were more successful, self-confident, and persistent.
- American college students with **moderate inferiority feelings** had higher GPAs than those with low or high inferiority feelings (Strano & Petrocelli, 2005).
- Research in China (teenagers and young adults): inferiority feelings change focus with age:
  - Younger teens: grades
  - Older teens: physical attractiveness
  - College students: lack of social skills (Kosaka, 2008)

#### Early Recollections

- **Jackson & Sechrest (1962)**: Anxiety neurotics had early memories of fear; depressed persons recalled abandonment; psychosomatic patients recalled illness.
- **Hafner, Fakouri, & Labrentz (1982)**: Alcoholics' early memories contained threatening events and being controlled by external circumstances (vs. control group showing neither theme).
- **Patterson (2014)**: Police officers with traumatic early recollections experienced more pronounced PTSD symptoms.
- **Hankoff (1987)**: Adult criminals' early recollections involved disturbing/aggressive interactions; more unpleasant events than control group.
- **Davidow & Bruhn (1990)**: Adolescent delinquents' early memories involved breaking rules, difficulty with social relationships, inability to cope alone, and distrust of parents.
- **Tobey & Bruhn (1992)**: Dangerous psychiatric patients showed more aggressive early memories; felt vulnerable, powerless; saw others as hostile and abusive.
- **Statton & Wilborn (1991)**: Research using objective scoring systems confirmed early recollections tend to be **subjective recreations** rather than actual events.
- **Buchanan, Kern, & Bell-Dumas (1991)**: When people fabricated early recollections for a hypothetical "someone else," themes were similar to their own — supporting Adler's contention that these memories reveal current style of life.
- **Clark (2005); Kasler & Nevo (2005)**: Early recollections in adults (US and Israel) predicted career preferences; physicists, mathematicians, and psychologists recalled themes of curiosity, independent thought, skepticism toward authority.

#### Neglect in Childhood

- **Crook, Raskin, & Eliot (1981)**: Adults hospitalized for depression rated their parents as hostile, detached, and rejecting; confirmed by interviews with siblings and friends.
- **Lefkowitz & Tesiny (1984)**: Parents of 8-year-olds rated for child-rearing behaviors; 10 years later, those 18-year-olds whose parents had been neglectful scored higher on the MMPI depression scale.
- Recent research: neglected children experienced more **shame, depression, PTSD symptoms, lower socialization skills** (Bennett, Sullivan, & Lewis, 2010; Lowell et al., 2011; Milot et al., 2013).
- Chinese children/adolescents (ages 5–17): those who experienced neglect were far more likely to develop **high anxiety** (Guan, Deng, & Luo, 2010).
- Neglected children may be more prone to **violence and excessive alcohol use** later in life (Widom et al., 2013).

#### Pampering in Childhood

- Research supports Adler's idea that pampering leads to lack of social feelings and excessive **narcissism**.
- Four types of pampering identified by research:

| Type | Description | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Overindulgence** | Persistent parental gratification of needs/desires | Feelings of entitlement; tyrannical, manipulative behavior |
| **Overpermissiveness** | Allowing any behavior regardless of effects on others | Disregard for social rules and others' rights |
| **Overdomination** | Exclusive parental decision-making | Lack of self-confidence; adult dependence on others |
| **Overprotection** | Excessive warnings of potential dangers | Generalized anxiety; tendency to avoid social situations |

- **Capron (2004)** (college students):
  - Children of over-domineering mothers were more likely to seek psychotherapy in college.
  - Students with overindulgent + overprotective parents had lower self-esteem.
  - Students with overindulgent + over-domineering parents scored high in narcissism.

#### Social Interest

- High social interest scorers (SIS): less stress, depression, anxiety, hostility; higher cooperation, empathy, responsibility, and popularity (Crandall, 1984; Watkins, 1994; Watkins & St. John, 1994).
- College students high in social interest scored high in **spirituality and religiosity** (positive, tolerant, helping nature — not fundamentalism) (Leak, 2006a).
- US and China studies: high social interest correlated with **subjective well-being, hope, optimism, agreeableness, self-identity**.

#### Birth-Order Effects

- Birth-order effects in research have been inconsistent and the subject of ongoing debate.
- Much evidence suggests that the influence of birth order on personality may be a matter of **belief** rather than demonstrated empirical fact.

## Reflections on Adler's Theory

### Contributions

- Provided an optimistic, hopeful alternative to Freud's pessimistic determinism.
- Pioneered the **social perspective** on personality: social (not biological) forces as primary shapers.
- Concept of **fictional finalism** influenced later cognitive approaches.
- **Birth order** research tradition is directly traceable to Adler.
- Early recollections technique is still used in therapy and research.
- Concepts of **inferiority feelings and compensation** are widely accepted and empirically supported.
- Group therapy techniques: Adler's Vienna child-counseling clinics were **forerunners of modern group therapy**.
- Many concepts were adopted without credit into later theories (ego psychology, humanistic psychology, cognitive-behavioral approaches).
- Direct influence on: **Karen Horney, Harry Stack Sullivan, Abraham Maslow, Albert Ellis, Aaron Beck**.

### Criticisms

- Primary research method (case study) lacks scientific rigor; cannot be replicated or controlled.
- Did not verify accuracy of patients' reports.
- Little interest in experimental method; little surviving data.
- Many propositions resist scientific validation.
- The concept of the creative self is vague and undefined — difficult to operationalize.
- Theory may be too culture-bound (reflects early 20th century Vienna).
- Later recognition was initially slow because of Freud's hostility.

### Later Recognition and Influence

- By the mid-20th century, Adler was largely forgotten — overshadowed by Freud.
- Later recognized by historians as having anticipated many ideas now attributed to others (cognitive therapy, humanistic psychology, social learning theory).
- "The most neglected person in the history of psychology" — yet his ideas are now ubiquitous.

## Chapter Summary

- Adler called his approach **individual psychology** because it stresses the uniqueness of each person.
- Humans are primarily **social beings**; personality is shaped by social interactions, not biological drives.
- **Inferiority feelings** are universal and are the source of all human striving.
- **Compensation** is the attempt to overcome inferiority.
- **Inferiority complex**: develops when compensation fails; three causes: organic inferiority, spoiling, neglect.
- **Superiority complex**: overcompensation for inferiority; involves boasting, vanity, denigration of others.
- The ultimate goal of life is **striving for superiority or perfection** (not about domination; about wholeness and completion).
- **Fictional finalism**: we are guided by imaginary, fictional ideals we cannot test against reality.
- **Style of life**: unique character pattern created by age 4–5; shaped by birth order and parent-child relationships.
- **Creative power of the self**: we use free will to create our style of life.
- **Four styles**: dominant, getting, avoiding, socially useful.
- **Social interest** (*Gemeinschaftsgefuhl*): innate potential for cooperation; developed through early social experience, especially with the mother.
- **Birth order**: first-born (dethroned; authoritarian; nostalgic), second-born (competitive; optimistic), youngest (fast developer; risk of pampering), only child (adult-oriented; difficulty sharing/competing).
- Assessment: **early recollections** and **dream analysis** (entrance gates to mental life); sleep position; BASIS-A, SIS, SII as formal measures.
- Research partially supports inferiority feelings, early recollections, neglect, pampering, social interest; birth-order effects remain debated.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Individual psychology** | Adler's theory of personality; emphasizes the uniqueness of each person and denies the universality of biological motives |
| **Inferiority feelings** | The normal condition of all people; the source of all human striving; arise from universal childhood helplessness |
| **Compensation** | Motivation to overcome inferiority; attempts to strive for higher levels of development |
| **Inferiority complex** | A condition that develops when a person is unable to compensate for normal inferiority feelings; results in poor self-image and inability to cope |
| **Organic inferiority** | Physical defects or weaknesses that motivate compensatory behavior; one of three causes of the inferiority complex |
| **Superiority complex** | A condition that develops when a person overcompensates for normal inferiority feelings; characterized by boasting, vanity, and denigration of others |
| **Masculine protest** | Adler's early term for compensation; the drive to overcome inferiority equated with femininity; rejected in favor of "striving for superiority" |
| **Striving for superiority** | The urge toward perfection or completion that motivates each of us; the fundamental fact of life; future-oriented; increases tension |
| **Fictional finalism** | The idea that imagined or potential goals guide our behavior as we strive toward a complete or whole state of being |
| **Finalism** | The notion that we have an ultimate goal, a final state of being, and a need to move toward it |
| **Style of life** | A unique character structure or pattern of personal behaviors and characteristics by which each person strives for perfection; crystallized by age 4–5 |
| **Creative power of the self** | The ability to create an appropriate style of life; reflects individual free will and active self-construction |
| **Dominant type** | A style of life characterized by ruling/dominant attitude with little social awareness |
| **Getting type** | The most common style of life; expects satisfaction from others; becomes dependent on them |
| **Avoiding type** | A style of life in which the person makes no attempt to face life's problems; avoids failure by avoiding engagement |
| **Socially useful type** | The healthy style of life; cooperates with others and acts in accordance with their needs within a framework of social interest |
| **Social interest** | The individual's innate potential to cooperate with others to achieve personal and societal goals; German: *Gemeinschaftsgefuhl* (community feeling) |
| **Gemeinschaftsgefuhl** | German term for social interest; literally "community feeling" |
| **Dethronement** | The experience of first-born children when a second child arrives and displaces them as the center of parental attention |
| **Early recollections** | A personality assessment technique in which earliest memories (real or fantasy) are assumed to reveal the primary interest of one's life and style of life |
| **Entrance gates to mental life** | Adler's term for his three primary assessment methods: order of birth, early recollections, and dream analysis |
| **Social Interest Scale (SIS)** | A personality measure of social interest using pairs of adjectives; developed by Crandall (1981) |
| **Social Interest Index (SII)** | A self-report inventory measuring ability to accept and cooperate with others; developed by Greever, Tseng, & Friedland (1973) |
| **BASIS-A** | Basic Adlerian Scales for Interpersonal Success; 65-item self-report inventory measuring lifestyle and social interest on five dimensions |
| **Overindulgence** | Type of pampering involving persistent gratification of a child's needs; leads to entitlement and manipulative behavior |
| **Overpermissiveness** | Type of pampering allowing any behavior without regard for others; leads to disregard for social rules |
| **Overdomination** | Type of pampering involving exclusive parental decision-making; leads to adult dependence |
| **Overprotection** | Type of pampering involving excessive warnings of danger; leads to generalized anxiety and social avoidance |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Adler's theoretical name**: "Individual psychology" — the name emphasizes the uniqueness of each person, NOT group differences.
2. **Core motivating force**: Inferiority feelings are the source of ALL human striving; they are universal, normal, and necessary — NOT pathological.
3. **Three causes of inferiority complex**: organic inferiority, spoiling/pampering, and neglect.
4. **Striving for superiority vs. superiority complex**: Striving for superiority = healthy, innate drive for perfection/completion (future-oriented). Superiority complex = pathological overcompensation (boasting, vanity, denigration of others).
5. **Fictional finalism**: Behavior is guided by fictional (imagined, subjective, untestable) ideals; the most pervasive fictional goal = perfection; the best human formulation of it = concept of God.
6. **Style of life crystallizes by age 4–5** and remains constant thereafter; shaped by birth order and parent-child relationships; created actively via the creative power of the self.
7. **Four styles of life**: dominant, getting (most common), avoiding, socially useful (only healthy type); the first three lack social interest and result in neuroses and psychoses.
8. **Social interest = Gemeinschaftsgefuhl**: Innate potential for cooperation; developed through early experience, especially with the mother; lack of social interest is the root of neurosis, criminality, war.
9. **Birth order characteristics**: First-born (nostalgic, authoritarian, power-oriented, intellectually advantaged via tutoring role, prone to neurosis); Second-born (competitive, optimistic, may become underachiever); Youngest (fast developer, risk of pampering); Only child (adult-like, difficulty sharing and competing).
10. **Dethronement**: The key experience of the first-born child when a sibling arrives; degree depends on prior pampering and age at time of dethronement.
11. **Assessment: "entrance gates to mental life"**: Order of birth, early recollections, dream analysis.
12. **Early recollections**: Whether real or fantasy, they reveal the style of life; Adler emphasizes what is REMEMBERED (vs. Freud who emphasizes what is FORGOTTEN via repression).
13. **Dream interpretation (Adlerian vs. Freudian)**: Adler: dreams reflect current problems and future intentions (not wish fulfillment or past conflicts); falling = fear of loss of esteem; flying = ambition; being chased = feeling of weakness.
14. **Social Interest Scale (SIS)** by Crandall (1981); Social Interest Index (SII) by Greever et al. (1973); BASIS-A = 65-item scale measuring five lifestyle dimensions.
15. **Adler vs. Freud key contrasts**: Social vs. biological forces; conscious vs. unconscious core; future-oriented vs. past-determined; optimistic vs. pessimistic view of human nature; unique vs. universal human nature; peer group vs. parental (especially maternal) emphasis.
