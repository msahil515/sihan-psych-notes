# Ch 4: Alfred Adler: Individual Psychology

Adler's **individual psychology**: humans are primarily social beings shaped by unique social environments, not biological instincts. Unlike Freud, Adler centered consciousness (not the unconscious), emphasized free will and future-oriented striving, and held that inferiority feelings are the universal source of all motivation. Each person creates a unique **style of life** to overcome inferiority and achieve perfection.

---

## The Life of Adler (1870–1937)

### Childhood and Adolescence

- Born 1870 in Vienna; died 1937 in Aberdeen, Scotland (heart attack on a 56-lecture tour).
- Had childhood **rickets** (limiting activity) → inferiority relative to healthier peers.
- Age 3: younger brother died in the bed next to him. Age 4: nearly died of pneumonia; overheard "Your boy is lost" and resolved to become a physician.
- **Pampered by his mother**, then dethroned at age 2 by a new sibling; afterward his father's favorite. He rejected the Oedipus complex (closer to father, not mother).
- Jealous of his vigorous **older brother** (named Sigmund).
- Initially a mediocre student (a teacher advised apprenticing him to a shoemaker); rose to top of class through hard work — a personal demonstration of compensation.
- Sociable; his theory later emphasized peer group over parental influence.

### Adulthood
- Studied medicine at the **University of Vienna**; began as an **ophthalmologist**, then general medicine, then **neurology/psychiatry**.

### Relations with Freud
- Associated with Freud **1902–1911** (~9 years), but was **never a student or disciple** and was never psychoanalyzed by him.
- By 1910: **president of the Vienna Psychoanalytical Society**, yet increasingly critical; he severed all connection and developed individual psychology.
- Mutual hostility: Freud called Adler "loathsome," "driven mad by ambition"; Adler called Freud a "swindler" and psychoanalysis "filth." Adler bristled at being called "a student of Freud's."

### Becoming a Celebrity in America
- **1912**: founded the **Society for Individual Psychology**.
- After WWI: organized Vienna **child-counseling clinics** with **group training/guidance** (forerunners of group therapy).
- **1926**: first U.S. visit; **1929**: moved to New York. Became "America's first popular psychologist."

## Inferiority Feelings: The Source of All Human Striving

- Core premise: **"To be a human being means to feel oneself inferior"** (Adler, 1933/1939).
- **Inferiority feelings** are the normal condition of all people, not weakness.
- **Source of all striving**: growth results from **compensation** — overcoming real or imagined inferiorities.
- Begins in **infancy**: infants are small, helpless, dependent, aware of adult power → inferiority feelings. Not genetic; a function of universal helplessness. **Inescapable and necessary** as motivation.

### The Inferiority Complex
- When a child **cannot compensate**, feelings intensify into an **inferiority complex**: poor self-opinion, helplessness, inability to cope. Adler found it in many adult patients' childhoods.

### Causes of Inferiority Complexes

**1. Organic Inferiority** — defective body parts shape personality via compensation efforts.
- Examples: **Demosthenes** (overcame a stutter to become an orator); **Theodore Roosevelt** (sickly child → fitness model).
- Failure to compensate → inferiority complex.

**2. Spoiling (Pampering)** — center of attention, every need met; expect deference. Shocked when no longer the focus (e.g., at school); lack social feeling and patience; blame personal deficiency → inferiority complex.

**3. Neglect** — unwanted/rejected children lack love/security; develop worthlessness, anger, distrust. A major form of child abuse.

### The Superiority Complex
- **Overcompensation** for inferiority → **superiority complex**: exaggerated opinion of one's abilities; boasting, vanity, self-centeredness, denigrating others.
- Two sub-types: (1) feels superior without demonstrating it; (2) works obsessively to be successful.
- Distinct from healthy "striving for superiority."

---

## Striving for Superiority, or Perfection

- Adler's view of the ultimate goal evolved:
  - **Early**: equated inferiority with femininity; compensation = the **masculine protest** (drive toward power, with aggression).
  - **Later**: rejected the femininity equation; broadened to the drive for **superiority or perfection**.
- **Striving for superiority** = the fundamental fact of life (Adler, 1930). NOT about being better than others or arrogance; a drive for **perfection** (Latin: to complete/finish) — to make oneself whole. Innate and **future-oriented** (vs. Freud's past).
- Characteristics: **increases tension** (vs. Freud's tension-reduction); manifested **individually and collectively**; individuals and society are interdependent.

### Fictional Finalism
- **Finalism**: we have an ultimate goal and a need to move toward it; goals are **potentialities, not actualities**.
- **Fictional finalism**: imagined ideals guide behavior toward a complete state of being. Examples: "all people are created equal," afterlife, God (Adler's best human formulation of perfection).
- Adler's own fictional goal: conquer the death he faced at 4, via becoming a physician.
- Adler preferred **"subjective final goal"** / **"guiding self-ideal,"** but "fictional finalism" persisted (Watts & Holden, 1994); influenced by **Hans Vaihinger's** "philosophy of as-if."

## The Style of Life

- Each person expresses the striving uniquely via a pattern of behaviors/habits — the **style of life** (distinctive character).
- **Crystallized by age 4–5**; hard to change after. Determines what we attend to and our attitudes. Shaped by **birth order** and the **parent-child relationship**.

### The Creative Power of the Self
- Resolves the apparent determinism: the **creative power of the self** — we actively create our style of life.
- We are **not passively shaped**; our **conscious attitude** toward experiences matters more than the experiences. Neither heredity nor environment alone determines personality; **free will** constructs the style of life from both. Once created, it **remains constant**.

### Four Basic Styles of Life

Universal problems: (1) behavior toward others, (2) occupation, (3) love.

| Style | Description | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Dominant type** | Ruling attitude, little social awareness; attacks others or self | Sadists, delinquents, sociopaths, addicts |
| **Getting type** | Expects satisfaction from others; dependent | Most common human type |
| **Avoiding type** | Avoids problems and possible failure | Neuroses and psychoses |
| **Socially useful type** | Cooperates; acts per others' needs | Healthy; framework of social interest |

- The first three lack **social interest** → neuroses/psychoses. The socially useful type is the healthy ideal.

## Social Interest

- **Social interest** (German *Gemeinschaftsgefuhl* = "community feeling"): innate potential to cooperate with others to achieve personal and societal goals.
- We are influenced more by social than biological forces, but the **potential is innate** (the biological element in an otherwise social theory). Its development depends on **early social experiences**.
- People need to **belong** to be healthy (Ferguson, 2010). Lack of social interest is the root of neuroses, criminality, war, racial hatred, drunkenness.

### The Role of the Mother
- The mother is the baby's **first social contact**; she can **foster or thwart** social interest. She must teach **cooperation, companionship, courage**. Children who view others with hostility approach life the same way.

### Adler's Life Experiences
- Early (in Freud's circle), Adler emphasized **power/dominance**; after breaking away, he shifted to **social interest** — mirroring his personal mellowing (autobiographical influence).

## Birth Order

- **Order of birth** is a major childhood social influence. Siblings share parents/home but NOT the same social environment. Adler claimed to guess birth order from behavior.

### The First-Born Child
- Initially gets **undivided attention**. **Dethronement**: the second child displaces them; first-borns may become stubborn, destructive. Degree of loss depends on prior pampering and age at sibling's arrival (older = less dethronement).
- **Characteristics**: oriented to the **past**, nostalgic, pessimistic; concerned with **power/authority**; intellectually advantaged (tutoring younger siblings → verbal fluency; Zajonc, 2001); good organizers, conscientious, **authoritarian, conservative**; may feel insecure/hostile. Adler noted many neurotics, perverts, criminals were first-borns; called Freud "a typical eldest son."

### The Second-Born Child
- No dethronement; parents more relaxed; always has the **older sibling as a pacesetter**. Adler was a second-born competing with his brother Sigmund.
- **Characteristics**: motivated to **catch up/surpass**; more **optimistic**, competitive, ambitious; may become an **underachiever** if the older sibling excels.

### The Youngest Child
- Never dethroned; often the **family pet**; driven to surpass older siblings; **develops fast**; often a **high achiever**. Risk: if pampered, retains **helplessness/dependency**.

### The Only Child
- Never loses primacy; spends time with adults, **matures early**; may struggle outside the home; learned **neither to share nor compete**.

> **Note**: Not firm rules. Birth order + early interactions + the creative self → **probable** (not certain) tendencies.

## Questions about Human Nature

- The **antithesis of Freud's pessimistic determinism**.
- **Optimistic**: free will shapes social influences and creates a unique style of life.
- **Uniqueness** emphasized (vs. Freud's universal nature).
- **Innate elements**: social interest potential and striving for perfection; experience determines realization.
- **Childhood influences important** (birth order, parent-child) but we are NOT victims.

## Assessment in Adler's Theory

- Developed theory through **case study**.
- Therapy: relaxed and informal — both sat in **chairs facing each other** ("chats between friends"), vs. Freud's couch. Used **humor**.
- Observed everything: walking, sitting, handshake, chair choice, even **sleep position** (back = wants to seem important; stomach = stubborn/negative; fetal = fearful; arms outstretched = needs nurturing).
- Primary methods, the **"entrance gates to mental life"**: (1) order of birth, (2) early recollections, (3) dream analysis.

### Early Recollections
- **Early recollections**: earliest memories (real or fantasy) from the first 4–5 years reveal the style of life. Real vs. fantasy makes **little difference** — both reveal primary interests. "The most satisfactory single indicators of lifestyle" (Manaster & Mays, 2004).
- Patterns: danger/punishment → **hostility**; sibling birth → continued **dethronement**; focus on one parent → **preference**; improper behavior → warning.
- Contrast: Adler emphasized **what is remembered** (selective memory supporting current style of life); Freud emphasized **what is forgotten** (repression).
- Adler's own fabricated memory (age 5): terror of a nonexistent cemetery he ran through to conquer his fear — symbolizing his inferiority and efforts to overcome it.

### Dream Analysis
- Adler: dreams do **NOT** fulfill wishes or reveal past conflicts; they involve **feelings about a current problem** and intended action — oriented to the **present/future**; a **manifestation of style of life**, never interpreted without knowing the person.
- Adler's pre-America dream: a capsized ship; he swam to land alone — symbolizing anxiety about his American reception and determination to succeed.

**Common Adlerian dream interpretations:**

| Dream Event | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Falling | Demotion/loss; fear of losing esteem or prestige |
| Flying | Ambitious style of life; desire to be above others |
| Flying + falling | Fear of being too ambitious and failing |
| Being chased | Feeling of weakness relative to others |
| Naked | Fear of giving oneself away |
| Paralyzed | Facing insoluble problems |
| School exams | Unprepared for life situations |
| Wrong clothes | Disturbed by one's faults |
| Sexual themes | Retreating from sex or inadequate sexual information |
| Rage | Angry/hostile style of life |
| Death | Unresolved issues about the dead person |

*(Source: Grey, 1998, p. 93)*

### Measures of Social Interest
- Adler **opposed** psychological tests (artificial, ambiguous); supported only memory/intelligence tests and therapist intuition. Later measures:

**Social Interest Scale (SIS)** — **Crandall (1981)**; pairs of adjectives; choose the attribute one wants. High-SIS words: helpful, sympathetic, considerate.

**Social Interest Index (SII)** — **Greever, Tseng, & Friedland (1973)**; self-report. High scorers: friendly, empathic, cooperative, tolerant, independent; lower in anxiety, hostility, depression, neuroticism (Leak, 2006).

**BASIS-A** (Basic Adlerian Scales for Interpersonal Success) — **65-item** self-report; five dimensions: social interest, going along, taking charge, wanting recognition, being cautious (Peluso et al., 2004).

## Research on Adler's Theory

- Primary method: **case study**; little data survived (only two case fragments, neither written by Adler). Same criticisms as Freud: not repeatable, controlled, or verified. "Adler wanted his psychology to be a science, but it has not been...easily verified" (Manaster, 2006).

### Key Studies / Findings

**Dreams:**
- **Grieser, Greenberg, & Harrison (1972)**: after an unsolvable-puzzle threat, those allowed to dream (NREM-awakened) recalled more of the puzzle than REM-deprived subjects — dreaming helped deal with the current threat. Supports Adler.
- **Breger, Hunter, & Lane (1971)**: students before stressful therapy and patients before surgery dreamed about their conscious worries — present/future, not past.

**Inferiority Feelings:**
- Low inferiority feelings → more successful, confident, persistent.
- **Moderate** inferiority feelings → higher GPA than low or high (Strano & Petrocelli, 2005).
- China study: focus changes with age — grades (younger teens) → appearance (older teens) → social skills (college) (Kosaka, 2008).

**Early Recollections:**
- **Jackson & Sechrest (1962)**: anxiety neurotics recalled fear; depressed recalled abandonment; psychosomatic recalled illness.
- **Hafner et al. (1982)**: alcoholics recalled threatening events and external control.
- **Patterson (2014)**: police with traumatic early recollections → more PTSD symptoms.
- **Hankoff (1987)**: adult criminals recalled more disturbing/aggressive interactions.
- **Davidow & Bruhn (1990)**: adolescent delinquents recalled rule-breaking, social difficulty, parental distrust.
- **Tobey & Bruhn (1992)**: dangerous psychiatric patients recalled aggressive memories; felt powerless.
- **Statton & Wilborn (1991)**: early recollections are **subjective recreations**, not actual events.
- **Buchanan et al. (1991)**: fabricated recollections for "someone else" share the person's own themes — they reveal current style of life.
- **Clark (2005); Kasler & Nevo (2005)**: recollections predict career preferences (scientists/psychologists recalled curiosity, independent thought, skepticism).

**Neglect:**
- **Crook, Raskin, & Eliot (1981)**: depressed adults rated parents as hostile/rejecting (confirmed by collaterals).
- **Lefkowitz & Tesiny (1984)**: parental neglect at age 8 predicted higher MMPI depression at 18.
- Neglect → more shame, depression, PTSD, lower socialization (Bennett et al., 2010; Lowell et al., 2011; Milot et al., 2013); higher anxiety in Chinese youth (Guan et al., 2010); later violence/alcohol use (Widom et al., 2013).

**Pampering:**

| Type | Description | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Overindulgence** | Persistent gratification | Entitlement; manipulative behavior |
| **Overpermissiveness** | Any behavior allowed | Disregard for rules/others' rights |
| **Overdomination** | Exclusive parental decisions | Low self-confidence; dependence |
| **Overprotection** | Excessive danger warnings | Anxiety; social avoidance |

- **Capron (2004)**: over-domineering mothers → more therapy-seeking; overindulgent + overprotective → lower self-esteem; overindulgent + over-domineering → high narcissism.

**Social Interest:**
- High SIS → less stress/depression/anxiety/hostility; more cooperation, empathy, popularity (Crandall, 1984; Watkins, 1994). Linked to **spirituality/religiosity** (tolerant, helping; Leak, 2006) and to well-being, hope, optimism, agreeableness.

**Birth-Order Effects:**
- Inconsistent and debated; may be more a matter of **belief** than demonstrated fact.

## Reflections on Adler's Theory

### Contributions
- Optimistic alternative to Freud; pioneered the **social perspective** on personality.
- **Fictional finalism** influenced cognitive approaches; **birth order** research traces to Adler; early recollections still used.
- **Inferiority feelings/compensation** widely accepted and supported.
- Vienna child clinics = **forerunners of group therapy**.
- Influenced **Horney, Sullivan, Maslow, Ellis, Beck** (often without credit).

### Criticisms
- Case study lacks rigor (not replicable/controlled); unverified patient reports; little data; many propositions resist validation; the **creative self** is vague; possibly culture-bound (early Vienna); slow recognition due to Freud's hostility.

### Later Recognition
- Largely forgotten mid-century, overshadowed by Freud; later recognized as anticipating cognitive therapy, humanistic psychology, social learning. "The most neglected person in the history of psychology" — yet his ideas are now ubiquitous.

## Chapter Summary

- **Individual psychology** stresses each person's uniqueness; humans are **social beings** shaped by social interaction.
- **Inferiority feelings** are universal and the source of all striving; **compensation** overcomes them.
- **Inferiority complex** (compensation fails): three causes — organic inferiority, spoiling, neglect.
- **Superiority complex**: overcompensation (boasting, vanity, denigration).
- Ultimate goal = **striving for superiority/perfection** (wholeness, not domination).
- **Fictional finalism**: guided by imagined, untestable ideals.
- **Style of life**: unique pattern created by age 4–5; shaped by birth order and parent-child relationships.
- **Creative power of the self**: free will creates the style of life.
- **Four styles**: dominant, getting, avoiding, socially useful (only healthy one).
- **Social interest** (*Gemeinschaftsgefuhl*): innate cooperation potential developed through early experience, especially the mother.
- **Birth order**: first-born (dethroned, authoritarian, nostalgic), second-born (competitive, optimistic), youngest (fast developer, pampering risk), only child (adult-oriented, poor at sharing/competing).
- Assessment: **early recollections**, **dream analysis** (entrance gates); BASIS-A, SIS, SII.
- Research supports inferiority feelings, early recollections, neglect, pampering, social interest; birth-order effects debated.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **"Individual psychology"** emphasizes each person's uniqueness, not group differences.
2. **Inferiority feelings** = source of ALL striving; universal, normal, necessary — not pathological.
3. **Three causes of inferiority complex**: organic inferiority, spoiling/pampering, neglect.
4. **Striving for superiority** (healthy, innate, future-oriented drive for perfection) vs. **superiority complex** (pathological overcompensation: boasting, vanity, denigration).
5. **Fictional finalism**: behavior guided by fictional, untestable ideals; most pervasive goal = perfection; best human formulation = God.
6. **Style of life** crystallizes by **age 4–5** and stays constant; shaped by birth order and parent-child; built via the creative power of the self.
7. **Four styles**: dominant, getting (most common), avoiding, socially useful (only healthy); first three lack social interest → neuroses/psychoses.
8. **Social interest = Gemeinschaftsgefuhl**: innate cooperation potential developed early, especially with the mother; lack of it underlies neurosis, crime, war.
9. **Birth order**: first-born (nostalgic, authoritarian, intellectually advantaged, neurosis-prone); second-born (competitive, optimistic, possible underachiever); youngest (fast developer, pampering risk); only child (adult-like, poor at sharing/competing).
10. **Dethronement**: first-born's displacement by a sibling; depends on prior pampering and age.
11. **"Entrance gates to mental life"**: order of birth, early recollections, dream analysis.
12. **Early recollections** (real or fantasy) reveal style of life; Adler emphasizes what is REMEMBERED vs. Freud's FORGOTTEN.
13. **Dreams (Adler vs. Freud)**: current problems and future intentions, not wish fulfillment; falling = loss of esteem; flying = ambition; chased = weakness.
14. **SIS** (Crandall, 1981); **SII** (Greever et al., 1973); **BASIS-A** = 65 items, five dimensions.
15. **Adler vs. Freud**: social vs. biological; conscious vs. unconscious; future vs. past; optimistic vs. pessimistic; unique vs. universal; peer group vs. maternal emphasis.
