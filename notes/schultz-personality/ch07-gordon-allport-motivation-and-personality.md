# Ch 7: Gordon Allport: Motivation and Personality

Gordon Allport (1897–1967) made personality academically respectable, challenging Freud by emphasizing conscious, rational, present-oriented motivation in emotionally healthy adults. His theory centers on each person's uniqueness, expressed through a hierarchical trait system, **functional autonomy**, and a seven-stage model of self (**proprium**) development.

---

## Allport Brings Personality into the Lab

- Allport's career spanned **four decades**; he moved personality into mainstream scientific psychology (psychoanalytic theories were not previously considered mainstream).
- **1937**: published *Personality: A Psychological Interpretation* — formalized personality as an academic discipline.
- Two contributions: (1) brought personality into the mainstream; (2) formulated a trait-based theory.

### Allport Challenges Freud

| Allport | Freud |
|---|---|
| Unconscious matters only for the disturbed; healthy adults are rational/conscious | Unconscious forces dominate |
| Present and future guide behavior; not prisoners of childhood | Past determines present — **historical determinism** |
| Clear normal/abnormal distinction (abnormal = infantile) | Continuum between normal and abnormal |

- People are "busy leading their lives into the future, whereas psychology...is busy tracing them into the past" (Allport, 1955).
- Opposed generalizing about normal adults from neurotics, children, or animals.

### Each Person Is Unique
- Defining emphasis: **uniqueness of personality** via individual traits; opposed universal laws. Personality is particular, not general.

---

## The Life of Allport (1897–1967)

### Background
- Born in **Montezuma, Indiana**; youngest of four sons. Mother a teacher; devout household (no smoking, drinking, dancing, bright colors). Father a salesman-turned-doctor.

### Isolation and Inferiority
- Isolated from peers; skillful with words but poor at sports: "I suffered agonies on the playground." Childhood isolation produced **inferiority feelings**, compensated by striving to excel.
- Strongly identified with eldest brother **Floyd** (noted social psychologist), whom he followed to Harvard. His concept of **functional autonomy** may partly assert independence from Floyd.

### College
- Applied to Harvard (1915); after poor initial grades, finished first year with straight A's. Graduated **1919**; spent a year at **Robert College, Istanbul**; returned for graduate study.

### Allport Meets Freud
- Stopped in Vienna (~1919); told Freud about a boy on a streetcar with an **obvious fear of dirt**. Freud asked: **"Was that little boy you?"** — interpreting the story as Allport's own unconscious conflict.
- Allport, shaken, called it "traumatic"; concluded psychoanalysis probes too deep — psychology should focus on **conscious motivations**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Kaufman & Bristol (2001) — presenting information in a **social/personal context** improves enjoyment, learning, and memory.

### Becoming a Success
- Ph.D. at Harvard, **1922**. Dissertation: *"An Experimental Study of the Traits of Personality"* — the **first US research on personality traits**.
- Taught the **first formal American college course on personality**; spent ~four decades at Harvard. Won APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award and the Gold Medal; APA president.

## The Nature of Personality

Allport reviewed ~**50 definitions** before offering his own.

> **Allport (1961):** "Personality is the dynamic organization within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine...characteristic behavior and thought."

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Dynamic organization** | Changing and growing, but organized — not random |
| **Psychophysical** | Mind and body as a unit |
| **Determine** | Personality activates/directs specific behavior and thought |
| **Characteristic behavior and thought** | Everything we think/do is unique to us |

### Heredity and Environment
- Both shape personality. **Heredity** provides raw materials (physique, intelligence, temperament); **environment** shapes/limits them. Genetics drives the **major portion** of uniqueness. No two people share the same genes (except identical twins) or environment → psychology must study the **individual case**.

### Two Personalities for Two Stages of Life
- Personality is **discontinuous**: **infant personality** is driven by biological urges/reflexes; **adult personality** is psychological and not constrained by childhood. Emphasizes conscious over unconscious, present/future over past, uniqueness, normal over abnormal.

## Personality Traits

**Traits** = predispositions to respond in the same/similar manner to different stimuli — consistent, enduring ways of reacting.

### Five Characteristics of Traits (Allport, 1937)
1. **Real** — they exist within us, not just labels.
2. **Determine/cause behavior** — they motivate us to seek appropriate stimuli, not just react.
3. Can be **demonstrated empirically** — via consistency over time.
4. **Interrelated** — may overlap (e.g., aggressiveness and hostility).
5. **Vary with the situation** — neat in one context, messy in another.

### Original Two Types
- **Individual traits**: unique to a person. **Common traits**: shared within a culture; vary across cultures and change over time.

### Personal Dispositions (Revised Terminology)
- "Common traits" → **traits**; "individual traits" → **personal dispositions**.

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Cardinal traits** | Most pervasive; a "ruling passion"; not everyone has one | Sadism, chauvinism |
| **Central traits** | 5–10 themes best describing behavior; mentioned in a recommendation | Aggressiveness, cynicism |
| **Secondary traits** | Least influential; inconsistent; only close friends notice | Food/music preference |

## Motivation: What We Strive For

- The **central problem** for any theory is motivation. Allport emphasized the **present**; the past matters only if it is a **current motivating force**. Cognitive processes (conscious plans/intentions) are vital. He explained the present via the **future**.

### Functional Autonomy
- **Functional autonomy of motives**: in the mature, healthy adult, motives are **not functionally connected** to the experiences in which they first appeared.
- **Tree analogy**: a grown tree no longer depends on the seed.
- **Career example**: a person achieves financial security by 55 but keeps working hard — the original motive (money) becomes an end in itself.
- Implication: adult motives can't be understood via childhood — investigate why people behave as they do **today**.

**Two Levels:**
1. **Perseverative functional autonomy** — elementary; addictions, repetitive habitual behaviors continuing **without external reward**; too low-level to be integral to personality. *Rat evidence*: a rat trained to run a maze for food keeps running it when well-fed.
2. **Propriate functional autonomy** — more important; derived from the **proprium** (ego/self); motives unique to each person. The ego keeps motives that enhance self-image. *Piano example*: forced lessons → proficiency → playing becomes part of self-image; the original motive vanishes.

**Three Principles of Propriate Functioning:**
1. **Organizing the energy level** — new motives consume excess energy (e.g., redirecting energy after retirement).
2. **Mastery and competence** — motivated to perform better, not just adequately.
3. **Propriate patterning** — striving for consistency/integration around the self.

- **Not** covered: reflexes, fixations, neuroses, biological-drive behaviors.

## Personality Development: The Unique Self

### The Proprium
- **Proprium**: Allport's term for the self/ego (rejected "self"/"ego"). From "appropriate" — aspects of personality distinctive and appropriate to one's emotional life; unites attitudes, perceptions, intentions.

**Before any stage**: the infant has no self-consciousness; reacts reflexively — **"unsocialized horrors"** (pleasure-seeking, destructive, selfish).

| Stage | Age | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Bodily self** | Birth–~3 | Awareness of a "bodily me"; distinguishes own body from environment |
| **2. Self-identity** | First 3 years | Continuity of identity despite changes; aided by learning one's name |
| **3. Self-esteem** | First 3 years | Discovers they can accomplish things independently; frustration → humiliation/anger |
| **4. Extension of self** | 4–6 | Awareness of things/people as belonging to oneself |
| **5. Self-image** | 4–6 | Actual and ideal self-images from parental interaction |
| **6. Self as rational coper** | 6–12 | Applies reason/logic to everyday problems |
| **7. Propriate striving** | Adolescence | Long-range goals/plans; proprium incomplete until now |
| **Adulthood** | — | Functionally autonomous; rational; creates own lifestyle |

### The Infant–Mother Bond
- The most important social interaction (affection/security).
- **If sufficient**: proprium develops steadily; childhood motives become autonomous propriate strivings → mature, healthy adult.
- **If frustrated**: self does not mature; child becomes insecure, aggressive, self-centered; adult motives stay tied to childhood → neurotic adult functioning at the level of childhood drives.

## The Healthy Adult Personality

**Six Criteria:**
1. **Extended sense of self** — to people/activities beyond the self.
2. **Warm relating to others** — intimacy, compassion, tolerance.
3. **Emotional security** — self-acceptance.
4. **Realistic perception** — plus personal skills and commitment to work.
5. **Self-objectification** — humor and insight into oneself.
6. **Unifying philosophy of life** — value system directing toward future goals.

Meeting all six = emotionally healthy and functionally autonomous.

---

## Questions about Human Nature

- **Free will vs. Determinism**: moderate — free choice about the future, but traits/dispositions (once formed) are hard to change.
- **Nature vs. Nurture**: both; genetics supplies raw materials, experience shapes them.
- **Uniqueness**: individual traits/dispositions describe a person most precisely.
- **Goal of life**: to **increase** tension (seek new challenges), opposite to Freud. "Getting there is more fun than being there."
- Overall: **optimistic** — adults consciously control behavior, plan, and are always "becoming."

## Assessment in Allport's Theory

- No single best technique; use many. **Allport's 11 methods**: (1) constitutional/physiological diagnosis, (2) cultural setting/role, (3) personal documents/case studies, (4) self-appraisal, (5) conduct analysis, (6) ratings, (7) tests/scales, (8) projective techniques, (9) depth analysis, (10) expressive behavior, (11) synoptic procedures.
- Relied most on the **personal-document technique** and the **Study of Values**; also observed expressive behavior.

### The Personal-Document Technique
- Examining diaries, letters, autobiographies to determine traits.
- **Jenny case**: ~**300 letters** over **12 years** by "Jenny" (later revealed as the mother of Allport's college roommate). **36 judges** identified ~**200 traits**, reduced to **8 categories**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Paige (1966) — computer analysis of Jenny's letters yielded **8 traits** consistent with Allport's 8 categories, validating his subjective approach.

### The Study of Values
- **Allport, Vernon, & Lindzey (1960)**: objective self-report test. Values = personality traits; everyone has all six, with one or two dominant.

| Value Type | Orientation |
|---|---|
| **Theoretical** | Discovery of truth; intellectual, rational |
| **Economic** | Useful and practical |
| **Aesthetic** | Form, harmony, grace |
| **Social** | Human relationships, altruism |
| **Political** | Power, influence, prestige |
| **Religious** | Mystical; understanding the universe |

## Research on Allport's Theory

- Allport criticized the insistence that experimental/correlational methods were the only legitimate ones; opposed applying clinical methods (case studies, TAT, Rorschach) to healthy adults. Suggested simply **asking people to describe themselves**.
- Favored the **idiographic approach** (individual case) but used **nomothetic methods** when appropriate (e.g., Study of Values).

### Expressive Behavior

| Feature | Expressive | Coping |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Spontaneous; reflects personality | Planned; purposeful |
| Awareness | Without conscious awareness | Consciously planned |
| Changeability | Hard to change | Can be altered by will |
| Purpose | None specific | Goal-directed |

- **Public speaking**: coping = planned content; expressive = gestures, vocal inflections, fidgeting.

> **KEY STUDY (Landmark):** Allport & Vernon (1933) — found **high consistency in voice, handwriting, posture, gestures**; deduced **introversion/extraversion** from these behaviors.

> **KEY STUDY:** Allport & Cantril (1934) — assessed expressive behaviors from **still photographs**.

**Other findings:** personality assessable from voice, films, faces. Observers accurately judged anxiety from a **30-second** film (Ambody & Rosenthal, 1992). Women and younger people read facial emotions better than men/older people (Sasson et al., 2010); children read expressions by **age 5** (Gao & Maurer, 2010); close friends decode emotions far better than acquaintances (Zhang & Parmley, 2011).

> **KEY STUDY (Yearbook):** Harker & Keltner (2001) — women with positive expressions at **21** scored higher on well-being at **27, 43, 52** and had better marriages.

**Clinical findings:** abused children (8–10) identified anger more readily (Pollak & Sinha, 2002); depressed adults needed greater intensity to identify happiness, less for sadness (Joormann & Gotlib, 2006).

### Coding Facial Expressions: FACS

> **KEY RESEARCHER: Paul Ekman** (UCSF). Named a *Time* 100 Most Influential (2009).

**Seven universal facial emotions:** anger, contempt, disgust, fear, sadness, surprise, happiness.
- Analyzed **43 facial muscles** → **3,000 configurations**; developed the **Facial Action Coding System (FACS)**, used by US police, CIA, FBI to detect lying.

**Traits revealed by expressions:** Neuroticism (anger, contempt, fear); Agreeableness (laughter, friendly interaction); Extraversion (smiles, enjoyment); Conscientiousness (embarrassment: controlled smile, averted gaze; Keltner, 1997); Type A (disgust, glaring, scowling; Chesney et al., 1997).
- **Smile recognition**: we recognize smiles by **unconsciously mimicking** them (Niedenthal et al., 2010).

> **KEY STUDY:** Heller & Haynal (1997) — Swiss study: facial expressions distinguished depressed patients who later attempted suicide from those who did not.

### Cultural Differences in Facial Expressions
- Some basic emotions show identical expressions across American and Chinese infants/adults (Albright et al., 1997), but Chinese infants showed **less variety** than American/Japanese (Camras, 1998). A remote **Namibian** tribe did not match American expressions — perceptions are **not fully universal** (Gendron et al., 2014).
- Each culture better recognizes **its own** expressions (Dailey et al., 2010). **Eastern cultures** express emotion via the **eyes**; **Western** via **eyebrows and mouth** (Jack et al., 2012).

### Computer Recognition
- A program at **30 fps** accurately recognized six basic emotions (Susskind et al., 2007). Dutch adolescents used **emoticons** more in social than task contexts, mirroring face-to-face expression (Derks et al., 2007).

## Reflections on Allport's Theory

### Criticisms
- Stimulated **little research** — concepts hard to operationalize (e.g., functional autonomy in the lab).
- **Functional autonomy**: no mechanism explained for how motives become autonomous → can't predict which childhood motives will.
- Emphasis on **uniqueness** prevents generalization; proposed **discontinuities** (child/adult, normal/abnormal) are challenged; the **idiographic** approach ran against mainstream **nomothetic** psychology and the clinical focus.

### Recognition
- Influenced humanists **Maslow** and **Rogers**; among the first to bring humanistic values to the field; his emotion-expression work was vital to **cognitive neuroscience**.
- Most enduring contributions: making personality academically respectable, and emphasizing genetic factors within a trait approach.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Allport's definition (1961):** "the dynamic organization within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine characteristic behavior and thought" — four testable components.

2. **Personal dispositions by power:** Cardinal (ruling passion; not everyone) > Central (5–10) > Secondary (inconspicuous).

3. **Functional autonomy** = adult motives independent of childhood origins. Levels: Perseverative (routine/addictive) and Propriate (self/values). Propriate principles: organizing energy, mastery/competence, propriate patterning.

4. **Seven proprium stages:** (1) Bodily self, (2) Self-identity, (3) Self-esteem (first 3 years); (4) Extension of self, (5) Self-image (4–6); (6) Self as rational coper (6–12); (7) Propriate striving (adolescence).

5. **Six healthy-adult criteria:** extended self, warm relating, emotional security, realistic perception + work, self-objectification, unifying philosophy.

6. **Allport vs. Freud:** unconscious only for neurotics; no historical determinism for healthy adults; clear normal/abnormal discontinuity.

7. **Freud–Allport meeting (~1919):** dirt-phobic boy story; Freud's "Was that little boy you?"; Allport rejected deep unconscious probing.

8. **Dissertation (1922):** *"An Experimental Study of the Traits of Personality"* — FIRST US trait research.

9. **Study of Values** (Allport, Vernon & Lindzey, 1960): Theoretical, Economic, Aesthetic, Social, Political, Religious.

10. **Jenny letters:** 300+ letters, 12 years, 36 judges, ~200 traits → 8 categories; Paige (1966) computer analysis also yielded 8.

11. **FACS** — Ekman; 43 muscles; 3,000 configurations; 7 universal emotions; used by CIA/FBI.

12. **Idiographic** (Allport) vs. **nomothetic** (mainstream) — a major criticism.

13. **Goal of life:** INCREASE tension (vs. Freud's reduction).

14. **Perseverative example:** rat keeps running the maze when well-fed.

15. **Expressive vs. coping:** expressive = spontaneous, unconscious, hard to change; coping = conscious, planned, purposeful.
