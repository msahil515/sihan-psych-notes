# Ch 7: Gordon Allport: Motivation and Personality

Gordon Allport (1897–1967) made personality an academically respectable field within scientific psychology, challenging Freudian psychoanalysis by emphasizing conscious, rational, present-oriented motivation in emotionally healthy adults. His theory centers on the uniqueness of every individual, expressed through a hierarchical trait system, the concept of functional autonomy, and a seven-stage model of self (proprium) development.

---

## Allport Brings Personality into the Classroom and the Psychology Lab

- Allport's career spanned more than **four decades**; he was the pivotal figure who moved personality into mainstream scientific psychology.
- Psychoanalytic personality theories were NOT considered part of mainstream scientific psychology before Allport.
- **1937**: Allport published *Personality: A Psychological Interpretation* — the book that formalized personality as an academic discipline; became an immediate classic.
- Two major contributions:
  1. Brought personality study into the mainstream.
  2. Formulated a trait-based theory of personality development.

### Allport Challenges Freud

Allport diverged from Freud on three fundamental points:

| Allport's Position | Freud's Position |
|---|---|
| Unconscious is important ONLY for neurotic/disturbed people; healthy adults function rationally and consciously | Unconscious forces dominate personality |
| Present and future guide behavior; NOT prisoners of childhood | Past (childhood conflicts) determines present — **historical determinism** |
| Clear distinction between normal and abnormal; abnormal = infantile functioning | Continuum between normal and abnormal |

- **Historical determinism**: Freud's view that past experiences determine present behavior — Allport explicitly rejected this for healthy adults.
- Allport quote: people are "busy leading their lives into the future, whereas psychology, for the most part, is busy tracing them into the past" (Allport, 1955, p. 51).
- Allport opposed data collection from neurotics, children, or animals to generalize about normal adults — saw NO functional similarity across these groups.

### Allport Makes Each Person Unique

- Defining emphasis: **uniqueness of personality** defined by each person's traits.
- Opposed traditional science's tendency to form universal laws or general constructs.
- Personality is not general or universal — it is particular and specific to the individual.

## The Life of Allport (1897–1967)

### Restrictions, Morality, and No Bright Colors

- Born in **Montezuma, Indiana**; youngest of four sons.
- Mother: teacher; devout religious beliefs dominated the household (no smoking, drinking, dancing, card playing, bright colors, distinctive clothing, or jewelry).
- Father: salesman turned doctor; financially desperate — smuggled drugs from Canada; later opened private practice in Indiana.
- Mother described as "on the severe side with a strong sense of right and wrong and quite strict in her moral ideals."

### Isolation and Rejection

- Too young to be playmate to older brothers; isolated from peers outside family.
- Self-described as skillful with words; NOT good at sports or games.
- "I suffered agonies on the playground." Never fit in with "the general boy assembly."
- Worked hard to be center of attention of the few friends he had.

### Inferiority

- Childhood isolation and rejection produced **inferiority feelings**; attempted to compensate by striving to excel.
- Identified strongly with eldest brother **Floyd** (noted social psychologist).
- Followed Floyd to Harvard; earned same Ph.D. in psychology.
- At age 31, Gordon described his own publications as "of no great importance and not to be confused with my more eminent brother."
- Allport may have declared adult motives independent of childhood (functional autonomy) partly as an assertion of independence from Floyd's influence.

### College Years

- Second in high school class of 100; applied to Harvard, summer 1915; "Overnight my world was remade."
- Shocked by low initial grades; doubled efforts; finished first year with straight A's.
- Strong interest in social ethics and service; volunteer work, probation officer.
- Graduated **1919** with B.A. (same day Floyd received his Ph.D.).
- Spent a year on faculty of **Robert College, Istanbul, Turkey**.
- Accepted fellowship at Harvard for graduate study in psychology.

### Allport Meets Freud

- Stopped in Vienna on return to USA (~1919–1920); sent Freud a note; was invited to visit.
- Awkward silence; Allport described a boy from the streetcar who had an **obvious fear of dirt** — insisted on changing seats so a "dirty man" wouldn't sit near him.
- Freud's response: **"Was that little boy you?"** — interpreting Allport's story as revealing his own unconscious conflicts.
- Freud perceived Allport as "neat, meticulous, orderly and punctual — possessing many characteristics associated with the compulsive personality."
- Allport was shaken; denied being the boy for the rest of his life; wrote "My single encounter with Freud was traumatic."
- Conclusion Allport drew: psychoanalysis probes the unconscious too deeply; psychology should focus on **conscious/visible motivations**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Kaufman & Bristol (2001) — used the Freud-Allport anecdote experimentally and found that presenting information in a **social and personal context** makes lectures more enjoyable and students learn and remember information better.

### Becoming a Success

- Completed Ph.D. at Harvard in **1922** (two years of graduate study).
- Dissertation: *"An Experimental Study of the Traits of Personality"* — the **first research ever conducted on personality traits in the United States**.
- Traveling fellowship; studied with noted psychologists in Germany and England.
- Returned to Harvard as instructor; offered what appears to be the **first formal American college course on personality**.
- Spent nearly **four decades** at Harvard.
- Awards: APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award, American Psychological Foundation's Gold Medal; president of the APA and the Society for the Psychological Study of Social Issues.

## The Nature of Personality

Allport reviewed approximately **50 definitions** of personality in *Pattern and Growth in Personality* before offering his own.

> **Allport's Definition (1961, p. 28):** "Personality is the dynamic organization within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine… characteristic behavior and thought."

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Dynamic organization** | Constantly changing and growing, but organized — not random |
| **Psychophysical** | Both mind and body functioning together as a unit; neither all mental nor all biological |
| **Determine** | All facets of personality activate or direct specific behaviors and thoughts |
| **Characteristic behavior and thought** | Everything we think and do is typical/unique to us — hence each person is unique |

### The Roles of Heredity and Environment

- Personality reflects BOTH heredity and environment.
- **Heredity** provides raw materials: physique, intelligence, temperament.
- **Environment** shapes, expands, or limits those raw materials.
- Genetic background responsible for the **major portion** of uniqueness.
- Infinite genetic combinations; except for identical twins, exact duplication is essentially impossible.
- No two people — not even siblings in the same household — have precisely the same environment.
- Conclusion: psychology must deal with the **individual case**, not group averages.

### Two Distinct Personalities for Two Stages of Life

- Personality is **discrete/discontinuous**: child and adult personality are fundamentally different.
- **Infant personality**: driven by primitive biological urges and reflexes.
- **Adult personality**: more psychological in nature; NOT constrained by childhood experiences.
- In a sense there are **two personalities**: one for childhood, one for adulthood.
- Emphasizes: conscious over unconscious; present/future over past; individual uniqueness; normal over abnormal.

## Personality Traits

**Definition:** Traits are **predispositions to respond, in the same or similar manner, to different kinds of stimuli** — consistent and enduring ways of reacting to the environment.

### Five Characteristics of Traits (Allport, 1937)

1. Traits are **real** — they exist within each of us; not theoretical constructs or labels.
2. Traits **determine or cause behavior** — they do not arise only in response to stimuli; they motivate us to seek appropriate stimuli.
3. Traits can be **demonstrated empirically** — observed through consistency of responses over time.
4. Traits are **interrelated** — they may overlap even though representing different characteristics (e.g., aggressiveness and hostility are distinct but related; often co-occur).
5. Traits **vary with the situation** — a person may display neatness in one context and disorderliness in another.

### Original Two Types of Traits

- **Individual traits**: unique to a person; define his or her character.
- **Common traits**: shared by members of a culture; vary across cultures; change over time as social standards change; subject to social, environmental, and cultural influences.

### Personal Dispositions (Revised Terminology)

Allport revised his terminology to avoid confusion:
- "Common traits" retained as **traits**.
- "Individual traits" renamed **personal dispositions** — traits peculiar to an individual.

**Three Levels of Personal Dispositions (by intensity/significance):**

| Type | Description | Key Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Cardinal traits** | Most pervasive and powerful; a "ruling passion" touching almost every aspect of life; NOT everyone has one; may not appear in every situation | Sadism, chauvinism |
| **Central traits** | 5–10 outstanding themes that best describe behavior; what you'd mention in a letter of recommendation | Aggressiveness, self-pity, cynicism |
| **Secondary traits** | Least influential; appear inconsistently and inconspicuously; only close friends may notice | Minor food preference, music preference |

## Motivation: What We Strive for

- The **central problem** for any personality theory is how it treats motivation.
- Allport emphasized the **present situation** — current state, not past childhood experiences.
- Whatever happened in the past is relevant ONLY if it exists as a **current motivating force**.
- **Cognitive processes** (conscious plans and intentions) are vital aspects of personality.
- What we **want** and **strive for** are keys to understanding behavior.
- Allport explained the present in terms of the **future**, not the past.

### Functional Autonomy

**Definition:** The **functional autonomy of motives** proposes that motives in the normal, mature, emotionally healthy adult are **not functionally connected** to the prior experiences in which they initially appeared. Forces that motivated us early in life become autonomous — independent of their original circumstances.

**The Tree Analogy:** A tree's development can be traced to its seed, but once fully grown the tree is self-determining — no longer functionally dependent on the seed.

**The Business Career Example:** A 25-year-old works hard for financial security. By 55, financial security is achieved, but the person continues working just as hard. The original motive (financial gain) has become an end in itself — a motive independent of its original source.

> Key implication: Adult motives CANNOT be understood by exploring childhood (contra Freud). Understanding adult motives requires investigating why people behave as they do **today**.

#### Two Levels of Functional Autonomy

**1. Perseverative Functional Autonomy**
- More **elementary** level.
- Concerns addictions and repetitive physical actions — habitual, routine, everyday behaviors.
- Behaviors continue **without external reward**; once served a purpose but no longer do.
- Too basic and low-level to be an integral part of personality.
- *Animal evidence*: A rat trained to run a maze for food will continue running the maze even when well-fed.
- *Human evidence*: Preference for routine, familiar behaviors in absence of external reinforcement.

**2. Propriate Functional Autonomy**
- More **important** than perseverative; essential to understanding adult motivation.
- Derived from **proprium** (Allport's term for the ego or self).
- Propriate motives are **unique to each individual**.
- The ego determines which motives are maintained (those that enhance self-esteem/self-image) and which are discarded.
- Direct relationship: we enjoy doing what we do well (interest follows ability).
- *Piano example*: Forced piano lessons in childhood → child becomes proficient → playing becomes necessary to self-image; original motive (fear of parental displeasure) vanishes.

#### Three Principles of Propriate Functioning (Organization)

Our propriate functioning is an organizing process maintaining sense of self — determines perception, memory, and directed thought. Governed by:

1. **Organizing the energy level**: New motives arise from necessity to consume excess energy that might otherwise be destructive. Example: upon retirement, extra energy should be directed toward new interests.

2. **Mastery and competence**: We are motivated to perform better and more efficiently — not merely adequate. Healthy adults seek to master new skills and increase competence.

3. **Propriate patterning**: Striving for consistency and integration of personality. We organize perceptions and cognitions around the self, keeping what enhances self-image and rejecting the rest.

#### What Functional Autonomy Does NOT Cover

- Reflexes, fixations, neuroses, behaviors arising from biological drives — these are not under control of functionally autonomous motives.

## Personality Development in Childhood: The Unique Self

### The Proprium

- Allport chose **proprium** for the self/ego, rejecting "self" and "ego" due to their diverse meanings in other theories.
- From "appropriate" — aspects of personality that are **distinctive** and **appropriate** to each individual's emotional life.
- The proprium unites attitudes, perceptions, and intentions; is unique to each person.

### Stages of Development

**Before any stage:** Infant has no self-consciousness. Receives sensory impressions and reacts automatically/reflexively. Allport called them **"unsocialized horrors"** — pleasure seeking, destructive, selfish, impatient, dependent, driven by reflexes to reduce tension and maximize pleasure. Little that could be called "personality."

| Stage | Age Period | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Bodily self** | Birth – ~age 3 | Infants become aware of a "bodily me"; distinguish own body from objects in environment |
| **2. Self-identity** | First 3 years | Sense of continuity of identity; child realizes they remain the same person despite changes; enhanced by learning their name |
| **3. Self-esteem** | First 3 years | Discovery that they can accomplish things independently; motivated to build, explore, manipulate; frustration at this stage leads to humiliation and anger |
| **4. Extension of self** | Ages 4–6 | Growing awareness of objects and people as belonging to oneself ("my house," "my parents," "my school") |
| **5. Self-image** | Ages 4–6 | Development of actual and ideal self-images from interaction with parents; awareness of parental expectations and extent to which they are met |
| **6. Self as a rational coper** | Ages 6–12 | Children realize reason and logic can be applied to solve everyday problems |
| **7. Propriate striving** | Adolescence | Formulation of long-range plans and goals; proprium remains incomplete until this stage |
| **Adulthood** | Adulthood | Functionally autonomous; independent of childhood motives; rational in the present; consciously creates own lifestyle |

### The Importance of the Infant–Mother Bond

- Most important of all social interactions: the **infant–mother bond** as a source of affection and security.

**If sufficient affection/security is provided:**
- Proprium develops gradually and steadily.
- Positive psychological growth.
- Childhood motives transform into autonomous propriate strivings.
- Pattern of personal dispositions forms.
- Result: mature, emotionally healthy adult.

**If childhood needs are frustrated:**
- Self does not mature properly.
- Child becomes insecure, aggressive, demanding, jealous, self-centered.
- Psychological growth stunted.
- Adult motives do NOT become functionally autonomous — remain tied to original childhood conditions.
- Traits/personal dispositions do not develop.
- Personality remains undifferentiated, as in infancy.
- Result: neurotic adult functioning at the level of childhood drives.

## The Healthy Adult Personality

The healthy personality changes from a biologically dominated infant organism to a mature psychological adult organism.

**Six Criteria for the Mature, Healthy Adult Personality:**

1. **Extended sense of self**: Extend sense of self to people and activities beyond the self.
2. **Warm relating to others**: Exhibit intimacy, compassion, and tolerance.
3. **Emotional security**: High degree of self-acceptance leads to emotional security.
4. **Realistic perception**: Realistic perception of life; develop personal skills; commit to some type of work.
5. **Self-objectification**: Sense of humor and insight into oneself (understanding of the self).
6. **Unifying philosophy of life**: A personal value philosophy directing personality toward future goals.

Meeting these six criteria = emotionally healthy and functionally autonomous; cope with the present and plan for the future without being victimized by early experiences.

## Questions about Human Nature

- **Free will vs. Determinism**: Moderate stance — free choice in deliberations about the future, but some behaviors are determined by traits/personal dispositions (once formed, difficult to change).
- **Nature vs. Nurture**: Both; genetics supplies basic physique, temperament, intelligence; learning and experience shape these raw materials.
- **Uniqueness**: Common traits show some universality, but individual traits/personal dispositions describe nature more precisely.
- **Goal of life**: NOT to reduce tension (contra Freud) but to **increase tension** — continually seek new sensations and challenges. "Getting there is more fun than being there." We constantly need new goals to maintain optimal tension.
- Overall view: **optimistic** — adults are in conscious control, rationally attend to current situations, plan for the future, and actively fashion an identity. Always in the process of **"becoming."**

## Assessment in Allport's Theory

From *Pattern and Growth in Personality* (1961): no single best technique; many techniques must be employed.

**Allport's 11 Major Methods of Personality Assessment:**
1. Constitutional and physiological diagnosis
2. Cultural setting, membership, role
3. Personal documents and case studies
4. Self-appraisal
5. Conduct analysis
6. Ratings
7. Tests and scales
8. Projective techniques
9. Depth analysis
10. Expressive behavior
11. Synoptic procedures (combining information from several sources in a synopsis)

Allport relied most heavily on: the **personal-document technique** and the **Study of Values**; also observed expressive behavior.

### The Personal-Document Technique

**Definition:** Examining diaries, autobiographies, letters, literary compositions, and other samples of a person's written or spoken records to determine the number and kinds of personality traits.

**Famous Case — Jenny:**
- Allport analyzed **more than 300 letters** written over a **12-year period** by a middle-aged woman called **"Jenny"** (Allport, 1965, 1966).
- Later revealed (Winter, 1993a): Jenny was the **mother of Allport's college roommate**; letters were written to Allport and his wife.
- **36 judges** read the letters; identified nearly **200 traits**.
- Many terms were synonymous; Allport reduced them to **8 categories**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Paige (1966) — Allport's student; performed a **computer analysis** of Jenny's letters, coding categories of words (e.g., anger, rage, hostility, aggression = trait of aggression). Yielded **8 prominent traits** consistent with Allport's original 8 categories. Allport concluded his subjective approach provided information **valid and comparable to the more objective computer analysis.**

### The Study of Values

- Developed by **Allport, Vernon, & Lindzey (1960)**: an **objective self-report assessment test**.
- Personal values are the basis of the unifying philosophy of life (criterion 6 for healthy personality).
- Values = personality traits; represent strongly held interests and motivations.
- Everyone possesses some degree of each value type; one or two will be more dominant.

**Six Types of Values:**

| Value Type | Orientation |
|---|---|
| **Theoretical** | Discovery of truth; empirical, intellectual, rational approach to life |
| **Economic** | Useful and practical |
| **Aesthetic** | Artistic experiences; form, harmony, and grace |
| **Social** | Human relationships, altruism, philanthropy |
| **Political** | Personal power, influence, and prestige in all endeavors (not just political activities) |
| **Religious** | Mystical concerns; understanding the universe as a whole |

## Research on Allport's Theory

- Allport criticized insistence that experimental and correlational methods were the ONLY legitimate ways to study personality.
- Opposed applying methods used with the emotionally disturbed (case studies, projective techniques such as TAT and Rorschach) to emotionally healthy people.
  - Case studies focus on the past — of no value for normal adults whose personality is divorced from childhood.
  - Projective techniques deal with unconscious forces that have little effect on the normal adult.
- Suggested simply **asking people to describe themselves** — reveals dominant traits directly.
- Favored **idiographic approach** (individual case study).
- Also used some **nomothetic methods** (statistical analysis of large samples) when appropriate — e.g., Study of Values.

### Expressive Behavior

**Expressive behavior** = spontaneous and seemingly purposeless behavior, usually displayed without conscious awareness, that expresses personality traits.

**Coping behavior** = consciously planned behavior determined by needs of a situation; designed for a specific purpose (usually to bring about change in the environment).

| Feature | Expressive Behavior | Coping Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Spontaneous; reflects basic personality | Planned; purposeful |
| Awareness | Without conscious awareness | Consciously planned |
| Changeability | Difficult to change | Can be altered by will |
| Purpose | No specific purpose | Specific goal-directed purpose |

**Public Speaking Example:**
- Coping level = formal planned lecture content.
- Expressive level = movements, gestures, vocal inflections, pacing, fidgeting — unplanned, reveal personality.

> **KEY STUDY (Landmark):** Allport & Vernon (1933) — gave subjects a variety of tasks; judged consistency of expressive movements over different situations. Found **high consistency in voice, handwriting, posture, and gestures**. Deduced existence of traits such as **introversion and extraversion** from these behaviors.

> **KEY STUDY:** Allport & Cantril (1934) — assessed expressive behaviors linked to specific traits from **still photographs**.

**Other Research Findings on Expressive Behavior:**

- Personality can be assessed from voice recordings, films, and videotapes.
- Facial expressions, vocal inflections, and gestures can reveal personality traits to trained observers.
- Reliable impressions of stranger's personality based solely on facial appearance and expression (Berry & Wero, 1993).
- Observers accurately assessed anxiety from watching a film of a person for **no more than 30 seconds** (Ambody & Rosenthal, 1992).

**Effects of Gender and Age:**
- Women and younger people are better at correctly recognizing emotions in facial expressions than men and older people (Sasson et al., 2010).
- Children can accurately read facial expressions as early as **age 5**; improves rapidly thereafter (Gao & Maurer, 2010).

> **KEY STUDY (Yearbook Photos):** Harker & Keltner (2001) — analyzed yearbook photographs of women college graduates. Women exhibiting positive emotional expressions at **age 21** scored higher on subjective well-being when tested at ages **27, 43, and 52**. They also reported better marriages and scored higher in affiliation, competence, and achievement orientation.

**Interpreting Facial Expressions — Clinical Findings:**
- Physically abused children (ages 8–10) more readily identified anger in pictures of female adults than non-abused controls (Pollak & Sinha, 2002).
- Adults with major depression needed **greater intensity** of facial expression to correctly identify happiness; needed **less intensity** to correctly identify sadness (Joormann & Gotlib, 2006).
- Children high in social anxiety were better at correctly interpreting facial expressions (Ale et al., 2010).
- Adults high in depression were better at recognizing sad facial expressions (Gollan et al., 2010).
- Japanese children who played more video games were better at recognizing facial expressions (Tamamiya & Jiraki, 2013).
- Close friends are far more accurate in decoding emotions (sadness, anger, happiness) than casual acquaintances (Zhang & Parmley, 2011).

### Coding Facial Expressions: FACS

> **KEY RESEARCHER: Paul Ekman** — Director, Human Interaction Laboratory, University of California, San Francisco.

**Seven Universal Facial Expressions of Emotion (Ekman):**
1. Anger
2. Contempt
3. Disgust
4. Fear
5. Sadness
6. Surprise
7. Happiness

- Analyzed **43 facial muscles** providing **3,000 different configurations**.
- Developed: **Facial Action Coding System (FACS)** — used by US police departments, CIA, and FBI to detect lying by criminal suspects and terrorists.
- Ekman named by *Time* magazine as one of the world's **100 most influential people** (2009).

**Personality Traits Revealed by Facial Expressions:**
- **Neuroticism**: anger, contempt, fear.
- **Agreeableness**: laughter, friendly social interaction expressions.
- **Extraversion**: smiles, laughter, expressions of enjoyment/amusement.
- **Conscientiousness**: expressions of embarrassment — tightly controlled smile, averted gaze, head down and away (Keltner, 1997).
- **Type A behavior**: disgust, glaring, grimacing, scowling (Chesney et al., 1997).
- Swiss study: Facial expressions distinguished depressed patients who later attempted suicide from those who did not (Heller & Haynal, 1997).
- Japanese college students high in anxiety: different facial expressions, particularly around the mouth and left side of the face (Nakamura, 2002).

**Smile Recognition:**
- We recognize smiles by **unconsciously mimicking them** — using the same facial muscles activates same brain regions as in the person smiling at us (Niedenthal et al., 2010; Zimmer, 2011).

### Cultural Differences in Facial Expressions

- American and Chinese infants/adults: some basic emotions shown by identical facial expressions in both cultures and both age groups (Albright et al., 1997; Camras et al., 1998).
- But: Chinese infants showed consistently **less variety** in facial expressive behavior than American and Japanese infants; American infants differed significantly from Chinese but not as much from Japanese (Camras, 1998).
- Remote tribe in Northwestern **Namibia** (Africa): facial expressions did NOT match American subjects — suggests perceptions of emotions are **not universal** (Gendron et al., 2014).
- 3-year-old girls: White children smiled more than mainland Chinese or Chinese-American children; maternal strictness and household composition influenced expression intensity (Camras et al., 2006).
- US vs. Japan: Each group much better at recognizing facial expressions of **their own culture** (Dailey et al., 2010); same cultural differences found with robot faces (Trovato et al., 2014).
- Digitized neutral faces: White faces judged to have angry expressions more often; Black faces judged to have happy or surprised expressions more often (Zebrowitz et al., 2010).
- Eastern vs. Western cultures: **Eastern cultures** express emotion primarily with the **eyes**; **Western cultures** use **eyebrows and mouth** (Jack et al., 2012).
- Hong Kong, Singapore, Taiwan Facebook photos: Less intense facial expressions than Americans (Huang & Park, 2013).
- Caucasian Americans vs. Asian Americans high in depression watching humorous film: Caucasian Americans exhibited **fewer smiles and facial reactions** (Chentsova-Dutton et al., 2010).

### Computer Recognition of Facial Expressions

- Computer program monitoring video at **30 frames per second** achieved high accuracy recognizing happiness, sadness, fear, disgust, anger, and surprise (Susskind et al., 2007).
- Study of Netherlands adolescents (avg. age 16): **Emoticons** used in simulated chat rooms; used more emoticons in social contexts than task-oriented contexts; pattern mirrored face-to-face emotional expression (Derks et al., 2007).

**Research on Expressive Behavior — Summary Highlights:**
- Personality traits can be assessed from facial expressions.
- Women and children are better than men and older people at reading facial expressions.
- Close friends are better than strangers at decoding facial expressions of emotion.
- As many as **seven separate emotions** can be identified in facial expressions.
- We recognize smiles by unconsciously mimicking them.
- Depressed people are better at recognizing sad expressions.
- Computers can recognize and express basic emotions.

## Reflections on Allport's Theory

### Questions and Criticisms

- Allport's theory as a whole has stimulated **little research** — concepts are difficult to translate into specific experimental terms (e.g., how do we observe functional autonomy or propriate striving in the laboratory?).
- **Functional autonomy**: Allport did not explain the mechanism by which an original motive becomes autonomous — without this, we cannot predict which childhood motives will become autonomous in adulthood.
- Emphasis on **uniqueness** makes generalization from one person to another impossible.
- Proposed **discontinuity** between child/adult, animal/human, normal/abnormal is challenged — research on these groups HAS yielded knowledge about normal adult functioning.
- Idiographic approach ran counter to mainstream **nomothetic** emphasis in contemporary psychology.
- Focus on emotionally healthy adults was at variance with prevalent clinical emphasis on neurotic/psychotic populations.

### Recognition and Influence

- Positively received; many psychologists consider his views a better basis for understanding personality than most other approaches (Piekkola, 2011).
- Emphasis on uniqueness and future goals reflected in the work of **Abraham Maslow** and **Carl Rogers** (humanistic psychologists; Chapters 9 and 10).
- Allport considered one of the **first psychologists to bring humanistic values and concerns** to the field (Jeshmaridian, 2007).
- Interest revived with current focus on personality traits — empirical support for his ideas.
- Major work on expression of emotions: vital importance in development of **cognitive neuroscience**.
- Personal-document technique has seen revival of interest (Barenbaum, 2008; Zunshine, 2010).
- Most enduring contributions:
  1. Making personality study academically respectable.
  2. Emphasizing genetic factors within a trait approach to personality.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Allport's definition of personality (1961):** "the dynamic organization within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine characteristic behavior and thought" — all four components (dynamic organization, psychophysical, determine, characteristic behavior and thought) are independently testable.

2. **Three types of personal dispositions in order of power:** Cardinal (ruling passion; not everyone has one) > Central (5–10 traits; mentioned in recommendations) > Secondary (least influential, inconspicuous, inconsistent).

3. **Functional autonomy** = adult motives are independent of their childhood origins. Two levels: Perseverative (lower — routine/addictive behaviors) and Propriate (higher — tied to self/values). Three principles of propriate functioning: organizing the energy level, mastery and competence, propriate patterning.

4. **Seven stages of proprium development:** (1) Bodily self, (2) Self-identity, (3) Self-esteem — all in first 3 years; (4) Extension of self, (5) Self-image — ages 4–6; (6) Self as rational coper — ages 6–12; (7) Propriate striving — adolescence.

5. **Allport's six criteria for the healthy adult personality:** extended self, warm relating to others, emotional security, realistic perception + skill + work commitment, self-objectification (humor + insight), unifying philosophy of life.

6. **Allport vs. Freud — three key oppositions:** (a) unconscious only relevant for neurotics; (b) no historical determinism for healthy adults; (c) clear discontinuity between normal and abnormal (not a continuum).

7. **The Freud–Allport meeting (~1919):** Allport told story of a dirt-phobic boy on a streetcar; Freud asked "Was that little boy you?" — interpreted Allport's story as revealing his own unconscious conflicts. Allport concluded psychoanalysis probes too deep; psychology should focus on conscious motivations.

8. **Allport's dissertation (1922):** *"An Experimental Study of the Traits of Personality"* — the FIRST research conducted on personality traits in the United States.

9. **Study of Values** (Allport, Vernon & Lindzey, 1960): six value types — Theoretical, Economic, Aesthetic, Social, Political, Religious. It is a nomothetic, objective self-report test; everyone has all six, but one or two dominate.

10. **Jenny letters study:** 300+ letters over 12 years; 36 judges; nearly 200 traits reduced to 8 categories; Paige (1966) computer analysis also yielded 8 traits — validated Allport's subjective approach.

11. **Facial Action Coding System (FACS)** — Paul Ekman; 43 facial muscles; 3,000 configurations; 7 universal emotions (anger, contempt, disgust, fear, sadness, surprise, happiness); used by CIA/FBI.

12. **Idiographic vs. Nomothetic:** Allport favored idiographic (individual case study); mainstream psychology uses nomothetic (large-group statistical analysis) — a major criticism of Allport.

13. **Goal of life per Allport:** to INCREASE tension (seek new sensations and challenges) — the opposite of Freud's tension reduction. "Getting there is more fun than being there."

14. **Perseverative functional autonomy — rat maze example:** A rat trained to run a maze for food continues running even when well-fed. Behaviors persevere without the original reward.

15. **Expressive vs. Coping behavior:** Expressive = spontaneous, unconscious, difficult to change, no specific purpose (e.g., gestures, vocal inflections). Coping = conscious, planned, purposeful, situation-driven (e.g., lecture content). Both are central to Allport's assessment approach.
