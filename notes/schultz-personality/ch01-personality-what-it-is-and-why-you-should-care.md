# Ch 1: Personality — What It Is and Why You Should Care

Introduces personality: etymology, definitions, shaping forces. Surveys assessment (self-report, projective, interviews, behavioral), cultural/demographic influences, social media's two-way effect, and research methods. Closes with the six philosophical questions every personality theory must answer.

---

## Take a Look at the Word

### Standard Dictionary Definitions of Personality

- "The state of being a person."
- "The characteristics and qualities that form a person's distinctive character."
- "The sum total of all the physical, mental, emotional, and social characteristics of a person."

### Adjective Check List (ACL)
- Widely used test offering **300 adjectives**; test-takers choose those that apply to themselves.
- Shows personality description needs more nuance than everyday labels.

---

### Everybody Has One

- Personality is one of a person's **most important assets**.
- Shapes success, fulfillment, health, relationships, career, and how others judge you.
- It both **restricts and expands** life options.
- We continually evaluate others and are evaluated in every new interaction.

---

### Describing Your Personality

- Casual labels ("terrific"/"terrible") fail because humans are complex and vary across situations.
- Psychologists build formal tests for precision; knowing yourself does not replace systematic measurement.

---

### How Does Personality Develop?

Theories organized by outlook on human nature:
1. **Psychoanalysis** (Freud) and its revisions
2. **Life-span approach** — birth to old age
3. **Trait theories**
4. **Psychological health theories**
5. **Predetermined behavior patterns**
6. **Cognitive-social learning theories**
7. Current 21st-century ideas

Methodological critique: earlier theorists rarely considered ethnic/cultural differences; many generalized from narrow samples (neurotic European women, American male college students).

---

### Ways of Looking at Personality

#### The Word "I" as Personality

- **Adams (1954)**: examining our intentions when we say **I** reveals personality — it sums up likes, dislikes, fears, virtues, strengths, weaknesses.

---

### How Others See Us

- **Etymology:** *personality* dates to ~**1500 CE**; from Latin **persona** = mask worn by actors.
- Implies personality = the **outward appearance**, the public face shown to others.
- Definition: personality is **the visible aspect of one's character as it impresses others**.
- Limitation: personality also includes hidden subjective, social, and emotional qualities.

---

### Stable and Predictable Characteristics

- Personality is **relatively stable and predictable**, though not rigid; it can vary with situation but resists sudden change.
- **Person vs. Situation Debate (1960s):** is behavior driven by enduring personal variables (traits, needs) or situational variables?
  - **Mischel (1968, 1973)** challenged the idea that stable traits predict behavior across situations, igniting the debate (lasted ~20 years).
  - **Resolution — Interactionist Approach:** the person/situation dichotomy "is and always was a fake" (Funder, 2001, p. 200).
  - Conclusion: **stable traits + situational aspects + their interaction** all matter.

> **KEY STUDY:** Mischel (1968) — situational variables more predictive than stable traits, launching two decades of debate. Resolved by interactionism (Funder, 2001).

---

### Unique Characteristics

- Personality implies **uniqueness**: each person has distinguishing properties.

> **Personality** = an enduring and unique cluster of characteristics that may change in response to different situations.

**Formal textbook definition:**
> **Personality** — the unique, relatively enduring internal and external aspects of a person's character that influence behavior in different situations.

## Personality and the Social Media

Three questions psychologists investigate:
1. Do we present our **real selves** online?
2. Does social media **influence/change** personality?
3. Do **different personalities** use social media differently?

---

### Are You the Same Person Online?

- Social networking creates another "face" — a possible **idealized self-image**; is it accurate or fabricated?

> **KEY STUDY:** Gosling, Gaddis, & Vazire (2007) and Back et al. (2010) — U.S. and German samples — social networking sites convey **accurate personality impressions**, as accurate as face-to-face.

> **KEY STUDY:** Blumer & Doring (2012) — large German study — many present themselves online as **more emotionally stable** than they are.

> **KEY STUDY:** Marriott & Buchanan (2014) — introverted, neurotic, lonely, socially awkward people find it **easier to express their true selves online**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Seidman (2014) — those who can express their true selves are **more active** on social media.

**Selfies:**
- More women than men send selfies.
- Excessive use can make the sender **less likeable**, reduce friendship intimacy, and reinforce appearance over behavior (Drexler, 2013; Rutledge, 2013).

> **KEY STUDY:** Goodman, Smith, Ivancevich, & Lundberg (2014) — Facebook pages with **negative content** (drunkenness, sexual display, profanity) create **false personality perceptions**; clean pages give more accurate evaluations affecting hiring/admissions.

---

### How Does the Social Media Influence Our Personality?

> **KEY STUDY:** Lam & Peng (2010) — Chinese adolescents (13-18) — excessive Internet use produced higher **anxiety and depression**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Blais et al. (2008); Huang (2010); Kross et al. (2013) — high social media use reduces **psychological well-being** and relationship quality.

> **KEY STUDY:** Gentzler et al. (2011) — U.S. college students who phoned parents had more satisfying relationships; those using social networking reported greater **loneliness, anxiety, and conflict** with parents.

> **KEY STUDY (cross-national):** Baek et al. (2013, Netherlands); Milosevic-Dordevic & Zezelj (2013, Serbia); Muusses et al. (2014, Hong Kong); Yao & Zhong (2014, Korea) — excessive use correlated with **loneliness, introversion, low self-esteem**.

- Excessive use can cause **addiction** comparable to alcohol/drug/gambling.
- Changes **brain areas linked to depression and irritability** (Mosher, 2011).

---

### How Does Our Personality Influence Our Use of Social Media?

> **KEY STUDY:** Kuo & Tang (2014); Michikyan et al. (2014); Ong et al. (2011); Panek et al. (2014); Winter et al. (2014) — **extraverts and narcissists** use Facebook more; narcissistic teens update status more often.

> **KEY STUDY:** Blackhart et al. (2014); Correa et al. (2010); Mehdizadeh (2010); Ross et al. (2009); Wilson et al. (2010) — high users tend to be more extraverted, more open, lower in self-esteem, socialization skills, conscientiousness, and emotional stability.

> **KEY STUDY (cell phones, Australia):** Bardi & Brady (2010); Butt & Phillips (2008); Walsh et al. (2011) — extraverts/strong self-identity made more calls and customized phones; **neurotics and less conscientious/shy** people texted more.

> **KEY STUDY (trolling):** Buckels, Trapnell, & Paulhus (2014); Lewis (2014) — trolls are mostly **male, average age 29**, high in **sadism**.

## The Role of Race and Gender in Shaping Personality

- All major theorists covered are **White**, European/American, and **almost all men** — a product of historical restrictions on women and minorities.
- Early theories used predominantly **White, male samples** yet were presented as universal.

> **KEY STUDY:** Barrett, Lane, Sechrest, & Schwartz (2000) — U.S. and German undergraduates/medical students — **women** showed greater **emotional complexity and intensity** in both countries.

> **KEY STUDY:** Alansari (2006) — 7,000+ students across 16 Islamic nations — women scored **higher in anxiety** than men in 11 of 16 samples.

---

## The Role of Culture in Shaping Personality

**Cross-cultural psychology** studies cultural influences on personality. Triandis & Suh (2002): cultural influences are among the most important environmental shapers of personality.

> **KEY STUDY:** Gungor et al. (2013) — Japanese emigrants to the U.S. became significantly more "American" in personality vs. those remaining in Japan.

> **KEY STUDY:** McCrae, Yi, Trapnell, Bond, & Paulhus (1998) — Chinese immigrants in Canada 10+ years scored significantly **higher in extraversion** than recent immigrants.

> **KEY STUDY:** Lee, Okazaki, & Yoo (2006) — diary study — Asian-American students reported more **negative emotions in social situations** than European-Americans.

**Cultural neuroscience:**
- Large-scale cultural differences in **brain activity and genetics** exist (Park & Huang, 2010).
- Freeman, Rule, & Ambady (2009): Japanese vs. American brain wave activity differed for the same stimuli, paralleling **submissiveness vs. dominance**.

---

### Different Cultural Beliefs about Destiny

- **Karma** (Hinduism, Buddhism; India and elsewhere): present and past actions determine future destiny.
- View of human nature: **fatalistic, deterministic** — fortune is preordained, independent of action.
- Result: **passive, resigned** personality. Contrast: American culture emphasizes **free choice, effort, initiative**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Norenzayan & Lee (2010) — substantial East-West differences in **fate attribution**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Wong, Shaw, & Ng (2010) — as Eastern cultures modernize/Westernize, fatalistic belief declines.

---

### Individualism

**Individualistic society:** personal freedom, choice, action. **Collectivist society:** group norms, role expectations, constraints.

Traits associated with individualism (vs. collectivism): greater **extraversion**, higher **self-esteem**, greater **happiness/well-being**, more **optimism**, stronger belief in **personal control**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Fischer & Boer (2011) — 400+ million people, 63 countries — **individualism** strongly linked to **positive well-being**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Chiao & Blizinsky (2010) — genetic differences: collectivistic cultures linked to **lower anxiety/depression**, individualistic to **higher**.

**Self-efficacy:** feeling adequate, efficient, competent in coping and controlling life events. U.S. students scored **higher in self-efficacy** than Japanese (Morling, Kitayama, & Miyamoto, 2002).

> **KEY STUDY:** Wong et al. (2009) — Australian students more **agreeable, conscientious, optimistic, satisfied** than Singapore students.

> **KEY STUDY:** Oishi & Diener (2001) — European-Americans gain well-being through **personal satisfaction goals**; Asian-Americans through **satisfying important others**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Kanagawa, Cross, & Markus (2001) — Americans use **positive** self-descriptions; Japanese use more **negative** terms.

> **KEY STUDY:** Kashima et al. (2004) — Australian students emphasized **individuality**; Japanese the **collective**.

---

### Child-Rearing Practices

- **Individualistic cultures** (e.g., USA): **noncoercive, democratic, permissive** parenting.
- **Collectivist cultures** (Asian, Arab): **authoritarian, restrictive, controlling** parenting.

> **KEY STUDY:** Dwairy et al. (2006) — Arab adolescents felt **stronger parental connections**, followed parental direction, and were not harmed by authoritarian parenting (unlike Western teens).

> **KEY STUDY:** Liu & Guo (2010) — Chinese mothers in Canada more **authoritarian** than non-Chinese Canadian mothers.

> **KEY STUDY:** Durgel et al. (2009) — more-assimilated Turkish mothers in Germany emphasized **individualistic goals** more than less-assimilated ones.

---

### Self-Enhancement

- **Self-enhancement**: promoting oneself aggressively and conspicuously (individualistic cultures).
- **Self-effacement**: self-criticism/humility (Asian and Nordic cultures).
- **Janteloven**: Nordic concept (Norway, Sweden, Denmark) enjoining people NOT to place personal interests above community; promotes humility.

> **KEY STUDY:** Heine, Takata, & Lehman (2000) — self-enhancement far higher in **Canadians**; self-criticism higher in **Japanese**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Heine & Renshaw (2002) — Japanese showed greater **self-criticism**, lower **self-enhancement** than Americans.

> **KEY STUDY:** Kurman (2001) — collectivist students (China/Singapore) showed greater self-criticism, lower self-enhancement than Jewish students in Israel.

> **KEY STUDY:** Church et al. (2014) — U.S., Mexico, Venezuela, China — Chinese showed **strongest self-effacement**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Silvera & Seger (2004) — Americans rated themselves **higher** on positive and **lower** on negative traits than Norwegians; self-enhancement is **culturally induced**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Rodriguez-Mosquera et al. (2000) — Spanish more concerned with **honor/family**; Dutch higher on **individualistic values**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Dufner et al. (2013) — Europe — self-enhancers rated by others as **emotionally stable, socially attractive, influential**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Brown, Brown, & Kovatch (2013) — high self-esteem/self-enhancement Americans seek **mates who share their characteristics**.

---

### A Diversity of Cultures

- Less research exists for **African and South American** nations; much non-English research is not widely available.
- Most studies use **American college students**. The textbook draws from **40+ countries** across diverse groups.

## Assessing Your Personality

**Assessment** = measuring/evaluating personality.

| Context | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Clinical psychology | Differentiate normal/abnormal; diagnose; plan therapy |
| School psychology | Uncover adjustment/learning problems |
| Industrial/organizational | Select job candidates |
| Counseling psychology | Match interests/needs to job requirements |
| Research psychology | Account for behavior; correlate traits |

---

### The Concepts of Reliability and Validity

> **Reliability** = consistency of response to an assessment device. Slight variation is normal; large variation = unreliable.

> **Validity** = the extent to which a device measures what it intends to measure. An invalid test misleads and cannot predict behavior.

Both are required for a useful test.

---

### Self-Report Personality Tests

**Self-report inventory (objective inventory):** people answer questions about their behavior and feelings across situations (symptoms, attitudes, interests, fears, values).

#### Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Created | 1943 |
| Revised | 1989 (MMPI-2) — contemporary, nonsexist language |
| Latest version | MMPI-2-RF (Restructured Form), 2008 |
| Format | True-false |
| Statements | 567 |
| Translations | 140+ languages |
| Status | World's most widely used psychological test |
| Adolescent version | MMPI-A (1992) — 478 items |

**Clinical scales measure:** gender role, defensiveness, depression, hysteria, paranoia, hypochondriasis, schizophrenia.

**Features:** items detect **deliberate faking**, carelessness, or misunderstanding. Used with adults for research, diagnosis, employee selection, and counseling.

> **KEY STUDY:** Crighton et al. (2013) — MMPI-2-RF distinguished **genuine physical pain** from **faking for disability claims**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Mason et al. (2013) — MMPI-2-RF distinguished **genuine PTSD** from faked PTSD.

> **KEY STUDY:** Hartmann & Hartmann (2014) — mentally ill people can learn online to respond so as to **hide symptoms**.

**Limitations:**
- Length (567 items) erodes interest/motivation.
- Privacy concerns, especially in hiring.
- Inappropriate for below-normal intelligence or limited reading.
- Minor wording changes alter results: 61.5% chose "think for themselves" when offered, only 4.6% gave it freely (Schwarz, 1999).
- **Social desirability bias**: socially acceptable answers, especially in job contexts (Holtgraves, 2004; McDaniel et al., 2009).

**Greatest advantage:** objective, rapid automated scoring producing complete diagnostic profiles.

---

### Online Test Administration

Advantages: (1) less time-consuming, (2) cheaper, (3) more objective scoring, (4) accepted by younger workers, (5) prevents looking ahead/changing answers.

- No significant differences between paper and online versions of most inventories (Chuah et al., 2006; Clough, 2009; Luce et al., 2007; Naus et al., 2009).
- **Anonymity effect:** people reveal more sensitive information online due to greater **anonymity/privacy**.

---

### Projective Techniques

**Projective test:** subjects **project** personal needs, fears, and values onto interpretation of an **ambiguous stimulus**.

- Inspired by **Freud's** emphasis on the **unconscious**.
- Limitation: **subjective interpretation**; low reliability/validity; low **inter-scorer reliability**.
- Despite this, widely used.

---

#### Rorschach Inkblot Technique

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Developer | **Hermann Rorschach** (1884-1922), Swiss psychiatrist |
| Year | **1921** |
| Childhood game | Klecksographie (Blotto) |
| Nickname | "Klecks" (German: blot of ink) |
| Creation method | Dropped ink blobs on paper, folded in half |
| Cards | **10 inkblots** (5 black, others color) — limited to 10 due to cost |
| Publication fate | Initial failure; Rorschach died 9 months after publication |

**Administration:** (1) cards shown one at a time, test-taker describes; (2) shown again with specific questions; (3) examiner observes gestures, reactions, attitude.

**Interpretation dimensions:** movement; human/animal figures; animate/inanimate objects; partial/whole figures.

**Comprehensive System:** developed by **Exner (1993)** — improved reliability/validity. Validity research is more supportive of **MMPI than Rorschach**; MMPI used with greater confidence for ethnic minority/diverse groups (Wood et al., 2002).

---

#### Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Developers | **Henry Murray and Christiana Morgan** (Morgan & Murray, 1935) |
| Year | **1935** |
| Format | **19 ambiguous pictures + 1 blank card** = 20 |
| Task | Tell a story: lead-up, thoughts/feelings, likely outcome |

**Interpretation factors:** kinds of relationships, motivations, contact with reality.

**Reliability/validity:** low for diagnosis. **Research utility:** high — scores need for **achievement, affiliation, power**. Still useful clinically (Gieser & Wyatt-Gieser, 2013).

---

#### Other Projective Techniques

- **Word-association test:** respond to each word with the first word that comes to mind; analyze commonplace vs. unusual responses, emotional tension, speed.
- **Sentence-completion test:** finish incomplete sentences ("My ambition is..."); highly subjective. **Rotter Incomplete Sentence Blank** offers more objective scoring.

---

### Clinical Interviews

- Talk directly to the person; investigate appearance, demeanor, expressions, posture, preoccupations, self-insight, contact with reality.
- MMPI results usually precede/guide interviews.
- **Limitation:** subjective; affected by interviewer's orientation. Most useful when supplemented by objective procedures.

---

### Behavioral Assessment

**Behavioral assessment:** an observer evaluates behavior in a situation. Accuracy improves the better/more frequently observers know the person (Connelly & Ones, 2010).

> **KEY STUDY:** Buss & Plomin (1984) — questionnaire assessing **temperaments** in same-sex twins; mothers evaluated children by observation.

---

### Thought and Experience Assessment

- **Experience sampling method (ESM) / ecological momentary assessment**: participants record thoughts/feelings/behaviors at random intervals or on signals.
- Captures real-time, real-world personality; reduces recall bias.

---

### Gender and Ethnic Issues That Affect Assessment

- Tools built on **White, American, male samples** may not be valid for other groups; cultural bias affects questions, norms, scoring.
- Example: a recent immigrant from a collectivist culture may score low on assertiveness, misread as a deficiency in an individualistic context.
- Assessment must account for **cultural context**. MMPI more supportive than Rorschach for diverse groups (Wood et al., 2002).

## Research in the Study of Personality

### The Clinical Method

- **Clinical method (case study):** in-depth study of one individual (patient/client).
- Data: therapy sessions, interviews, tests, life history, dreams, free associations.
- Used by Freud, Jung, Adler, and most early theorists.
- **Advantage:** rich, detailed data. **Limitation:** no control; poor generalizability; therapist bias.

### The Experimental Method

- **Experimental method:** manipulate an **independent variable**, measure its effect on a **dependent variable**, control others; random assignment to experimental/control conditions.
- **Advantage:** allows **cause-and-effect** conclusions; most rigorous.
- **Limitation:** artificial settings; many variables can't be manipulated (e.g., childhood trauma); ethical limits.

### Virtual Research

- Uses **Internet/virtual environments**; large-scale, cross-cultural, fast, cheap.
- **Limitation:** can't control who responds; non-representative; dishonest responding possible.

### The Correlational Method

- **Correlational method:** measures relationship between variables without manipulation.
- **Correlation coefficient:** +1.0 (perfect positive) to -1.0 (perfect negative); 0 = none.
- **Advantage:** studies non-manipulable variables in natural settings.
- **Critical limitation:** **correlation does not imply causation**.

## The Role of Theory in Personality Theories

A **theory** is a set of assumptions/propositions that explains phenomena and predicts related events.

Functions: (1) **guide research**, (2) **organize/interpret data**, (3) **generate testable predictions (hypotheses)**.

Evaluated by: fit to data, internal consistency, predictive power, **parsimony** (fewest assumptions), and **heuristic value** (generating new research).

### The Autobiographical Nature of Personality Theories

- Major theme: **personality theories are often autobiographical** — shaped by the theorist's own life, conflicts, and culture.
- Examples: Freud's neuroses; Adler's childhood illness/inferiority; Jung's mysticism.
- Implication: no theory is value-free or culture-free.

---

## Questions about Human Nature: What Are We Like?

Every theory takes a position on **six fundamental questions** — the organizing framework of the book and heavily tested.

### 1. Free Will versus Determinism

- **Free will:** we consciously choose and direct our behavior.
- **Determinism:** behavior is determined by forces beyond conscious control (drives, childhood, reinforcement, unconscious forces).
- Most theorists fall on a continuum. Freud = strong **determinism**; Rogers/Maslow = strong **free will**.

### 2. Nature or Nurture?

- **Nature (heredity):** genetic, biological, innate factors.
- **Nurture (environment):** learning, experience, culture.
- Consensus: **interactionist** — both interact.

### 3. Dependent or Independent of Childhood?

- **Childhood-dependent:** early experiences have lasting, deterministic effects (Freud).
- **Childhood-independent:** people can change; development continues lifelong (Erikson, Bandura).

### 4. Uniqueness or Universality?

- **Uniqueness:** each person is an individual configuration not reducible to general laws.
- **Universality:** shared underlying processes; general laws apply. Also touches cross-cultural universality vs. specificity.

### 5. Satisfaction or Growth?

- **Satisfaction/tension-reduction:** reduce tension, satisfy drives, seek pleasure (mechanistic) — Freud.
- **Growth/self-actualization:** develop potential (proactive, humanistic) — Maslow, Rogers.

### 6. Optimism or Pessimism?

- **Optimistic:** people are inherently good, capable of growth — humanists.
- **Pessimistic:** people driven by dark, destructive forces — Freud.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Etymology:** from Latin **persona** (actor's mask, ~1500 CE); personality = outward visible impression.

2. **Definition:** "The unique, relatively enduring internal and external aspects of a person's character that influence behavior in different situations."

3. **Person-situation debate:** started by **Mischel (1968)**; declared "fake" by **Funder (2001)**; outcome = **interactionist approach**.

4. **MMPI:** 567-item true-false; first **1943**; revised 1989 (MMPI-2); latest **MMPI-2-RF (2008)**; **140+ languages**; **most widely used** test; adolescent = **MMPI-A (1992, 478 items)**.

5. **Rorschach:** **1921**, **Hermann Rorschach** (Swiss); **10 inkblots**; ink dropped and folded; limited to 10 by cost; **Comprehensive System** by Exner (1993); lower validity than MMPI.

6. **TAT:** **1935**, **Murray and Morgan**; **19 pictures + 1 blank card**; no objective diagnostic scoring; research measures needs for achievement, affiliation, power.

7. **Reliability** = consistency; **validity** = measures what it claims. Both required.

8. **Social desirability bias:** socially acceptable rather than truthful answers; key self-report limitation.

9. **Projective tests:** grounded in **Freud's** unconscious; low inter-scorer reliability.

10. **Individualism vs. collectivism:** individualistic (USA, Australia) = higher extraversion, self-esteem, well-being, self-enhancement; collectivist (Japan, China) = self-criticism, self-effacement, group orientation. **Fischer & Boer (2011)** — 63 countries, 400+ million — individualism linked to well-being.

11. **Karma/fate attribution:** Hindu/Buddhist fatalism → passive personality; vs. American agency. East-West differences (**Norenzayan & Lee, 2010**).

12. **Janteloven:** Nordic — don't place self above community; self-effacement (**Silvera & Seger, 2004**).

13. **Self-efficacy:** U.S. students higher than Japanese (Morling et al., 2002).

14. **Six questions about human nature:** (1) free will vs. determinism; (2) nature vs. nurture; (3) childhood dependence vs. independence; (4) uniqueness vs. universality; (5) satisfaction vs. growth; (6) optimism vs. pessimism.
