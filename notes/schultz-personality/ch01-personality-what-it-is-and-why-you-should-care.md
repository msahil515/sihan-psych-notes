# Ch 1: Personality — What It Is and Why You Should Care

Chapter 1 introduces the concept of personality: its etymology, definitions, and the forces that shape it. It surveys how personality is assessed (self-report inventories, projective tests, clinical interviews, behavioral methods), how cultural and demographic factors mold personality, how social media both reflects and shapes it, and how researchers study personality. It closes by framing the six enduring philosophical questions about human nature that every personality theory must answer.

---

## Take a Look at the Word

### Standard Dictionary Definitions of Personality

Three representative definitions:
- "The state of being a person."
- "The characteristics and qualities that form a person's distinctive character."
- "The sum total of all the physical, mental, emotional, and social characteristics of a person."

### Adjective Check List (ACL)
- A widely used personality test offering **300 adjectives** that describe personality; test-takers choose those that apply to themselves.
- Illustrates that personality description requires far more nuance than everyday terms like "terrific" or "terrible."

---

### Everybody Has One

- Personality is described as one of a person's **most important assets**.
- It shapes: success, life fulfillment, health, interpersonal relationships, career prospects, and the choices others make about you.
- Personality both **restricts and expands** options in life — it can hold people back or open new opportunities.
- People simultaneously evaluate others' personalities and are evaluated by them; this happens in every new social interaction.

---

### Describing Your Personality

- Casual labels ("terrific" / "terrible" personality) are insufficient because humans are complex and change across situations and relationships.
- Psychologists develop formal tests to assess personality with greater precision.
- Key point: knowing yourself does not replace systematic measurement, especially when honesty with oneself is required.

---

### How Does Personality Develop?

The book organizes theories in terms of their outlook on human nature:
1. **Psychoanalysis** (Freud) and its extensions/revisions
2. **Life-span approach** — development from birth to old age
3. **Trait theories** — individual personality traits
4. **Psychological health theories**
5. **Predetermined behavior patterns**
6. **Cognitive-social learning theories**
7. Current 21st-century ideas

Key methodological critique: earlier theorists rarely considered ethnic and cultural differences; many generalized from narrow samples (e.g., neurotic European women, American male college students).

---

### Ways of Looking at Personality

#### The Word "I" as Personality

- Psychologist **Adams (1954)** suggested that examining our intentions when we say the word **I** reveals the meaning of personality — it sums up everything about us: likes, dislikes, fears, virtues, strengths, weaknesses.

---

### How Others See Us

- **Etymology:** The word *personality* dates to approximately **1500 CE**; derives from the Latin **persona**, meaning a mask worn by actors in a play.
- This derivation suggests personality = the **outward appearance**, the public face displayed to others.
- Definition from this view: personality is **the visible aspect of one's character as it impresses others** — the mask we wear facing the outside world.
- Limitation: personality encompasses more than external/visible characteristics; it includes subjective, social, and emotional qualities that may be hidden.

---

### Stable and Predictable Characteristics

- Personality is assumed to be **relatively stable and predictable**, though not rigid.
- Personality **can vary with the situation**, yet is generally resistant to sudden change.
- **The Person vs. Situation Debate (1960s):** controversy about whether behavior is determined by enduring personal variables (traits, needs) or situational variables.
  - **Mischel (1968, 1973)** challenged the view that stable traits predict behavior across situations, igniting the debate.
  - The debate lasted approximately 20 years.
  - **Resolution — Interactionist Approach:** The dichotomy between person effects and situation effects on behavior "is and always was a fake" (Funder, 2001, p. 200).
  - Accepted conclusion: **enduring stable personal traits + changing situational aspects + their interaction** must all be considered.

> **KEY STUDY:** Mischel (1968) — argued that situational variables are more predictive of behavior than stable traits, launching two decades of person-situation debate. Resolved by the interactionist position (Funder, 2001).

---

### Unique Characteristics

- Personality also implies **human uniqueness**: each person possesses properties that distinguish them from all others.
- Working definition combining stability and uniqueness:

> **Personality** = an enduring and unique cluster of characteristics that may change in response to different situations.

**Formal textbook definition (margin):**
> **Personality** — the unique, relatively enduring internal and external aspects of a person's character that influence behavior in different situations.

## Personality and the Social Media

Three key questions psychologists investigate regarding social media and personality:
1. Do we present our **real selves** on social media?
2. Does social media **influence or change** our personalities?
3. Do people with **different personalities** use social media in different ways?

---

### Are You the Same Person Online?

- Social networking sites (Facebook, etc.) create another "face" — a possible **idealized self-image**.
- Research question: is this face accurate or fabricated?

**Key studies on online accuracy:**

> **KEY STUDY:** Gosling, Gaddis, & Vazire (2007) and Back et al. (2010) — U.S. and German samples — found that social networking sites convey **accurate personality impressions**, at least as accurate as face-to-face interactions.

> **KEY STUDY:** Blumer & Doring (2012) — large-scale German study — found many people present themselves online as **more emotionally stable** than they actually are.

> **KEY STUDY:** Marriott & Buchanan (2014) — introverted, neurotic, lonely, and socially awkward individuals find it **easier to express their true selves online** rather than in person.

> **KEY STUDY:** Seidman (2014) — those who feel they can express their true selves are **more active** on Facebook and other social media.

**On selfies:**
- More women than men send selfies.
- Excessive selfie use can: (a) make the sender **less likeable**, (b) reduce intimacy of friendships, (c) reinforce appearance over actual behavior (Drexler, 2013; Rutledge, 2013).

**Career consequences:**
> **KEY STUDY:** Goodman, Smith, Ivancevich, & Lundberg (2014) — Facebook pages with **negative content** (drunkenness, sexual display, profanity) lead to **false perceptions** of personality; no inappropriate content produces more accurate evaluations that can affect hiring/graduate school admission.

---

### How Does the Social Media Influence Our Personality?

> **KEY STUDY:** Lam & Peng (2010) — Chinese adolescents (ages 13-18) — excessive Internet use produced significantly higher **anxiety and depression** compared to lighter users.

> **KEY STUDY:** Blais, Craig, Pepler, & Connolly (2008); Huang (2010); Kross et al. (2013) — high social media use reduces **psychological well-being** and decreases relationship quality with friends and romantic partners.

> **KEY STUDY:** Gentzler, Oberhauser, Westerman, & Nadorff (2011) — U.S. college students who communicated with parents **via phone** had more satisfying relationships; those who communicated **via social networking sites** reported greater **loneliness, anxiety, and conflict** with parents.

> **KEY STUDY (cross-national):** Baek, Bae, & Jang (2013 — Netherlands); Milosevic-Dordevic & Zezelj (2013 — Serbia); Muusses et al. (2014 — Hong Kong); Yao & Zhong (2014 — Korea) — excessive social media use correlated with greater **loneliness, introversion, and low self-esteem**.

- Excessive online use can lead to **addiction** comparable to alcohol, drug, or gambling addiction.
- Excessive online use changes **brain areas linked to depression and irritability** (Mosher, 2011).

---

### How Does Our Personality Influence Our Use of Social Media?

> **KEY STUDY (multiple):** Kuo & Tang (2014); Michikyan, Subrahmanyam, & Dennis (2014); Ong et al. (2011); Panek, Nardis, & Konrath (2014); Winter et al. (2014) — **extraverts and narcissists** are much more likely to use Facebook; more narcissistic teens update their Facebook status more frequently.

> **KEY STUDY:** Blackhart et al. (2014); Correa, Hinsley, & de Zuniga (2010); Mehdizadeh (2010); Ross et al. (2009); Wilson, Fornasier, & White (2010) — high social media users tend to be: more extraverted, more open to new experiences, lower in self-esteem and socialization skills, less conscientious, lower in emotional stability.

**Cell phone use and personality (Australia):**
> **KEY STUDY:** Bardi & Brady (2010); Butt & Phillips (2008); Walsh et al. (2011) — extraverts and those with strong self-identity spent more time making calls and changing ring tones/wallpaper; **neurotics and less conscientious/shy individuals** spent more time texting.

**Internet trolling:**
> **KEY STUDY:** Buckels, Trapnell, & Paulhus (2014); Lewis (2014) — trolls are mostly **male, average age 29**, score high in **sadism**; they take pleasure in degrading others.

## The Role of Race and Gender in Shaping Personality

- All major personality theorists covered in the textbook are **White**, of European or American heritage, and **almost all are men** — a product of historical educational and professional restrictions on women and ethnic minorities.
- Earlier theories were based on predominantly **White samples** (even "laboratory rats were white").
- The majority of subjects and patients were also **men**, yet theories were presented as universal.
- No theorist explicitly limited their theory to a specific demographic — but they often ignored gender and ethnic background.

**Gender differences — Key Studies:**

> **KEY STUDY:** Barrett, Lane, Sechrest, & Schwartz (2000) — large-scale study of U.S. and German undergraduates and medical students — **women** displayed greater **emotional complexity and intensity** than men in both countries.

> **KEY STUDY:** Alansari (2006) — 7,000+ college students across 16 Islamic nations — women scored significantly **higher in anxiety** than men in 11 of 16 samples.

---

## The Role of Culture in Shaping Personality

**Cross-cultural psychology** is the specialty area studying cultural influences on personality.

Key finding (Triandis & Suh, 2002): "Among the most important [environmental factors shaping personality] are cultural influences."

> **KEY STUDY:** Gungor et al. (2013) — Japanese emigrants to the U.S. vs. those remaining in Japan — emigrants became significantly more "American" in personality, demonstrating culture's direct effect on personality change.

> **KEY STUDY:** McCrae, Yi, Trapnell, Bond, & Paulhus (1998) — Chinese immigrants to Canada: recent immigrants showed same low introversion as Hong Kong Chinese; those living in Canada 10+ years scored significantly **higher in extraversion**, showing Western cultural influence.

> **KEY STUDY:** Lee, Okazaki, & Yoo (2006) — daily diary study — Asian-American students reported far more **negative emotions in social situations** than European-American students.

**Cultural neuroscience:**
- Large-scale cultural differences in **brain activity and genetic makeup** exist.
- Brain wave activity differs between Eastern and Western cultures in response to identical stimuli (Park & Huang, 2010).
- Freeman, Rule, & Ambady (2009): Japanese vs. American brain wave activity differed for the same visual stimuli, paralleling measured **submissiveness vs. dominance** levels.

---

### Different Cultural Beliefs about Destiny

- **Karma** (Hinduism, Buddhism — prevalent in India and other countries): consequences of present and past actions determine future destiny/fate.
- View of human nature: **fatalistic and deterministic** — fortune/misfortune are preordained, independent of personal action.
- Personality result: **passive, resigned** personality type; acceptance rather than action.
- Contrast: American culture — emphasizes **free choice, personal effort, initiative** as causes of success/failure.

> **KEY STUDY:** Norenzayan & Lee (2010) — substantial cultural differences between East and West in **fate attribution / destiny** beliefs.

> **KEY STUDY:** Wong, Shaw, & Ng (2010) — as Eastern cultures (e.g., China) **modernize and Westernize**, the fatalistic cultural belief is reduced.

---

### Individualism

**Individualistic society:** focus on personal freedom, choice, and action.

**Collectivist society:** focus on group norms, role expectations, and cultural constraints on behavior.

Personality traits associated with individualism (vs. collectivism):
- Greater **extraversion**
- Higher **self-esteem**
- Greater **happiness / subjective well-being**
- More **optimism** about the future
- Stronger belief in **personal control**

> **KEY STUDY:** Fischer & Boer (2011) — massive study of 400+ million people in 63 countries — **individualism** strongly and consistently related to **positive well-being**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Chiao & Blizinsky (2010) — genetic differences between collectivistic vs. individualistic cultures linked to **lower anxiety and depression** in collectivistic cultures, **higher** in individualistic cultures.

**Self-efficacy and culture:**
- **Self-efficacy**: the feeling of being adequate, efficient, and competent in coping with life and exerting control over life events.
- U.S. college students scored significantly **higher in self-efficacy** than Japanese college students (Morling, Kitayama, & Miyamoto, 2002).

**Australia vs. Singapore:**
> **KEY STUDY:** Wong et al. (2009) — Australian students significantly more **agreeable, conscientious, optimistic, and satisfied** with their lives than Singapore students.

**European-American vs. Asian-American well-being:**
> **KEY STUDY:** Oishi & Diener (2001) — European-American students attained well-being through **personal satisfaction goals**; Asian-American students attained well-being by **achieving goals to satisfy important others** (parents, meeting expectations of others).

> **KEY STUDY:** Kanagawa, Cross, & Markus (2001) — American students more likely to use **positive terms** to describe themselves; Japanese students more likely to use **negative terms**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Kashima et al. (2004) — Australian college students emphasized **individuality** more than Japanese students, who were more oriented toward the **collective/group**.

---

### Child-Rearing Practices

Cultural differences in parenting style:
- **Individualistic cultures** (e.g., USA): **noncoercive, democratic, permissive** child-rearing.
- **Collectivist cultures** (e.g., Asian, Arab): **authoritarian, restrictive, controlling** child-rearing.

> **KEY STUDY:** Dwairy, Achoui, Abouserie, & Farah (2006) — Arab adolescents felt **stronger connections with parents**, followed parental direction in all areas of life, and did not feel adversely affected by authoritarian parenting; concluded that authoritarian practices did not harm mental health in Arab teenagers (unlike in Western cultures).

> **KEY STUDY:** Liu & Guo (2010) — Chinese mothers in Canada were more **authoritarian** in child-rearing than non-Chinese Canadian mothers.

> **KEY STUDY:** Durgel et al. (2009) — Turkish mothers in Germany more assimilated into German culture emphasized **individualistic goals** for children more than less-assimilated Turkish mothers.

---

### Self-Enhancement

- **Self-enhancement**: the tendency to promote oneself aggressively and make oneself conspicuous.
- **Self-effacement**: the opposite; considered more aligned with Asian cultural values.
- **Janteloven**: Nordic cultural concept (Norway, Sweden, Denmark) that enjoins people NOT to place personal interests above community interests; promotes humility in others' presence.

> **KEY STUDY:** Heine, Takata, & Lehman (2000) — lab study comparing Canadian and Japanese students — self-enhancement far more prevalent in **Canadians**; self-criticism significantly more evident in **Japanese** students.

> **KEY STUDY:** Heine & Renshaw (2002) — Japanese vs. American college students: Japanese showed greater **self-criticism** and lower **self-enhancement**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Kurman (2001) — Chinese high school/college students in Singapore vs. Jewish students in Israel: collectivist culture (China/Singapore) = greater self-criticism, lower self-enhancement.

> **KEY STUDY:** Church et al. (2014) — U.S., Mexico, Venezuela, China — Chinese demonstrated the **strongest tendency toward self-effacement**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Silvera & Seger (2004) — U.S. vs. Norwegian college students — Americans rated themselves significantly **higher** on positive traits and **lower** on negative traits than Norwegians; suggests self-enhancement is **culturally induced**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Rodriguez-Mosquera, Manstead, & Fischer (2000) — Spain vs. Netherlands — Spanish people more concerned with **honor and family values**; Dutch scored higher on **individualistic values** (ambition, capability, independence).

**Positive aspects of self-enhancement:**
> **KEY STUDY:** Dufner et al. (2013) — European study — self-enhancers rated by others as **emotionally stable, socially attractive, and socially influential**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Brown, Brown, & Kovatch (2013) — American college students high in self-esteem and self-enhancement seek **mates who share their own characteristics**.

---

### A Diversity of Cultures

- Less research on personality exists for **African and South American nations** compared to English-speaking countries, Europe, and Asia.
- Much non-English research is not widely available in English-language sources.
- Limitation: majority of personality studies use **American college students** as subjects.
- The textbook draws from studies in more than **40 different countries** across diverse age groups, cultures, religions, and ethnic backgrounds.

## Assessing Your Personality

**Assessment** = measuring or evaluating personality.

Applications of personality assessment:
| Context | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Clinical psychology | Differentiate normal from abnormal; diagnose disorders; plan therapy |
| School psychology | Uncover causes of adjustment/learning problems |
| Industrial/organizational psychology | Select candidates for jobs |
| Counseling psychology | Match person's interests/needs to job requirements |
| Research psychology | Account for behavior; correlate traits with other measurements |

---

### The Concepts of Reliability and Validity

**Reliability:**
> **Reliability** = the consistency of response to an assessment device.
- A reliable test yields consistent results across administrations.
- Some slight variation across retests is normal; large variation = unreliable test.

**Validity:**
> **Validity** = the extent to which an assessment device measures what it is intended to measure.
- A valid test accurately measures its claimed construct (e.g., intelligence, anxiety).
- An invalid test produces misleading portraits and cannot predict behavior.
- Both reliability and validity are required for a test to be useful in clinical/research settings.

---

### Self-Report Personality Tests

**Self-report inventory (objective inventory):** asks people to report on themselves by answering questions about their behavior and feelings in various situations.
- Test items cover: symptoms, attitudes, interests, fears, values.
- Test-takers indicate how closely a statement describes them or how much they agree.

#### Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Created | 1943 |
| Revised | 1989 (MMPI-2) — more contemporary, nonsexist language |
| Latest version | MMPI-2-RF (Restructured Form), 2008 |
| Format | True-false |
| Number of statements | 567 |
| Translations | More than 140 languages |
| Status | World's most widely used psychological test |
| Adolescent version | MMPI-A (1992) — 478 items |

**Clinical scales measure:**
- Gender role
- Defensiveness
- Depression
- Hysteria
- Paranoia
- Hypochondriasis
- Schizophrenia

**Special features:**
- Items can detect **deliberate faking**, carelessness, or misunderstanding of instructions.
- Used with adults for: research, diagnostic tool for personality problems, employee selection, vocational and personal counseling.

**Key validity studies:**
> **KEY STUDY:** Crighton et al. (2013) — MMPI-2-RF successfully distinguished between **genuine physical pain** and **faking for disability claims**.

> **KEY STUDY:** Mason et al. (2013) — MMPI-2-RF distinguished between **genuine PTSD symptoms** and those who were faking PTSD.

> **KEY STUDY:** Hartmann & Hartmann (2014) — mentally ill individuals can learn through online instruction to respond on the MMPI so as to **hide symptoms** and appear mentally healthy.

**Limitations of self-report inventories:**
- Length (567 items) leads to loss of interest and motivation.
- Privacy concerns, especially in job application contexts.
- Not appropriate for people with below-normal intelligence or limited reading skills.
- Minor changes in wording can drastically alter results:
  - 61.5% chose "think for themselves" when it was an option; only 4.6% gave that answer when asked freely (Schwarz, 1999).
- **Social desirability bias**: test-takers give answers that appear socially acceptable, especially in job contexts.
  - Students instructed to appear good were more careful and took longer on tests (Holtgraves, 2004).
  - Most subjects can easily give false answers when asked (McDaniel et al., 2009).

**Greatest advantage:** objective, rapid scoring through automated personality assessment programs; produces complete diagnostic profiles.

---

### Online Test Administration

Advantages of computerized/online test administration:
1. Less time-consuming for applicant and organization
2. Less expensive
3. More objective scoring
4. Readily accepted by younger workforce
5. Prevents looking ahead or changing previous answers

**Key finding:** No significant differences found in responses between paper-and-pencil and online versions of most self-report inventories (Chuah et al., 2006; Clough, 2009; Luce et al., 2007; Naus et al., 2009).

**Anonymity effect:** people are significantly more likely to reveal sensitive/embarrassing information online than on paper tests given in person, due to the greater sense of **anonymity and privacy**.

---

### Projective Techniques

**Projective test:** a personality assessment device in which subjects are presumed to **project** personal needs, fears, and values onto their interpretation or description of an **ambiguous stimulus**.

- Inspired by **Freud's** emphasis on the importance of the **unconscious**.
- Theory: when presented with an ambiguous stimulus (inkblot, picture), a person projects their innermost needs, fears, and values.
- Limitation: **interpretation is subjective** — not high in reliability or validity.
- **Inter-scorer reliability** is often low (different scorers reach different conclusions).
- Despite limitations, widely used for assessment and diagnostic purposes.

---

#### Rorschach Inkblot Technique

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Developer | **Hermann Rorschach** (1884-1922), Swiss psychiatrist |
| Year developed | **1921** |
| Childhood game | Klecksographie (Blotto) — children interpreted inkblot designs |
| Nickname | "Klecks" (German: blot of ink) |
| Method of creating inkblots | Dropped blobs of ink on paper, folded paper in half |
| Number of cards | **10 inkblots** (5 black, others in color) — limited to 10 due to cost |
| Publication fate | Initially a failure — few copies sold, negative reviews; Rorschach died 9 months after publication |

**Administration procedure:**
1. Cards shown one at a time — test-taker describes what they see.
2. Cards shown a second time — psychologist asks specific questions about earlier answers.
3. Examiner observes gestures, reactions to particular inkblots, general attitude.

**Interpretation dimensions:**
- Presence of movement
- Human or animal figures
- Animate or inanimate objects
- Partial or whole figures

**Standardization — Comprehensive System:**
- Developed by **Exner (1993)**.
- Claims improved reliability and validity based on considerable research.
- No universal agreement about Rorschach's usefulness even with the Comprehensive System.
- Overall: validity research more supportive of **MMPI than Rorschach**.
- MMPI is used with greater confidence, especially for ethnic minority and diverse cultural groups (Wood et al., 2002).

---

#### Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Developers | **Henry Murray and Christiana Morgan** (Morgan & Murray, 1935) |
| Year | **1935** |
| Format | **19 ambiguous pictures** (one or more persons) + **1 blank card** = 20 total |
| Task | Tell a story: what led up to it, what people are thinking/feeling, likely outcome |

**Clinical interpretation factors:**
- Kinds of personal relationships
- Motivations of characters
- Degree of contact with reality

**Reliability/validity:** Low when used for diagnostic purposes.

**Research utility:** High — scoring systems devised to measure specific personality aspects:
- Need for **achievement**
- Need for **affiliation**
- Need for **power**

Continues to be useful in clinical practice (Gieser & Wyatt-Gieser, 2013).

---

#### Other Projective Techniques

**Word-association test:**
- A list of words is read one at a time; subject responds with the first word that comes to mind.
- Analysis: commonplace vs. unusual responses, emotional tension indicators, sexual conflict relationships, speed of response.

**Sentence-completion test:**
- Subject finishes incomplete sentences (e.g., "My ambition is ...", "What worries me is ...").
- Highly subjective interpretation.
- **Rotter Incomplete Sentence Blank**: provides more objective scoring.

---

### Clinical Interviews

- Complement formal tests by talking directly to the person being evaluated.
- Range of behaviors investigated:
  - General appearance, demeanor, and attitude
  - Facial expressions, posture, gestures
  - Preoccupations
  - Degree of self-insight
  - Level of contact with reality

**Process:** MMPI results are usually administered before/during interview sessions; psychologist focuses on problems indicated by test results.

**Limitation:** Interpretation is subjective and affected by the interviewer's theoretical orientation and personality.

**Status:** Remains widely used; most useful when supplemented by more objective procedures.

---

### Behavioral Assessment

**Behavioral assessment:** an observer evaluates a person's behavior in a given situation.

Key finding: the better observers know the people being assessed and the more frequently they interact with them, the more accurate the evaluations (Connelly & Ones, 2010).

> **KEY STUDY:** Buss & Plomin (1984) — developed a questionnaire to assess the degree of various **temperaments** present in same-sex twins; mothers of twins were asked to evaluate their children based on observation.

---

### Thought and Experience Assessment

- **Experience sampling method (ESM) / ecological momentary assessment**: participants record their thoughts, feelings, and behaviors at random intervals or in response to signals (beepers/prompts).
- Captures personality in real-world, real-time contexts.
- Reduces recall bias compared to retrospective self-report.
- Provides naturalistic data on personality expression across daily situations.

---

### Gender and Ethnic Issues That Affect Assessment

- Assessment tools developed on predominantly **White, American, and male samples** may not be valid for other groups.
- Cultural bias can affect test validity: questions, norms, and scoring standards may not generalize across cultures.
- A recent immigrant from a collectivist culture may score low on competitiveness/assertiveness, which would be misread as a personality deficiency in an individualistic assessment context.
- Key principle: personality assessment must account for the **cultural context** of the test-taker.
- MMPI validity research more supportive than Rorschach, particularly for ethnic minority and diverse cultural groups (Wood et al., 2002).

## Research in the Study of Personality

Three principal research methods are used in personality psychology:

---

### The Clinical Method

- **Clinical method (case study method):** in-depth study of a single individual, typically a patient or client in therapy.
- Data sources: therapy sessions, interviews, psychological tests, life history, dreams, free associations.
- Used by: Freud, Jung, Adler, and many other major theorists — the basis for most early personality theories.
- **Advantage:** rich, detailed data; ideal for understanding the complexity of individual lives.
- **Limitation:** lack of control; findings may not generalize beyond the individual case; subject to therapist bias.

---

### The Experimental Method

- **Experimental method:** researcher manipulates one variable (**independent variable**) and measures its effect on another (**dependent variable**), while controlling all other variables.
- Participants are randomly assigned to experimental and control conditions.
- **Advantage:** allows **cause-and-effect** conclusions; the most scientifically rigorous method.
- **Limitation:** laboratory settings may be artificial; many important personality variables cannot be experimentally manipulated (e.g., childhood trauma); ethical constraints limit what can be studied.

---

### Virtual Research

- Uses the **Internet and virtual environments** to conduct personality research.
- Allows large-scale, cross-cultural data collection rapidly and inexpensively.
- Participants can be recruited globally.
- **Limitation:** cannot control who is responding; sample may not be representative; potential for dishonest responding.

---

### The Correlational Method

- **Correlational method:** measures the relationship (correlation) between two or more variables without manipulating them.
- **Correlation coefficient:** ranges from +1.0 (perfect positive relationship) to -1.0 (perfect negative/inverse relationship); 0 = no relationship.
- **Advantage:** can study variables that cannot be manipulated; can assess relationships in natural settings.
- **Critical limitation:** **correlation does not imply causation** — a correlation between two variables does not mean one causes the other.

---

## The Role of Theory in Personality Theories

A **theory** is a set of assumptions, principles, or propositions that attempts to explain phenomena and predict related events.

Functions of a personality theory:
1. **Guide research** — points researchers toward what to investigate.
2. **Organize and interpret data** — makes sense of existing findings.
3. **Generate predictions (hypotheses)** — can be tested and either confirmed or disconfirmed.

A theory is evaluated by:
- How well it **fits the available data**
- Its **internal consistency**
- Its **predictive power**
- Its **parsimony** (explanatory power with fewest assumptions)
- Its **heuristic value** (ability to generate new research)

---

### The Autobiographical Nature of Personality Theories

- A major theme of the textbook: **personality theories are often autobiographical**.
- Theorists' own life experiences, childhood, relationships, conflicts, and cultural backgrounds shape the theories they construct.
- Examples: Freud's theory reflects his own neuroses; Adler's concepts of inferiority reflect his childhood illnesses; Jung's mysticism reflects his own experiences and family background.
- Key implication: no personality theory is value-free or culture-free — each reflects the personal and cultural lens of its creator.

## Questions about Human Nature: What Are We Like?

Every personality theory implicitly or explicitly takes a position on **six fundamental questions** about human nature. These are the organizing framework of the book and heavily tested on exams.

---

### 1. Are We in Charge of Our Lives? Free Will versus Determinism

- **Free will:** we are largely responsible for what we are; we consciously choose and direct our behavior.
- **Determinism:** our behavior is determined by forces beyond our conscious control — biological drives, early childhood experiences, environmental reinforcements, unconscious forces.
- Most theorists fall on a continuum between the two extremes.
- Example positions:
  - Freud: strong **determinism** (behavior driven by unconscious)
  - Humanistic theorists (Rogers, Maslow): strong **free will**

---

### 2. What Dominates Us? Our Inherited Nature or Our Nurturing Environment?

- **Nature (heredity):** personality is determined primarily by genetic factors, biological predispositions, and innate characteristics.
- **Nurture (environment):** personality is shaped primarily by learning, experiences, and cultural/social influences.
- Current consensus: **interactionist view** — both nature and nurture contribute; genes and environment interact.

---

### 3. Are We Dependent or Independent of Childhood?

- **Childhood-dependent view:** early experiences have lasting, deterministic effects on adult personality; the adult cannot easily overcome childhood trauma or conditioning.
- **Childhood-independent view:** people can change and are not permanently fixed by early experiences; development continues throughout life.
- Theorists like Freud heavily emphasize childhood; Erikson, Bandura, and others allow for change across the life span.

---

### 4. Is Human Nature Unique or Universal?

- **Uniqueness:** each person is fundamentally unique; personality is an individual configuration that cannot be reduced to general laws.
- **Universality:** all humans share basic underlying processes; general laws of personality apply across individuals.
- This question also touches on **cross-cultural universality** vs. cultural specificity of personality.

---

### 5. Our Life Goals: Satisfaction or Growth?

- **Satisfaction / tension-reduction:** the primary motivation is to reduce tension, satisfy biological drives, and seek pleasure — a **mechanistic, reactive** view.
- **Growth / self-actualization:** the primary motivation is to grow, develop potential, and self-actualize — a **proactive, humanistic** view.
- Example: Freud = tension-reduction; Maslow and Rogers = growth and self-actualization.

---

### 6. Our Outlook: Optimism or Pessimism?

- **Optimistic view of human nature:** people are inherently good, capable of growth, positive change, and self-determination.
- **Pessimistic view of human nature:** people are driven by dark, destructive forces (unconscious impulses, aggression); significant limitations on human potential.
- Example: Freud = pessimistic; humanistic theorists = optimistic.

## Chapter Summary

Key takeaways from Chapter 1:
1. **Personality** is defined as the unique, relatively enduring internal and external aspects of a person's character that influence behavior across situations.
2. The word derives from the Latin **persona** (actor's mask), suggesting the outward face we present to the world.
3. Personality is **stable but not rigid**; interactionist position resolves the person-situation debate.
4. Social media both **reflects and shapes** personality; excessive use linked to loneliness, depression, and lower well-being.
5. **Race, gender, and culture** profoundly shape personality; individualistic vs. collectivist cultures differ in self-enhancement, self-efficacy, child-rearing, and well-being.
6. Major **assessment methods**: self-report inventories (MMPI), projective tests (Rorschach, TAT), clinical interviews, behavioral assessment, experience sampling.
7. **Reliability and validity** are the cornerstones of good assessment.
8. Research methods: clinical, experimental, virtual, correlational.
9. Personality theories are often **autobiographical** — shaped by the theorist's own life.
10. Six questions about human nature organize all personality theories: free will vs. determinism; nature vs. nurture; childhood dependence vs. independence; uniqueness vs. universality; satisfaction vs. growth; optimism vs. pessimism.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Etymology of "personality":** derived from Latin **persona** (actor's mask, ~1500 CE); suggests personality = outward visible impression made on others.

2. **Formal textbook definition:** "The unique, relatively enduring internal and external aspects of a person's character that influence behavior in different situations."

3. **Person-situation debate:** started by **Mischel (1968)**; resolved as "fake dichotomy" by **Funder (2001)**; outcome = **interactionist approach** (traits + situation + interaction all matter).

4. **MMPI facts:** 567-item true-false test; first published **1943**; revised 1989 (MMPI-2); latest version = **MMPI-2-RF (2008)**; translated into **140+ languages**; world's **most widely used** psychological test; adolescent version = **MMPI-A (1992, 478 items)**.

5. **Rorschach facts:** developed **1921** by Swiss psychiatrist **Hermann Rorschach**; **10 inkblots**; created by dropping ink on paper and folding in half; limited to 10 cards due to cost; **Comprehensive System** by Exner (1993); lower validity than MMPI; Rorschach died 9 months after publication.

6. **TAT facts:** developed **1935** by **Henry Murray and Christiana Morgan** (Morgan & Murray, 1935); **19 ambiguous pictures + 1 blank card**; no objective scoring for diagnostic use; used in research to measure needs for achievement, affiliation, and power.

7. **Reliability vs. validity:** reliability = consistency of scores; validity = measures what it claims to measure. Both required for a good assessment tool.

8. **Social desirability bias:** test-takers give socially acceptable rather than truthful answers, especially in job-application contexts — a key limitation of self-report tests.

9. **Projective tests and the unconscious:** grounded in **Freud's** emphasis on the unconscious; ambiguous stimuli elicit projection of inner needs/fears/values; low inter-scorer reliability.

10. **Individualism vs. collectivism:** individualistic cultures (USA, Australia) = higher extraversion, self-esteem, well-being, self-enhancement; collectivist cultures (Japan, China) = greater self-criticism, self-effacement, group orientation. **Fischer & Boer (2011)** — 63 countries, 400+ million people — individualism strongly linked to positive well-being.

11. **Karma and fate attribution:** Hindu/Buddhist fatalistic view — events are preordained; produces passive, resigned personality type; contrasts with American emphasis on personal agency. East-West differences confirmed by **Norenzayan & Lee (2010)**.

12. **Janteloven:** Nordic cultural concept (Norway, Sweden, Denmark) — do not place personal interests above community; produces self-effacement and less self-enhancement than American culture. Confirmed by **Silvera & Seger (2004)**.

13. **Self-efficacy cultural difference:** U.S. college students scored significantly higher than Japanese in self-efficacy (Morling, Kitayama, & Miyamoto, 2002).

14. **Six questions about human nature** (exam-critical list):
    - (1) Free will vs. determinism
    - (2) Nature vs. nurture
    - (3) Childhood dependence vs. independence
    - (4) Uniqueness vs. universality
    - (5) Satisfaction (tension-reduction) vs. growth (self-actualization)
    - (6) Optimism vs. pessimism

15. **Autobiographical nature of personality theories:** theorists' own life experiences shape their theories; no theory is value-free or culture-free — a key critical lens for evaluating all personality theories covered in the course.
