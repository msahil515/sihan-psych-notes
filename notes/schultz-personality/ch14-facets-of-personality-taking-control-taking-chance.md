# Ch 14: Facets of Personality: Taking Control, Taking Chances, and Finding Happiness

Chapter 14 covers three limited-domain personality constructs: Rotter's locus of control (perceived source of reinforcement), Zuckerman's sensation seeking (need for novel, intense experience), and Seligman's learned helplessness, explanatory style, and positive psychology. All three are densely tested in MCQ exams for clinical psychology entrance.

---

## Julian Rotter: Locus of Control

### Biographical Background
- Julian Rotter (1916-2014), Brooklyn, New York; youngest of three brothers.
- Family lost business in the **1929 Great Depression** — a pivotal event that sparked his "lifelong concern with social injustice" and showed him how personality is affected by situational conditions.
- Discovered Freud and Adler in high school; met Alfred Adler at Brooklyn College and switched major to psychology.
- Ph.D. from **Indiana University, 1951**.
- Worked at a state mental hospital in Connecticut; U.S. Army psychologist in WWII.
- Faculty at **Ohio State University** (alongside **George Kelly**, who directed the clinical psychology program).
- Moved to **University of Connecticut at Storrs** in 1963.
- Received **APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award, 1988**.
- Died 2014; described as "one of the most eminent psychologists of the 20th century."

---

### Internal versus External Control of Reinforcement

- Rotter's approach combined external reinforcements and internal cognitive processes.
- Core finding: people differ in whether they believe rewards depend on their own actions or on outside forces.
- Named the concept **locus of control**.

**Internal locus of control**: Belief that reinforcement is brought about by one's own behavior and abilities.

**External locus of control**: Belief that reinforcement is under the control of other people, fate, or luck; feeling powerless against outside forces.

#### Behavioral Consequences

**Internals (high internal LOC):**
- Perform at a higher level on the job.
- Less susceptible to social influence attempts.
- Place higher value on their own skills.
- More alert to environmental cues.
- Report lower anxiety, higher self-esteem, greater happiness.
- Enjoy greater mental and physical health (Saric & Pahic, 2013).

**Externals (high external LOC):**
- See little value in exerting effort.
- No expectation of controlling present or future events.
- More prone to anxiety, depression, hopelessness.

---

### Assessment of Locus of Control

#### The I-E Scale (Rotter, 1966)
- **Internal-External (I-E) Scale**: **23 forced-choice item pairs**; subject selects the belief that best describes them.
- Published in *Psychological Monographs*, 80, 1966.
- Sample pairs: "Bad luck causes unhappiness" (External) vs. "Misfortunes result from one's own mistakes" (Internal).

#### Children's Nowicki-Strickland Internal-External Scale (Nowicki & Strickland, 1973)
- **40-item** scale; translated into over **24 languages**.
- Also available: adult form; cartoon version for preschool children (Nowicki & Duke, 1983).

#### Variants
- I-E Scale variants measure domain-specific LOC (e.g., dieting, weight loss, academic performance).

---

### Age and Gender Differences

- Attempts to control the environment begin in **infancy**, becoming more pronounced between **ages 8 and 14**.
- Norwegian study of 14- and 15-year-olds: **girls scored significantly higher** than boys on internal LOC (Manger & Ekeland, 2000).
- College students generally show **internal** orientation.
- People become more internally oriented with age, **peaking in middle age** (Heckhausen & Schulz, 1995).
- U.S. study (ages 60-75): internal LOC **significantly improved by cognitive training** (Wolinsky et al., 2009).
- Adult U.S. men vs. women: **no significant overall differences** on I-E Scale (DeBrabander & Boone, 1990).
- England study (ages 18-29): women had **more external** control than men (Holland, Geraghty, & Shah, 2010).
- China: **men score higher** in internal control than women (Tong & Wang, 2006).

---

### Cultural Differences

#### Ethnic Group Comparisons
- Native Africans and American-born Blacks scored **higher in external LOC** than American-born Whites.
- Botswana: Black adolescents scored higher in external LOC than U.S. White adolescents.
- In both countries: higher **socioeconomic status (SES)** correlated with higher internal control.
- Caucasian American teens valued internal LOC more than Hispanic, Asian, or African-American teens (Kang et al., 2015).
- Netherlands: **immigrants** (Moroccan, Turkish) scored much higher in external LOC than native-born Dutch.
- Hispanic immigrants to U.S. with higher internal LOC adapted better to new culture and discrimination (Llamas & Consoli, 2014).

#### Collectivist vs. Individualistic Cultural Differences
- **Asians more externally oriented** than Americans — cultural collectivism vs. individualism.
- Greater contact with Americans: Asians become more internally oriented.
- Hierarchy: Chinese in Hong Kong (most external) > Chinese Americans > European Americans (Uba, 1994).
- 18-culture comparison: collectivist cultures (e.g., China) scored higher in external control (Cheng et al., 2013).
- South Africa (individualistic) vs. Lebanon (collectivist): South Africans scored significantly higher in internal LOC (Nasser & Abouchedid, 2006).

---

### Behavioral Differences

- Chinese workers and Swedish athletes high in internal LOC: more able to **adapt to change** and scored higher on mental skills tests.
- High internal LOC at work: positively related to **job satisfaction, job commitment, and life satisfaction** (Wang, Bowling, & Eschleman, 2010).
- Korean online students: high internal academic LOC correlated with greater **persistence** in online college programs.
- Turkish college students: high internal academic LOC correlated with **less Internet addiction**.
- **Collective locus of control** in entrepreneurial teams (Austria): highest internal LOC teams performed most efficiently (Khan et al., 2014).

---

### Mental Health Differences

- Internals: less likely to have emotional problems, become alcoholics; better stress copers.
- German nurses: higher burnout and work stress correlated with **external LOC** (Schmitz et al., 2000).
- External LOC associated with: fantasies, anxiety, depression, fear of death, psychotic episodes, hopelessness, cyberbullying victimization.
- **Compulsive hoarding behavior** associated with high external LOC.
- Internals: less lonely, less likely to attempt suicide, find greater meaning in life.
- Israeli teens during 1990 **Persian Gulf War**: those higher in perceived control experienced significantly **less anxiety and fewer stress symptoms** (Zeidner, 1993).

---

### Physical Health Differences

- Internals: physically healthier, lower blood pressure, fewer heart attacks.
- Cardiac problems: cooperate better with hospital staff, released earlier, return to work sooner (Norway, Bergvik et al., 2012).
- Coronary artery bypass surgery (6 weeks and 6 months post-op): internals had **higher physical functioning** (Barry et al., 2006).
- Older cancer patients (Netherlands): internals experienced **less depression** (Aarts et al., 2015).
- Internals more likely to: wear seat belts, eat well, exercise, quit smoking, undergo cancer screenings.
- Netherlands cancer patients high in external control: showed **more trust in their oncologists**.
- Contrary finding (France, Germany, South Africa): high internal health LOC subjects took **less care of health generally**, but had fewer eating disorders and fewer HIV-risk behaviors.

---

### Developing Locus of Control in Childhood

- LOC is **learned in childhood** and related to parental behavior (Ahlin & Lobo Antunes, 2015).

**External LOC more likely in children from:**
- Homes without an adult male role model.
- Large number of siblings.
- Single-parent families headed by women.
- Children of depressed mothers with little education or income.
- Low-income families.

**Internal LOC in children whose parents:**
- Are highly supportive.
- Offer praise (positive reinforcement) for achievements.
- Are consistent, non-authoritarian in discipline.
- Encourage independence as the child grows.

---

### Reflections on Locus of Control

- Strong relationship found between Rotter's LOC and Bandura's **self-efficacy** (Judge et al., 2002).
- Key distinction:
  - **LOC**: generalized across many situations.
  - **Self-efficacy**: specific to a particular situation.
- Bandura's position: "Beliefs about whether one can produce certain actions (perceived self-efficacy) cannot, by any stretch of the imagination, be considered the same as beliefs about whether actions affect outcomes (locus of control)." (Bandura, 1997, p. 20)
- Rotter: LOC became "one of the most studied variables in psychology" (1990, p. 489).

**HIGHLIGHTS — High Internal LOC Associated With:**
- Living in wealthy, individualistic cultures
- Performing well in school
- Not becoming addicted to Internet use
- High job and life satisfaction
- Low anxiety and depression
- Better stress coping
- Physical health
- Parents who also score high in internal control

## Marvin Zuckerman: Sensation Seeking

### Biographical Background
- Marvin Zuckerman (1928-), **University of Delaware**.
- Research on sensation seeking began in the **1970s**.
- Trait has a large **hereditary component**, initially noted by Eysenck.

### Definition

> **Sensation seeking**: "A desire for varied, novel, complex, and intense sensations and experience, and the willingness to take physical, social, legal, and financial risks for the sake of such experience." (Zuckerman, 1994a, p. 27)

---

### Assessing Sensation Seeking

#### Sensation Seeking Scale (SSS)
- **40-item paper-and-pencil questionnaire** with forced-choice format.
- Validated on: volunteers for novel-experience experiments, physical-danger jobs (police, race-car drivers), drug/sex experimenters vs. those avoiding novel/risky activities.
- Now in its **5th version (Form V)**; also has a children's version.

#### Four Components of Sensation Seeking (Zuckerman, 1983; via factor analysis)

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Thrill and adventure seeking** | Desire for physical activities involving speed, danger, novelty, and defiance of gravity (parachuting, scuba diving, bungee jumping) |
| **Experience seeking** | Search for novel experiences through travel, music, art, nonconformist lifestyle |
| **Disinhibition** | Need to seek release in uninhibited social activities: risky sex, impulsiveness, aggressiveness, antisocial behaviors |
| **Boredom susceptibility** | Aversion to repetitive experiences, routine work, predictable people; restless discontent when exposed to them |

#### Good and Bad Sensation Seeking (Zuckerman, later)

| Type | Components |
|------|-----------|
| **Non-impulsive socialized sensation seeking** ("good") | Thrill and adventure seeking only |
| **Impulsive unsocialized sensation seeking** ("bad") | Disinhibition + experience seeking + boredom susceptibility + high Eysenck psychoticism |

#### Other Scales
- German: **"Need Inventory of Sensation Seeking"** (Roth & Hammelstein, 2012); may be more reliable than original SSS (Marker & Schneider, 2015).
- China: **Brief Sensation Seeking Scale** — valid for predicting reckless motorcycle riding, excessive alcohol/cigarette use, risky sexual behaviors (Chen et al., 2013).

---

### Characteristics of Sensation Seekers

#### Age Differences
- Differences appear at **very young age**: second-grade high-SS children chose scary shark video over funny bunny video (Trice, 2010).
- Sensation seeking begins to increase in **middle school** and **decreases in one's twenties**.
- **No significant differences** based on educational level (college vs. non-college).
- Zuckerman's self-report: thrill/adventure seeking and disinhibition fall rapidly with age; **experience seeking does not change with age**.

#### Gender Differences
- **Men consistently score higher** in sensation seeking and lower in impulse control (U.S. and Iran).
- Men score higher on: thrill and adventure seeking, disinhibition, boredom susceptibility.
- **Women score higher** on: **experience seeking**.
- Results replicated in U.S., England, Scotland, Japan, Thailand.

#### Racial and Cultural Differences
- **Asians scored lower** on the SSS than Western countries.
- **White subjects scored higher** in sensation seeking than non-Whites.

---

### Behavioral Differences between High and Low Sensation Seekers

#### Physical Risk Taking
- High SSS scorers: skydivers, firefighters, riot-control police, bungee jumpers, race-car drivers.
- American Motocross study: most experienced drivers had highest SSS scores (Smith et al., 2014).
- Israeli male college students: high SS more likely to do risky sports and volunteer for military combat units.

#### Three Types of Risk Takers (Levenson, 1990)

| Type | Examples | SSS Score |
|------|---------|-----------|
| Antisocial risk takers | Drug addicts, criminals | High |
| Adventurous risk takers | Mountain climbers, skydivers | High |
| Pro-social risk takers | Police, firefighters | Lower (motives are other than thrill-seeking) |

#### Drugs, Drinking, Crime, Fast Cars, Online Poker
- High SS: more likely to use/sell illicit drugs (at earlier ages), drink alcohol, shoplift, commit delinquent behavior.
- U.S. high school/college students: more likely to smoke, drive fast, have accidents, engage in frequent sex.
- Spain and France: confirmed link between high SS and speeding/drunk driving.
- French online poker players: higher sensation seeking + stronger arousal during play.

#### Risky Sexual Behaviors
- Young Black women (15-21) high in sexual sensation seeking: more intercourse, more partners, less condom use.
- Zuckerman's data: **16% of high SS** reported risky homosexual encounters vs. **7% of low SS**.
- Gay men: SS-to-risky-sex correlation so strong — high-SS males labeled **a high-risk group for AIDS** (Fisher & Misovich, 1990; Kalichman et al., 1994).

#### Other Behavioral Differences
- More likely to cross streets **against red lights** (Israel, Rosenbloom, 2006).
- Preference for **hot colors** (red, orange) over pastels.
- **More tattoos and body piercings** (German youth ages 14-24, Stirn et al., 2006).
- More likely to **cheat on academic tests** (U.S. college students).
- Chinese students: more likely to become **obsessed with computer games and Internet use**.
- Low-level U.S. workers high in SS: scored **lower on job performance** (Reio & Sanders-Reio, 2006); low-level jobs insufficiently stimulating.

---

### Personality Differences

- SSS disinhibition component correlates with Eysenck's **extraversion** and **psychoticism (asocial tendencies)**.
- High sensation seekers are **egocentrically extraverted**: concerned with others as audience/source of stimulation only, not in a dependent or nurturing way.
- Positive correlation with Jung's **extraversion** (measured by MBTI).
- **No correlation with neuroticism**; neuroses (phobias, OCD) may be related to **low** sensation seeking.
- Five-factor model (Netherlands): high SS correlated with **openness to experience, extraversion, conscientiousness**.
- HEXACO model (Netherlands): high SS correlated with **emotionality and honesty-humility**.
- High SS: high autonomy, expressive, assertive, nonconforming, risk-taking, independent of social conventions.
- Five-factor model: also positively correlated with **agreeableness** (Roberti, 2004).

---

### Cognitive Processes

- SS and intelligence: **positive but not high** correlation.
- Republic of Mauritius study: children high in SS at age 3 scored **12 points higher** on intelligence tests at age 11 (Raine et al., 2002); held for both sexes, unaffected by parental education/occupation.
- High SS: do **not earn better grades** (more time on recreation than study).
- High SS: greater capacity for **original/creative thinking** but don't express it in schoolwork.
- Attracted to **speculative, bizarre, pseudoscientific ideas**.
- Engage in **primary-process thought** (Freud's term): vivid images, dreams, daydreams that blur with reality.
- If novel experiences unavailable externally: may **look inward and create a fantasy world**.

---

### Occupational Preferences

- High SS correlated positively with **scientific interests** and negatively with **clerical interests** (Kuder Occupational Interest Survey).
- **Men high in SS**: high interest in helping professions (psychologist, physician, psychiatrist, social worker, minister); low interest in business (accountant, banker, purchasing agent).
- **Women high in SS**: high interest in **law**; low interest in **elementary school teaching**.
- Both sexes high in SS who chose helping professions preferred **risky, cutting-edge roles** (crisis intervention, paramedic, emergency response).

---

### Attitudes

- High SS: more **liberal** in political and religious attitudes.
- More likely to express **atheistic views**.
- More **permissive** attitudes toward sexual behavior.
- Low SS: more likely to be **frequent churchgoers**.
- Low SS: scored high on **authoritarianism** (rigid opinions, prejudiced attitudes).
- Low SS: **low tolerance for ambiguity** (view ambiguous ideas/situations as threats, not challenges).

---

### Heredity versus Environment

- Strong **hereditary basis** for sensation seeking (Zuckerman, 2013).
- Eysenck: **58% of sensation seeking** accounted for by genetic factors (1983).
- Zuckerman and **Sybil Eysenck** twin study: found **even greater** genetic component (Zuckerman, 1993).
- Turkey vs. Wales (14-20-year-olds): small but significant cultural differences suggest role of **learning** (Kloep et al., 2009).
- Environmental factors:
  - Low-SS parents: overly fearful/protective, inhibit adventurous behaviors.
  - High-SS parents: encourage and reinforce unusual activities.
  - Privileged schools (Sweden): high-SS/impulsive teenagers committed **fewer crimes** (Eklund & Fritzell, 2014).
  - Institutionalized children (orphanages): high SS much **less likely to get into trouble** than equally high-SS children in private homes (Loman et al., 2014).
- **Birth order**: early studies found first-borns and only-borns higher in SS; later English study found **no correlation** (Crozier & Birdsea, 2003).

**HIGHLIGHTS — High Sensation Seekers Tend To:**
- Score high in adventure-seeking, disinhibition, boredom susceptibility
- Live in Westernized cultures
- Take risks, use drugs/alcohol, drive fast, engage in frequent sex
- Spend a lot of time on computer games
- Be extraverted, open to new experiences, conscientious, emotional
- Be interested in helping professions (not business)
- Have liberal political and religious views
- Be the only child or first-born
- Have more body piercings and tattoos

#### Self-descriptions by Sensation Seekers

| High Sensation Seekers | Low Sensation Seekers |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| Enthusiastic | Frightened |
| Playful | Panicky |
| Adventurous | Tense |
| Elated | Nervous |
| Imaginative | Shaky |
| Daring | Fearful |
| Zany | Worried |
| Mischievous | Upset |

---

### Reflections on Sensation Seeking

- Research spans behavioral, cognitive, personality, and physiological variables.
- Emphasis on **heritability** distinguishes Zuckerman's work from behavioral and social-learning approaches.
- Commonsense appeal: people readily identify their own and others' sensation-seeking levels.

## Martin E. P. Seligman: Learned Helplessness and the Optimistic/Pessimistic Explanatory Style

### Biographical Background
- Martin Seligman (1942-), **University of Pennsylvania**.
- Began research on **learned helplessness** in the **mid-1960s** as a graduate student.
- First witnessed the phenomenon on his **first day as a graduate student** in an experiment on dogs.
- Later led the **happiness and positive psychology** movement.

---

### A Shocking Finding: The Original Observation

- Dogs were first conditioned (Pavlovian classical conditioning) to associate a **high-pitched tone with electric shock**.
- Second phase: dogs placed in a **two-compartment shuttle box** with a low barrier; shock delivered through floor.
- Expected: dogs jump over barrier to escape.
- **Actual finding**: dogs conditioned to unavoidable shock in Phase 1 **lay down, whimpered, and made no effort to escape** in Phase 2 — even though escape was possible.
- Seligman: dogs had learned they were **helpless** in Phase 1; generalized to Phase 2.

> "I was stunned by the implications. If dogs could learn something as complex as the futility of their actions, here was an analogy to human helplessness." — Seligman (1990, p. 20)

---

### Definition

> **Learned helplessness**: A condition resulting from the **perception that we have no control over our environment** — that there is nothing we can do to change our situation (Seligman).

---

### Early Research

#### KEY STUDY: Dogs — Overmier & Seligman (1967); Seligman & Maier (1967)

| Group | Procedure | Outcome |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Control (no prior shock) | Placed directly in shuttle box; shock through floor | Raced around, accidentally leapt barrier; learned to escape faster each trial |
| Experimental (prior inescapable shock) | Exposed to inescapable harness shocks first; then placed in shuttle box | Raced ~30 seconds, then lay down whimpering; **never learned to escape** even when lured with food |

#### KEY STUDY: Humans — Hiroto (1974)

| Group | Procedure | Outcome |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Experimental | Loud irritating noise; pressing buttons (no correct sequence = inescapable) | Could not learn simple hand-move response to stop noise in Phase 2 |
| Control | Noise stoppable by learning a simple button sequence | Rapidly learned hand-movement in Phase 2 |

- Learned helplessness also occurs when humans **observe helpless models** (especially when they identify with them) — "If the models can't do anything about this, then neither can I" (Chamber & Hammonds, 2014).
- **Meta-analysis of 132 studies**: effects of inescapable shock were even **stronger in humans than in animals** (Villanova & Peterson, 1991).
- Documented across: adult men and women, college students, adolescents, children, elderly, unemployed women, psychiatric patients.
- Occurs in everyday settings: noise from neighbors, cyberbullying on social media.

---

### Learned Helplessness as Torture

- CIA and counterterrorism organizations applied learned helplessness techniques to prisoners:
  - Placed in coffin for days.
  - Waterboarded 83 times over 17 days.
  - Stripped, placed in solitary confinement with constant loud rock music.
- Prisoners became **compliant and passive** — same symptoms as laboratory subjects.
- Seligman, when he learned of this use years later, stated he was **"grieved and horrified"** (McCoy, 2014).

---

### Learned Helplessness and Emotional Health

- Cancer patients (ages 29-80) with **highest perception of control** were better adjusted, even if physically debilitated.
- People can **learn to increase their sense of control**:

**KEY STUDY: Dental anxiety / stress inoculation (Law, Logan, & Baron, 1994)**
- Patients with low perceived dental control but strong desire for control benefited most from **stress inoculation training video**.
- They reported more perceived control and **less pain** during actual dental treatment.

---

### Animal Research on Learned Helplessness and Physical Health

#### KEY STUDY: Rats and Tumor Rejection (Visintainer, Volpicelli, & Seligman, 1982)

| Group | Condition | Tumor Rejection Rate |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| Control | No electric shock | **50%** (baseline expected) |
| Escapable shock | Shock from which they could escape (controllable) | **70%** rejected tumors |
| Inescapable shock (helpless) | Shock from which they could not escape | **Only 27%** rejected tumors |

- Young rats taught helplessness: as adults, **majority failed to reject tumors**.
- Young rats taught control: as adults, **majority rejected tumors**.
- Seligman: "**Childhood mastery immunized, and early helplessness put adult rats at risk for cancer**" (1990, p. 170).

#### KEY STUDY: Immune System (Maier, Laudenslager, & Ryan, 1985)
- Inescapable shock weakened the **immune system** of rats.
- In helpless rats:
  - **T-cells** no longer multiplied in response to specific invaders.
  - **NK (natural killer) cells** lost ability to destroy infections.
- Provides physiological explanation for tumor rejection failure in helpless rats.

#### Gender Difference in Rats (Dalla et al., 2008)
- Most **male rats** exposed to uncontrollable stress **never learn to escape**.
- Most **female rats do learn to escape**.
- Learned helplessness is **easier to establish in males** than females.
- Females less subject to the dangerous physiological changes found in the earlier study.

---

### Explanatory Style: Optimism and Pessimism

- Seligman expanded learned helplessness into **explanatory style**: how we explain our lack of control to ourselves.
- **Optimistic explanatory style**: prevents helplessness.
- **Pessimistic explanatory style**: spreads helplessness to all facets of life.

> **Explanatory style**: A way of explaining to ourselves our relative lack of control over our environment.

> "Optimists are people who expect good things to happen to them; pessimists are people who expect bad things to happen to them." (Carver & Scheier, 2002, p. 231)

- Optimists: build better social networks; better cope with physical pain (Carver & Scheier, 2014).

#### Physical Health and Explanatory Style
- Pessimists believe actions have little consequence; **unlikely to prevent illness** (smoking, diet, exercise, medical attention).
- Young adults study: optimists less likely to get sick; when sick, more likely to take responsibility for care (Peterson, Maier, & Seligman, 1993).
- College undergraduates: **pessimists had twice as many infectious illnesses** over one year compared to optimists.
- Finland: optimists recovered positive attitude **more quickly** after severe illness or death of family member (Kivimaki et al., 2005).
- Optimists: better cope with health problems, recover faster from medical procedures, manage stress better.

---

### Development of Learned Helplessness in Childhood

- Roots of optimistic vs. pessimistic explanatory style lie in **childhood experiences**.
- Parents who are critical, emphasize failure, and attribute bad events to stable/global causes raise more **pessimistic** children.
- Parents who model optimism and explain adversity as temporary and specific raise more **optimistic** children.
- Seligman: childhood mastery immunizes against adult helplessness; early helplessness creates adult vulnerability.

---

### Reflections on Learned Helplessness

- Applied to understanding depression, stress, health, education, therapy.
- Criticized for oversimplification: not all uncontrollable situations produce helplessness in all people.
- **Explanatory style reformulation** added cognitive nuance to the original behavioral model.

## Martin Seligman: Positive Psychology

### The Life of Seligman (1942-)
- Served as **president of the American Psychological Association, 1998**.
- Used his presidential address to launch the **positive psychology movement**.
- Redirected psychology from primarily studying pathology to studying what makes life worth living.

### Definition

> **Positive psychology**: The scientific study of positive human functioning — strengths, virtues, and what enables individuals, communities, and institutions to thrive.

Three pillars:
1. Positive experiences
2. Positive individual traits (character strengths)
3. Positive institutions

---

### Characteristics and Causes of Happiness

#### Money
- Beyond a basic comfort level, **more money does not buy significantly more happiness**.
- Lottery winners return to baseline happiness relatively quickly.
- **Relative income** (compared to others) matters more than absolute income.

#### Physical Attractiveness
- **No significant relationship** between physical attractiveness and happiness.

#### Health
- **Objective health** does not strongly predict happiness.
- **Subjective perception of health** is more important.
- People adapt to chronic illness via **hedonic adaptation**.

#### Age

##### Adolescence
- Adolescents experience significant emotional turbulence; not consistently the least or most happy period.

##### Older People
- Contrary to stereotypes, older people are often **among the happiest age groups**.
- Older adults regulate emotions better and focus on positive experiences.
- Well-being is often **U-shaped**: high in youth, lower in midlife, higher again in old age.

#### Marriage and Social Support
- **Marriage** is one of the strongest predictors of happiness; married people report higher happiness than unmarried.
- **Social support** (close relationships, friends, community ties) is a key predictor.

#### Culture
- **Individualistic cultures** (U.S., Northern Europe) tend to report higher subjective well-being than collectivist cultures.
- Cultural norms about expressing positive vs. negative emotions influence self-reports.

#### Geography: Where You Live Is Important
- Country-level factors (democracy, rule of law, civil liberties, wealth) predict national happiness.
- Within nations: safety, walkability, community trust affect happiness.

#### Race and Ethnicity
- In the U.S., racial/ethnic minorities often report lower happiness, partly due to discrimination and socioeconomic disparities.

#### Personality
- **Personality is the strongest predictor of happiness**.
- Traits most associated with happiness: **extraversion** and **emotional stability (low neuroticism)**.
- **Genetic set point**: each person has a baseline happiness level largely determined by genetics; circumstances can move one above or below it temporarily.

#### Self-Efficacy and Internal Locus of Control
- Both positively correlated with happiness.
- Feeling in control of one's life is a robust predictor of well-being.

#### Thinking Positively
- Optimistic thinking and positive reappraisal promote well-being.
- Seligman's **learned optimism** techniques can be taught.

#### Goals
- Having meaningful goals and making progress toward them is strongly associated with happiness.
- **Intrinsically motivated goals** predict more well-being than extrinsically motivated goals (money, approval).

#### Happiness and Success
- Happiness is not just a result of success: **happiness tends to cause success**.
- Happy people are more creative, productive, socially connected, and healthy.
- Relationship is **bidirectional**.

#### Internet Use
- Heavy social media/Internet use linked to lower happiness in some studies, particularly adolescents.
- **Passive consumption** (scrolling) associated with lower well-being; **active use** (communication) less harmful or neutral.

---

### Different Kinds of Happiness: Meaning and Flourishing

#### Three Routes to Happiness (Original Model)

| Route | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Pleasant life** (*vita voluptuosa*) | Maximizing positive emotions and pleasures; hedonic happiness |
| **Engaged life** (*vita activa*) | Flow and engagement; using character strengths; eudaimonic |
| **Meaningful life** (*vita contemplativa*) | Belonging to and serving something larger than oneself |

#### PERMA Model (Later Expansion — Flourishing)

Seligman argued that happiness/well-being is better captured by **PERMA**:

| Letter | Element | Description |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **P** | Positive Emotions | Pleasure, joy, gratitude, serenity, love |
| **E** | Engagement | Flow; deep absorption using character strengths |
| **R** | Relationships | Positive social connections |
| **M** | Meaning | Belonging to and serving something larger than oneself |
| **A** | Accomplishment/Achievement | Pursuing achievement and mastery for its own sake |

> **Flourishing**: The highest form of well-being; achieved when PERMA elements are strong.

---

### Comment

- Positive psychology generated substantial empirical research and practical applications in education, therapy, organizations, and public policy.
- Critics: early positive psychology overemphasized the positive and neglected the role of negative emotions and adversity.
- Now central to contemporary clinical and counseling psychology.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Locus of control** | Perceived source (internal vs. external) of reinforcements; proposed by Rotter |
| **Internal locus of control** | Belief that one's own behavior and abilities determine reinforcement received |
| **External locus of control** | Belief that fate, luck, or other people control one's reinforcements |
| **I-E Scale** | Rotter's 23-item forced-choice questionnaire measuring internal vs. external control (1966) |
| **Sensation seeking** | Zuckerman's trait: desire for varied, novel, complex, intense sensations and willingness to take risks |
| **Sensation Seeking Scale (SSS)** | 40-item Zuckerman questionnaire assessing sensation seeking; currently Form V |
| **Thrill and adventure seeking** | SSS component: desire for physically dangerous, novel activities |
| **Experience seeking** | SSS component: seeking novelty through travel, art, music, nonconformist lifestyle |
| **Disinhibition** | SSS component: seeking release through uninhibited social activities |
| **Boredom susceptibility** | SSS component: aversion to routine and predictable experiences |
| **Non-impulsive socialized sensation seeking** | "Good" type; thrill and adventure seeking only |
| **Impulsive unsocialized sensation seeking** | "Bad" type; disinhibition + experience seeking + boredom susceptibility + high psychoticism |
| **Egocentric extraversion** | High SS social style: relating to others only as audience or stimulation source |
| **Learned helplessness** | Seligman: condition resulting from perception of having no control over one's environment |
| **Explanatory style** | How one explains one's lack of control; optimistic prevents helplessness; pessimistic spreads it |
| **Optimistic explanatory style** | Explains adversity as temporary, specific, external; prevents learned helplessness |
| **Pessimistic explanatory style** | Explains adversity as permanent, global, internal; spreads helplessness to all facets of life |
| **Positive psychology** | Seligman's movement: scientific study of positive human functioning, strengths, and well-being |
| **PERMA model** | Seligman's model of flourishing: Positive emotions, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment |
| **Flourishing** | Highest form of well-being; achieved when PERMA elements are strong |
| **Pleasant life** | Happiness via maximizing positive emotions and pleasures (hedonic) |
| **Engaged life** | Happiness via flow and use of character strengths (eudaimonic) |
| **Meaningful life** | Happiness via serving something larger than oneself |
| **Hedonic adaptation** | Tendency to return to baseline happiness after positive or negative life changes |
| **Primary-process thought** | Freud's term (used by Zuckerman): vivid images/dreams blurring with reality; found in high SS |
| **NK cells** | Natural killer cells; suppressed by learned helplessness (inescapable shock) in rats |
| **T-cells** | Immune cells; fail to multiply in learned-helpless rats |
| **Stress inoculation training** | Intervention increasing sense of control and reducing pain/anxiety |
| **Collective locus of control** | LOC at the group level; high-internal teams perform most efficiently |
| **Shuttle box** | Two-compartment apparatus used in learned helplessness research |
| **Genetic set point** | Baseline happiness level largely determined by genetics |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Rotter's I-E Scale has 23 forced-choice pairs** (not 40 — the Nowicki-Strickland Children's scale has 40 items).

2. **Four components of sensation seeking** (Zuckerman, 1983, via factor analysis): Thrill/Adventure Seeking, Experience Seeking, Disinhibition, Boredom Susceptibility.

3. **"Bad" sensation seeking** = impulsive unsocialized = disinhibition + experience seeking + boredom susceptibility + high **psychoticism** (Eysenck scale).

4. Gender: men score higher on thrill/adventure, disinhibition, boredom susceptibility; **women score higher on experience seeking**.

5. **Seligman first observed learned helplessness on his first day as a graduate student** watching dogs in a conditioning experiment.

6. **Key rat tumor study** (Visintainer et al., 1982): escapable shock = **70%** tumor rejection; no shock = **50%**; inescapable shock (helpless) = **only 27%** tumor rejection.

7. **Learned helplessness is easier to establish in male rats than female rats** (Dalla et al., 2008) — females more likely to learn to escape.

8. **Pessimists had twice as many infectious illnesses** as optimists over one year (college undergraduates study).

9. **PERMA model**: Positive emotions, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment — Seligman's model of **flourishing**.

10. LOC **peaks in internal direction in middle age** (Heckhausen & Schulz, 1995); it **develops in childhood** based on parenting practices and SES.

11. **Collectivist cultures** (Asia, Lebanon) score higher in **external** LOC; **individualistic cultures** (U.S., South Africa) score higher in **internal** LOC.

12. **Eysenck estimated 58% of sensation seeking is genetic** (1983); Zuckerman and Sybil Eysenck twin study found an even higher genetic component.

13. **Three routes to happiness** (Seligman): pleasant life (hedonic/positive emotions), engaged life (flow/strengths), meaningful life (serving a larger purpose).

14. **Personality** (especially extraversion and low neuroticism/emotional stability) is the **strongest predictor of happiness** — not money, attractiveness, or objective health.

15. **LOC vs. self-efficacy**: LOC is generalized across situations; self-efficacy is specific to a particular situation. Bandura explicitly rejected equivalence between the two constructs (Bandura, 1997, p. 20).
