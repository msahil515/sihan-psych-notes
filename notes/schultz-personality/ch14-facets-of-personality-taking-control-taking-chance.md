# Ch 14: Facets of Personality: Taking Control, Taking Chances, and Finding Happiness

Three limited-domain constructs: Rotter's **locus of control**, Zuckerman's **sensation seeking**, and Seligman's **learned helplessness, explanatory style, and positive psychology** — all densely tested in clinical-psychology entrance MCQs.

---

## Julian Rotter: Locus of Control

### Background
- Julian Rotter (1916–2014), Brooklyn; family lost its business in the **1929 Depression** (sparked his concern with social injustice). Met **Adler** at Brooklyn College. **Ph.D. Indiana, 1951**; faculty at **Ohio State** (with **George Kelly**), then **University of Connecticut** (1963). APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award (**1988**).

### Internal vs. External Control of Reinforcement
- People differ in whether they believe rewards depend on their own actions or outside forces — **locus of control**.
- **Internal locus of control**: reinforcement results from one's own behavior/abilities.
- **External locus of control**: reinforcement is controlled by others, fate, or luck.

**Internals**: higher job performance, less susceptible to influence, more alert to cues, lower anxiety, higher self-esteem, better health. **Externals**: see effort as futile, more anxiety/depression/hopelessness.

### Assessment
- **I-E Scale** (Rotter, 1966): **23 forced-choice item pairs** (e.g., "bad luck causes unhappiness" [E] vs. "misfortunes result from one's own mistakes" [I]).
- **Children's Nowicki-Strickland I-E Scale** (1973): **40 items**, **24+ languages**; cartoon version for preschoolers (Nowicki & Duke, 1983).

### Age, Gender, Culture
- Control efforts begin in infancy, pronounced ages **8–14**; LOC becomes more internal with age, **peaking in middle age** (Heckhausen & Schulz, 1995); cognitive training improves internal LOC in older adults (Wolinsky et al., 2009).
- Gender results mixed: girls higher in Norway (Manger & Ekeland, 2000); no overall U.S. difference (DeBrabander & Boone, 1990); men higher in China (Tong & Wang, 2006).
- **Culture**: collectivist cultures (Asia, Lebanon) score higher in **external** LOC; individualistic cultures (U.S., South Africa) higher in **internal** LOC; higher **SES** → more internal control. Immigrants higher in external LOC; higher internal LOC eases adaptation (Llamas & Consoli, 2014).

### Behavioral, Mental, Physical Differences
- Internals: better adapt to change; higher job/life satisfaction (Wang et al., 2010); more persistence; less Internet addiction.
- Internals: fewer emotional problems, less likely alcoholics, better stress coping, less lonely, less suicidal; externals linked to burnout (Schmitz et al., 2000), anxiety, depression, hopelessness, compulsive hoarding. Israeli teens with higher control had less Gulf War anxiety (Zeidner, 1993).
- Internals: physically healthier, lower blood pressure, fewer heart attacks; recover better from cardiac surgery (Barry et al., 2006); more likely to wear seat belts, exercise, get screenings.

### Development in Childhood
- LOC is **learned in childhood**. **External** LOC: homes without a male role model, many siblings, single-mother/low-income/depressed-mother families. **Internal** LOC: supportive parents who praise achievement, are consistent and non-authoritarian, and encourage independence.

### Reflections
- LOC relates to Bandura's **self-efficacy** but is distinct: **LOC is generalized**; **self-efficacy is situation-specific** (Bandura, 1997). One of "the most studied variables in psychology."

> **High internal LOC** ↔ wealthy/individualistic cultures, school success, no Internet addiction, high job/life satisfaction, low anxiety/depression, better coping, health, and parents who are also internal.

## Marvin Zuckerman: Sensation Seeking

- Marvin Zuckerman (1928–), University of Delaware; research began in the **1970s**; strong **hereditary** component (noted by Eysenck).

> **Sensation seeking**: "A desire for varied, novel, complex, and intense sensations and experience, and the willingness to take physical, social, legal, and financial risks for the sake of such experience" (Zuckerman, 1994).

### Assessment
- **Sensation Seeking Scale (SSS)**: **40-item** forced-choice questionnaire; now **Form V**; children's version exists.

**Four components** (factor analysis, 1983):

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Thrill and adventure seeking** | Speed, danger, novelty (parachuting, diving) |
| **Experience seeking** | Novelty via travel, art, nonconformist lifestyle |
| **Disinhibition** | Release via uninhibited social activities, risky sex |
| **Boredom susceptibility** | Aversion to routine and predictability |

- **"Good"** = non-impulsive socialized (thrill/adventure only); **"Bad"** = impulsive unsocialized (disinhibition + experience seeking + boredom susceptibility + high **psychoticism**).

### Characteristics
- **Age**: appears young (high-SS 2nd graders chose a shark video; Trice, 2010); rises in middle school, **decreases in the twenties**; experience seeking doesn't change with age.
- **Gender**: **men higher** on thrill/adventure, disinhibition, boredom susceptibility; **women higher on experience seeking** (replicated across countries).
- **Culture/race**: Asians scored lower; White subjects higher than non-Whites.

### Behavioral Differences
- High SS: skydivers, firefighters, race-car drivers; more drug use, drinking, fast/drunk driving, frequent/risky sex (high-SS gay men labeled a **high-risk group for AIDS**); more tattoos/piercings; cheat more on tests; obsessed with computer games.
- **Levenson (1990)** three risk-taker types: antisocial (criminals, high), adventurous (climbers, high), pro-social (police/firefighters, lower — non-thrill motives).

### Personality and Cognition
- Disinhibition correlates with Eysenck's **extraversion** and **psychoticism**; high SS are **egocentrically extraverted**; **no correlation with neuroticism** (neuroses may relate to **low** SS). Five-factor: SS correlates with **openness, extraversion, conscientiousness, agreeableness**.
- SS–intelligence: **positive but not high**; high-SS 3-year-olds scored 12 IQ points higher at 11 (Raine et al., 2002); greater **creative** capacity but not better grades; engage in **primary-process thought**; may build a fantasy world if novelty is unavailable.

### Occupation and Attitudes
- High SS: scientific interests, low clerical; men → helping professions (preferring risky roles), low business; women → law, low elementary teaching.
- High SS: more **liberal**, atheistic, sexually permissive. Low SS: frequent churchgoers, high **authoritarianism**, low tolerance for ambiguity.

### Heredity vs. Environment
- Strong **hereditary basis**: Eysenck estimated **58% genetic** (1983); Zuckerman & Sybil Eysenck twin study found an even larger component. Environment matters too: low-SS parents inhibit, high-SS parents encourage; cultural differences (Turkey vs. Wales) suggest learning. **Birth order**: early studies found first-borns/only-borns higher; later study found no correlation.

> **High sensation seekers**: adventure-seeking, disinhibited; Westernized cultures; risk/drugs/fast driving/frequent sex; extraverted, open, conscientious; helping professions; liberal views; more tattoos/piercings.

## Martin E. P. Seligman: Learned Helplessness and Explanatory Style

- Martin Seligman (1942–), University of Pennsylvania; began **learned helplessness** research in the **mid-1960s** (witnessed it on his **first day as a graduate student**); later founded positive psychology.

### The Original Observation
- Dogs conditioned to associate a tone with shock; then placed in a **two-compartment shuttle box** where shock was escapable. Dogs previously given **unavoidable** shock **lay down and made no effort to escape** — they had learned they were **helpless** and generalized it.

> **Learned helplessness**: a condition resulting from the **perception that we have no control** over our environment.

### Early Research

> **KEY STUDY: Dogs (Overmier & Seligman, 1967; Seligman & Maier, 1967)** — control dogs learned to escape; dogs given prior **inescapable** shock **never learned to escape**, even when lured with food.

> **KEY STUDY: Humans (Hiroto, 1974)** — subjects exposed to inescapable noise later **could not learn** a simple response to stop noise; controls learned rapidly.

- Also occurs from **observing helpless models** (Chamber & Hammonds, 2014). Meta-analysis of **132 studies**: effects even **stronger in humans** than animals (Villanova & Peterson, 1991). Occurs across all ages and in everyday settings (cyberbullying).
- **Misuse as torture**: CIA used learned-helplessness techniques on prisoners; Seligman was "grieved and horrified" (McCoy, 2014).

### Emotional and Physical Health
- Cancer patients with highest perceived control adjusted best. **Stress inoculation training** can increase perceived control and reduce pain (Law, Logan, & Baron, 1994).

> **KEY STUDY: Rat tumors (Visintainer, Volpicelli, & Seligman, 1982)** — tumor rejection: **escapable shock 70%**, **no shock 50%**, **inescapable (helpless) only 27%**. "Childhood mastery immunized, and early helplessness put adult rats at risk for cancer."

> **KEY STUDY: Immune system (Maier, Laudenslager, & Ryan, 1985)** — inescapable shock weakened rats' immune systems (**T-cells** stopped multiplying; **NK cells** lost ability to destroy infections).

- **Gender (Dalla et al., 2008)**: most **male** rats never learn to escape; most **female** rats do — learned helplessness is **easier to establish in males**.

### Explanatory Style
> **Explanatory style**: how we explain our lack of control. **Optimistic** (adversity = temporary, specific, external) prevents helplessness; **pessimistic** (permanent, global, internal) spreads it.

- Optimists build better social networks and cope with pain better (Carver & Scheier, 2014).
- **Health**: pessimists less likely to prevent illness; college **pessimists had twice as many infectious illnesses** in a year; optimists recover faster (Kivimaki et al., 2005).
- **Childhood**: critical parents who attribute bad events to stable/global causes raise **pessimistic** children; optimistic-modeling parents raise **optimistic** children. Childhood mastery immunizes against adult helplessness.

## Martin Seligman: Positive Psychology

- **APA president, 1998**; used his address to launch the **positive psychology** movement.

> **Positive psychology**: the scientific study of positive human functioning — strengths, virtues, and what helps people and institutions thrive. Three pillars: positive experiences, positive traits, positive institutions.

### Causes of Happiness
- **Money**: beyond comfort, more money ≠ more happiness; **relative income** matters more; lottery winners return to baseline.
- **Physical attractiveness**: no significant relationship.
- **Health**: **subjective** perception matters more than objective health (**hedonic adaptation** to chronic illness).
- **Age**: well-being is often **U-shaped** — older people are often among the **happiest** (better emotion regulation).
- **Marriage/social support**: among the strongest predictors.
- **Culture/geography**: individualistic cultures report higher well-being; national factors (democracy, rule of law, wealth) and local safety/trust matter.
- **Personality** = the **strongest predictor**: **extraversion** and **low neuroticism**; a largely genetic **set point**.
- **Self-efficacy and internal LOC**: both positively correlated.
- **Positive thinking** (learned optimism) and **meaningful, intrinsic goals** promote well-being.
- **Happiness causes success** (not just the reverse) — bidirectional. **Internet**: passive use lowers well-being; active use is neutral.

### Different Kinds of Happiness

**Three routes (original):**
| Route | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Pleasant life** | Maximizing positive emotions (hedonic) |
| **Engaged life** | Flow; using character strengths (eudaimonic) |
| **Meaningful life** | Serving something larger than oneself |

**PERMA model (flourishing):** **P**ositive emotions, **E**ngagement, **R**elationships, **M**eaning, **A**ccomplishment. **Flourishing** = highest well-being when PERMA elements are strong.

- Critics: early positive psychology overemphasized the positive and neglected adversity. Now central to clinical/counseling psychology.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **I-E Scale = 23 forced-choice pairs** (the Children's Nowicki-Strickland scale = 40 items) — classic distractor.
2. **Four SS components**: Thrill/Adventure, Experience, Disinhibition, Boredom Susceptibility.
3. **"Bad" SS** = disinhibition + experience seeking + boredom susceptibility + high **psychoticism**.
4. Gender: men higher on thrill/disinhibition/boredom; **women higher on experience seeking**.
5. **Seligman observed learned helplessness on his first day as a graduate student** (dogs).
6. **Rat tumor study**: escapable **70%**, none **50%**, inescapable **27%**.
7. **Learned helplessness easier in male than female rats** (Dalla et al., 2008).
8. **Pessimists had twice as many infectious illnesses** as optimists in a year.
9. **PERMA**: Positive emotions, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment (flourishing).
10. **LOC peaks internal in middle age**; learned in childhood via parenting and SES.
11. **Collectivist** cultures → **external** LOC; **individualistic** → **internal**.
12. **Eysenck: 58% of sensation seeking is genetic** (1983).
13. **Three routes to happiness**: pleasant (hedonic), engaged (flow), meaningful (purpose).
14. **Personality** (high extraversion, low neuroticism) = **strongest predictor of happiness** (not money/looks/health).
15. **LOC vs. self-efficacy**: LOC generalized; self-efficacy situation-specific (Bandura, 1997).
