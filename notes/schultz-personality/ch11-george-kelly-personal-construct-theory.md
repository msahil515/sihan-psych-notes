# Ch 11: George Kelly: Personal Construct Theory

George Kelly proposed a uniquely cognitive theory of personality built on the idea that each person functions like a scientist, constructing hypotheses (personal constructs) to predict and interpret events. Unlike psychoanalytic or behaviorist frameworks, Kelly's system contains no unconscious, no drives, no reinforcement — personality is understood entirely through the cognitive constructs people use to anticipate their world.

---

## The Cognitive Movement in Psychology

### Overview of Kelly's Departure from Other Theories

- **Personal construct theory**: Kelly's description of personality in terms of cognitive processes. People are capable of interpreting behaviors and events and using this understanding to guide behavior and predict others' behavior.
- Kelly explicitly rejected the following concepts from his system: the **unconscious**, the **ego**, **needs**, **drives**, **stimuli and responses**, **reinforcement**, **motivation**, and **emotion**.
- Central claim: each person creates a set of **cognitive constructs** about the environment — an interpretive system or pattern used to organize life events and social relationships.
- On the basis of these patterns, people make **predictions** about themselves, others, and events, then use those predictions to formulate responses and guide actions.
- Kelly's key principle: **our interpretation of events is more important than the events themselves.**

### Kelly vs. Behaviorism and Psychoanalysis

- Kelly opposed both the behavioral and psychoanalytic approaches (like Maslow).
- **Behaviorism** critique: viewed people as passive responders to environmental stimuli.
- **Psychoanalysis** critique: viewed people as passive responders to unconscious forces.
- Kelly's alternative: "people are forms of motion and we propel ourselves. No one or no thing does it to us." (Fransella & Neimeyer, 2003, p. 25)

### An Experiential-Based Theory

- Kelly's theory derived from his experience as a **clinician**, not from laboratory research.
- He concluded that people function the same way **scientists** do: constructing theories/hypotheses and testing them against reality.
- Key critique: psychologists do not attribute to their subjects the same rational abilities they ascribe to themselves — they have one theory for scientists (rational) and another for everyone else (emotional, irrational, driven by unconscious).
- Kelly's answer: psychologists are no different from the people they study. Both seek to predict and control events; both are capable of doing so rationally.
- All of us construct theories — which Kelly called **personal constructs** — by which we predict and control life events.
- To understand personality = examine a person's personal constructs.

### Kelly and the Cognitive Movement

- The **cognitive movement** began around **1960** and came to dominate mainstream experimental psychology.
- Despite similar terminology, mainstream cognitive psychology did **not** embrace Kelly's work.
- Differences:
  - Kelly: clinician focused on **conscious constructs** by which people arrange their lives; studied in a **clinical** setting; focused exclusively on **personality**.
  - Cognitive psychologists: interested in both cognitive variables and **overt behavior**; studied primarily in **experimental** settings; study learning and social situations.
- Kelly had **little influence** on the cognitive movement.
- At best, Kelly's theory is a **precursor** to contemporary cognitive psychology.
- The two share the term "cognitive" (implying conscious activities) but little else.

## The Life of Kelly (1905–1967)

### Early Life

- Born **1905** on a farm in **Kansas**.
- **Only child**; received great attention and affection from parents.
- Parents were **fundamentalist** in religious beliefs, committed to helping the less fortunate; opposed frivolous entertainment (dancing, card playing).
- Age 4: family traveled by covered wagon to Colorado to try farming, then returned to Kansas.

### An Erratic Education

- Early education erratic, conducted as much by parents as by schoolteachers.
- Age 13: went to high school in **Wichita**; seldom lived at home after that.
- **1926**: earned a **bachelor's degree in physics and mathematics** from **Park College**, Parkville, Missouri.
- Interests shifted from science to social problems after graduating.
- Worked briefly as an **engineer**.
- Taught at a **labor college in Minneapolis**.
- Became an instructor in **speech** for the **American Banking Association**.
- Taught **citizenship courses to immigrants**.
- Received a **master's degree in educational sociology** from the **University of Kansas** in Lawrence.
- Accepted a junior college job in Iowa; taught various courses and coached the **drama program**.

### No Interest in Psychology

- In college, Kelly was unimpressed by psychology coursework.
- Attended a psychology course; was unimpressed by the **S → R** (stimulus-response) approach.
- Never understood what the arrow between S and R stood for; gave up after several more meetings.
- Also explored **psychoanalysis** and found it unpersuasive: "I do remember the mounting feeling of incredulity that anyone could write such nonsense, much less publish it" (about Freud's writing, Kelly, 1969, p. 47).

### Finding Himself and Psychology

- **1929**: awarded a fellowship at the **University of Edinburgh**, Scotland.
- Earned a **Bachelor of Education** degree there and finally developed an interest in psychology.
- Returned to the United States; doctoral studies at the **State University of Iowa**.
- Received his **Ph.D. in 1931**.

### Finding an Intellectual Approach to Counseling

- Began academic career at **Fort Hays Kansas State College** during the economic **depression of the 1930s**.
- Switched to **clinical psychology** (no money for physiological psychology research, his specialty).
- Developed a clinical psychology service for the local public school system and college students.
- Established **traveling clinics** (school to school), gaining exposure to a wide variety of problems and treatment approaches.
- Not committed to any particular therapeutic technique or theory; taught himself therapy.
- Treated each patient as a scientist like himself (Routh, 2011).
- His patients were **students referred by teachers for counseling** — NOT severely disturbed psychotics or neurotics; could discuss concerns **rationally** and in **intellectual terms**.
- Key implication: Had Kelly worked with schizophrenics in a mental institution, his theory might not have depended so heavily on cognitive information-processing abilities.

### Becoming a Major Force

- **World War II** interrupted his career; joined the **U.S. Navy**, serving as a psychologist in the **Bureau of Medicine and Surgery**, Washington DC.
- After the war (**1945**), taught for a year at the **University of Maryland**.
- Joined the faculty of **Ohio State University**, replacing **Carl Rogers**.
- Spent **19 years** at Ohio State teaching, refining his theory, and conducting research.
- Lectured worldwide on how personal construct theory could resolve **international tensions**.
- **1965**: accepted an invitation from **Abraham Maslow** for an endowed chair at **Brandeis University**; died shortly after (**1967**).
- Held presidencies of:
  - **Clinical** and **Consulting** divisions of the **American Psychological Association**
  - **American Board of Examiners in Professional Psychology**

## Personal Construct Theory

### Life Is a Construct

- People perceive and organize experience as **scientists** do: formulating hypotheses about the environment and testing them against daily reality.
- Each person's unique interpretation of experience = their unique view of events.
- Kelly: we look at the world through "transparent patterns that fit over the realities of which the world is composed" (Kelly, 1955, pp. 8–9).
- Analogy: **sunglasses** with different tints — the same scene is perceived differently by different people.
- The unique pattern created by each individual = **construct system**.
- **Construct**: an intellectual hypothesis devised to interpret or explain life events; always **bipolar/dichotomous** (e.g., tall vs. short, honest vs. dishonest).
- We behave in accordance with the expectation that our constructs will predict and explain reality; constantly test these hypotheses.

#### Example: Student and Professor
- Student constructs: professor has an inflated self-image.
- Hypothesis: flattering the professor will produce a favorable response.
- Test: student praises the professor's published article.
- If professor gives a good grade → construct confirmed; retained for future use.
- If student fails → construct found inappropriate; must be revised and replaced.

### Constructive Alternativism: Adapting to the World

- **Constructive alternativism**: the idea that we are free to revise or replace our constructs with alternatives as needed.
- We develop constructs for almost every type of person or situation encountered.
- We expand our construct inventory as we meet new people and face new situations.
- We alter or discard constructs as situations change — revision is necessary and continuous.
- If constructs were inflexible (as total childhood determinism would imply), we could not cope with new situations.
- Emphasizes **adaptability** and freedom from determinism.

## Ways of Anticipating Life Events

### The Fundamental Postulate

- Kelly organized his theory into a **fundamental postulate** and **11 corollaries**.
- **Fundamental postulate**: "A person's psychological processes are directed by the ways in which he/she anticipates events."
- Key word **processes**: personality is a continually flowing, moving process.
- Key word **anticipate**: constructs are anticipatory — used to predict the future and gauge consequences of actions.

---

### The 11 Corollaries

#### 1. The Construction Corollary — Similarities Among Repeated Events
- No life event can be reproduced exactly as first experienced.
- However, **recurrent features/themes** emerge across repeated events.
- We predict/anticipate how we will deal with a type of event based on these similarities.
- Predictions rest on the idea that future events will be **similar** (not identical) to past events.
- Example: liking car chase scenes the first time predicts liking them again.

#### 2. The Individuality Corollary — Individual Differences in Interpreting Events
- People differ in how they perceive or interpret events.
- Because people construe events differently, they form **different constructs**.
- Constructs reflect unique interpretation, not the objective reality of the event.

#### 3. The Organization Corollary — Relationships Among Constructs
- We organize constructs into a **hierarchical pattern** according to their interrelationships.
- Some constructs are **subordinate** to others.
- Example: the construct "good" may include subordinates "intelligent" and "moral."
- People who hold similar constructs may still differ if they **organize** those constructs in different patterns.
- Construct relationships are usually more enduring than specific constructs.
- Only valid test for a construct system: **predictive efficiency**.

#### 4. The Dichotomy Corollary — Two Mutually Exclusive Alternatives
- All constructs are **bipolar or dichotomous**.
- Necessary for correctly anticipating future events.
- Example: "honest" requires the contrasting pole "dishonest."
- Without the contrasting pole, the construct cannot help us anticipate or predict anything.
- Constructs must always be framed in terms of a pair of mutually exclusive alternatives.

#### 5. The Choice Corollary — Freedom of Choice
- For every situation, we choose the alternative that works best for us — allowing the best anticipation/prediction of outcome.
- Kelly describes this as a choice between **security** and **adventure**:
  - **Secure (low-risk) choice**: similar to past choices; high predictive efficiency; further **defines** and limits the construct system.
  - **Adventurous (high-risk) choice**: new experiences; lower predictive efficiency; **extends** the construct system.
- Example: choosing a familiar easy course (secure) vs. a new challenging course (adventurous).
- Important: choices are made in terms of **predictive certainty**, not necessarily what is best.
- Explains maladaptive behavior: aggressive people persist with aggression because they know what to expect — they don't know how others will react to friendliness.

#### 6. The Range Corollary — Range of Convenience
- **Range of convenience**: the spectrum of events to which a construct can be applied.
- Few personal constructs are relevant for all situations.
- Example: "tall vs. short" applies to buildings, trees, basketball players — NOT to pizza or weather.
- Some constructs apply to many situations/people; others are limited to one person/situation.
- Equally important to know what is **excluded** from a construct's range as what is included.

#### 7. The Experience Corollary — Exposure to New Experiences
- Each construct is a hypothesis tested against reality by determining how well it predicted an event.
- Most people encounter new experiences daily; testing is ongoing.
- If a construct is not a valid predictor → must be **reformulated or replaced**.
- Constructs that worked at age 16 may be useless or harmful at age 40.
- Without new experiences, the construct system would never need to change.

#### 8. The Modulation Corollary — Adapting to New Experiences
- Constructs differ in their **permeability**.
- **Permeable construct**: allows new elements into the range of convenience; open to revision by new experiences.
- **Impermeable (rigid) construct**: not capable of being changed regardless of experience — a barrier to learning.
- Example: a bigoted person holding an impermeable construct about an ethnic group will not modify it regardless of contradicting evidence.
- How much a construct system can be **modulated** (adjusted) depends on the permeability of individual constructs.

#### 9. The Fragmentation Corollary — Competition Among Constructs
- Within our construct system, some constructs may be **incompatible** yet coexist within the overall pattern.
- New constructs do not necessarily derive from old ones.
- In one situation, two constructs may be compatible; in another, they become inconsistent.
- Example: a man finds a woman fits his "friend" construct in class (shared interests), but "enemy" at a political rally (opposite political views). The broader construct (liberals = friends, conservatives = enemies) remains undisturbed; inconsistency is at the **subordinate level**.
- Allows us to **tolerate subordinate inconsistencies** without damaging the overall construct system.

#### 10. The Commonality Corollary — Similarities Among People in Interpreting Events
- Although individual constructs are unique, people in compatible groups or cultures may hold **similar** constructs.
- If several people construe an experience similarly → their **cognitive processes** are similar.
- People from the same culture may resemble each other in behaviors/characteristics even with different individual life events.

#### 11. The Sociality Corollary — Interpersonal Relationships
- It is not enough to construe experiences in the same way as another person.
- Must also **construe the other person's constructs** — understand how the other person thinks to predict what they will do.
- Example: driving requires anticipating what other drivers will do (stop at red, go at green).
- Each person assumes a **role** with respect to others — a behavior pattern that evolves from understanding how the other person construes events.
- We play different roles with different people (partner, child, supervisor).
- We fit ourselves into the other person's constructs.

## Questions about Human Nature

- Kelly's view is **optimistic and flattering** — people are rational beings capable of forming a construct framework (Kelly, 1969).
- **Free will**: we are authors, not victims, of our destiny; we choose the direction of our lives and can change when necessary.
- **Change**: we can revise old constructs and form new ones; not committed to a path laid down in childhood or adolescence.
- **Future orientation**: constructs anticipate and predict events; the direction of personality is toward the future.
- **Rejected determinism**: past events are not determinants of present behavior.
- Not prisoners of toilet training, early sex experiences, parental rejection, biological instincts, or unconscious forces.
- **No internal drives needed**: we are motivated simply by **the fact of being alive** — Kelly saw no reason to invoke additional motivational explanations.
- **Heredity**: Kelly did not discuss heredity's role but noted we are not totally determined by environment either; rational mental processes, not specific events, form personality.
- **Ultimate goal** (inferred): establish a construct system that enables prediction of events.
- **Uniqueness vs. Universality**: moderate position:
  - Commonality corollary: people in the same culture develop similar constructs.
  - Individuality corollary: uniqueness of many constructs and therefore of the self.

## Assessment in Kelly's Theory

### The Interview

- Kelly's **primary** assessment technique.
- Quote: "If you don't know what is going on in a person's mind, ask him; he may tell you!" (1958, p. 330).
- Kelly adopted a **"credulous attitude"**: accepted the client's words at face value as the best way to determine constructs.
- Recognized clients might lie or distort; nevertheless, client's words must be respected.

### Self-Characterization Sketches

- **Self-characterization sketch**: a technique designed to assess a person's construct system — how a person perceives themselves in relation to others.
- Kelly's instructions: "I want you to write a character sketch of [client's name] just as if he were the principal character in a play. Write it as it might be written by a friend who knew him very intimately and very sympathetically, perhaps better than anyone ever really could know him." (Kelly, 1955, p. 323)
- Useful for learning how clients perceive themselves in relation to other people.

### The Role Construct Repertory (REP) Test

- **Role Construct Repertory (REP) Test**: designed by Kelly to uncover the constructs a person applies to the important people in their life.
- Client lists by name people who have played significant roles: mother, father, spouse, closest friend, most intelligent/interesting person known, etc.
- **24 role titles** in the standard form (Table 11.2 in text).
- Names are sorted **three at a time**; client selects the **two most alike** and notes how they differ from the third.
- Example triad: most threatening person, successful person, attractive person — describe how any two are similar and how they differ from the third.

#### The Repertory Grid
- Information organized in a **repertory grid** diagram.
- For each row, client judges three people and formulates a construct.
- **Emergent Pole**: word/phrase describing two of the three people (e.g., "happy").
- **Implicit Pole**: opposite word describing the third person (e.g., "sad").
- Client places check marks in squares of anyone else in the grid sharing the Emergent Pole characteristics.

#### The Role of Dichotomies
- REP Test is based on the **dichotomy corollary**: people construe events in dichotomies (like vs. unlike, similar vs. dissimilar).
- Forcing repeated judgments about social relationships uncovers the client's anticipations and expectations.
- REP Test was **NOT** intended as a standardized, objective self-report inventory.
- Designed as a way to assess constructs as a stage in **psychotherapy**.
- Computer programs have since been developed to analyze repertory grids more objectively (Grice, 2002).

### Fixed Role Therapy

- **Fixed role therapy**: a psychotherapeutic technique in which the client acts out constructs appropriate for a fictitious person, showing the client how new constructs can be more effective than the old ones.
- Therapist prepares a **fixed role sketch** — a description of a fictitious character whose constructs differ from the client's negative self-perceptions revealed in the self-characterization sketch.
- The client is told the fixed role sketch is about a **fictitious character** and is asked to act out that character in the therapist's office and later in everyday life.
- Through **role-playing**, the client projects personal needs and values onto the fictitious character.
- Goal: client discovers that new constructs work better in anticipating events and incorporates them into the overall construct system.
- **Origin of fixed role therapy**: Kelly observed a friend who came to live the role he was performing in a college play — behavior offstage gradually became more like the character.
- Goal stated simply: **first play a role, then come to live it.**
- Behavioral changes from fixed role therapy are reported to last **far beyond the two-week** role-playing period.
- Limited controlled research on effectiveness; one study found it reduced **social anxiety** in Japan (Abe, Imai, & Nedate, 2011).

#### Case Example: Roy Taylor
- Client overly concerned with finding a female companion; difficulty being open and assertive because assertiveness/extraversion = negative in his construct system; believed his opinions were always correct; felt isolated at work (believed he was of a higher social class).
- Fixed role sketch: framed around the client's skill at **tennis**; encouraged curiosity about and tolerance of different kinds of people and their views; no mention of his desire for an intimate relationship.
- Client agreed to try behaving like the character for **two weeks**; asked to think, act, and talk like the character.

## Research on Kelly's Theory

### KEY STUDIES — Callout

> **Study 1 — Stability of Constructs Over Time**
> - Method: REP Test administered twice using different role figures each time.
> - Finding: Constructs remained stable even when role models changed.

> **Study 2 — Friendships and Construct Similarity (Duck & Spencer, 1972)**
> - Method: Students took REP Test in first week at college and again 6 months later.
> - Finding: Similarity in constructs among friends did NOT develop during the 6-month period — it had **existed before** the relationships formed.
> - Conclusion: We seek as friends those whose constructs are already similar to ours.

> **Study 3 — Marital Happiness (Neimeyer, 1984)**
> - Finding: Spouses with more alike constructs reported greater marital happiness than couples whose constructs were more unlike.

> **Study 4 — Emotional State and Construing Others (Sechrest, 1968)**
> - Among student nurses: highly anxious nurses used "anxious vs. non-anxious" as a construct for evaluating others; those judged as friendly used "friendly vs. unfriendly."
> - Finding: Personal characteristics correspond with the ways one construes other people.

> **Study 5 — Schizophrenics and the REP Test (Bannister, Fransella & Agnew, 1971)**
> - Finding: Schizophrenics were unstable/inconsistent in construing **people** but stable/consistent in construing **objects** — thought disorders specifically apply to social situations. Thought processes also characterized by paranoid delusions and irrational links between constructs.

> **Study 6 — Repeat vs. First-Time Psychiatric Patients (Smith et al., 1991)**
> - Finding: Repeat patients construed their social network as **small and limited** (few dependable people); first-time patients construed it as significantly **larger**.

> **Study 7 — Juvenile and Adult Offenders (Needs, 1988)**
> - Delinquents tended to identify with **action-oriented TV heroes** rather than real adults.
> - Newly released prisoners: poor self-esteem, lowered future aspirations.
> - Rapists: felt inadequate, immature, preoccupied with personal failure.

> **Study 8 — Cognitive Complexity and Life Span (Crockett, 1982)**
> - Finding: Construct system becomes increasingly differentiated and integrated over the life span; can process more information in more abstract terms.

> **Study 9 — Reckless Driving (McNally & Bradley, 2014, Australia)**
> - Finding: Degree of reckless behavior in automobile drivers could be related to the constructs they held.

> **Study 10 — Islamic Terrorists in India (Canter, Sarangi & Youngs, 2014, British psychologists)**
> - Finding: Convicted terrorists unable to re-construe their future lives in ways not linked to terrorism were least likely to disengage from future terrorist acts.

### Real-World Applications of the REP Test
- **Market research**: assessing criteria consumers use to evaluate products.
- **Industrial-organizational psychology**: vocational counseling, employee selection, performance evaluation, evaluation of training programs (Benjafield, 2008).

## Cognitive Complexity and Cognitive Simplicity

### Definitions

- Research on cognitive styles derived from the REP Test.
- **Cognitive complexity**: a cognitive style characterized by the ability to perceive differences among people; ability to discriminate in applying personal constructs; placing a person in many categories.
- **Cognitive simplicity**: a cognitive style characterized by a relative inability to perceive differences among people; placing others in only one or two categories.

### Personality and Behavioral Correlates

**High cognitive complexity is associated with:**
- Better ability to make predictions about others' behavior.
- More readily recognize differences between self and others.
- Display more **empathy** toward others.
- Deal better with inconsistent information when construing others (Crockett, 1982).
- Lower anxiety and instability.
- Better adjustment to stresses of college life.
- Tend to possess **more than the traditional five personality factors**.
- **Moderate and liberal** political views (Tetlock, 1983, 1984).
- Mental health counselors/therapists with more training and experience (Granello, 2010; Owen & Lindley, 2010).

**High cognitive simplicity is associated with:**
- Fewer than the five personality factors.
- **Conservative** political views.

### Development Over Time

- Cognitive complexity generally **increases with age**: adults > children.
- Age is not a complete explanation; many adults still possess cognitive simplicity.
- Adults high in cognitive complexity typically had **more diverse childhood experiences**.
- Their parents were **less authoritarian** and **more likely to grant autonomy** (Sechrest & Jackson, 1961).

### Cognitive Complexity in Couples (Adams-Webber, 2001)

- Study of 40 couples.
- Women scored significantly **higher** in cognitive complexity than men.
- High **correlation** in cognitive complexity between male and female partners.
- Partners may have chosen each other due to similar pre-existing levels, or developed similarity through living together.

### Cultural Factors (Benet-Martinez, Lee & Leu, 2006)

- Compared **monocultural Anglo-American** vs. **bicultural Chinese-American** college students (born in China, lived at least 5 years in U.S.).
- Bicultural students scored **higher in cognitive complexity** than monocultural students.

### Attributional Complexity

- **Attributional complexity**: a variant of cognitive complexity — the extent to which people prefer complex rather than simple explanations for social behavior; attributing others' behavior to complicated, multifaceted, and sophisticated causes.
- People **high in attributional complexity**:
  - More sensitive to subtle signs of **racism**.
  - Show greater **empathy** and greater **understanding** of others (Foels & Reid, 2010; Reid & Foels, 2010).
  - Business leaders: more effective in management behavior (Sun & Anderson, 2012).

### HIGHLIGHTS: Research Summary on Kelly's Ideas

**REP Test research has found:**
- Personal constructs remain stable over time.
- We choose friends whose constructs are like ours.
- Spouses whose constructs were alike were happier.
- Schizophrenics formed stable constructs of objects but NOT of people.
- Delinquents identified with action heroes rather than real people.

**People high in cognitive complexity tend to:**
- Score low in anxiety.
- Have more than the traditional five personality factors.
- Be good at predicting how others will behave.
- Have moderate to liberal political views.
- Have had more diverse childhood experiences.
- Adjust better to the stresses of college.

**People high in attributional complexity:**
- Attribute others' behavior to complex causes.
- Have greater empathy and understanding of others.
- Are sensitive to subtle signs of racism.

## Reflections on Kelly's Theory

### Strengths

- Unique and radical theory that did not derive from or build on other theories.
- Emerged from Kelly's own clinical practice.
- Originality mirrors its message: we are capable of developing the framework for our own lives.
- Strong support especially in **Europe, Canada, and Australia** (less so in the U.S.).
- In the mid-1980s, the **Centre for Personal Construct Psychology** was established in **England**.

### Criticisms

1. **Overemphasis on rationality**: focuses on intellectual/rational aspects to the exclusion of emotional aspects. Kelly's image of a rationally constructing person does not match clinical experience with extreme behavior.
2. **Unrepresentative sample**: based largely on **Midwestern young adults** (college students) — not representative of broader human experience. (cf. Freud: unrepresentative neurotic Viennese patients.)
3. **Unanswered questions**:
   - Why does one person construe an event one way while another person construes it differently? What mechanism accounts for this?
   - What determines whether a person opts for security vs. adventure?
4. Kelly recognized emotions as **personal constructs** similar in formation to other constructs — but did not deal with them explicitly.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Kelly's theory explicitly excludes**: unconscious, ego, needs, drives, S-R framework, reinforcement, motivation, and emotion as theoretical concepts — unique among major personality theories.
2. **Fundamental postulate** (exact wording to know): "A person's psychological processes are directed by the ways in which he/she anticipates events." — the single postulate underlying the entire theory.
3. **Number of corollaries = 11**. The four most MCQ-tested: Dichotomy (all constructs are bipolar), Choice (security vs. adventure), Range (range of convenience), Modulation (permeability).
4. **Constructive alternativism**: the concept that we are free to revise or replace constructs — directly opposes psychoanalytic determinism and behaviorist S-R chains.
5. **All constructs are bipolar/dichotomous** per the Dichotomy Corollary — a defining structural feature of the entire theory.
6. **REP Test mechanics**: triads of 24 role figures; Emergent Pole = similarity; Implicit Pole = contrast; NOT a standardized self-report; validity heavily dependent on the psychologist's skill; designed as a psychotherapy assessment tool, not a research instrument per se.
7. **Fixed role therapy**: client acts a fictitious character for ~2 weeks; originated from Kelly observing a friend who came to live a drama role; changes last beyond 2 weeks; one RCT in Japan confirmed reduction of social anxiety (Abe et al., 2011).
8. **Choice corollary — two options**: secure/low-risk = defines construct system = repeats past experience; adventurous/high-risk = extends construct system = new experience. Maladaptive behavior (e.g., chronic aggression) explained as persistence with the secure/predictable choice.
9. **Permeability**: permeable constructs can be revised; impermeable constructs resist change even in the face of contradictory experience (example: bigotry).
10. **Duck & Spencer (1972)**: friendship is based on pre-existing construct similarity, NOT on constructs becoming similar after the friendship forms.
11. **Cognitive complexity vs. simplicity**: high CC associated with empathy, better prediction, lower anxiety, moderate/liberal politics, diverse childhood, bicultural background; conservatives high in cognitive simplicity (Tetlock, 1983, 1984).
12. **Schizophrenics (REP Test)**: unstable in construing people but stable in construing objects — thought disorders are specifically social/interpersonal.
13. **Kelly's view of human nature**: optimistic, forward-looking, free will; motivation = simply being alive (no separate drive theory); rejected both hereditary determinism and environmental determinism; moderate position on uniqueness vs. universality.
14. **Kelly's academic biography facts**: Ph.D. from State University of Iowa (1931); replaced Carl Rogers at Ohio State; invited by Maslow to Brandeis in 1965; died 1967; his clinical patients were college students (NOT psychotics), which explains the theory's rational/cognitive emphasis.
15. **Attributional complexity**: variant of cognitive complexity; preference for complex explanations; high AC associated with greater empathy, sensitivity to racism, and effective business leadership.
