# Ch 11: George Kelly: Personal Construct Theory

Kelly proposed a cognitive theory built on the idea that each person functions like a **scientist**, constructing hypotheses (**personal constructs**) to predict and interpret events. His system contains no unconscious, drives, or reinforcement — personality is the cognitive constructs people use to anticipate their world.

---

## The Cognitive Movement in Psychology

- **Personal construct theory**: personality described in terms of cognitive processes; people interpret events and use this to guide behavior and predict others'.
- Kelly **rejected**: the unconscious, ego, needs, drives, stimuli/responses, reinforcement, motivation, and emotion.
- Each person creates a set of **cognitive constructs** to organize life events; uses them to **predict** and respond. Key principle: **our interpretation of events matters more than the events themselves.**

### Kelly vs. Behaviorism and Psychoanalysis
- Opposed both: behaviorism (passive responder to stimuli) and psychoanalysis (passive responder to unconscious forces). Kelly: "people are forms of motion and we propel ourselves" (Fransella & Neimeyer, 2003).

### An Experiential-Based Theory
- Derived from his **clinical** experience. People function like **scientists**, forming and testing hypotheses. Critique: psychologists wrongly assume they alone are rational. We all build **personal constructs** to predict and control events; understanding personality = examining them.

### Kelly and the Cognitive Movement
- The **cognitive movement** began ~**1960** but did NOT embrace Kelly. Kelly: clinician focused on **conscious constructs** and personality; cognitive psychologists: overt behavior in **experimental** settings. Kelly is at best a **precursor**; they share the word "cognitive" but little else.

## The Life of Kelly (1905–1967)

- Born **1905** on a **Kansas** farm; **only child** of **fundamentalist** parents.
- **1926**: B.A. in **physics and mathematics** from **Park College**; worked as an engineer, taught at a labor college, taught immigrants; M.A. in educational sociology (**University of Kansas**); coached drama.
- Unimpressed by psychology's **S → R** approach and by psychoanalysis ("anyone could write such nonsense").
- **1929**: fellowship at **University of Edinburgh**, developed interest in psychology; **Ph.D., State University of Iowa, 1931**.
- During the 1930s depression, switched to **clinical psychology** at **Fort Hays Kansas State**; ran **traveling clinics**; treated each patient as a scientist (Routh, 2011). His patients were **students referred by teachers** (not psychotics) — able to discuss concerns **rationally**, which shaped the theory's cognitive emphasis.
- **WWII**: U.S. Navy psychologist. Then **University of Maryland**, then **Ohio State (19 years)**, replacing **Carl Rogers**. **1965**: invited by **Maslow** to Brandeis; died **1967**. Held APA Clinical/Consulting division presidencies.

## Personal Construct Theory

### Life Is a Construct
- People organize experience as **scientists** do, forming and testing hypotheses. We view the world through "transparent patterns" (like tinted **sunglasses** — same scene perceived differently). The pattern = **construct system**.
- **Construct**: an intellectual hypothesis to interpret events; always **bipolar/dichotomous** (tall vs. short).
- *Example*: a student constructs that a professor has an inflated self-image → hypothesis: flattery works → tests it; a good grade confirms the construct, a fail forces revision.

### Constructive Alternativism
- **Constructive alternativism**: we are free to **revise or replace** constructs as needed. We expand our inventory with new situations and discard constructs as situations change — emphasizing **adaptability** and freedom from determinism.

---

## Ways of Anticipating Life Events

### The Fundamental Postulate
- One **fundamental postulate** + **11 corollaries**.
- **Fundamental postulate**: "A person's psychological processes are directed by the ways in which he/she anticipates events." Key words: **processes** (personality is a flowing process) and **anticipate** (constructs predict the future).

### The 11 Corollaries

1. **Construction** — repeated events share **recurring themes**; we anticipate based on similarity (not identity) to the past.
2. **Individuality** — people construe events differently → form **different constructs**.
3. **Organization** — constructs are organized in a **hierarchy** (some subordinate to others, e.g., "good" includes "intelligent," "moral"); only valid test = **predictive efficiency**.
4. **Dichotomy** — all constructs are **bipolar** (honest requires dishonest); needed to anticipate events.
5. **Choice** — we choose the alternative allowing best prediction: a **secure (low-risk)** choice (repeats the past, **defines/limits** the system) or an **adventurous (high-risk)** choice (new experience, **extends** the system). Choices favor **predictive certainty**; explains why aggressive people persist (they know what to expect).
6. **Range** — each construct has a **range of convenience** (events it applies to); e.g., "tall vs. short" fits buildings/people, not pizza.
7. **Experience** — constructs are hypotheses **continuously tested** against reality and **reformulated** if invalid (constructs useful at 16 may be useless at 40).
8. **Modulation** — constructs differ in **permeability**: **permeable** ones admit new elements and can be revised; **impermeable/rigid** ones cannot (e.g., bigotry).
9. **Fragmentation** — **incompatible subordinate constructs can coexist** without disrupting the overall system (e.g., someone is a "friend" in class but "enemy" at a political rally; the broad construct stays intact).
10. **Commonality** — people in the same group/culture may hold **similar** constructs (similar cognitive processes).
11. **Sociality** — positive relationships require **construing the other person's constructs** (understanding how they think), not just sharing similar ones; we each assume a **role** with respect to others.

## Questions about Human Nature

- **Optimistic and flattering**: people are rational construct-builders.
- **Free will**: authors, not victims, of our destiny; can revise constructs.
- **Future orientation**: constructs anticipate events; **rejected determinism** (not prisoners of childhood, instincts, or the unconscious).
- **No internal drives**: motivated simply by **being alive**.
- **Heredity/environment**: not totally determined by either; rational processes form personality.
- **Uniqueness vs. universality**: moderate (commonality + individuality corollaries).

## Assessment in Kelly's Theory

### The Interview
- **Primary** technique: "If you don't know what is going on in a person's mind, ask him." Adopted a **"credulous attitude"** (accept the client's words at face value).

### Self-Characterization Sketch
- **Self-characterization sketch**: the client writes about themselves as if described by "a friend who knew him very intimately and very sympathetically" — reveals how the client sees themselves relative to others.

### The Role Construct Repertory (REP) Test
- **REP Test**: uncovers constructs applied to significant people. Client lists people for **24 role titles** (mother, father, spouse, etc.); names sorted **three at a time** — pick the **two most alike** and note how they differ from the third.
- **Repertory grid**: **Emergent Pole** (word describing the two alike) vs. **Implicit Pole** (opposite, describing the third); client checks others sharing the Emergent Pole.
- Based on the **dichotomy corollary**; **not** a standardized self-report — a psychotherapy assessment stage. Computer analysis now available (Grice, 2002).

### Fixed Role Therapy
- **Fixed role therapy**: the client acts out constructs of a **fictitious character** (in a therapist-written **fixed role sketch**) that differ from the client's negative self-perceptions. Through role-play, the client discovers new constructs work better. **Origin**: Kelly observed a friend who came to live a drama role. Goal: **first play a role, then come to live it.** Changes last beyond the **two-week** period; one study reduced **social anxiety** in Japan (Abe, Imai, & Nedate, 2011).
- *Case (Roy Taylor)*: a man whose construct system equated assertiveness with negativity; the sketch (built around his tennis skill) encouraged curiosity and tolerance; he acted the character for two weeks.

## Research on Kelly's Theory

> **1 — Stability**: REP Test given twice with different role figures; constructs remained stable.
> **2 — Friendships (Duck & Spencer, 1972)**: construct similarity among friends **pre-existed** the friendships — we choose friends whose constructs are already like ours.
> **3 — Marital happiness (Neimeyer, 1984)**: spouses with more alike constructs were happier.
> **4 — Emotional state (Sechrest, 1968)**: anxious nurses used "anxious vs. non-anxious" to evaluate others — personal characteristics correspond to how we construe others.
> **5 — Schizophrenics (Bannister, Fransella & Agnew, 1971)**: unstable construing **people** but stable construing **objects** — thought disorder is specifically social.
> **6 — Repeat patients (Smith et al., 1991)**: repeat psychiatric patients construed their social network as **smaller** than first-time patients.
> **7 — Offenders (Needs, 1988)**: delinquents identified with **action-oriented TV heroes**; rapists felt inadequate and immature.
> **8 — Life span (Crockett, 1982)**: the construct system becomes more **differentiated and integrated** with age.
> **9 — Reckless driving (McNally & Bradley, 2014)**: reckless driving related to drivers' constructs.
> **10 — Terrorists (Canter, Sarangi & Youngs, 2014)**: terrorists unable to **re-construe** their future away from terrorism were least likely to disengage.

- **Applications**: market research and industrial-organizational psychology (selection, performance evaluation; Benjafield, 2008).

## Cognitive Complexity and Cognitive Simplicity

- **Cognitive complexity**: ability to perceive differences among people; placing others in many categories.
- **Cognitive simplicity**: relative inability to perceive differences; few categories.

**High cognitive complexity**: better prediction of others, more **empathy**, deals with inconsistent information, lower anxiety, better college adjustment, **more than five personality factors**, **moderate/liberal** politics (Tetlock, 1983), and seen in experienced counselors.
**High cognitive simplicity**: fewer than five factors; **conservative** politics.

- Increases **with age**, though many adults remain simple; high-CC adults had **more diverse childhoods** and **less authoritarian, autonomy-granting parents** (Sechrest & Jackson, 1961).
- **Couples (Adams-Webber, 2001)**: women scored higher; high partner correlation. **Culture (Benet-Martinez et al., 2006)**: bicultural Chinese-Americans scored higher than monocultural Anglo-Americans.

### Attributional Complexity
- **Attributional complexity**: preference for complex, multifaceted explanations of social behavior. High AC → more sensitive to **racism**, greater **empathy**, more effective business leadership.

## Reflections on Kelly's Theory

### Strengths
- Unique, radical, original; from his own clinical practice. Strong support in **Europe, Canada, Australia**; the **Centre for Personal Construct Psychology** established in England (mid-1980s).

### Criticisms
1. **Overemphasis on rationality** (neglects emotion).
2. **Unrepresentative sample** (Midwestern college students).
3. **Unanswered questions**: why do people construe the same event differently? What determines security vs. adventure?
4. Treated emotions as personal constructs but did not address them explicitly.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Kelly excludes**: unconscious, ego, needs, drives, S-R, reinforcement, motivation, emotion — unique among major theories.
2. **Fundamental postulate** (verbatim): "A person's psychological processes are directed by the ways in which he/she anticipates events."
3. **11 corollaries**; most-tested: Dichotomy (bipolar), Choice (security vs. adventure), Range (range of convenience), Modulation (permeability).
4. **Constructive alternativism**: free to revise/replace constructs — opposes determinism.
5. **All constructs are bipolar/dichotomous** (Dichotomy corollary).
6. **REP Test**: triads of **24 role figures**; **Emergent Pole** = similarity, **Implicit Pole** = contrast; not a standardized self-report.
7. **Fixed role therapy**: act a fictitious character for ~2 weeks; "play a role, then come to live it"; reduced social anxiety in Japan (Abe et al., 2011).
8. **Choice corollary**: secure (defines) vs. adventurous (extends); explains persistent aggression (predictable choice).
9. **Permeability**: permeable = revisable; impermeable = rigid (bigotry).
10. **Duck & Spencer (1972)**: friendship rests on **pre-existing** construct similarity.
11. **Cognitive complexity vs. simplicity**: high CC → empathy, lower anxiety, moderate/liberal politics, diverse childhood, bicultural; conservatives high in simplicity (Tetlock).
12. **Schizophrenics (REP Test)**: unstable with people, stable with objects — thought disorder is social.
13. **Human nature**: optimistic, free will; motivation = being alive; rejects hereditary AND environmental determinism.
14. **Biography**: Ph.D. Iowa (1931); replaced Rogers at Ohio State; invited by Maslow to Brandeis (1965); patients were college students (not psychotics).
15. **Attributional complexity**: complex explanations → empathy, sensitivity to racism, effective leadership.
