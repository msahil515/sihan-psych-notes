# Ch 2: Sigmund Freud: Psychoanalysis

Freud's **psychoanalysis** is the first formal personality theory and the most influential. Behavior is driven by unconscious instinctual forces, shaped by early childhood, and mediated by three structures (id, ego, superego). Covers levels of consciousness, anxiety and defense, and the psychosexual stages forming personality by age 5.

---

## The Life of Freud (1856–1939)

### The Early Years

- Born **1856** in **Freiberg, Moravia** (now Pribor, Czech Republic); died **1939** in London.
- Father Jacob: wool merchant, **40** at Sigmund's birth, strict and authoritarian.
- Mother Amalia (Jacob's **third wife**): only **20** at his birth; warm, devoted to Sigmund.
- Freud felt **passionate, near-sexual attachment** to his mother — conceptual seed of the **Oedipus complex**.
- Family moved Freiberg → Leipzig → **Vienna** (age 4); Freud stayed ~80 years.
- Eight children; Freud resented siblings as competitors for his mother's attention.
- Traits: **high self-confidence, intense ambition, dreams of fame**.
- Quote: "A man who has been the indisputable favorite of his mother keeps for life the feeling of a conqueror."
- Entered high school early, often top of class. Knew German, Hebrew, Latin, Greek, French, English; self-taught Italian and Spanish.
- Chose medicine as a path to **scientific research** and fame, not to be a physician.

### The Cocaine Episode

- Experimented with **cocaine** (then legal) in medical school.
- Published an article in **1884** praising it, hoping for fame; later judged a major contributor to the **cocaine epidemic** into the 1920s.
- Used cocaine ~**1884–1896**, then spent his life trying to erase the endorsements from his bibliography.

### Finding the Sexual Basis of Neurosis

- Opened private practice as a **clinical neurologist** in **1881**.
- Studied in Paris with **Jean-Martin Charcot**, a **hypnosis** pioneer who alerted Freud to the possible **sexual basis of neurosis**: *"In this sort of case, it's always a question of the genitals — always, always, always."*

### Childhood Sexual Abuse: Fact or Fantasy?

- Freud became convinced **sexual conflicts** caused all neuroses.
- Many women reported **childhood sexual trauma** (seduction by older male relatives) → he published the **seduction theory** (early traumas cause adult neurosis).
- ~A year later he **retracted** it, calling the reports **fantasies** — but ones **psychologically real** to patients; sex remained the cause.
- Quote (1898): "The most immediate and most significant causes of neurotic illness are to be found in factors arising from sexual life."
- Scholars argue more patients may have told the truth than Freud admitted (possibly retracting to protect fathers, including his own). He later privately admitted curing "several cases of real incest."

### Freud's Own Sex Life

- Paradox: emphasized sex yet had many personal sexual conflicts.
- **Shy, afraid of women**; a **virgin when he married at 30** (Martha Bernays, after a 4-year engagement).
- Apparently **ended his sex life at 41**: "Sexual excitation is of no more use to a person like me." Occasionally impotent; disliked available birth control; resented Martha's pregnancies.

### Freud's Neurotic Episode

- In his **40s**: a **severe neurotic episode** — "cloudy thoughts and veiled doubts."
- Symptoms: **migraines, urinary problems, spastic colon**, travel anxiety, fear of open spaces, fear of dying.
- Self-diagnosed **anxiety neurosis** and **neurasthenia** (weakness, worry, digestive/circulatory disturbances), tracing both to **accumulated sexual tension** (neurasthenia → masturbation; anxiety neurosis → coitus interruptus, abstinence).
- "Freud's theory of actual neurosis is thus a theory of his own neurotic symptoms" (Krüll, 1986).

### Analyzing Freud's Dreams

- **Psychoanalyzed himself** through his own dreams for life: "The chief patient I am busy with is myself."
- Dream analysis revealed hostility toward his father, sexual longing for his mother, and a sex wish toward his eldest daughter — much of his theory formed around these conflicts.

### Freud's Ideas Attract Attention

- Weekly discussion group formed (first topic: psychology of cigar making).
- Early disciples **Carl Jung** and **Alfred Adler** broke away to form their own theories; Freud saw them as **traitors**.
- His aunt's critique: "The trouble with you, Sigi, is that you just don't understand people."

### Freud Comes to America

- **1909**: lectured at **Clark University** (Worcester, MA); received an **honorary doctorate**. He disliked the U.S.
- Followers founded the **American Psychoanalytic Association** and **New York Psychoanalytic Society** two years later.
- By **1920**, **200+ books** on his work were published in the U.S. **Benjamin Spock's** child care books drew on Freud.

### The Final Years

- **1923–1939**: **33 operations** for **mouth cancer** (from ~20 cigars daily); near-constant pain, mostly refusing medication.
- **1933**: Nazis burned his books — "In the Middle Ages they would have burnt me; nowadays they are content with burning my books."
- **1938**: Nazis occupied Austria; Freud left only after daughter **Anna was arrested**; four sisters died in concentration camps.
- Died **September 1939** in London; physician **Max Schur** gave three morphine injections at Freud's request.

## Instincts: The Propelling Forces of the Personality

- **Instincts** (German *Trieb*): basic motivating forces that drive behavior and set its direction.
- A form of **energy** — physiological energy transformed, connecting bodily needs to mental wishes.
- Process: bodily need → physiological excitation → mind transforms it into a **wish** (the instinct) → drives behavior to satisfy the need. The instinct is the **bodily need transformed into a mental state**, not the bodily state itself.
- Need creates **tension/pressure**; the instinct's **aim** is to reduce it. Freud's theory is **homeostatic** — restore physiological equilibrium.
- Instincts always influence behavior; we cannot escape internal pressures.
- **Psychic energy** can be **displaced** to substitute objects — the basis of personality diversity. All adult interests/attitudes are **displacements of energy** from original objects.

### Two Types of Instincts

#### The Life Instincts (Eros)
- Serve survival of **individual and species**: food, water, air, **sex**; oriented toward **growth**.
- Energy: **libido**. **Cathexis** = investment of libido in an object/person.
- Most important for personality: **sex** (broadly — all pleasurable, affectionate impulses).
- **Erogenous zones**: mouth, anus, sex organs. Freud saw humans as **pleasure-seeking**.

#### The Death Instincts (Thanatos)
- All living things decay and die → unconscious wish to die.
- Key component: the **aggressive drive** — the death wish turned outward; compels us to destroy and kill.
- Freud saw aggression as compelling as sex.
- Developed late (influenced by his aging, cancer, his daughter's death at 26, **WWI**); achieved **only limited acceptance** even among followers.

| Instinct Type | Also Called | Purpose | Energy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Life instincts | Eros | Survival, growth, pleasure | Libido |
| Death instincts | Thanatos | Decay, destruction, aggression | — |

## The Levels of Personality

Freud's original **topographic model**: three levels of consciousness.

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Conscious** | What one is aware of at any moment; the "tip of the iceberg" |
| **Preconscious** | Memories/thoughts not currently conscious but easily retrievable |
| **Unconscious** | Vast repository of instincts, repressed wishes, desires; chief driver of behavior; largest portion |

- **Iceberg metaphor**: conscious = tip; unconscious = vast invisible portion below.
- The **unconscious** is the focus of psychoanalysis; the **preconscious** sits just below awareness.
- Later revised by the **structural model** (id, ego, superego); both coexist.

## The Structure of Personality

### The Id
- Corresponds to the earlier **unconscious**; the **reservoir of instincts and libido** powering the other two.
- Operates on the **pleasure principle**: immediate gratification, avoid pain; **no awareness of reality**; selfish, amoral, insistent.
- Like a **newborn**; satisfies needs via:
  1. **Reflex action** (crying, sneezing).
  2. **Primary-process thought**: wish-fulfilling hallucinatory/fantasy experience.
- Cannot delay satisfaction.

### The Ego
- The **rational master** of personality; helps the id obtain realistic tension reduction (not to thwart it).
- Operates on the **reality principle**: determines appropriate times, places, objects for satisfaction.
- Uses **secondary-process thought**: perception, judgment, memory. Controls and postpones id impulses (delays/redirects, not eliminates).
- Analogy: a **rider on a horse** — guides the raw power of the id (horse).
- Serves the id and reality; gets its energy **from the id**. If it loses control, the id dominates.

### The Superego
- The **moral aspect**: internalized parental/societal values, usually by **age 5–6**.
- **Two components:**
  1. **Conscience**: behaviors **punished** — what one should NOT do; causes guilt/shame.
  2. **Ego-ideal**: behaviors **praised** — what one SHOULD strive toward.
- Replaces parental control with **self-control**; strives for **moral perfection**, admits no compromise.
- The ego is caught between **three masters**: id, reality, superego.

| Structure | Principle | Thinking | Domain |
|---|---|---|---|
| Id | Pleasure principle | Primary-process | Unconscious |
| Ego | Reality principle | Secondary-process | Conscious + Unconscious |
| Superego | Morality principle | — | Mostly unconscious |

## Anxiety: A Threat to the Ego

- **Anxiety**: fear and dread without obvious cause; an "objectless fear." Fundamental to all neurotic/psychotic behavior.
- **Prototype of all anxiety**: the **birth trauma** — at birth the infant is thrust into a hostile environment, nervous system bombarded; first experience of anxiety (tension/fear that id needs won't be met).
- **Traumatic anxiety**: ego overwhelmed → state of **infantile helplessness** at any age.

### Reality Anxiety (Objective Anxiety)
- Fear of **real external dangers** (fire, animals, cars); serves the **positive purpose** of guiding escape; subsides when the threat passes. Can become extreme. **Source: external world.**

### Neurotic Anxiety
- Rooted in **childhood conflict** between instinctual gratification and reality.
- **Unconscious fear of being punished** for id-dominated behavior — fear of the **consequences** of gratification, not the instincts themselves.
- Conflict: **id vs. ego**; has some basis in reality.

### Moral Anxiety
- Conflict: **id vs. superego**; a **fear of one's own conscience**.
- An impulse contrary to moral code → superego retaliates with **shame/guilt**. Proportional to superego strength. **Source: conscience.**

### The Purpose of Anxiety
- A **warning signal** that the ego is threatened; induces tension → becomes a **drive** to be reduced.
- Ego's rational options: (1) flee the situation; (2) inhibit the impulse; (3) obey conscience; (4) if these fail → **defense mechanisms**.

## Defenses against Anxiety

- **Defense mechanisms**: nonrational, unconscious ego strategies against anxiety from everyday conflicts.
- All share two traits: (1) they are **denials/distortions of reality**; (2) they operate **unconsciously**.
- We usually use **several at once**; defenses must always operate to some degree. When they break down → **overwhelming anxiety** → neurotic/psychotic symptoms. They are therefore **necessary to mental health**.

> **KEY NOTE:** Defense mechanisms were systematically elaborated by **Anna Freud** in *The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defense* (1936).

### Repression
- **Most fundamental and frequently used** defense; **involuntary removal** of content from awareness (unconscious forgetting).
- Operates on memories, present perceptions, even physiological functioning (e.g., repressing sex drive → impotence).
- Hard to eliminate (circular trap); **basis of much of Freud's theory** and all neurotic behavior.

### Denial
- Denying the existence of an **external threat/traumatic event** (e.g., terminally ill person denying death). Focuses on **external** reality (vs. repression's internal content).

### Reaction Formation
- Defending against a disturbing impulse by **expressing the opposite**, usually in **exaggerated, rigid** form (e.g., repressed lust → crusading against pornography).

### Projection
- **Attributing** one's disturbing impulse to **someone else**: "I don't hate him. He hates me."

### Regression
- **Retreating to behaviors of an earlier, more secure stage** (usually a childhood psychosexual stage) under overwhelming stress (e.g., adult tantrum).

### Rationalization
- **Reinterpreting behavior** to seem rational/acceptable (e.g., fired person: "I didn't like that job anyway").

### Displacement
- **Shifting id impulses** from a threatening/unavailable object to an available substitute (e.g., yelling at the dog after the boss). The substitute reduces tension less satisfactorily → buildup of tension.

### Sublimation
- A **form of displacement** diverting instinctual energy into **socially valued behaviors** (sex → art; aggression → athletics).
- A compromise (incomplete satisfaction), but the **only entirely positive/adaptive** defense.

### Summary Table of Defense Mechanisms

| Defense | Core Action | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Repression** | Unconscious removal of anxiety-causing content | Forgetting a traumatic event |
| **Denial** | Denying an external threat/traumatic event | Terminal patient denying prognosis |
| **Reaction Formation** | Expressing the opposite impulse | Crusading against pornography from repressed lust |
| **Projection** | Attributing one's impulse to another | "He hates me" (when one hates him) |
| **Regression** | Retreating to an earlier stage | Adult tantrum under stress |
| **Rationalization** | Reinterpreting behavior as acceptable | "I didn't like that job anyway" |
| **Displacement** | Shifting impulse to an available substitute | Yelling at dog after boss criticizes you |
| **Sublimation** | Diverting energy into valued activities | Aggression → competitive sport |

## Psychosexual Stages of Personality Development

### Growing Up Is Not Easy
- Adult personality is **shaped by age 5**. Strong sexual conflicts in young children revolve around **erogenous zones**, each dominant at a different age → **psychosexual stages**.
- Each stage's **conflict must be resolved** before progressing.

**Fixation**: failure to move from a stage (conflict unresolved or needs over-gratified); libido stays invested there, leaving less for later stages.

### Full Table of Psychosexual Stages

| Stage | Ages | Erogenous Zone | Key Conflict | Fixation Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Oral** | Birth–1 yr | Mouth | Dependency vs. independence; weaning | Oral passive or oral aggressive |
| **Anal** | 1–3 yr | Anus | Toilet training; control vs. autonomy | Anal retentive or anal expulsive |
| **Phallic** | 3–6 yr | Genitals | Oedipus/Electra complex; castration anxiety/penis envy | Phallic character |
| **Latency** | ~6 yr–Puberty | None | Sublimation of sex instinct | — |
| **Genital** | Puberty–Adulthood | Genitals (mature) | Mature love and work | Inability to love/work maturely |

### The Oral Stage (Birth–~1 Year)
- Pleasure from the **mouth**; infant totally **dependent on mother** (primary libidinal object). How she responds shapes the infant's sense of the world.
- **Two modes:**
  1. **Oral incorporative** (taking in): if over-gratified → **oral passive personality**: optimism, dependency, gullibility, trust.
  2. **Oral aggressive/sadistic** (biting): if fixated → **oral aggressive personality**: pessimism, hostility, sarcasm ("biting remarks"), envy, exploitation.
- Ends at **weaning**.

### The Anal Stage (~1–3 Years)
- Focus: **anus**; pleasure from **defecation**. Major event: **toilet training** — first externally regulated gratification and first encounter with **external authority**.
- Child can use feces as a **weapon** (giving/withholding).
- **Two modes:**
  1. **Anal expulsive**: fixation → **anal expulsive personality**: messy, disorganized, destructive, cruel, hostile.
  2. **Anal retentive**: fixation → **anal retentive personality** (the **anal triad**): **Orderliness + Parsimony + Obstinacy**.

> **HIGH YIELD MCQ:** Anal retentive triad = Orderliness + Parsimony + Obstinacy.

### The Phallic Stage (~3–6 Years)
- Erogenous zone: **genitals**; child becomes curious about anatomical differences. Central conflict: **incestuous fantasies** about the opposite-sex parent. **Superego is formed** during resolution.

**Boys — Oedipus Complex:** boy desires mother, sees father as rival, fears **castration** (**castration anxiety**) → resolves via **identification** with father → internalizes his values; **superego crystallizes**; adopts masculine identity.

**Girls — Electra Complex:** girls discover they lack a penis → **penis envy**; blame mother, transfer love to father. No castration anxiety → **weaker motivation** to resolve; resolve via identification with mother. Freud's controversial claim: women develop **weaker superegos** (criticized as sexist).

**Phallic character:** narcissistic, reckless, boastful (males); flirtatious/seductive or frigid (females).

### The Latency Period (~6 Years–Puberty)
- **Not a true stage** (no new erogenous zone). Sexual impulses **repressed/sublimated** into social skills, intellect, same-sex peers. A period of relative **calm**.

### The Genital Stage (Puberty–Adulthood)
- **Sexual impulses reawaken**, now directed outward in mature form. If earlier stages resolved → **mature love and work**.
- Freud's healthy ideal: **"lieben und arbeiten"** — to love and to work. **Genital character** = the mature person.

### The Importance of Childhood
- Personality **crystallized by age 5**; early parent–child interactions are most critical. Unique character arises from **unique childhood relationships**.

## Questions about Human Nature

| Dimension | Freud's Position |
|---|---|
| **Free will vs. Determinism** | Completely **deterministic** — unconscious, irrational forces |
| **Nature vs. Nurture** | **Both** — biological instincts shaped by early experience |
| **Past vs. Present** | **Past** — determined by the first 5 years |
| **Uniqueness vs. Universality** | Universal drives/stages with **unique** expression via fixations/defenses |
| **Optimism vs. Pessimism** | **Pessimistic** — governed by primitive forces; civilization requires renunciation |
| **Conscious vs. Unconscious** | **Unconscious dominant** — we are largely strangers to ourselves |

## Assessment in Freud's Theory

### Free Association
- Patient **reclines on the couch** and says whatever comes to mind without censorship, to bypass defenses and reach unconscious material. Freud listened out of sight.
- **Resistance**: reluctance/inability to discuss certain material (forgetting, changing subject, going blank, hostility) — itself **diagnostic** of repressed conflict. No associations are truly random.

### Dream Analysis
- Dreams = the **"royal road to the unconscious"**; express unconscious wishes in disguise.
- **Manifest content**: the remembered surface story. **Latent content**: the hidden symbolic unconscious wish.
- **Dream work** transforms latent → manifest via:
  - **Condensation**: several latent elements combined into one image.
  - **Displacement**: emotional significance shifted to an innocuous object.
  - **Symbolism**: latent content represented by symbols.
  - **Secondary elaboration**: imposing a logical narrative after waking.

### Other Assessment Tools
- **Parapraxes** (Freudian slips): errors in speaking/writing/forgetting that reveal unconscious wishes (*The Psychopathology of Everyday Life*, 1901).
- **Transference**: patient transfers feelings from early relationships onto the analyst — a key therapeutic tool.
- **Countertransference**: analyst's emotional reactions to the patient — must be monitored.

### Uncovering the Conflicts
- Goal: bring unconscious material to **conscious awareness** by dismantling defenses, interpreting resistance, working through transference. **Insight** = conscious recognition of previously unconscious material.

## Criticisms of Freud's Research

### Freud's Negative Views on Experimental Research
- Freud **opposed experimental research**, favoring the case study.
- He **never recorded sessions** (wrote from memory); small, unrepresentative **sample** (upper-class Viennese neurotic women); **retrospective reconstruction** of childhood; **experimenter bias**; no control groups.

### Scientific Testing of Freudian Concepts
- **Unconscious processing**: strongly supported (implicit memory, priming, subliminal perception).
- **Repression**: mixed evidence. **Defense mechanisms**: projection and reaction formation have lab support.
- **Psychosexual stages / Oedipus complex**: little/no cross-cultural support.
- **Fisher & Greenberg** (1977, 1996): meta-analyses found mixed support.
- Key criticism: **unfalsifiability** — many concepts cannot be disproved.

## Extensions of Freudian Theory

### Ego Psychology: Anna Freud (1895–1982)
- Freud's youngest daughter; **loyal** to his theory. Major work: ***The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defense*** (1936).
- **Contributions:**
  - Expanded/systematized the **defense mechanisms**.
  - Elevated the **ego** to a more **autonomous** structure.
  - Pioneered **child psychoanalysis**; worked with WWII-traumatized children in London; founded the **Hampstead Child Therapy Clinic**.
- Laid the groundwork for later **ego psychology** (Hartmann, Kris, Loewenstein).

---

## Reflections on Freud's Theory

### The Decline of Freudian Psychotherapy
- Declined since the 1960s–70s due to: shorter effective therapies (CBT, behavioral, pharmacotherapy); psychoanalysis being **time-consuming and expensive**; limited evidence of effectiveness; managed care preferring time-limited treatments.

### Criticisms
- **Sexist** (penis envy, weaker superegos); **culturally biased** (Victorian Vienna); **unscientific/unfalsifiable**; **overemphasis on sex/aggression** (rejected by Adler, Jung); **unrepresentative sample**; distorted by Freud's **personal biases**.

### Lasting Influence
- The **unconscious mind**; **defense mechanisms** (heavily researched); **developmental approach** (childhood shapes adulthood); **talk therapy** (Freud and Breuer's "talking cure" — origin of all modern psychotherapy); dream analysis, transference, countertransference; huge cultural influence; sparked all later theories (neo-Freudians, humanists, behaviorists).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Freud: 1856–1939.** Born Freiberg, Moravia; ~80 years in Vienna; died in London after fleeing the Nazis.

2. **Three structures:** id (pleasure principle), ego (reality principle), superego (morality). The ego serves three masters: id, reality, superego.

3. **Id** = pleasure principle + **primary-process thought**; **ego** = reality principle + **secondary-process thought**.

4. **Superego components:** **conscience** (punishments → guilt) and **ego-ideal** (praises → ideals).

5. **Three anxieties:** reality (external), neurotic (id vs. ego), moral (id vs. superego). **Birth trauma** = prototype of all anxiety.

6. **Repression** = most fundamental/frequent defense. All defenses distort reality and are unconscious.

7. **Anal retentive triad:** **Orderliness + Parsimony + Obstinacy**. Anal expulsive → messiness, cruelty, destructiveness.

8. **Superego forms during Oedipus resolution** in the **phallic stage (3–6)**. Boys: castration anxiety + identification with father; girls: penis envy + identification with mother (less complete → weaker superego per Freud).

9. **Stages in order:** Oral (0–1) → Anal (1–3) → Phallic (3–6) → Latency (6–puberty) → Genital (puberty on).

10. **"Royal road to the unconscious"** = dream analysis. Manifest vs. latent content; dream work via condensation, displacement, symbolism, secondary elaboration.

11. **Clark University, 1909** (Worcester, MA); honorary doctorate from G. Stanley Hall; APsaA founded two years later.

12. **Anna Freud (1895–1982):** *The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defense* (1936); founded ego psychology; pioneered child psychoanalysis.

13. **Sublimation** = only entirely adaptive defense (a form of displacement); id energy → art, science, athletics.

14. **Death instinct (Thanatos)** developed late (post-WWI), limited acceptance; its **aggressive drive** was more accepted.

15. **Freud opposed experimental research**; small unrepresentative retrospective sample, experimenter bias → criticisms of unfalsifiability.
