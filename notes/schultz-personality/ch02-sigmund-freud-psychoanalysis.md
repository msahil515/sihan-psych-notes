# Ch 2: Sigmund Freud: Psychoanalysis

Freud's **psychoanalysis** is the first formal theory of personality and remains the most influential. It proposes that behavior is driven by unconscious instinctual forces, shaped by early childhood experiences, and mediated by three mental structures (id, ego, superego). The theory covers levels of consciousness, the nature of anxiety and defense, and a sequence of psychosexual stages through which personality is formed by age 5.

---

## The Life of Freud (1856–1939)

### The Early Years

- Born **1856** in **Freiberg, Moravia** (now Pribor, Czech Republic); died **1939** in London.
- Father: Jacob Freud, a relatively unsuccessful wool merchant; **40 years old** when Sigmund was born; strict and authoritarian.
- Mother: Amalia Freud (father's **third wife**); only **20 years old** at Sigmund's birth; warm, protective, devoted to Sigmund.
- Freud felt **passionate, even sexual attachment** to his mother — set the stage conceptually for the **Oedipus complex**.
- Family moved: Freiberg → Leipzig → **Vienna** (when Freud was 4); Freud remained in Vienna for almost 80 years.
- Eight children in the family (including two adult half-brothers); Freud resented all siblings as competitors for his mother's attention.
- Lifelong traits: **high self-confidence, intense ambition, dreams of fame and glory**.
- Famous quote: "A man who has been the indisputable favorite of his mother keeps for life the feeling of a conqueror, that confidence of success that often induces real success."
- Entered high school a year early; frequently at the head of his class.
- Languages: German, Hebrew, Latin, Greek, French, English; self-taught Italian and Spanish.
- Chose medicine not out of desire to be a physician but as a path to **scientific research** and the fame he craved.

### The Cocaine Episode

- While in medical school, Freud experimented with **cocaine** (then legal).
- Published an article in **1884** about cocaine's beneficial effects, hoping for fame.
- The article was later judged a major contributor to the **cocaine epidemic** that swept Europe and the United States into the 1920s.
- Freud used cocaine approximately **1884–1896**, then switched to wine.
- Spent the rest of his life trying to erase references to his cocaine endorsements from his bibliography.

### Finding the Sexual Basis of Neurosis

- Established private practice as a **clinical neurologist** in **1881**.
- Studied several months in Paris with **Jean-Martin Charcot**, a pioneer in the use of **hypnosis**, who alerted Freud to the possible **sexual basis of neurosis**.
- Charcot's famous remark: *"In this sort of case, it's always a question of the genitals — always, always, always."*
- A Viennese colleague described a patient's anxiety stemming from her husband's 18-year impotence → reinforced Freud's thinking about sexual origins of emotional disturbance.

### Childhood Sexual Abuse: Fact or Fantasy?

- After years of clinical practice, Freud became convinced that **sexual conflicts** were the primary cause of all neuroses.
- Many women patients reported **traumatic sexual experiences in childhood** (seduction by older male relatives, typically the father) — what we now call child abuse/incest.
- He initially published the **seduction theory**: early sexual traumas cause adult neurosis.
- About a year later, Freud **retracted** this theory, claiming the abuse reports were **fantasies**.
- His revised position: the fantasies were **psychologically real** to patients; because they still focused on sex, sex remained the cause of neurosis.
- Key quote (1898): "The most immediate and most significant causes of neurotic illness are to be found in factors arising from sexual life."
- Controversy: contemporary scholars suggest more of Freud's patients may have been telling the truth than he was willing to admit; possible reason for retraction: protecting fathers (possibly his own) from accusations.
- Freud later admitted privately that traumatic childhood experiences were frequently genuine, and that he had "analyzed and cured several cases of real incest."

### Freud's Own Sex Life

- Paradox: Freud emphasized sex in emotional life but had many personal sexual conflicts.
- Described as **shy and afraid of women**; was a **virgin when he married at age 30**.
- Married **Martha Bernays** after a 4-year engagement.
- Apparently **ended his own sex life at age 41**: "Sexual excitation is of no more use to a person like me."
- Occasionally impotent during marriage; also abstained due to dislike of available birth control methods.
- Blamed Martha for the termination of sexual life; resented her repeated pregnancies.

### Freud's Neurotic Episode

- In his **40s**, Freud experienced a **severe neurotic episode**: "odd states of mind not intelligible to consciousness — cloudy thoughts and veiled doubts."
- Physical symptoms: **migraine headaches, urinary problems, spastic colon**, anxiety about travel, fear of open spaces, worry about dying.
- Diagnosed himself with **anxiety neurosis** and **neurasthenia** (characterized by weakness, worry, digestive and circulatory disturbances).
- Traced his condition to **accumulated sexual tension**:
  - Neurasthenia in men → caused by masturbation.
  - Anxiety neurosis → caused by abnormal sexual practices (coitus interruptus, abstinence).
- His self-diagnosis embodied his own theory: "Freud's theory of actual neurosis is thus a theory of his own neurotic symptoms" (Krüll, 1986).

### Analyzing Freud's Dreams

- Freud **psychoanalyzed himself** through the study of his own dreams — continued for the rest of his life.
- Quote: "The chief patient I am busy with is myself."
- Through dream analysis he realized the extent of his hostility toward his father, his childhood sexual longings for his mother, and a sex wish toward his eldest daughter.
- Much of his theory was **formulated around his own neurotic conflicts** and childhood experiences as filtered through dream interpretation.
- Quote: "The most important patient for me was my own person."

### Freud's Ideas Attract Attention

- Weekly discussion group formed — first meeting topic: **psychology of cigar making**.
- Early disciples included **Carl Jung** and **Alfred Adler**, who later broke with Freud and developed their own theories.
- Freud considered them **traitors** and never forgave them.
- Freud's aunt's critique: "The trouble with you, Sigi, is that you just don't understand people."

### Freud Comes to America

- **1909**: Invited to give lectures at **Clark University**, Worcester, Massachusetts; received an **honorary doctoral degree**.
- Freud disliked the United States (informality, bad cooking, scarcity of bathrooms).
- American followers founded the **American Psychoanalytic Association** and **New York Psychoanalytic Society** two years after his visit.
- By **1920**, more than **200 books** on his work had been published in the United States.
- **Dr. Benjamin Spock's** child care books (which influenced generations of American children) were based on Freud's teachings.

### The Final Years

- From **1923** until death in **1939**: underwent **33 operations** for **cancer of the mouth** (attributed to smoking 20 cigars daily).
- Portions of palate and upper jaw surgically removed; experienced near-constant pain but refused medication for most of this period.
- **1933**: Nazis publicly burned his books when they came to power in Germany.
- Freud's response: "In the Middle Ages they would have burnt me; nowadays they are content with burning my books."
- **1938**: Nazis occupied Austria; Freud refused to leave until daughter **Anna was arrested** (later released); four sisters died in Nazi concentration camps.
- Died **September 1939** in London; physician **Max Schur** administered three morphine injections at Freud's own request.
- The New York Times called him "the most effective disturber of complacency in our time."

## Instincts: The Propelling Forces of the Personality

- **Instincts** (German: *Trieb* — a driving force or impulse): basic elements of personality; the motivating forces that drive behavior and determine its direction.
- Instincts are a form of **energy** — transformed physiological energy — connecting bodily needs with mental wishes.
- Stimuli for instincts are **internal** (e.g., hunger, thirst).
- Process: bodily need → physiological excitation → mind transforms this into a **wish** → this wish is the instinct → drives behavior to satisfy the need.
- The instinct is **not the bodily state itself** but the bodily need **transformed into a mental state** (a wish).
- When the body is in a state of need, the person experiences **tension or pressure**.
- The **aim** of an instinct: satisfy the need and reduce tension.
- Freud's theory is therefore **homeostatic**: motivation to restore and maintain physiological equilibrium.
- Instincts always influence behavior — we cannot escape internal pressures as we might external stimuli.
- **Psychic energy** can be displaced to substitute objects — primary importance for personality diversity.
- All adult interests, preferences, and attitudes are **displacements of energy** from original objects satisfying instinctual needs.

### Two Types of Instincts

#### The Life Instincts (Eros)

- Serve survival of the **individual and the species**: food, water, air, and **sex**.
- Oriented toward **growth and development**.
- Psychic energy manifested by life instincts: **libido**.
- **Cathexis**: investment of libido in an object or person.
- Most important life instinct for personality: **sex** (broadly defined — not just erotic but all pleasurable behaviors and thoughts, including "affectionate and friendly impulses").
- **Erogenous zones**: mouth, anus, sex organs — sources of erotic wishes.
- Freud viewed humans as predominantly **pleasure-seeking beings**.

#### The Death Instincts (Thanatos)

- All living things decay and die, returning to inanimate state → unconscious wish to die.
- Key component: the **aggressive drive** — the wish to die turned against others; compels us to destroy, conquer, and kill.
- Freud came to view aggression as compelling a part of human nature as sex.
- Developed late in Freud's career, influenced by his own aging and cancer, the death of his daughter at age 26, and the carnage of **World War I**.
- Concept achieved **only limited acceptance** even among dedicated followers.
- One psychoanalyst called it fit to be "relegated to the dustbin of history" (Sulloway, 1979).

| Instinct Type | Also Called | Purpose | Energy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Life instincts | Eros | Survival, growth, pleasure | Libido |
| Death instincts | Thanatos | Decay, destruction, aggression | — |

## The Levels of Personality

Freud's original **topographic model**: three levels of consciousness.

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Conscious** | Sensations and experiences of which one is aware at any given moment; the "tip of the iceberg" |
| **Preconscious** | Storehouse of memories, perceptions, thoughts not currently conscious but easily retrievable |
| **Unconscious** | Vast repository of instincts, repressed wishes, desires; primary driving force of behavior; the largest portion |

- Freud used the **iceberg metaphor**: conscious = tip above water; unconscious = vast invisible portion below.
- The **unconscious** is the focus of psychoanalytic theory; cannot be seen or controlled.
- The **preconscious** sits between: material is just below awareness and can be brought to consciousness with effort.
- Freud later revised this model by introducing the **structural model** (id, ego, superego); both models coexist.

## The Structure of Personality

Freud's **structural model** introduced three components:

### The Id

- Corresponds to Freud's earlier notion of the **unconscious**.
- The **reservoir of instincts and libido** — the psychic energy that drives the other two components.
- Operates according to the **pleasure principle**: seeks immediate gratification, avoids pain.
- Has **no awareness of reality**; knows only instant gratification — selfish, amoral, insistent, rash.
- Like a **newborn baby**: no knowledge of how to bring about satisfaction on its own.
- Two ways id attempts to satisfy needs:
  1. **Reflex action**: automatic responses (crying, sneezing).
  2. **Primary-process thought**: wish-fulfilling hallucinatory or fantasy experience (childlike thinking).
- Cannot delay or postpone satisfaction for any reason.

### The Ego

- The **rational master** of the personality.
- Purpose: **not to thwart the id** but to help the id obtain tension reduction in a realistic way.
- Operates according to the **reality principle**: perceives reality and determines appropriate times, places, and objects for id satisfaction.
- Uses **secondary-process thought**: mature, rational cognitive processes (perception, recognition, judgment, memory).
- Controls and postpones id impulses — does not eliminate them, only delays or redirects.
- Freud's analogy: the ego is a **rider on a horse** — must guide and check the raw power of the id (horse), or it could bolt.
- Serves **two masters**: the id and reality (constantly mediating and compromising).
- Gets its power and energy **from the id** — never truly independent of it.
- If ego loses control, the id may dominate → danger to self and society.

### The Superego

- The **moral aspect of personality**: internalization of parental and societal values.
- Usually learned by **age 5 or 6**.
- **Two components:**
  1. **Conscience**: behaviors for which the child has been **punished** — what one should NOT do; causes guilt/shame when violated.
  2. **Ego-ideal**: behaviors for which the child has been **praised** — what one SHOULD strive toward; ideal standards.
- Through internalization: parental control is replaced by **self-control**; rewards and punishments become self-administered.
- Strives for **moral perfection** — not pleasure (id) nor realistic goals (ego), but perfection above all.
- As relentless and demanding as the id; admits no compromise.
- The ego is caught between **three masters**: id, reality, and superego.

| Structure | Principle | Thinking | Domain |
|---|---|---|---|
| Id | Pleasure principle | Primary-process | Unconscious |
| Ego | Reality principle | Secondary-process | Conscious + Unconscious |
| Superego | Morality principle | — | Mostly unconscious |

## Anxiety: A Threat to the Ego

- **Anxiety**: a feeling of fear and dread without an obvious cause; an "objectless fear."
- Freud made anxiety **fundamental** to all neurotic and psychotic behavior.
- **Prototype of all anxiety**: the **birth trauma**.
  - Fetus in womb: stable, every need met immediately.
  - At birth: thrust into hostile environment; nervous system bombarded with stimuli; massive motor movements, heightened breathing, increased heart rate.
  - This birth trauma — tension and fear that id instincts won't be satisfied — is the **first experience of anxiety**.
- **Traumatic anxiety**: when the ego cannot cope with anxiety and is overwhelmed; person reduced to a state of **infantile helplessness** regardless of age.
- Freud proposed **three types of anxiety**:

### Reality Anxiety (Objective Anxiety)

- Fear of **real dangers** in the real world (fire, hurricane, earthquake, wild animals, speeding cars).
- Serves the **positive purpose** of guiding behavior to escape actual dangers.
- Fear subsides when the threat passes.
- Can be carried to extremes (e.g., unable to leave home for fear of being hit by a car).
- **Source**: external world.

### Neurotic Anxiety

- Basis in **childhood conflict** between instinctual gratification and reality.
- Children punished for overtly expressing sexual or aggressive impulses → wish to gratify certain id impulses generates anxiety.
- **Unconscious fear of being punished** for impulsively displaying id-dominated behavior.
- Fear is NOT of the instincts themselves, but of the **consequences of gratifying them**.
- Conflict is between **id and ego**.
- Has some **basis in reality** (children are actually punished for id-driven behavior).

### Moral Anxiety

- Results from conflict between **id and superego**.
- Essentially a **fear of one's own conscience**.
- When motivated to express an instinctual impulse contrary to moral code, superego retaliates with **shame or guilt** (being "conscience-stricken").
- Proportional to how well-developed the superego is: strong conscience → greater moral anxiety.
- Source: **conscience/superego**, from within.

### The Purpose of Anxiety

- Anxiety is a **warning signal**: something is amiss; the ego is threatened.
- Anxiety induces tension → becomes a **drive** (like hunger or thirst) that must be reduced.
- Ego's rational options:
  1. Run away from the threatening situation.
  2. Inhibit the impulsive need.
  3. Obey the dictates of conscience.
  4. If rational techniques fail: resort to **defense mechanisms**.

## Defenses against Anxiety

- **Defense mechanisms**: nonrational, unconscious strategies the ego uses to defend itself against anxiety provoked by conflicts of everyday life.
- Two characteristics shared by ALL defense mechanisms:
  1. They are **denials or distortions of reality** (necessary, but distortions nonetheless).
  2. They all operate **unconsciously** — we are unaware of using them.
- We rarely use just **one** defense mechanism — typically use **several simultaneously**.
- Freud believed defenses must **always be in operation** to some degree.
- When defenses break down (under unusual stress or during psychoanalysis): **overwhelming anxiety** → depression, worthlessness → neurotic or psychotic symptoms.
- Defense mechanisms are therefore **necessary to mental health**.

> **KEY NOTE:** Defense mechanisms were systematically elaborated by **Anna Freud** in *The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defense* (1936).

### Repression

- The **most fundamental and most frequently used** defense mechanism.
- **Involuntary removal** of something from conscious awareness — unconscious type of forgetting.
- Can operate on:
  - Memories of situations or people
  - Perception of present events (failing to see disturbing things right in front of us)
  - Body's physiological functioning (e.g., repressing sex drive → impotence)
- Difficult to eliminate once operating: circular trap — to remove it, one must realize the danger no longer exists, but one can't realize this without releasing the repression.
- **Basis of much of Freud's personality theory**; involved in all neurotic behavior.

### Denial

- Related to repression; involves **denying the existence of an external threat or traumatic event**.
- Example: terminally ill person denying imminence of death; parents keeping a deceased child's room unchanged.
- Focuses on **external** reality (whereas repression focuses on internal mental content).

### Reaction Formation

- Defending against a disturbing impulse by **actively expressing the opposite impulse**.
- Example: person threatened by sexual longings becomes a crusader against pornography; person with aggressive impulses becomes overly friendly and solicitous.
- "Lust becomes virtue and hatred becomes love" in the unconscious.
- Usually involves an **exaggerated, rigid** expression of the opposite impulse.

### Projection

- **Attributing** a disturbing impulse to **someone else** rather than acknowledging it in oneself.
- "I don't hate him. He hates me." — impulse is still manifested but in a less threatening way.
- Lustful, aggressive, or other unacceptable impulses are perceived as belonging to others.
- Example: a mother projecting her sex drive onto her adolescent daughter.

### Regression

- **Retreating to behaviors characteristic of an earlier, more secure developmental stage**.
- Usually involves return to one of the psychosexual stages of childhood.
- Triggered when current stress is overwhelming.
- Example: adult throwing a tantrum, becoming excessively dependent, or engaging in childish behaviors under stress.

### Rationalization

- **Reinterpreting behavior** to make it seem more rational and therefore more acceptable.
- Excusing or justifying threatening thoughts or actions with plausible-sounding explanations.
- The person who is fired: "I didn't like that job anyway."
- The rejected lover suddenly finds many faults in the person who rejected them.
- Less threatening to blame others or circumstances than to blame oneself.

### Displacement

- **Shifting id impulses** from a threatening or unavailable object to a substitute object that is available.
- Example: child who hates parents but fears punishment hits a younger sibling; adult who hates boss shouts at the dog.
- The substitute object will **not reduce tension as satisfactorily** as the original object.
- Accumulation of undischarged tension → increasing drive to find new outlets.

### Sublimation

- A **form of displacement** in which instinctual energy itself is altered and diverted into **socially acceptable and admirable behaviors**.
- Sexual energy sublimated into artistic creativity; aggressive energy into athletic competition.
- Freud believed many human achievements — particularly artistic ones — are sublimated id impulses.
- Like displacement, it is a compromise: does **not bring total satisfaction** → buildup of undischarged tension.
- Sublimation is the **only defense mechanism considered entirely positive/adaptive**.

### Summary Table of Defense Mechanisms

| Defense Mechanism | Core Action | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Repression** | Unconscious removal of anxiety-causing content from awareness | Forgetting a traumatic event |
| **Denial** | Denying existence of an external threat or traumatic event | Terminally ill patient denying prognosis |
| **Reaction Formation** | Expressing the opposite of the disturbing impulse | Crusading against pornography due to repressed lust |
| **Projection** | Attributing one's own disturbing impulse to someone else | "He hates me" (when one hates him) |
| **Regression** | Retreating to behaviors of an earlier developmental stage | Adult throwing tantrum under stress |
| **Rationalization** | Reinterpreting behavior to make it seem acceptable | "I didn't like that job anyway" after being fired |
| **Displacement** | Shifting impulse from unavailable to available substitute | Yelling at dog after boss criticizes you |
| **Sublimation** | Diverting instinctual energy into socially valued activities | Channeling aggression into competitive sport |

## Psychosexual Stages of Personality Development

### Growing Up Is Not Easy

- Adult personality **firmly shaped and crystallized by age 5**.
- Freud observed that patients' memories invariably reached far back into childhood → adult neurosis formed in early years.
- Strong sexual conflicts exist in infants and young children, revolving around **specific body regions** (erogenous zones).
- Each erogenous zone assumes importance at a different age → **psychosexual stages of development**.
- In each stage, a **conflict must be resolved** before progressing to the next.

**Fixation**: when a person is unable or unwilling to move from one stage (conflict unresolved, or needs excessively gratified). A portion of libido remains invested in that stage, leaving less energy for subsequent stages.

### Full Table of Psychosexual Stages

| Stage | Ages | Erogenous Zone | Key Conflict | Fixation Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Oral** | Birth–1 yr | Mouth | Dependency vs. independence; weaning | Oral passive or oral aggressive personality |
| **Anal** | 1–3 yr | Anus | Toilet training; control vs. autonomy | Anal retentive or anal expulsive personality |
| **Phallic** | 3–6 yr | Genitals | Oedipus/Electra complex; castration anxiety/penis envy | Phallic character (narcissism, recklessness) |
| **Latency** | ~6 yr–Puberty | None | Sublimation of sex instinct | — |
| **Genital** | Puberty–Adulthood | Genitals (mature) | Achieving mature love and work | Inability to love/work maturely |

---

### The Oral Stage (Birth–~1 Year)

- Principal source of pleasure: the **mouth** (sucking, biting, swallowing).
- Infant is totally **dependent on mother/caregiver** — the primary object of the infant's libido.
- How mother responds to id demands shapes the infant's experience of the world as good/bad, safe/perilous.
- **Two modes of oral behavior:**
  1. **Oral incorporative behavior** (taking in): first to occur; pleasurable stimulation of mouth by others and food.
     - If excessively gratified → **oral passive personality**: excessive optimism, dependency, gullibility, trust ("swallows" anything they're told).
  2. **Oral aggressive / oral sadistic behavior** (biting, spitting out): occurs during painful teething; infant views mother with hatred as well as love.
     - If fixated → **oral aggressive personality**: excessive pessimism, hostility, aggressiveness, sarcasm ("biting remarks"), envy, tendency to exploit and manipulate.
- Stage concludes at **weaning**.

### The Anal Stage (~1–3 Years; Begins ~18 Months)

- New focus: the **anus**; pleasure derived from **defecation**.
- Major event: **toilet training** — first time gratification of an instinctual impulse is externally regulated.
- First significant encounter with **external authority** imposing demands of reality and morality on the child.
- Child discovers feces can be used as a **weapon** against parents (giving or withholding on demand).
- **Two modes and fixation types:**
  1. **Anal expulsive** (letting go, expelling): pleasure in expelling; if fixated → **anal expulsive personality**: messy, disorganized, destructive, cruel, hostile.
  2. **Anal retentive** (holding back, withholding): pleasure in withholding; if fixated → **anal retentive personality** (Freud's classic triad):
     - **Orderliness** (excessive cleanliness, tidiness)
     - **Parsimony** (miserliness, stinginess)
     - **Obstinacy** (stubbornness, inflexibility)
- These three traits together are known as the **"anal character"** or **anal triad**.

> **HIGH YIELD MCQ:** The anal retentive triad = Orderliness + Parsimony + Obstinacy.

### The Phallic Stage (~3–6 Years)

- Erogenous zone: the **genitals**.
- Child becomes curious about anatomical differences between sexes.
- Central conflict: **incestuous fantasies** about the opposite-sex parent.
- **Superego is formed** during the resolution of this stage's central conflict.

**In Boys — The Oedipus Complex:**
- Boy develops **sexual desire for his mother** and views his father as a rival.
- Fears **castration** by the father as punishment (**castration anxiety**) → this fear motivates resolution.
- Resolution via **identification** with the father (the aggressor): boy internalizes father's values and moral code.
- Result: **superego is crystallized**; Oedipus complex is destroyed (not merely repressed).
- Boy adopts **masculine sex-role identity** through identification.

**In Girls — The Electra Complex:**
- Girls discover they lack a penis → **penis envy**.
- Girl blames mother for her "castration" and transfers love/desire to father.
- Because there is no castration anxiety equivalent, girls have **less powerful motivation** to resolve the conflict.
- Resolution: gradual identification with mother.
- Freud's controversial claim: women develop **weaker superegos** than men because of less complete resolution; women remain more dependent (heavily criticized as sexist).

**Phallic character** (fixation at phallic stage): narcissistic, reckless, boastful (males); flirtatious/seductive or frigid/submissive (females).

### The Latency Period (~6 Years–Puberty)

- **Not a true psychosexual stage** — no new erogenous zone.
- Sexual impulses are **repressed and sublimated** into other activities.
- Child focuses on **social skills, intellectual development**, and same-sex peer relationships.
- A period of relative **calm** in psychosexual development; id impulses remain but are dormant.

### The Genital Stage (Puberty–Adulthood)

- **Reawakening of sexual impulses** (phallic desires re-emerge but now directed outward in mature form).
- If earlier stages have been resolved adequately → person achieves **mature capacity for love and work**.
- Focus shifts from narcissistic gratification to **genuine concern for others**.
- Freud's ideal of healthy personality: the ability to **"lieben und arbeiten"** — to love and to work.
- **Genital character**: the fully mature person, capable of adult love and productive work.

### The Importance of Childhood

- Adult personality is **crystallized by age 5**.
- Early parent–child interactions are the most critical determinants of personality.
- Child constantly tries to maximize pleasure (id demands) while parents impose reality and morality.
- Unique character type arises from **unique childhood relationships** — no two people have precisely the same experiences.

## Questions about Human Nature

Freud's positions on major philosophical dimensions of personality theory:

| Dimension | Freud's Position |
|---|---|
| **Free will vs. Determinism** | Completely **deterministic** — behavior determined by unconscious, irrational forces beyond conscious control |
| **Nature vs. Nurture** | **Both** — biological instincts (nature) shaped by early childhood experience (nurture) |
| **Past vs. Present** | **Past** is paramount — adult personality determined by the first 5 years of life |
| **Uniqueness vs. Universality** | Universal drives and stages with **unique** expression through specific fixations and defenses |
| **Optimism vs. Pessimism** | **Pessimistic** — humans are governed by primitive, irrational forces; civilization requires constant instinctual renunciation |
| **Conscious vs. Unconscious** | **Unconscious dominant** — the vast majority of mental life is unconscious; we are largely strangers to ourselves |

## Assessment in Freud's Theory

### Free Association

- Patient **reclines on the couch** and says whatever comes to mind, regardless of how embarrassing, trivial, or irrational — without censorship or editing.
- Purpose: bypass defenses, reach unconscious material.
- Freud listened from behind (out of sight) to avoid influencing responses.

**Free Association Is Not Always So Free:**
- **Resistance**: patient's reluctance or inability to discuss certain thoughts or topics.
  - May involve sudden forgetting, changing the subject, going blank, or becoming hostile.
  - Resistance itself is **diagnostic** — indicates repressed material the patient is defending against.
- Despite the name, all associations ultimately lead back to underlying conflicts — none are truly random.

### Dream Analysis

- Freud called dreams the **"royal road to the unconscious."**
- Dreams express unconscious wishes in disguised form.

**Two Levels of Dream Content:**
1. **Manifest content**: the actual, remembered content of the dream as the dreamer reports it (the surface story).
2. **Latent content**: the hidden, symbolic, unconscious wishes expressed in the dream — the real meaning.

**Dream Work**: the psychological process transforming latent content into manifest content through:
- **Condensation**: several latent elements combined into a single manifest image.
- **Displacement**: emotional significance shifted from the true object to an innocuous one.
- **Symbolism**: latent content represented by symbols (weapons, boxes, etc. for genitals).
- **Secondary elaboration**: dreamer imposes a logical narrative on the dream after waking.

### Other Assessment Tools

- **Parapraxes** (Freudian slips / *Fehlleistungen*): errors in speaking (slips of the tongue), writing, or forgetting — reveal unconscious wishes and conflicts. Analyzed in *The Psychopathology of Everyday Life* (1901).
- **Transference**: patient unconsciously transfers feelings, expectations, and conflicts from significant early relationships (e.g., parents) onto the analyst — crucial therapeutic tool.
- **Countertransference**: analyst's emotional reactions to the patient — must be monitored.

### Uncovering the Conflicts

- Goal of psychoanalytic therapy: bring unconscious material to **conscious awareness**.
- Requires dismantling defenses, interpreting resistances, and working through transference.
- **Insight**: the conscious recognition of previously unconscious material.

## Criticisms of Freud's Research

### Freud's Negative Views on Experimental Research

- Freud himself was **opposed to experimental research** — believed the case study method was superior for clinical phenomena.
- He **never recorded therapy sessions** — wrote notes from memory afterward, introducing substantial bias.
- His sample was **small, unrepresentative**: mostly upper-class Viennese women with neurotic disorders — results cannot be generalized.
- **Retrospective reconstruction**: rebuilding childhood from adult memories — subject to distortion and experimenter suggestion.
- **Experimenter bias**: Freud may have unwittingly influenced patients' reports through leading questions.
- No **control groups** or systematic data collection.

### Scientific Testing of Freudian Concepts

- Despite methodological weaknesses, researchers have attempted to test Freudian concepts:
  - **Unconscious processing**: strongly supported by cognitive neuroscience (implicit memory, priming, subliminal perception).
  - **Repression-like phenomena**: mixed evidence; some support for motivated forgetting.
  - **Defense mechanisms**: projection and reaction formation have received laboratory support.
  - **Psychosexual stages**: little empirical support for the specific sequence.
  - **Oedipus complex**: not universally supported in cross-cultural research.
- **Fisher & Greenberg** (1977, 1996): meta-analyses concluded that some Freudian concepts have empirical support, others do not.
- Key criticism: **unfalsifiability** — many psychoanalytic concepts cannot be disproved (not falsifiable in the Popperian sense).

## Extensions of Freudian Theory

### Ego Psychology: Anna Freud (1895–1982)

- Sigmund Freud's youngest daughter; remained **loyal to his theory**.
- Major work: ***The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defense*** (1936) — the most systematic account of defense mechanisms ever written.
- **Key contributions:**
  - Expanded and systematized the **list of defense mechanisms**.
  - Elevated the **ego** from servant of the id to a structure with more **autonomous functions**.
  - Emphasized **child psychoanalysis** — brought psychoanalytic therapy to children.
  - Described how the ego mediates between id, superego, and external reality more comprehensively than her father had.
- Worked extensively with children traumatized by **World War II** in London.
- Founded the **Hampstead Child Therapy Course and Clinic** in London.
- Her work laid the groundwork for later **ego psychology** (Hartmann, Kris, Loewenstein).

## Reflections on Freud's Theory

### The Decline of Freudian Psychotherapy

- Psychoanalysis as a clinical treatment has **declined significantly** since the 1960s–1970s.
- Reasons:
  - Emergence of shorter, more effective therapies (CBT, behavioral therapies, pharmacotherapy).
  - Psychoanalysis is **enormously time-consuming and expensive** (years of multiple weekly sessions).
  - Limited empirical evidence for its clinical effectiveness relative to alternatives.
  - Managed care systems prefer time-limited, evidence-based treatments.

### Criticisms of Psychoanalysis

- **Sexist**: systematically devalued women (penis envy, weaker superegos, women as "deficient men").
- **Cultural bias**: based on late-19th-century Victorian-era, upper-class Viennese culture.
- **Unscientific**: core concepts are unfalsifiable; theory can explain anything after the fact.
- **Overemphasis on sex and aggression**: many critics (including early followers Adler and Jung) rejected this.
- **Unrepresentative sample**: small, atypical patient population.
- **Freud's personal biases**: his own neuroses, sexual conflicts, and cultural context distorted the theory.

### Freud's Lasting Influence

- The concept of the **unconscious mind** — now widely accepted across psychology and neuroscience.
- **Defense mechanisms**: one of the most extensively researched areas in personality psychology.
- **Developmental approach**: childhood experiences shape adult personality — universally influential.
- **Talk therapy / psychotherapy**: Freud and Breuer invented the "talking cure" — all modern psychotherapy descends from this.
- **Dream analysis**, transference, and countertransference as clinical concepts.
- Immense influence on literature, art, film, and the broader cultural vocabulary of the 20th century.
- Sparked all subsequent personality theories: neo-Freudians, humanists, and behaviorists defined themselves partly in relation to Freud.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Freud's birth year is 1856; death year is 1939.** Born in Freiberg, Moravia (now Czech Republic); lived in Vienna for ~80 years; died in London after fleeing Nazi occupation.

2. **The three structures of personality: id (pleasure principle), ego (reality principle), superego (morality).** The ego serves three masters: the id, reality, and the superego.

3. **The id operates on the pleasure principle** using **primary-process thought** (wish-fulfilling fantasy). The ego operates on the **reality principle** using **secondary-process thought** (rational cognition).

4. **The superego has two components:** the **conscience** (internalized punishments → guilt/shame when violated) and the **ego-ideal** (internalized praises → ideal standards to strive for).

5. **Three types of anxiety:** reality anxiety (external threat), neurotic anxiety (id vs. ego conflict), moral anxiety (id vs. superego / fear of one's own conscience). The **birth trauma** is the prototype of all anxiety.

6. **Repression is the most fundamental and most frequently used defense mechanism** — all others build on it. All defense mechanisms share two features: they distort reality and they are unconscious.

7. **The anal retentive triad** (fixation at anal retentive mode): **Orderliness + Parsimony + Obstinacy** — the classic "anal character." Anal expulsive fixation → messiness, cruelty, destructiveness.

8. **The superego is formed during resolution of the Oedipus complex** in the **phallic stage** (ages 3–6). Boys resolve via castration anxiety + identification with father; girls via penis envy + identification with mother (less complete → weaker superego per Freud).

9. **Psychosexual stages in order:** Oral (0–1 yr) → Anal (1–3 yr) → Phallic (3–6 yr) → Latency (6 yr–puberty) → Genital (puberty onward). Fixation leaves libido invested in that stage.

10. **"Royal road to the unconscious"** = **dream analysis**. Dreams have manifest content (surface story) and latent content (hidden meaning). Dream work transforms latent into manifest via condensation, displacement, symbolism, and secondary elaboration.

11. **Freud lectured at Clark University in 1909** (Worcester, Massachusetts); received honorary doctorate from G. Stanley Hall; American Psychoanalytic Association founded two years later.

12. **Anna Freud (1895–1982)** wrote *The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defense* (1936) — the foundational text of ego psychology; systematized defense mechanisms; pioneered child psychoanalysis.

13. **Sublimation is the only entirely adaptive defense mechanism**: id energy redirected into socially valued activities (art, science, athletics). It is a form of displacement. All other defense mechanisms have predominantly maladaptive components.

14. **Freud's death instinct (Thanatos)** was developed late in his career (post-WWI) and achieved only limited acceptance even among loyal followers. Its component — the **aggressive drive** — was more widely accepted.

15. **Freud himself opposed experimental research** on psychoanalysis, preferring the case study method. His sample was small and unrepresentative (upper-class Viennese women with neuroses), retrospective, and subject to experimenter bias — major scientific limitations that led to later criticisms of unfalsifiability.
