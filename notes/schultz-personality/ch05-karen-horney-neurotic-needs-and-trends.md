# Ch 5: Karen Horney: Neurotic Needs and Trends

Karen Horney (1885–1952) was a neo-psychoanalytic theorist who broke from orthodox Freudianism, arguing that social forces — not biological drives — shape personality. Her theory centres on the childhood **safety need**, **basic anxiety** as the root of neurosis, and three **neurotic trends** (compliant, aggressive, detached) arising from maladaptive coping. She is also a pioneering feminist critic of Freud, proposing **womb envy** as a counter to penis envy.

---

## The Life of Horney (1885–1952)

### Early Childhood and Family Dynamics

- Born **Karen Danielsen** in a small village near Hamburg, Germany, 1885.
- Second-born child; envied her older brother **Berndt**, the adored first-born.
- Father: 50-year-old Norwegian ship's captain — religious, domineering, imperious, morose.
- Mother: 17 years younger — attractive, spirited, freethinking; told Karen she married out of fear, not love.
- Parental conflict was frequent; father spent long periods at sea.
- Karen felt unloved and inferior; diary at 16: "Why is everything beautiful on earth given to me, only not the highest thing, not love!"

### A Neglected Second-Born

- Doubted her parents wanted her; believed they loved Berndt more.
- Envied Berndt partly because he was male — girls were considered inferior.
- Confided: "I know that as a child I wanted for a long time to be a boy."
- Father intimidated her; made disparaging comments about her intelligence and appearance.

### Rebellion and Hostility

- Until age 8, acted the adoring, obedient, compliant daughter to retain her mother's affection.
- When compliance failed to secure sufficient love, she shifted tactics: became ambitious and rebellious.
- "If I couldn't be beautiful, I decided I would be smart."
- This pattern — compliant first, then hostile/ambitious — directly informed her later personality theory.
- Biographer Paris (1994): "In all her psychoanalytic writings Karen Horney was struggling to make sense of herself."

### Adolescence and the Search for Love

- At 14 developed a crush on a teacher; continued infatuations through adolescence (Seiffge-Krenke & Kirsch, 2002).
- Realized at 17 that being in love temporarily eliminated anxiety and insecurity.
- Decided at age 12 to become a doctor after being treated kindly by a physician.
- Entered the **University of Freiburg medical school in 1906** (only 6 years after the first woman had been reluctantly admitted).

### Marriage and Career

- Met two men in medical school; fell in love with one, married the other — **Oskar Horney** (political science Ph.D., later a businessman).
- Received her medical degree from the **University of Berlin in 1913**.
- Early marriage marked by great distress: crying spells, stomach pains, chronic fatigue, compulsive behaviors, frigidity, longing for death.
- Marriage ended after 17 years; had three daughters.
- Multiple love affairs; without a lover she felt lost, lonely, desperate, sometimes suicidal.

### Horney's Psychoanalysis

- Her analyst, **Karl Abraham** (Freudian loyalist), attributed her problems to residue of childhood Oedipal longings for her powerful father.
- Analysis was unsuccessful; she concluded Freudian psychoanalysis was of minimal help.
- Turned to **self-analysis**, practiced for the rest of her life.
- Strongly influenced by **Alfred Adler's** notion of compensation for inferiority feelings.
- Concluded she "needed to feel superior because of her lack of beauty and sense of inferiority as a woman, which led her to masculine protest."

### Later Career (USA)

- Most intense love affair: analyst **Erich Fromm** (15 years younger), lasted ~20 years; ended partly because she persuaded Fromm to analyze her daughter Marriane.
- **1932–1952**: faculty of psychoanalytic institutes in Chicago and New York.
- Founder of the **Association for the Advancement of Psychoanalysis** and the **American Institute for Psychoanalysis**.
- In **1941**: founded the **American Journal of Psychoanalysis**.
- Key observation: American patients' neuroses differed markedly from her earlier German patients — social and cultural forces, not biology, shape personality.

## The Childhood Need for Safety and Security

### Core Premise

- Agreed with Freud: early childhood years are crucial in shaping adult personality.
- Disagreed on specifics: **social forces**, not biological forces, determine personality development.
- No universal developmental stages; no inevitable childhood conflicts (contra Freud).
- The **social relationship between children and their parents** is the key factor.

### The Safety Need

- **Safety need**: the child's fundamental need for **security and freedom from fear** (Horney, 1937).
- Whether infants experience security and absence of fear is decisive for normal personality development.
- Security depends entirely on parental behavior.
- The major way parents undermine security: **displaying a lack of warmth and affection**.
- Children can withstand traumatic events (abrupt weaning, occasional beatings, premature sexual experiences) if they feel **wanted, loved, and secure**.

### Ways of Undermining a Child's Security

Parental behaviors that undermine security and induce hostility:
1. Obvious preference for one sibling over another
2. Unfair punishment
3. Erratic behavior
4. Promises not kept
5. Ridicule
6. Humiliation
7. Isolation of the child from peers

- Children recognize whether parental love is genuine; false demonstrations of affection do not fool children.

### Repressing Hostility toward Parents

Four reasons children repress hostility:

| Reason | Child's Internal Logic |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Helplessness** | "I have to repress my hostility because I need you." Excessively dependent state fosters helplessness; more helpless = less able to rebel. |
| **Fear** | "I must repress my hostility because I am afraid of you." Punishment, physical abuse, or subtle intimidation deepens repression. |
| **Love (paradoxical)** | Parental love is insincere but is all that is available. Child represses hostility to avoid losing even these unsatisfactory expressions. |
| **Guilt** | Children made to feel guilty or sinful for harboring resentment; the more guilt, the deeper the repression. |

- This repressed hostility, resulting from various parental behaviors undermining the safety need, produces **basic anxiety**.

## Basic Anxiety: The Foundation of Neurosis

### Definition

- **Basic anxiety**: "an insidiously increasing, all-pervading feeling of being lonely and helpless in a hostile world" (Horney, 1937, p. 89).
- Foundation on which **all later neuroses develop**.
- Inseparably tied to feelings of hostility, helplessness, and fear.
- The subjective feeling: "small, insignificant, helpless, deserted, endangered, in a world that is out to abuse, cheat, attack, humiliate, betray" (Horney, 1937, p. 92).

### Four Self-Protective Mechanisms Against Basic Anxiety

#### 1. Securing Affection
- Logic: "If you love me, you will not hurt me."
- Methods: doing whatever the other person wants, bribing others, threatening others to provide affection.

#### 2. Being Submissive
- Logic: "If I give in, I will not be hurt."
- Complying with the wishes of one person or everyone in the social environment.
- Avoiding anything that might antagonize others; never assertive or critical.
- Repressing personal desires; cannot defend against abuse.
- Submissive people typically believe they are unselfish and self-sacrificing.
- Mirrors Horney's own childhood behavior until age 8–9.

#### 3. Attaining Power
- Logic: "If I have power, no one will harm me."
- Compensating for helplessness through success or a sense of superiority.
- Mirrors Horney's shift to academic ambition after age 8–9.

#### 4. Withdrawing
- Logic: Become psychologically independent so no one can hurt you.
- Not physical but **psychological withdrawal**.
- Becomes independent of others for satisfaction of internal and external needs.
- Blunts or minimizes emotional needs; becoming aloof.
- By renouncing needs, guards against being hurt.

### Characteristics of Self-Protective Mechanisms

- **Single goal**: to defend against basic anxiety.
- Motivate the person to seek **security and reassurance**, not happiness or pleasure.
- Defense against pain, not pursuit of well-being.
- **Power and intensity**: can be more compelling than sexual or physiological needs.
- Cost: usually an **impoverished personality**.
- Using more than one mechanism creates incompatibilities that lay the groundwork for additional conflicts.

## Neurotic Needs

### Definition

- **Neurotic needs**: Ten irrational defenses against anxiety that become permanent parts of personality and affect behavior (Horney).
- Any self-protective mechanism can become so permanent that it assumes the characteristics of a drive or need.
- Called "neurotic" because they are **irrational solutions** to problems.

### The 10 Neurotic Needs

| # | Neurotic Need | Associated Trend |
|---|---------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Affection and approval | Compliant |
| 2 | A dominant partner | Compliant |
| 3 | Power | Aggressive |
| 4 | Exploitation | Aggressive |
| 5 | Prestige | Aggressive |
| 6 | Admiration | Aggressive |
| 7 | Achievement or ambition | Aggressive |
| 8 | Self-sufficiency | Detached |
| 9 | Perfection | Detached |
| 10 | Narrow limits to life | Detached |

### Key Points

- Everyone manifests these needs to some degree — none is inherently abnormal in a transient sense.
- What makes them **neurotic**: intensive and **compulsive** pursuit of their satisfaction as the "only" way to resolve basic anxiety.
- When they become neurotic, they grow increasingly severe, then **tyrannical**, dominating the personality (Hess & Hess, 2010).
- Satisfying neurotic needs does not produce safety and security — only helps escape discomfort from anxiety (treats symptoms, not cause).

## Neurotic Trends

### Definition

- **Neurotic trends**: Three categories of behaviors and attitudes toward oneself and others, representing Horney's reformulation of the 10 neurotic needs (Horney, 1945).
- Three directional movements reflecting attitudes toward the self and others.
- Involve **compulsive** attitudes and behaviors — neurotic persons are compelled to behave in accordance with at least one trend.
- Displayed **indiscriminately**, in any and all situations.

### The Three Neurotic Trends (Full Table)

| Trend | Direction | Personality Type | Associated Needs |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|------------------|
| Movement **toward** people | Approach | **Compliant personality** | Affection and approval; a dominant partner |
| Movement **against** people | Attack | **Aggressive personality** | Power; exploitation; prestige; admiration; achievement |
| Movement **away from** people | Avoidance | **Detached personality** | Self-sufficiency; perfection; narrow limits to life |

### The Compliant Personality

- Intense and continuous need for **affection and approval**.
- Urge to be loved, wanted, and protected — displayed toward everyone.
- Usually needs one dominant person (friend/spouse) to take charge of their life.
- **Manipulates** others — often seems unusually considerate, appreciative, responsive, understanding, sensitive.
- Concerned with living up to others' ideals and expectations; appears unselfish and generous.
- Conciliatory; subordinates personal desires; assumes blame; defers to others; never assertive, critical, or demanding.
- Self-attitude: consistent **helplessness and weakness** — "Look at me. I am so weak and helpless that you must protect and love me."
- Views others as superior; even when competent, sees self as inferior.
- Excessively dependent; any sign of rejection (actual or imagined) is terrifying.
- Source: **repressed hostility** — actually has profound defiance and vindictiveness; desires to control and exploit others, opposite of what their behavior expresses.

### The Aggressive Personality

- Moves **against** other people.
- View: everyone is hostile; only the fittest and most cunning survive. Life is a jungle.
- Paramount virtues: supremacy, strength, ferocity.
- Same underlying motivation as compliant type: to alleviate basic anxiety.
- Never displays fear of rejection; acts tough and domineering; no regard for others.
- Must consistently perform at a high level; finds satisfaction in having superiority affirmed.
- Judges everyone by the benefit they will provide.
- Makes no effort to appease others; will argue, criticize, demand to achieve and retain power.
- May become **highly successful** in careers, but work provides no intrinsic satisfaction — means to an end.
- Appears confident and uninhibited but is driven by **insecurity, anxiety, and hostility**.

### The Detached Personality

- Moves **away from** other people; maintains emotional distance.
- Must not love, hate, or cooperate with others; avoids all involvement.
- Strives to become **self-sufficient**; relies on own well-developed resources.
- Almost desperate desire for **privacy**; disturbed by sharing even experiences like listening to music.
- Sensitive to any attempt to influence, coerce, or obligate them.
- Avoids constraints: timetables, schedules, long-term commitments (marriage, mortgages), even physical constraints (belt, necktie).
- Needs to feel superior but believes greatness should be **recognized automatically**, without struggle.
- Manifests a sense of **uniqueness**: feeling different and apart from everyone else.
- Suppresses or denies all feelings toward others, particularly love and hate.
- Places great stress on **reason, logic, and intelligence** due to emotional constriction.

### Comparison with Adler's Types

| Horney | Adler |
|--------|-------|
| Compliant personality | Getting type |
| Aggressive personality | Dominant/Ruling type |
| Detached personality | Avoiding type |

### The Dominance of One Trend

- In the neurotic, **one trend is dominant**; the other two are present to a lesser degree.
- The dominant trend controls behaviors and attitudes; deviation from it is threatening.
- The other two trends must be actively **repressed** — this itself creates additional problems.
- Any indication that a repressed trend is pushing for expression causes inner conflict.

### Conflict

- **Conflict** (Horney): the basic **incompatibility** of the three neurotic trends; the core of neurosis.
- All people — normal and neurotic — experience some conflict among these irreconcilable modes.
- Difference: **intensity** — much more intense in the neurotic.
- Neurotic people are **rigid and inflexible**: meet all situations with the dominant trend regardless of its suitability.

### Expression of All Three Trends (Normal Person)

- In the non-neurotic person, **all three trends can be expressed** as circumstances warrant.
- The trends are not mutually exclusive; can be **integrated harmoniously**.
- The normal person is **flexible** and can adapt to changing situations.

## The Idealized Self-Image

### Normal vs. Neurotic Self-Image

| | Normal Self-Image | Neurotic/Idealized Self-Image |
|--|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Basis | Realistic appraisal of abilities, potentials, weaknesses, goals, relationships | Illusion; unattainable ideal of absolute perfection |
| Nature | Flexible, dynamic; adapts as individual develops | Static, inflexible, unyielding |
| Function | Provides unity, integration; a goal to strive toward | A fixed idea; hindrance demanding rigid adherence |
| Outcome | Guides self-realization | Leads to self-hatred; perpetuates conflict |

- **Idealized self-image**: For neurotics, based on an inflexible, unrealistic self-appraisal (Horney's definition).
- Neurotics build an idealized self-image to **unify the personality** (same purpose as normal people), but the attempt is doomed because it is based on an illusion.
- Self-realization requires a self-image that **clearly reflects the true self**.

### Tyranny of the Shoulds

- **Tyranny of the shoulds**: An attempt to realize an unattainable idealized self-image by denying the true self and behaving in terms of what one thinks one *should* be doing (Horney).
- Neurotics tell themselves they *should* be the best or most perfect student, spouse, parent, lover, employee, friend, or child.
- They believe they must live up to their illusory idealized self-image (virtuous, honest, generous, considerate, courageous).
- They deny their real selves and try to become what they think they should be.
- Doomed to failure: they can never achieve the unrealistic self-image; end up in **self-hatred** with no ability to forgive themselves or others (Kerr, 1984).

### The Neurotic's Self-Image: Key Features

- Although not matching reality, the idealized self-image is **real and accurate to the neurotic** who created it.
- Others can see through it; the neurotic cannot.
- The idealized self-image is like a **house filled with dynamite**: always-ready potential for self-destruction.
- The slightest crack in the false self-picture threatens the false sense of superiority and security.
- It is an unsatisfactory substitute for a reality-based sense of self-worth.
- It **alienates** the neurotic from the true self.
- Far from resolving neurotic conflict, it adds to a growing sense of futility.

### Externalization

- **Externalization**: Defending against the conflict caused by the discrepancy between idealized and real self-images by **projecting the conflict onto the outside world**.
- May temporarily alleviate anxiety but does nothing to reduce the gap.
- Involves experiencing conflicts as though occurring **outside of oneself**.
- Depicts external forces as the source of conflicts.
- Example: Neurotics experiencing self-hatred due to the gap between real and idealized selves may project that hatred onto other people/institutions.

## Feminine Psychology

### Background

- Horney began work on feminine psychology in **1922**.
- She was the **first woman to present a paper on feminine psychology** at an international psychoanalytic congress (Berlin, chaired by Sigmund Freud).
- Early feminist: argued psychoanalysis focused more on men's development than women's.

### Critique of Freud on Women

- Freud's view: Women are victims of their anatomy, forever envious and resentful of men for possessing a penis (**penis envy**).
- Freud: women have **poorly developed superegos** (due to inadequately resolved Oedipal conflicts) and **inferior body images** (women believed they were really castrated men).
- Horney's critique: Freud derived these ideas from inadequate evidence (neurotic women only; strictly male point of view; women were second-class citizens at the time).

### Womb Envy

- **Womb envy**: The envy a male feels toward a female because she can bear children and he cannot (Horney's counter to Freud's penis envy).
- Based on the pleasure Horney said she experienced in childbirth.
- "When one begins to analyze men only after a fairly long experience of analyzing women, one receives a most surprising impression of the intensity of this envy of pregnancy, childbirth, and motherhood" (Horney, 1967, pp. 60–61).
- Horney: "I know just as many men with womb envy as women with penis envy."
- Men have a small part in creating new life; they **sublimate womb envy** by seeking achievement in work (Gilman, 2001).
- Womb envy manifests unconsciously in behaviors that **disparage and belittle women**, reinforcing their inferior status (denying rights, minimizing opportunities, downgrading achievements).
- Underlying typical male disparaging behavior: **a sense of inferiority** deriving from womb envy.
- Horney did not deny many women believed themselves inferior to men; she questioned Freud's claim of a **biological basis** for these feelings, arguing instead it is due to treatment in male-dominated cultures.

### The Flight from Womanhood

- **Flight from womanhood**: Women's denial of their femininity and unconscious wish to be men, resulting from feelings of inferiority; can lead to **sexual inhibitions** (Horney, 1926).
- Arises partly from childhood fantasies about the size difference between the adult penis and the female child's vagina (fantasies of vaginal injury, pain of forcible penetration).
- Produces a conflict between unconscious desire to have a child and fear of intercourse.
- If conflict is sufficiently strong: emotional disturbances in relations with men — distrust, resentment, rejection of sexual advances.

### Horney's Reinterpretation of the Oedipus Complex

- Horney did not deny conflicts between children and parents but rejected their **sexual origin**.
- Reinterpreted the Oedipus complex as a conflict between **dependence on parents and hostility toward them** (not libidinal/sexual).
- Child's internal logic: "I have to repress my hostility because I need you."
- "The resulting picture may look exactly like what Freud describes as the Oedipus complex: passionate clinging to one parent and jealousy toward the other" (Horney, 1939, p. 83).
- Explanation: **neurotic conflicts evolving from parent-child interactions**, not sexual/biological forces.
- Key difference from Freud: Horney's Oedipal feelings are **not universal** — they develop only when parents undermine the child's security.

### Freud's Response to Horney

- Freud did not respond directly or alter his views.
- Thinly veiled allusion (Freud, 1940): "We shall not be very greatly surprised if a woman analyst, who has not been sufficiently convinced of the intensity of her own wish for a penis, also fails to attach proper importance to that factor in her patients."
- Freud remarked of Horney: "She is able but malicious."

### Motherhood or Career?

- Horney pointed out psychological conflicts between the traditional ideal of womanhood and the modern view (Horney, 1967).
- Traditional scheme: woman's role is to love, admire, and serve her man; identity solely a reflection of her husband's.
- Horney argued women must seek their own identity by developing abilities and pursuing careers.
- Westkott (1986): "Modern women are caught between wanting to make themselves desirable to men and pursuing their own goals. The competing purposes elicit conflicting behaviors: seductive versus aggressive, deferential versus ambitious."

### Cultural Influences on Feminine Psychology

- Different cultures view women's roles differently; therefore there can be **many different feminine psychologies**.
- "The American woman is different from the German woman; both are different from certain Pueblo Indian women" (Horney, 1939, p. 119).
- Example — traditional Chinese society:
  - **Yang**: male element — vital, positive, strong, active.
  - **Yin**: female element — dark, weak, passive.
  - Created a hierarchy where men were superior, women inferior.
  - **Confucius (551–479 B.C.)**: his teachings were ruling ideology of China for centuries; strict rules for women — submissive, obedient, respectful, chaste, unselfish.
  - Chinese word for "woman" literally meant **"inside person"** (restricted to the home).
  - Women expected to obey father, then husband, then eldest son (when widowed).
- Conclusion: the **feminine psyche is influenced, even determined, by cultural forces**.

## Questions about Human Nature

- Horney's view of human nature is considerably **more optimistic** than Freud's.
- Biological forces do not condemn us to conflict, anxiety, neurosis, or universality in personality.
- Each person is **unique**.
- Neurotic behavior results from **social forces in childhood**, not biology.
- Parent-child relationships either satisfy or frustrate the child's need for safety.
- Neuroses and conflicts **can be avoided** if children are raised with love, acceptance, and trust.
- Each of us has the innate potential for **self-realization** — the ultimate and necessary goal in life.
- Potential blossoms as inevitably as an acorn grows into an oak tree — unless thwarted by unmet safety needs.
- Human nature is **flexible**; not immutably formed in childhood.
- Adult experiences may be **as important as** those of childhood.
- Emphasized **self-analysis** in therapeutic work; wrote *Self-Analysis* (Horney, 1942).
- On free will vs. determinism: Horney argued in favor of **free will** — we can all shape our lives and achieve self-realization.

## Assessment in Horney's Theory

### General Approach

- Methods: **free association** and **dream analysis**, with modifications (same broad methods as Freud, but differently applied).
- Key difference from Freud in analyst-patient relationship:
  - Freud: too passive, too distant and intellectual.
  - Horney: analysis should be an **"exquisitely cooperative enterprise"** between patient and therapist.
- Adopted an attitude she called **constructive friendliness**.
- Used a couch but not with every patient; encouraged patients to sit, lie down, walk around — whatever they wanted.

### Free Association

- Did not follow Freud's lead in probing the unconscious mind.
- Believed patients could distort or hide aspects of their inner lives or falsify feelings.
- Focused on patients' **visible emotional reactions** toward her — these explained their attitudes toward other people.
- Did not delve into presumed infantile sexual fantasies.
- Inquired about early years only **after** evaluating present attitudes, defenses, and conflicts.
- Each attitude or feeling resulted from a deeper, pre-existing attitude — like **peeling layers of an onion**.

### Dream Analysis

- Believed dreams could reveal a person's **true self**.
- Dreams represent attempts to **solve problems**, constructively or neurotically.
- Dreams can show a set of attitudes that differ from those of one's self-image.
- Did not offer a list of universal dream symbols (contra Freud/Jung).
- Each dream must be explained within the context of the **patient's specific conflict**.
- "The safest clue to the understanding of a dream is in the feelings of the patient as he has them in the dream" (Horney, 1987, p. 61).

### Self-Report Inventories (Developed by Later Researchers)

| Instrument | Developer | Items | Measures |
|-----------|-----------|-------|---------|
| **CAD** (Compliant-Aggressive-Detached) | Cohen, 1967 | 35 items | Horney's three neurotic trends |
| **HCTI** (Horney-Coolidge Type Indicator) | Coolidge et al., 2001 | 57 items | Horney's three neurotic trends; validated with children and college students |
| **Horney-Coolidge Tridimensional Inventory** | Coolidge, Segal, Estey, & Neuzil, 2011 | — | Three neurotic trends for children/adolescents ages 5–17 |

#### HCTI Research Findings
- Men: tended to score higher on **aggressive and detached** scales.
- Women: tended to score higher on **compliance**.
- Aggression and detachment correlated highly with **psychoticism**.
- Compliance was associated with **neuroticism** (Coolidge et al., 2001; Shatz, 2004).

## Research on Horney's Theory

> **Methodological Note**: Horney used the case study method; her data and interpretations face the same criticisms as Freud, Jung, and Adler. She was opposed to verbatim note-taking: "I don't see how anybody can employ a wholehearted receptivity and productivity of attention at the same time that he is anxiously scribbling everything down."

### KEY STUDIES: Neurotic Trends

---

> **Study: Caspi, Elder, & Bem (1987, 1988) — Longitudinal Validation of Neurotic Trends**
> - **Method**: Behavior of children compared with their behavior 30 years later. Redefined trends as: moving against (ill-tempered), moving away (shy), moving toward (dependent).
> - **Findings**:
>   - **Ill-tempered children** (both sexes): became ill-tempered adults, prone to divorce and downward occupational mobility.
>   - **Shy boys**: became aloof adults with marital and job instability. Shy girls showed no such adult problems.
>   - **Dependent boys**: became agreeable, warm, socially poised adults with stable marriages and careers. Dependent girls showed the opposite pattern.
>   *(Follow-up: Caspi, Bem, & Elder, 1989)*

---

> **Study: Moskowitz & Schwartzman (1989) — Aggressive and Detached Children, 5–7 year follow-up**
> - **Method**: Measured aggressive and detached children ages 7–13; compared with behavior 5–7 years later.
> - **Findings**:
>   - High-aggression children: low school achievement, psychiatric problems.
>   - Detached/withdrawn children: inaccurate and negative self-images.
>   - **Conclusion**: Horney's personality types had **predictive value** for later behavior.

---

> **Study: CAD Inventory and Career Choice (Cohen, 1967; Rendon, 1987)**
> - **Method**: CAD scores for college students preparing for different career paths.
> - **Findings**:
>   - Helping professions (nursing, social work): higher **compliance**.
>   - Business students (competitive path): higher **aggression**.
>   - Science students: highest **detachment**.
>   - Results consistent with Horney's neurotic trend descriptions.

---

> **Study: Haghayegh & Oreyzia (2009) — Aggression and Automobile Accidents (Iran)**
> - People who scored high in aggression had significantly **more automobile accidents** than those who scored low.

---

### KEY STUDIES: Feminine Psychology

- **Hall & Van de Castle (1965)**: Classic dream study — provided support for Freud's penis envy concept; **fails to support** Horney's questioning of it.
- Research refuting Freud's notion that women have inadequately developed superegos and inferior body images can be taken as **indirect support** for Horney's views.

---

### KEY STUDIES: Tyranny of the Shoulds

> **Study: Berg, Janoff-Bulman, & Cotter (2001)**
> - **Method**: College students recalled three things they did in the prior week; indicated whether they did them because they **wanted to** or because they **should/ought to**.
> - **Finding**: Students who did more things because they genuinely wanted to scored significantly higher on **general life satisfaction** than those whose behavior was directed primarily by what they believed they ought to do.
> - Supports Horney's concept of the tyranny of the shoulds.

---

### KEY STUDIES: Neurotic Competitiveness

- **Neurotic competitiveness** (Horney): An indiscriminate need to **win at all costs**.
- Horney's analogy: "a jockey in a race, for whom only one thing matters — whether he is ahead of the others" (Horney, 1937, p. 189).

**Instruments Developed:**

| Scale | Developer | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| **Hyper-competitive Attitude Scale (HCA)** | Ryckman, Thornton, & Butler, 1994 | Sample item: "Winning in competition makes me feel more powerful as a person." 5-point scale. |
| **Neurotic Competitiveness Inventory (NCI)** | Deneui, 2001 | — |

**Research Findings:**
- High scorers on competitiveness: high in **narcissism, neuroticism, authoritarianism, dogmatism, and mistrust**; low in **self-esteem and psychological health**.
- Those who deliberately avoided competitiveness: higher neuroticism, lower drive to prove themselves (Ryckman, Thornton, & Gold, 2009).
- Hyper-competitive men: hyper-masculine/macho; believe women are sex objects deserving neither respect nor consideration.
- **US vs. Netherlands** (Dru, 2003; Ryckman et al., 1990, 1994, 2002): **Americans scored higher in hyper-competitiveness** — supports cultural differences.

---

### KEY STUDIES: Two Types of Competitiveness

> **Study: Hibbard & Buhrmester (2010) — CW vs. CE in Adolescents**
> - **Two types**:
>   - **Competing to Win (CW)**: to dominate others.
>   - **Competing to Excel (CE)**: to surpass personal goals.
> - **Findings**:
>   - **CE**: linked to **high self-esteem and low depression** among high school students.
>   - Teen boys scored higher on CW than teen girls.
>   - Girls who scored high on CW: greater **depression and loneliness**, fewer close friends (vs. girls who scored low on CW).

## Reflections on Horney's Theory

### Contributions

- Books written in a style readily understood by non-professionals — large public following.
- Theory has **commonsense appeal** and applicability to everyday personality observation.
- Ideas may be more relevant to problems inherent in **American culture** today than Freud, Jung, or Adler.
- Neurotic trends: valuable way to **categorize deviant behavior**.
- Widely accepted concepts: self-esteem, need for safety and security, role of basic anxiety, importance of the idealized self-image.
- Significant impact on **Erik Erikson** and **Abraham Maslow**:
  - Maslow used her concept of the **real self and self-realization**.

| **Neurotic needs** | Ten irrational defenses against anxiety that become permanent parts of personality and affect behavior |
| **Neurotic trends** | Three categories of behaviors and attitudes toward oneself and others (compliant, aggressive, detached); Horney's 1945 reformulation of the neurotic needs |

| **Aggressive personality** | Neurotic trend of moving against people; domineering, controlling, driven to surpass others; views life as a jungle |
| **Detached personality** | Neurotic trend of moving away from people; intense need for privacy, self-sufficiency, emotional distance; believes greatness should be recognized automatically |
| **Conflict** | In Horney's system, the basic incompatibility of the three neurotic trends; the core of neurosis |
| **Idealized self-image** | For neurotics, an unrealistic, inflexible, unattainable picture of the self based on illusion rather than realistic self-appraisal |
| **Tyranny of the shoulds** | An attempt to realize an unattainable idealized self-image by denying the true self and behaving in terms of what one thinks one should be |
| **Externalization** | Defending against the conflict between idealized and real self-images by projecting the conflict onto the outside world |
| **Womb envy** | The envy a male feels toward a female because she can bear children and he cannot; Horney's counter to Freud's penis envy |
| **Feminine psychology** | Horney's revision of psychoanalysis to encompass the psychological conflicts inherent in the traditional ideal of womanhood and women's roles |
| **Flight from womanhood** | Women's denial of their femininity and unconscious wish to be men, resulting from feelings of inferiority; can lead to sexual inhibitions (Horney, 1926) |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Basic anxiety definition** (word-for-word): "an insidiously increasing, all-pervading feeling of being lonely and helpless in a hostile world" (Horney, 1937). It is the **foundation of all neuroses**.

2. **The 10 neurotic needs**: Know all 10, which need maps to which trend, and which trend maps to which self-protective mechanism. Affection/approval and dominant partner = compliant; power/exploitation/prestige/admiration/achievement = aggressive; self-sufficiency/perfection/narrow limits = detached.

3. **Three neurotic trends**: Compliant (toward), Aggressive (against), Detached (away from). These are Horney's **1945** reformulation of the 10 neurotic needs into three directional movements.

4. **Womb envy vs. penis envy**: Horney proposed **womb envy** (men envy women's capacity for childbirth) as a counter to Freud's penis envy. First presented at the **1922 international psychoanalytic congress in Berlin** (chaired by Freud).

5. **The safety need**: The dominant childhood need (security and freedom from fear). Its frustration leads to basic anxiety, which leads to neurotic trends.

6. **Four self-protective mechanisms** (in order): securing affection ("if you love me, you won't hurt me"), being submissive ("if I give in, I won't be hurt"), attaining power ("if I have power, no one will harm me"), withdrawing (psychological independence from others).

7. **Four reasons for repressing hostility**: helplessness, fear, love (paradoxical), guilt.

8. **Tyranny of the shoulds research**: Berg, Janoff-Bulman, & Cotter (2001) — acting on what one genuinely wants (vs. "should") predicts significantly higher **life satisfaction**.

9. **Comparison of Horney's types with Adler's**: Compliant = Getting; Aggressive = Dominant/Ruling; Detached = Avoiding.

10. **CAD inventory** (Cohen, 1967, 35 items) and **HCTI** (Coolidge et al., 2001, 57 items) both measure Horney's three neurotic trends. HCTI research: men score higher on aggressive/detached; women higher on compliance. Aggression/detachment correlated with **psychoticism**; compliance with **neuroticism**.

11. **Horney vs. Freud on the Oedipus complex**: Horney reinterpreted it as dependence vs. hostility toward parents (not sexual). It is **not universal** — develops only when parents undermine the child's security.

12. **Flight from womanhood** (Horney, 1926): Women's denial of femininity and unconscious wish to be men; can lead to sexual inhibitions.

13. **Impact on later theorists**: Horney influenced Maslow (real self, self-realization) and Erikson (basic anxiety is similar to basic mistrust).

14. **Caspi et al. longitudinal study**: ill-tempered children = ill-tempered adults (divorce, occupational decline); shy boys = aloof adults with instability; dependent boys = warm, stable adults. Gender differences are a key MCQ target.

15. **Neurotic competitiveness** (HCA scale): Americans scored higher than Dutch; hyper-competitive individuals high in narcissism, neuroticism, authoritarianism, low in self-esteem. Two types: CW (dominate others) vs. CE (surpass personal goals) — CE linked to high self-esteem, low depression.
