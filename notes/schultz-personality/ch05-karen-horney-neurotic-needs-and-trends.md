# Ch 5: Karen Horney: Neurotic Needs and Trends

Karen Horney (1885–1952), a neo-psychoanalyst, broke from orthodox Freudianism, arguing **social forces** (not biological drives) shape personality. Her theory centers on the childhood **safety need**, **basic anxiety** as the root of neurosis, and three **neurotic trends** (compliant, aggressive, detached). A pioneering feminist critic of Freud, she proposed **womb envy** to counter penis envy.

---

## The Life of Horney (1885–1952)

### Early Childhood and Family Dynamics
- Born **Karen Danielsen** near Hamburg, Germany, 1885; second-born; envied her adored older brother **Berndt**.
- Father: 50-year-old Norwegian ship's captain — religious, domineering, morose. Mother: 17 years younger, spirited, freethinking; said she married out of fear, not love.
- Karen felt unloved and inferior; diary at 16: "Why is everything beautiful on earth given to me, only not...love!"

### A Neglected Second-Born
- Doubted her parents wanted her; believed they loved Berndt more, partly because he was male ("I...wanted for a long time to be a boy"). Her father disparaged her intelligence and appearance.

### Rebellion and Hostility
- Until age 8: the compliant daughter. When compliance failed to win love, she became ambitious and rebellious: "If I couldn't be beautiful, I decided I would be smart."
- This pattern (compliant → hostile/ambitious) directly informed her later theory. Paris (1994): "In all her...writings Karen Horney was struggling to make sense of herself."

### Adolescence and Career Decision
- Realized at 17 that being in love eliminated anxiety. Decided at 12 to become a doctor.
- Entered **University of Freiburg medical school in 1906** (only 6 years after the first woman was admitted).

### Marriage and Career
- Married **Oskar Horney**; received her medical degree from the **University of Berlin in 1913**.
- Early marriage marked by crying spells, fatigue, frigidity, longing for death; ended after 17 years (three daughters). Felt lost without a lover.

### Horney's Psychoanalysis
- Analyst **Karl Abraham** (Freudian) attributed her problems to childhood Oedipal longings for her father; analysis was unsuccessful.
- Turned to lifelong **self-analysis**; strongly influenced by **Adler's** compensation for inferiority. Concluded she needed to feel superior due to her sense of inferiority as a woman (masculine protest).

### Later Career (USA)
- Most intense affair: analyst **Erich Fromm** (~20 years); ended after she had Fromm analyze her daughter.
- **1932–1952**: psychoanalytic institutes in Chicago and New York. Founded the **Association for the Advancement of Psychoanalysis**, **American Institute for Psychoanalysis**, and the **American Journal of Psychoanalysis (1941)**.
- Key observation: American patients' neuroses differed from her German patients' — social/cultural forces, not biology, shape personality.

## The Childhood Need for Safety and Security

- Agreed with Freud that early childhood is crucial, but held that **social forces** (not biology) determine development. No universal stages or inevitable conflicts. The key factor is the **parent-child social relationship**.

### The Safety Need
- **Safety need**: the child's fundamental need for **security and freedom from fear** (Horney, 1937), decisive for normal development; depends entirely on parental behavior.
- The major way parents undermine security: **lack of warmth and affection**. Children withstand trauma (weaning, beatings) if they feel **wanted, loved, secure**.

### Ways of Undermining Security
Parental behaviors inducing hostility: (1) preferring one sibling, (2) unfair punishment, (3) erratic behavior, (4) broken promises, (5) ridicule, (6) humiliation, (7) isolating the child from peers. Children recognize whether love is genuine; false affection does not fool them.

### Repressing Hostility toward Parents

| Reason | Child's Logic |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Helplessness** | "I must repress hostility because I need you" — dependence breeds helplessness. |
| **Fear** | "I'm afraid of you" — punishment/intimidation deepens repression. |
| **Love (paradoxical)** | Insincere love is all that's available; represses hostility to avoid losing it. |
| **Guilt** | Made to feel guilty for resentment; more guilt = deeper repression. |

- This repressed hostility produces **basic anxiety**.

---

## Basic Anxiety: The Foundation of Neurosis

- **Basic anxiety**: "an insidiously increasing, all-pervading feeling of being lonely and helpless in a hostile world" (Horney, 1937). The foundation on which **all later neuroses develop**; tied to hostility, helplessness, fear.

### Four Self-Protective Mechanisms

1. **Securing Affection** — "If you love me, you won't hurt me"; by pleasing, bribing, or threatening others.
2. **Being Submissive** — "If I give in, I won't be hurt"; complies, never assertive/critical; believes self unselfish (mirrors Horney's early childhood).
3. **Attaining Power** — "If I have power, no one will harm me"; compensates via success/superiority (mirrors her later ambition).
4. **Withdrawing** — psychological (not physical) independence; blunts emotional needs; aloof, guarding against hurt by renouncing needs.

### Characteristics
- **Single goal**: defend against basic anxiety; seek **security/reassurance**, not happiness (defense against pain, not pursuit of well-being).
- Can be more compelling than sexual/physiological needs; cost = an **impoverished personality**. Using more than one creates conflicting incompatibilities.

---

## Neurotic Needs

- **Neurotic needs**: ten irrational defenses against anxiety that become permanent parts of personality. Any self-protective mechanism can become a fixed drive. "Neurotic" = **irrational solutions**.

### The 10 Neurotic Needs

| # | Neurotic Need | Trend |
|---|---------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Affection and approval | Compliant |
| 2 | A dominant partner | Compliant |
| 3 | Power | Aggressive |
| 4 | Exploitation | Aggressive |
| 5 | Prestige | Aggressive |
| 6 | Admiration | Aggressive |
| 7 | Achievement or ambition | Aggressive |
| 8 | Self-sufficiency | Detached |
| 9 | Perfection | Detached |
| 10 | Narrow limits to life | Detached |

- Everyone shows these somewhat. They become **neurotic** when pursued intensely and **compulsively** as the "only" way to resolve anxiety; then they grow **tyrannical** (Hess & Hess, 2010). Satisfying them only escapes discomfort (treats symptoms, not cause).

---

## Neurotic Trends

- **Neurotic trends**: three categories of attitudes toward self/others — Horney's **1945** reformulation of the 10 needs. **Compulsive** and displayed **indiscriminately**.

| Trend | Direction | Personality Type | Associated Needs |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Toward** people | Approach | **Compliant** | Affection/approval; dominant partner |
| **Against** people | Attack | **Aggressive** | Power; exploitation; prestige; admiration; achievement |
| **Away from** people | Avoidance | **Detached** | Self-sufficiency; perfection; narrow limits |

### The Compliant Personality
- Intense need for **affection and approval**; needs a dominant person to run their life. **Manipulates** via seeming considerate; defers, assumes blame, never assertive.
- Self-attitude: **helplessness/weakness** ("I am so weak you must protect me"); sees self as inferior; terrified of rejection.
- Source: **repressed hostility** — actually harbors defiance and a desire to control/exploit, the opposite of the behavior.

### The Aggressive Personality
- Moves **against** people; sees life as a jungle where only the fit/cunning survive; values supremacy, strength.
- Same motive as compliant type (alleviate basic anxiety). Acts tough, domineering; judges others by usefulness; argues/demands for power.
- May be **highly successful** in careers (means to an end, no intrinsic satisfaction); appears confident but driven by **insecurity, anxiety, hostility**.

### The Detached Personality
- Moves **away from** people; emotional distance; avoids love/hate/cooperation. Strives for **self-sufficiency**; desperate for **privacy**; avoids constraints (schedules, commitments, even neckties).
- Needs to feel superior but believes greatness should be **recognized automatically**; manifests **uniqueness**; suppresses feelings; stresses **reason, logic, intelligence**.

### Comparison with Adler

| Horney | Adler |
|--------|-------|
| Compliant | Getting type |
| Aggressive | Dominant/Ruling type |
| Detached | Avoiding type |

### Dominance and Conflict
- In the neurotic, **one trend dominates**; the other two are **repressed**, creating inner conflict when they push for expression.
- **Conflict** (Horney): the basic **incompatibility** of the three trends; the core of neurosis. All people feel some conflict; the neurotic's is more **intense**, **rigid, and inflexible**.
- The normal person can express **all three** flexibly, integrating them harmoniously.

## The Idealized Self-Image

| | Normal Self-Image | Neurotic/Idealized |
|--|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Basis | Realistic appraisal | Illusion; unattainable perfection |
| Nature | Flexible, dynamic | Static, inflexible |
| Function | Unity; goal to strive toward | Fixed idea demanding rigid adherence |
| Outcome | Guides self-realization | Self-hatred; perpetuates conflict |

- **Idealized self-image** (neurotic): inflexible, unrealistic self-appraisal. Built to **unify the personality** (as normals do) but doomed because illusory. Self-realization needs an image reflecting the **true self**.

### Tyranny of the Shoulds
- **Tyranny of the shoulds**: trying to realize the unattainable idealized image by denying the true self and acting as one *should*. Neurotics believe they must be the most perfect student, spouse, parent. Doomed to fail → **self-hatred** (Kerr, 1984).

### Key Features
- The idealized image is **real and accurate to the neurotic** (others see through it; they cannot). Like a **house filled with dynamite** — any crack threatens the false security; it **alienates** the neurotic from the true self and adds futility.

### Externalization
- **Externalization**: defending against the real/idealized discrepancy by **projecting the conflict onto the outside world** — experiencing conflicts as occurring outside oneself and blaming external forces (e.g., projecting self-hatred onto others). Temporarily eases anxiety but doesn't close the gap.

## Feminine Psychology

- Began work on feminine psychology in **1922**; the **first woman to present a paper on feminine psychology** at an international psychoanalytic congress (Berlin, chaired by Freud). Argued psychoanalysis focused on men.

### Critique of Freud on Women
- Freud: women are victims of anatomy, envious of the penis (**penis envy**), with **poorly developed superegos** and **inferior body images** (felt like castrated men).
- Horney: Freud derived this from inadequate evidence (neurotic women, a male viewpoint, an era of female subordination).

### Womb Envy
- **Womb envy**: a male's envy of a female's capacity to bear children (counter to penis envy), based on Horney's pleasure in childbirth. "I know just as many men with womb envy as women with penis envy."
- Men **sublimate womb envy** into work achievement (Gilman, 2001); it manifests unconsciously in behaviors that **disparage women**, rooted in **male inferiority**.
- Horney didn't deny women feel inferior, but questioned a **biological basis**, attributing it to male-dominated cultures.

### The Flight from Womanhood
- **Flight from womanhood**: women's denial of femininity and unconscious wish to be men, from inferiority feelings; can cause **sexual inhibitions** (Horney, 1926). Tied to childhood fantasies about penis/vagina size, producing conflict between wanting a child and fearing intercourse (distrust, resentment of men).

### Reinterpreting the Oedipus Complex
- Horney accepted parent-child conflict but rejected its **sexual origin**, reinterpreting it as **dependence vs. hostility** toward parents ("I must repress my hostility because I need you").
- The result may look like Freud's Oedipus complex but stems from **neurotic parent-child interactions**, not sex. Crucially, it is **not universal** — develops only when parents undermine security.

### Freud's Response
- Freud didn't alter his views; alluded (1940) that a woman analyst "not sufficiently convinced of...her own wish for a penis" might miss it in patients. Called Horney "able but malicious."

### Motherhood or Career?
- Horney noted conflict between the traditional womanhood ideal (identity as a reflection of one's husband) and the modern view; women must seek their own identity via abilities and careers. Westkott (1986): modern women are caught between desirability to men and their own goals — seductive vs. aggressive, deferential vs. ambitious.

### Cultural Influences
- Different cultures view women differently → **many feminine psychologies**. "The American woman is different from the German woman; both...from certain Pueblo Indian women" (Horney, 1939).
- Traditional China: **Yang** (male: vital, strong, active) vs. **Yin** (female: dark, weak, passive); **Confucius (551–479 B.C.)** enforced women's submission; the word for "woman" meant **"inside person"**; women obeyed father, then husband, then eldest son. Conclusion: the feminine psyche is **determined by cultural forces**.

## Questions about Human Nature

- Considerably **more optimistic** than Freud. Biology does not condemn us to conflict; each person is **unique**.
- Neurosis results from **childhood social forces**, not biology, and **can be avoided** with love, acceptance, trust.
- Each of us has innate potential for **self-realization** (the ultimate goal), blossoming like an acorn into an oak unless safety needs are unmet.
- Human nature is **flexible**; adult experiences may be **as important as** childhood. Emphasized **self-analysis** (*Self-Analysis*, 1942) and **free will**.

## Assessment in Horney's Theory

### General Approach
- Methods: **free association** and **dream analysis**, applied differently from Freud. Rejected Freud's distant stance: analysis should be an **"exquisitely cooperative enterprise."** Adopted **constructive friendliness**; used a couch flexibly.

### Free Association
- Did not probe the unconscious as Freud did (patients can distort/hide). Focused on patients' **visible emotional reactions** toward her, explaining their attitudes toward others. Asked about early years only **after** evaluating present attitudes — like **peeling an onion**.

### Dream Analysis
- Dreams reveal the **true self** and are attempts to **solve problems** (constructively or neurotically). No universal symbol list (vs. Freud/Jung); each dream interpreted within the patient's **specific conflict**. "The safest clue...is in the feelings of the patient as he has them in the dream."

### Self-Report Inventories (Later Researchers)

| Instrument | Developer | Items | Measures |
|-----------|-----------|-------|---------|
| **CAD** (Compliant-Aggressive-Detached) | Cohen, 1967 | 35 | Three neurotic trends |
| **HCTI** (Horney-Coolidge Type Indicator) | Coolidge et al., 2001 | 57 | Three neurotic trends; children and college students |
| **Horney-Coolidge Tridimensional Inventory** | Coolidge et al., 2011 | — | Three trends, ages 5–17 |

**HCTI findings:** men higher on **aggressive/detached**; women higher on **compliance**. Aggression/detachment correlated with **psychoticism**; compliance with **neuroticism** (Coolidge et al., 2001; Shatz, 2004).

## Research on Horney's Theory

> **Methodological Note**: Horney used the case study method (same criticisms as Freud/Jung/Adler) and opposed verbatim note-taking: "I don't see how anybody can employ a wholehearted receptivity...at the same time that he is anxiously scribbling everything down."

### Neurotic Trends

> **Caspi, Elder, & Bem (1987, 1988):** children compared with themselves 30 years later (trends: against = ill-tempered, away = shy, toward = dependent).
> - **Ill-tempered children** → ill-tempered adults, prone to divorce and downward occupational mobility.
> - **Shy boys** → aloof adults with marital/job instability (shy girls: no such problems).
> - **Dependent boys** → agreeable, warm, stable adults (dependent girls: the opposite).

> **Moskowitz & Schwartzman (1989):** aggressive/detached children (7–13), 5–7 year follow-up. High-aggression → low achievement, psychiatric problems; detached → negative self-images. Horney's types had **predictive value**.

> **CAD and Career (Cohen, 1967; Rendon, 1987):** helping professions = higher **compliance**; business = higher **aggression**; science = highest **detachment**.

> **Haghayegh & Oreyzia (2009, Iran):** high-aggression scorers had significantly **more automobile accidents**.

### Feminine Psychology
- **Hall & Van de Castle (1965):** dream study supporting Freud's penis envy; **fails to support** Horney.
- Research refuting Freud's weak-superego/inferior-body-image claims = **indirect support** for Horney.

### Tyranny of the Shoulds
> **Berg, Janoff-Bulman, & Cotter (2001):** students who did things because they genuinely **wanted to** (vs. **should**) scored higher on **life satisfaction**. Supports the tyranny of the shoulds.

### Neurotic Competitiveness
- **Neurotic competitiveness** (Horney): indiscriminate need to **win at all costs** — "a jockey...for whom only one thing matters — whether he is ahead."

| Scale | Developer |
|-------|-----------|
| **Hyper-competitive Attitude Scale (HCA)** | Ryckman, Thornton, & Butler, 1994 |
| **Neurotic Competitiveness Inventory (NCI)** | Deneui, 2001 |

- High competitiveness → high **narcissism, neuroticism, authoritarianism, dogmatism, mistrust**; low **self-esteem** (Ryckman et al., 2009). Hyper-competitive men = hyper-masculine, view women as objects.
- **US > Netherlands** in hyper-competitiveness (Dru, 2003; Ryckman et al.) — cultural difference.

> **Hibbard & Buhrmester (2010):** **Competing to Win (CW)** = dominate others; **Competing to Excel (CE)** = surpass personal goals. CE → **high self-esteem, low depression**. Teen boys > girls on CW; high-CW girls → more **depression/loneliness**, fewer friends.

## Reflections on Horney's Theory

### Contributions
- Accessible writing → large public following; **commonsense appeal**; possibly more relevant to **American culture** than Freud/Jung/Adler.
- Neurotic trends usefully **categorize deviant behavior**. Widely accepted: self-esteem, safety/security need, basic anxiety, idealized self-image.
- Influenced **Erikson** (basic anxiety ≈ basic mistrust) and **Maslow** (real self, self-realization).

### Criticisms
- Case study method shares the rigor problems of Freud, Jung, and Adler (not replicable/controlled; unverified reports).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Basic anxiety** (verbatim): "an insidiously increasing, all-pervading feeling of being lonely and helpless in a hostile world" (Horney, 1937) — the **foundation of all neuroses**.

2. **The 10 neurotic needs** and their trend mapping: affection/approval, dominant partner = compliant; power, exploitation, prestige, admiration, achievement = aggressive; self-sufficiency, perfection, narrow limits = detached.

3. **Three neurotic trends**: Compliant (toward), Aggressive (against), Detached (away) — the **1945** reformulation.

4. **Womb envy vs. penis envy**: Horney's womb envy (men envy childbearing) countered Freud; first presented at the **1922 Berlin congress** (chaired by Freud).

5. **Safety need**: dominant childhood need; frustration → basic anxiety → neurotic trends.

6. **Four self-protective mechanisms**: securing affection; being submissive; attaining power; withdrawing.

7. **Four reasons for repressing hostility**: helplessness, fear, love (paradoxical), guilt.

8. **Tyranny of the shoulds** (Berg et al., 2001): acting on "want to" vs. "should" predicts higher **life satisfaction**.

9. **Horney vs. Adler types**: Compliant = Getting; Aggressive = Dominant/Ruling; Detached = Avoiding.

10. **CAD** (Cohen, 1967, 35 items) and **HCTI** (Coolidge et al., 2001, 57 items): men higher on aggressive/detached, women on compliance; aggression/detachment ↔ psychoticism; compliance ↔ neuroticism.

11. **Oedipus complex (Horney)**: dependence vs. hostility (not sexual); **not universal** — only when parents undermine security.

12. **Flight from womanhood** (1926): denial of femininity, wish to be male; sexual inhibitions.

13. **Impact**: influenced Maslow (self-realization) and Erikson (basic mistrust ≈ basic anxiety).

14. **Caspi et al.**: ill-tempered → ill-tempered adults; shy boys → aloof/unstable; dependent boys → warm/stable. Gender differences are key.

15. **Neurotic competitiveness** (HCA): Americans > Dutch; high in narcissism/neuroticism, low self-esteem. CW (dominate) vs. CE (excel) — CE → high self-esteem, low depression.
