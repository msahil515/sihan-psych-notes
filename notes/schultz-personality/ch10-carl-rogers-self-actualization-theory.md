# Ch 10: Carl Rogers: Self-Actualization Theory

Rogers developed **person-centered therapy** (originally non-directive/client-centered), rooted in humanistic psychology. Humans are conscious, rational beings driven by a single innate motivation: the tendency to actualize the self. The **fully functioning person** — his ideal of health — is always in a state of becoming, never a finished product.

---

## The Life of Rogers (1902–1987)

- Born **1902 in Oak Park, Illinois**; fourth of six children; strict fundamentalist parents (no dancing, cards, movies, drinking). Described himself as **shy, solitary, dreamy**; read voraciously, learning to rely on his own experience. This loneliness shaped his interest in therapy ("a socially approved way of getting really close to individuals").
- Age 12: a farm move sparked an interest in **science** (bred moths; appreciated control groups and statistics).
- A trip to **Beijing, China** (junior year) shifted him from fundamentalist to liberal religious views and emotional independence.
- **1924**: graduated University of Wisconsin; enrolled at **Union Theological Seminary**, then transferred to **Teachers College, Columbia**. **Ph.D., 1931**.
- Worked in Rochester, NY (delinquent/underprivileged children); **Ohio State (1940)**; **University of Chicago (1945–1957)**, developing the **Counseling Center**; later **Western Behavioral Sciences Institute**. **APA president, 1946**.
- Suffered a **nervous breakdown** after failing to help a disturbed client; personal therapy revealed deep insecurity and restored his capacity for close relationships.

## The Self and the Tendency toward Actualization

### Self-Insight
- Rogers rated children on **external factors** (family, health, intellect, economics, culture) and one **internal factor**, **self-insight** (acceptance of self/reality and self-responsibility). He initially thought external factors mattered more.

> **KEY STUDY: Kell & McNeil** — William Kell tried to predict delinquent behavior from Rogers's ratings. Rogers expected family/social factors to dominate, but **self-insight** was the single best predictor. "I was simply not prepared to accept this finding." Helen McNeil **replicated** it. Implication: counselors should modify **self-insight**, not external circumstances.

### Actualizing Tendency
- **Actualizing tendency**: the innate tendency to **actualize, maintain, and enhance the self**; encompasses all physiological and psychological needs.
- Begins **in the womb**; responsible for **maturation** (genetically determined development). Progress is **not effortless** — e.g., a child learning to walk persists despite falls because the actualization tendency outweighs the urge to regress.

### Organismic Valuing Process
- **Organismic valuing process**: lifelong evaluation of experiences by how well they serve the actualizing tendency. Experiences promoting actualization = **good** (sought); hindering = **undesirable** (avoided).

---

## The Experiential World

- Reality depends on **perception**, which may differ from objective reality; two people may react to the same experience differently. The **experiential world** is the frame of reference influencing growth. "Experience is, for me, the highest authority."

---

## The Development of the Self in Childhood

- As the experiential field grows, one part becomes **differentiated** — the **self/self-concept**, defined by **I, me, myself**: our image of what we are, should be, and would like to be. The self strives to be a **consistent whole** (e.g., someone who believes they shouldn't be aggressive denies aggression).

### Positive Regard
- **Positive regard**: a **universal, persistent**, probably learned need for acceptance, love, and approval from others (notably the **mother**). Without it, the actualizing tendency is hampered; infants perceive disapproval of behavior as disapproval of the self.

- **Unconditional positive regard**: love granted **freely**, not contingent on behavior; positive regard for the person persists even when behavior is punished. It is **reciprocal**. We internalize others' attitudes, refining the self-concept.

- **Positive self-regard**: in time, regard comes from within — a feeling of contentment with oneself (linked to mental health); also reciprocal.

### Conditions of Worth
- **Conditions of worth**: belief that we are worthy only when expressing approved behaviors — Rogers's version of the **Freudian superego**.
- Derived from **conditional positive regard** (approval only for desirable behaviors). External standards are internalized; the self-concept becomes a **parental surrogate**; full actualization is **inhibited**.

> **Research:** Conditional positive regard tied to academic achievement → erratic self-worth (Assor & Tai, 2012). A three-generation study found conditional regard produced desired behaviors but also poor coping, fluctuating self-esteem, low self-worth, and resentment — absent under unconditional regard (Assor, Roth, & Deci, 2004).

### Incongruence and Congruence
- **Incongruence**: discrepancy between self-concept and experience. We evaluate experiences by whether they bring positive regard rather than actualization. Incongruent experiences become **threatening** → **anxiety**; defended against by **distortion** of perception (e.g., denying hatred to maintain a "loves all humanity" self-concept).
- **Congruence**: correspondence between self-concept and experience — the basis of psychological health. Healthy people perceive accurately, are open to new experiences, feel worthy under all conditions, and progress toward becoming a **fully functioning person**.

## Characteristics of Fully Functioning Persons

> Rogers used **actualizing** (not *actualized*) — always in process. "Being fully functioning is a direction, not a destination" (1961). Captured in *On Becoming a Person* (1961).

| # | Characteristic | Details |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Awareness of all experience** | No experience denied; open to positive AND negative feelings; more emotional |
| 2 | **Living fully in every moment** | Experiences fresh and new, participated in fully |
| 3 | **Trust in one's own organism** | Trusts own reactions over others' opinions; decisions intuitive |
| 4 | **Freedom of choice** | A sense of power; future depends on own actions |
| 5 | **Creativity and spontaneity** | Flexible; seeks new challenges; needs no predictability |
| 6 | **Continual need to grow** | Always actualizing; if growth ceases, loses openness |
| 7 | **State of actualizing** (not actualized) | Self-development always in progress |

- Not always easy, happy, or blissful — more accurately **enriching, exciting, meaningful**.

## Questions about Human Nature

| Dimension | Rogers |
|---|---|
| **Free will vs. determinism** | Strongly **free will**; no aspect predetermined |
| **Nature vs. nurture** | Actualization is innate, but the process is shaped more by **social** than biological forces |
| **Past vs. present** | **Present feelings** more vital than childhood events |
| **Universality vs. uniqueness** | Universal characteristics with unique expression |
| **Optimism vs. pessimism** | Strongly **optimistic**; humans basically healthy; not ruled by the first 5 years (vs. Freud) |

- As more people self-actualize, **society improves**.

## Assessment in Rogers's Theory

- Only valid assessment: the person's **subjective experiences**. Rogers called them **"clients,"** never "patients."

### Person-Centered Therapy
- The therapist explores the client's feelings without preconceptions, offers **unconditional positive regard**, and no judgments/advice; the client is responsible for change.
- **Limitations**: only conscious events; therapist inferences may be projections; relies on imperfect communication.
- Rogers **opposed** free association, dream analysis, and case histories (they create dependence and remove responsibility). Advantage: no predetermined theoretical structure.

### Encounter Groups
- **Encounter groups**: **8–15 people**, **20–60 hours**, **no formal structure**; **facilitators** create an atmosphere for self-expression. Faith, Wong, & Carpenter (1995): results comparable to traditional psychotherapies; **larger, more frequent groups** = better outcomes.

### Psychological Tests
- Rogers used none himself. Others devised: **Experience Inventory** (Coan, 1972; openness to experience) and **Experiencing Scale** (Gendlin & Tomlinson, 1967; self-trust). Greatest therapy improvement = increased self-trust (Klein et al., 1969).

## Research on Rogers's Theory

- Rogers favored self-reports over experiments ("I never learned anything from research") but did use lab methods.
- **Innovation**: introduced **recording/filming of therapy sessions** — "a microscope with which to examine the molecules of personality change" (1974). Before this, only therapists' reconstructed notes existed.

### The Q-Sort
- **Q-sort technique** (William **Stephenson**, 1953): clients sort self-statements from **most to least descriptive**, defining the self-image. Used for **perceived self** vs. **ideal self**; correlated to measure congruence and track therapy change.

> **KEY STUDY: Mrs. Oak (Rogers, 1954)** — perceived-vs-ideal self correlation rose from **+.36** (pre) to **+.79** (post-therapy); she became more secure and relational.

> **KEY STUDY: Butler & Haigh (1954)** — 25 clients; correlation rose from **.01** to **+.31** after therapy.

- **Successful therapy** more likely when therapists are perceived as empathic, showing **unconditional positive regard**, and **congruent/genuine** (Cain, 2013).

### Openness to Experience
> **Chodorkoff (1954)**: all subjects slower to perceive threatening than neutral words, but the effect was stronger in the **poorly adjusted** group; well-adjusted people showed less perceptual defense and closer self/clinician agreement.

### Acceptance of Self
> **Medinnus & Curtis (1963)**: self-accepting mothers were more accepting of their children; the child's self-acceptance partly depended on the mother's.

- Additional: unconditional acceptance + democratic child-rearing → higher self-esteem (Baldwin, 1949); affectionate, reward-using parents → high-self-esteem children (Coopersmith, 1967); unconditional positive regard → greater creativity (Harrington et al., 1987) and confidence (Harter et al., 1996); high self-esteem from teachers' unconditional regard (Nolan, 2008).

### Emotional Adjustment
- A larger **perceived–ideal self discrepancy** correlates with higher anxiety, insecurity, depression, social incompetence, low self-actualization (Achenbach & Zigler, 1963; Moretti & Higgins, 1990); such people are rated as awkward and unfriendly (Gough et al., 1978).

> **Ford (1991)**: using Buss & Plomin's EAS temperaments (Emotionality, Activity, Sociability), greater discrepancy between childhood temperament potential and adult realization = greater **maladjustment** (supports Rogers).

### Organismic Valuing Process and Culture
- Positive self-regard may be **less prevalent in collectivist cultures** (e.g., Japan) than individualistic (e.g., US) (Heine et al., 1999).

## Reflections on Rogers's Theory

### Criticisms
- **Ignored unconscious factors**; clients may **distort self-reports** (repress, invent, idealize).

### Influence of WWII
- Person-centered therapy spread rapidly post-**WWII (1945)**: veterans needed counseling, and the method was "simple, informal, and brief, requiring little training" (DeCarvalho, 1999). More than **400 college counseling centers** were established under the **Veterans Administration**.

### Acceptance and Theory
- Adopted across helping professions, business, and training. **200+ training centers** (mostly Europe); the journal *Person-Centered & Experiential Psychotherapies* began in **2002**.
- His personality theory (less influential than his therapy) is widely recognized, especially for the **self-concept**, stimulating research on therapy and the self.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Single basic motivation**: the **actualizing tendency** (vs. Freud's multiple drives).

2. **Three therapy names**: **non-directive → client-centered → person-centered**; Rogers used **"client,"** not "patient."

3. **Kell (replicated by McNeil)**: **self-insight** was the single best predictor of delinquent behavior — redirecting Rogers's career.

4. **Unconditional vs. conditional positive regard**: unconditional = love regardless of behavior (healthy); conditional = contingent (creates conditions of worth).

5. **Conditions of worth = Rogers's Freudian superego**; inhibits full actualization.

6. **Incongruence** (self-concept vs. experience) = source of anxiety/maladjustment; larger discrepancy → more anxiety/depression.

7. **Fully functioning person — seven characteristics**: awareness of all experience; living fully in the moment; trust in one's organism; freedom of choice; creativity/spontaneity; continual need to grow; **actualizing** (not actualized) — "a direction, not a destination."

8. **Q-sort** by **Stephenson (1953)** (not Rogers). Mrs. Oak: **+.36 → +.79**; Butler & Haigh (25 clients): **.01 → +.31**.

9. **Encounter groups**: **8–15 people**, **20–60 hours**, no structure, facilitators; larger/more frequent = better (Faith et al., 1995).

10. **Innovation**: recording/filming therapy sessions — "a microscope...for molecules of personality change."

11. **WWII impact**: rapid adoption; **400+ college counseling centers** under the Veterans Administration; valued as simple/brief.

12. **Culture**: positive self-regard less prevalent in **collectivist** (Japan) than individualistic (US) cultures (Heine et al., 1999).

13. **Human nature**: optimistic, free will; not controlled by the first 5 years (vs. Freud); environment over heredity; present over past.

14. **Positive self-regard is reciprocal**.

15. **Criticisms**: ignored the unconscious; relied on distortable self-reports; yet used quantitative tools (Q-sort, correlations).
