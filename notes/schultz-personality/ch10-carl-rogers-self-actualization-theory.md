# Ch 10: Carl Rogers: Self-Actualization Theory

Carl Rogers developed **person-centered therapy** (originally called non-directive or client-centered therapy) rooted in humanistic psychology. His personality theory holds that humans are conscious, rational beings driven by a single innate motivation: the tendency to actualize the self. The fully functioning person — Rogers's ideal of psychological health — is always in a state of becoming, not a finished product.

---

## The Life of Rogers (1902–1987)

### Relying on His Own Experience

- Born **1902 in Oak Park, Illinois** (suburb of Chicago); fourth of six children.
- Parents held strict fundamentalist religious views; suppressed emotion; emphasized hard work and moral behavior.
- Prohibited behaviors included: dancing, playing cards, attending movies, smoking, drinking, and showing sexual interest.
- Rogers described himself as **shy, solitary, dreamy**, and often lost in fantasy as a child.
- Had little social life outside family; believed parents favored an older brother, creating sibling competitiveness.
- A biographer (Milton, 2002) noted Rogers grew up with "bitter memories of being the inevitable butt of his brother's jokes."
- To escape loneliness, Rogers read voraciously — including dictionaries and encyclopedias — developing a habit of relying on his own experiences and resources.
- Rogers later recognized this early loneliness directly shaped his interest in interviewing and therapy: "Here was a socially approved way of getting really close to individuals and thus filling some of the hunger I had undoubtedly felt" (Rogers, 1980, p. 34).

### Scientific Awakening (Age 12)

- Family moved to a farm 30 miles from Chicago; rural life awakened interest in science.
- Became fascinated with a **species of moth**: observed, captured, and bred them over many months.
- Developed appreciation for scientific method: control groups, variable isolation, statistical analysis — unusual for an adolescent.
- Described his emotional life during this period as turbulent; fantasies were "definitely bizarre, and probably would be classified as schizoid by a diagnostician" (1980, p. 30).

### Finding Freedom in China

- Enrolled at **University of Wisconsin** to study agriculture (following family tradition).
- After sophomore year, shifted to preparation for ministry.
- Selected for international Christian student conference in **Beijing, China** in junior year; spent 6 months traveling.
- Religious views shifted from fundamentalist to liberal; gained emotional and intellectual independence.
- Conclusion: all human beings must learn to rely on their own experiences, ideas, and beliefs.
- Hospitalized 5 weeks for ulcers (possibly stress-induced) after returning; recuperated at family farm for a year.

### A Unique Approach to Counseling

- **1924**: Graduated from University of Wisconsin; married childhood friend; enrolled at **Union Theological Seminary**, New York.
- After two years, transferred to **Teachers College, Columbia University** to study clinical and educational psychology.
- **1931**: Received his **Ph.D.**
- Joined the **Child Study Department of the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children**, Rochester, New York — diagnosing and treating delinquent and underprivileged children.
- **1940**: Appointed professor of psychology at **Ohio State University**; began formulating person-centered views.
- **1945–1957**: University of Chicago; developed the **Counseling Center**.
- Clients during academic years: young, intelligent, highly verbal college students facing adjustment problems — distinctly different from Freudian clientele.
- **1964**: Resident fellow at **Western Behavioral Sciences Institute** in California.
- Applied person-centered philosophy to international conflict (Protestants/Catholics in Northern Ireland; Jews/Arabs in the Middle East).
- Served as **president of the American Psychological Association in 1946**.
- Received APA **Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award** and **Distinguished Professional Contribution Award**.

### A Breakdown and Therapy

- Unable to help a severely disturbed client, Rogers suffered a **nervous breakdown**.
- Felt "deeply certain of my complete inadequacy as a therapist, my worthlessness as a person, and my lack of any future in the field of psychology" (1967, p. 367).
- Retreated to upstate New York cabin for 6 months of seclusion.
- Underwent personal therapy; discovered deep insecurity: "no one could ever love me, even though they might like what I did."
- Recovery gave him new ability to give and receive love and form deep emotional relationships, including with clients.

## The Self and the Tendency toward Actualization

### Self-Insight

- In the 1930s, Rogers rated children on factors believed to influence behavior.
- **External factors**: family environment, health, intellectual development, economic circumstances, cultural influences, social interactions, level of education.
- **Internal factor**: **self-insight** — defined as acceptance of self and reality, and a sense of responsibility for the self.
- Rogers initially believed external factors were more important.

#### KEY STUDY: Kell & McNeil

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Researcher | William Kell (Rogers's student) |
| Method | Attempted to predict behavior of delinquent children using Rogers's factor ratings |
| Rogers's prediction | Family environment and social interactions would correlate most strongly with delinquent behavior |
| Finding | **Self-insight** was the single factor that most accurately predicted later behavior |
| Rogers's reaction | "I was simply not prepared to accept this finding, and the study was put on the shelf" (Rogers, 1987, p. 119) |
| Replication | Helen McNeil replicated with a different group; same result — self-insight was the single most important predictor |
| Significance | Counselors should focus on modifying self-insight rather than altering external circumstances |

- This research drove Rogers to focus on developing a psychotherapy centered on "greater awareness of self-understanding, self-direction, and personal responsibility" (1987, p. 119).

### Actualizing Tendency

- **Actualizing tendency**: The basic human motivation — the innate tendency to **actualize, maintain, and enhance the self**.
- Part of a larger tendency encompassing ALL physiological and psychological needs.
- Begins **in the womb**: facilitates differentiation of physical organs and physiological functioning.
- Responsible for **maturation**: genetically determined development (fetal growth through secondary sex characteristics at puberty).
- Progress toward full development is **not automatic or effortless** — involves struggle and pain.
- Example: Children learning to walk fall and hurt themselves yet persist because the actualization tendency is stronger than the urge to regress.

### Organismic Valuing Process

- **Organismic valuing process**: The governing process throughout the life span by which we evaluate all life experiences in terms of how well they serve the actualization tendency.
- Experiences **promoting actualization** = **good/desirable** = positive value = sought out.
- Experiences **hindering actualization** = **undesirable** = negative value = avoided.
- These perceptions influence behavior: we prefer to repeat desirable experiences and avoid undesirable ones.

## The Experiential World

- Reality of our environment depends on our **perception** of it — which may not coincide with objective reality.
- Two people may react to the exact same experience entirely differently.
- Perceptions change with time and circumstances.
- The **experiential world** provides a frame of reference or context that influences growth.
- As the actualization tendency leads infants to grow, the experiential world broadens; infants respond to stimulation as subjectively perceived.
- "Experience is, for me, the highest authority. The touchstone of validity is my own experience" (Rogers, 1961, p. 23).
- Higher levels of development ultimately lead to the development of the self.

## The Development of the Self in Childhood

- As infants develop a more complex experiential field from widening social encounters, one part of experience becomes **differentiated** from the rest.
- This separate part — defined by **I, me, and myself** — is the **self** or **self-concept**.
- The **self-concept** is:
  - Our image of what we are.
  - Our image of what we should be.
  - Our image of what we would like to be.
- The self is ideally a **consistent pattern** — an organized whole. All aspects strive for consistency.
  - Example: A person who believes they should not be aggressive will deny expressing aggressive behaviors to maintain self-concept consistency.

### Positive Regard

- As the self emerges, infants develop a need for **positive regard**: acceptance, love, and approval from others — most notably from the **mother** during infancy.
- This need is **probably learned** and is **universal and persistent**.
- Infants find it satisfying to receive positive regard; frustrating to have it withdrawn.
- Without sufficient positive regard, the infant's innate tendency toward actualization is **hampered**.
- Infants perceive parental disapproval of behavior as disapproval of the emerging self.

#### Unconditional Positive Regard

- **Unconditional positive regard**: The mother's love and approval are granted **freely and fully**, not contingent on the child's behavior.
- Even when specific behaviors bring punishment, positive regard for the infant as a person persists.
- Positive regard is **reciprocal** — when we satisfy someone else's need for positive regard, we experience satisfaction of that need ourselves.
- Because of this importance, we become sensitive to the feedback of others, which refines the self-concept. We **internalize the attitudes of other people**.

#### Positive Self-Regard

- In time, positive regard comes more from within than from others: **positive self-regard**.
- Positive self-regard becomes as strong a need as positive regard from others.
- Example: Children rewarded with affection when happy learn to generate positive self-regard whenever they behave happily — essentially learning to reward themselves.
- Defined as a **feeling of contentment with oneself**; related to positive mental health (Leising et al., 2013).
- Also **reciprocal**: people who receive positive regard and develop positive self-regard may provide positive regard to others.

### Conditions of Worth

- **Conditions of worth**: The belief that we are worthy of approval only when we express certain desirable behaviors and attitudes, and refrain from those that bring disapproval.
- Rogers's version of the **Freudian superego**.
- Derived from **conditional positive regard**: parents withhold affection for behaviors that annoy, frighten, or bore them.
- Infants learn: parental affection has a price — it depends on behaving in certain acceptable ways.
- **Conditional positive regard**: approval, love, or acceptance granted only when a person expresses desirable behaviors and attitudes.
- External standards become internalized and personal: children come to punish themselves as their parents did.
- Children develop self-regard only in situations that have brought parental approval; the self-concept comes to function as a **parental surrogate**.
- Result: children believe they are worthy only under conditions defined by the parents.
- This **inhibits full actualization**.

#### Research: Conditional Positive Regard

- **Assor & Tai (2012)** — adolescents; mothers used conditional positive self-regard for academic achievement: feelings of self-worth became erratic — self-aggrandizing with good grades; shame with poor grades.
- **Assor, Roth, & Deci (2004)** — three-generation study; conditional regard produced desired behaviors BUT also: poor coping skills, fluctuating self-esteem, low self-worth, feelings of parental disapproval, resentment toward parents. None of these negative consequences found in children raised with unconditional regard.

### Incongruence

- **Incongruence**: A discrepancy between a person's self-concept and aspects of his or her experience.
- We come to evaluate experiences in terms of whether they bring positive regard from others — NOT in terms of how they contribute to actualization.
- This leads to **incongruence between the self-concept and the experiential world**.
- Experiences incongruent with the self-concept become **threatening** and manifest as **anxiety**.
  - Example: A person who believes they love all humanity meets someone toward whom they feel hatred — must deny the hatred to maintain self-concept.
- Defense against anxiety: **distortion** of perception; closing off a portion of the experiential field. Result: rigidity of perceptions.

### Congruence and Emotional Health

- Psychological adjustment = function of the degree of **congruence** between self-concept and experiences.
- **Psychologically healthy people**:
  - Perceive themselves, others, and events much as they really are.
  - Open to new experiences — nothing threatens the self-concept.
  - No need to deny or distort perceptions.
  - Feel worthy under all conditions.
  - Can develop and actualize all facets of the self.
  - Progress toward becoming a **fully functioning person** — living "the good life."

## Characteristics of Fully Functioning Persons

Rogers's term **fully functioning person** represents the most desirable end result of psychological development and social evolution (Rogers, 1961).

> Rogers used the word **actualizing** (not *actualized*) — always in process, never a finished product. "Being fully functioning is a direction, not a destination" (Rogers, 1961, p. 186). Captured in the title *On Becoming a Person* (1961).

### The Seven Characteristics (Table 10.1)

| # | Characteristic | Details |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Awareness of all experience** | No experience distorted or denied; open to positive feelings (courage, tenderness) AND negative feelings (fear, pain); more emotional — wider range, felt more intensely |
| 2 | **Freshness of appreciation / Living fully in every moment** | All experiences potentially fresh and new; cannot be predicted or anticipated; participated in fully, not merely observed |
| 3 | **Trust in one's own organism** | Trusts own reactions rather than being guided solely by opinions of others, social codes, or intellectual judgments; behaving in a way that feels right is a good guide; decisions appear more intuitive than intellectual |
| 4 | **Freedom of choice without constraints or inhibitions** | Brings a sense of power — future depends on own actions, not circumstances, past events, or others; not compelled to behave in only one way |
| 5 | **Creativity and spontaneity** | Flexible; seeks new experiences and challenges; does not require predictability, security, or freedom from tension |
| 6 | **Continual need to grow / Strive to maximize potential** | Always in process of actualizing; if striving and growing cease, the person loses spontaneity, flexibility, and openness |
| 7 | **In a state of actualizing (not actualized)** | Self-development always in progress |

- Being fully functioning is **not always easy** — involves complexity and challenge; not necessarily happy or blissful — more accurately: **enriching, exciting, and meaningful**.

## Questions about Human Nature

| Dimension | Rogers's Position |
|---|---|
| **Free will vs. determinism** | Strongly **free will** — fully functioning persons have free choice in creating their selves; no aspect of personality predetermined |
| **Nature vs. nurture** | Prominent role to **environment** (nurture); actualization tendency is innate, but the actualizing process influenced more by **social** than biological forces |
| **Past vs. present** | **Present feelings** more vital than childhood events; current experiences have greater influence |
| **Universality vs. uniqueness** | Recognized universal qualities (fully functioning persons share characteristics); unique expression of these characteristics is possible |
| **Optimism vs. pessimism** | Strongly **optimistic**; humans have a basically healthy nature; not ruled by instinctive forces or controlled by first 5 years of life (direct contrast with Freud) |

- Rogers at age 85 (1987): "In working with individuals and working with groups my positive view of human nature is continually reinforced."
- As more people in a culture become self-actualizing, the **improvement of society** naturally follows.

## Assessment in Rogers's Theory

- The only valid way to assess personality: the person's **subjective experiences** — events as he or she perceives and accepts them as real.
- Rogers called his clients **"clients"** — never **"patients"** — deliberately distancing from the medical model.

### Person-Centered Therapy

- Therapist explores client's feelings and attitudes toward self and others.
- Listens without preconceptions; tries to understand the client's experiential world as the client views it.
- Therapist gives the client **unconditional positive regard**; offers no judgments or advice.
- Everything centers on the client — including responsibility for changing behavior and reevaluating relationships.
- **Limitations**:
  - Focuses only on consciously expressed events; non-conscious experiences remain hidden.
  - Therapist inferences about non-conscious experiences may represent the therapist's own projections.
  - Depends on the client's ability to communicate; communication is imperfect.
- Rogers **opposed** free association, dream analysis, and case histories — these made clients dependent on the therapist and removed personal responsibility.
- Advantage: does not rely on a predetermined theoretical structure like Freudian psychoanalysis.

### Encounter Groups

- **Encounter groups**: Group therapy technique in which people learn about their feelings and how they relate to one another.
- Group size: **8 to 15 people**.
- Typically met **20 to 60 hours** over several sessions.
- Began with **no formal structure or agenda**.
- **Facilitators** (not leaders in the usual sense) established an atmosphere for self-expression.
- Rogers believed most (though not all) participants would become more fully functioning.
- Large-scale analysis (Faith, Wong, & Carpenter, 1995): results comparable to traditional psychotherapies; **larger groups meeting more frequently** produced more favorable outcomes.
- Less popular today than during Rogers's era but still conducted by his followers.

### Psychological Tests

Rogers did not use or develop psychological tests. Other psychologists devised measures:

| Test | Developer(s) | Year | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Experience Inventory** | Coan | 1972 | Openness/receptivity to experience — characteristic of the fully functioning person |
| **Experiencing Scale** | Gendlin & Tomlinson | 1967 | Level of self-trust; subjects' talk rated for degree of self-trust |

#### Experiencing Scale Research (Klein, Malthieu, Gendlin, & Kiesler, 1969)

- Greatest improvement in therapy = increase in self-trust from pre- to post-therapy.
- Little improvement in therapy = small or no increase in self-trust.
- Less severe emotional disorders = greater self-trust than those with more severe disorders.

## Research on Rogers's Theory

- Rogers believed person-centered interviews (self-reports) were of greater value than experimental methods.
- Quote: "I never learned anything from research. Most of my research has been to confirm what I already felt to be true" (quoted in Bergin & Strupp, 1972, p. 314).
- Despite this claim, Rogers DID use laboratory methods to verify clinical observations.
- **Innovation**: Introduced **recording and filming of therapy sessions** — a radical procedure at the time (Goldfried, 2007).
  - Before Rogers: only therapist's after-the-fact reconstructions (notes made some time after the session) — subject to memory distortion; missed emotional state, body language, tone of voice.
  - With recorded sessions, everything became available for study.
  - Rogers: recordings were "a microscope with which to examine the 'molecules of personality change'" (1974, p. 120).
  - Always obtained client's permission; presence of equipment did not impede therapy.

### Evaluating Person-Centered Therapy: The Q-Sort

- **Q-sort technique** (William Stephenson, 1953): Clients sort statements about the self-concept into categories from **most descriptive to least descriptive**.
- Empirically defines the client's self-image.

**Typical Q-sort statements:**
- "I enjoy being alone."
- "I feel helpless."
- "I am emotionally mature."

**Uses:**
1. Sort in terms of **perceived self** (how I am now).
2. Sort in terms of **ideal self** (how I would most like to be).
3. Correlational method: determine how closely perceived self corresponds to ideal self.
4. Track changes in self-concept from before to after therapy.

#### KEY STUDY: Mrs. Oak (Rogers, 1954)

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Subject | "Mrs. Oak" — a client |
| Pre-therapy correlation (perceived vs. ideal self) | **+.36** |
| Post-therapy correlation (1 year after therapy) | **+.79** |
| Interpretation | Perceived self became much more congruent with ideal self; dramatic increase in emotional health |
| Pre-therapy self-description | Dependent, passive, felt rejected by others |
| Post-therapy self-description | More like her desired self; more secure, less fearful, better able to relate to others |

**Mrs. Oak's Q-sort statements (Table 10.2):**

| Self Before Therapy | Self 12 Months After Therapy |
|---|---|
| I usually feel driven | I express my emotions freely |
| I am responsible for my troubles | I feel emotionally mature |
| I am really self-centered | I am self-reliant |
| I am disorganized | I understand myself |
| I feel insecure within myself | I feel adequate |
| I have to protect myself with excuses, with rationalizing | I have a warm emotional relationship with others |

#### KEY STUDY: Butler & Haigh (1954)

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Sample | 25 clients |
| Method | Measured discrepancy between perceived self and ideal self over therapy |
| Pre-therapy correlation coefficient | **.01** |
| Post-therapy correlation coefficient | **+.31** |
| Finding | Discrepancy decreased over time during and following therapy |

#### Conditions for Successful Person-Centered Therapy (Cain, 2013)

Therapy more likely to succeed when clients perceive therapists to:
1. Be empathic and understanding.
2. Demonstrate **unconditional positive regard**.
3. Show **congruence** — being genuine in the therapeutic relationship.

#### Sexual Satisfaction Q-Sort (McClelland, 2014a, 2014b)

- 63 statements concerning behaviors, feelings, and experiences during sexual satisfaction.
- Four categories: **emotional and masculine, relational and feminine, partner focused, orgasm focused**.

### Openness to Experience

#### KEY STUDY: Chodorkoff (1954)

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Sample | College students |
| Method | Q-sort + clinical descriptions (TAT and Rorschach); measured perceptual defense against threatening vs. neutral words |
| Groups | Good-adjustment vs. poor-adjustment |
| Finding 1 | All subjects slower to perceive threatening words than neutral words |
| Finding 2 | Effect more marked in the **defensive, poorly adjusted** group |
| Finding 3 | Significantly less perceptual defense in the **good-adjustment** group |
| Finding 4 | Closer agreement between students' self-descriptions and clinicians' descriptions = better adjusted |

### Acceptance of Self

#### KEY STUDY: Medinnus & Curtis (1963)

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Sample | 56 mothers |
| Hypothesis | Self-accepting people (congruent perceived and ideal selves) more likely to accept others as they really are |
| Finding 1 | Self-accepting mothers more accepting of their children's nature |
| Finding 2 | Child's degree of self-acceptance partly depended on the mother's degree of self-acceptance |

#### Additional Research

| Study | Finding |
|---|---|
| Pedrotti & Edwards (2010) | Multiracial adults with high self-acceptance about racial identities showed stronger positive growth |
| Baldwin (1949) | Unconditional acceptance + democratic child-rearing = children with higher self-esteem and greater emotional security |
| Coopersmith (1967) | Parents of high-self-esteem children: affectionate, used reward. Parents of low-self-esteem children: aloof, less loving, used punishment |
| Harrington, Block, & Block (1987) | Adolescents raised with unconditional positive regard developed greater creative potential |
| Harter et al. (1996) | Adolescents receiving unconditional positive regard: more confident, hopeful, acted in line with true selves. Conditional positive regard: lacked confidence, took actions inconsistent with true selves to obtain parental approval |
| Nolan (2008) | High school students receiving unconditional positive regard from teachers scored higher in positive self-esteem |
| Shallcross et al. (2013) | Growing older increases self-adjustment; decreases feelings of anger and anxiety |

### Emotional Adjustment

- Multiple studies support Rogers's suggestion that **incongruence between perceived self and ideal self** indicates poor emotional adjustment.
- Greater discrepancy between perceived and ideal self correlates with:
  - Higher anxiety, insecurity, self-doubt, depression
  - Social incompetence and other psychological disorders
  - Low self-actualization and low self-esteem
  - (Achenbach & Zigler, 1963; Gough et al., 1983; Mahoney & Hartnett, 1973; Moretti & Higgins, 1990; Straumann et al., 1991)
- Persons with large discrepancies rated by others as **awkward, confused, and unfriendly** (Gough, Lazzari, & Fioravanti, 1978).

#### KEY STUDY: Ford (1991) — Temperament and Maladjustment

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Purpose | Test Rogers's idea that failure to realize innate actualization tendency leads to maladjustment |
| Theoretical basis | Buss & Plomin's EAS temperaments: Emotionality, Activity, Sociability |
| Method | EAS Temperament Survey; parents recalled children's early temperaments; compared with students' current self-perceptions |
| Finding | Greater discrepancy between childhood temperament potential and adult realization = greater maladjustment (supports Rogers) |

### Organismic Valuing Process Research

- Some evidence supports the organismic valuing process.
- Positive self-regard may be **less prevalent in collectivist cultures** (e.g., Japan) than in individualistic cultures (e.g., US): Heine et al. (1999); Joseph & Linley (2005); Sheldon et al. (2003).

## Reflections on Rogers's Theory

### Criticisms

- Rogers insisted only person-centered therapy examining subjective experiences via self-reports could explore personality.
- Critics charge he **ignored unconscious factors** that could influence behavior without the client's awareness.
- Clients may **distort self-reports**: repressing some events, elaborating on or inventing others, presenting an idealized self-image.

### The Influence of World War II on Person-Centered Therapy

- Person-centered therapy rapidly became popular partly due to **post-WWII social circumstances (1945)**.
- Veterans needed help readjusting to civilian life; enormous demand for accessible counseling techniques.
- Training in traditional psychoanalysis required a medical degree and lengthy specialization.
- Person-centered therapy: "simple, informal, and brief, and it required little training" (DeCarvalho, 1999, p. 142).
- More than **400 college counseling centers** based on Rogers's teachings established post-WWII under the **Veterans Administration**.
- Hundreds more colleges established their own counseling centers.
- Rogers acknowledged post-WWII stresses "catapulted his method and his name to prominence and worldwide fame."

### Acceptance of Rogers's Therapy

- Applications beyond emotional disturbance treatment:
  - Enhancing self-image
  - Business world: training method for managers
  - Helping professions: training clinical psychologists, social workers, and counselors
- Psychotherapists of many orientations have adopted Rogers's core concepts.
- More than **200 training centers** (mostly in Europe) promote Rogers's form of therapy.
- Several dozen journals devoted to research and application of Rogers's ideas.
- Journal *Person-Centered & Experiential Psychotherapies* began publication in **2002**.

### Rogers's Personality Theory

- Less influential than his psychotherapy but widely recognized — particularly for emphasis on the **self-concept**.
- Rogers did not believe he had influenced academic or scientific psychology.
- Nonetheless, stimulated significant research on: nature of psychotherapy, client-therapist interaction, and the self-concept.
- Significant impact on psychology's theoretical and empirical definitions of the self.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Actualizing tendency** | The basic human motivation — the innate tendency to actualize, maintain, and enhance the self; encompasses all physiological and psychological needs |
| **Organismic valuing process** | The governing process throughout the life span by which all life experiences are evaluated in terms of how well they serve the actualizing tendency; positive value = promotes actualization; negative value = hinders it |
| **Self-concept** | The organized, consistent perceptual gestalt defined by I, me, and myself; includes image of what we are, should be, and would like to be |
| **Positive regard** | The universal, persistent need for acceptance, love, and approval from others — most notably from the mother during infancy |
| **Unconditional positive regard** | Approval granted regardless of a person's behavior; love and acceptance not contingent on the child's actions; the condition Rogers advocated in therapy |
| **Conditional positive regard** | Approval, love, or acceptance granted only when a person expresses desirable behaviors and attitudes; creates conditions of worth |
| **Positive self-regard** | The condition under which we grant ourselves acceptance and approval; a feeling of contentment with oneself; develops from internalization of positive regard from others |
| **Conditions of worth** | The belief that we are worthy of approval only when we express desirable behaviors and attitudes; Rogers's version of the Freudian superego; derived from conditional positive regard |
| **Incongruence** | A discrepancy between a person's self-concept and aspects of his or her experience; leads to anxiety and defensive distortion of perception |
| **Congruence** | Compatibility/correspondence between the self-concept and experiences; characteristic of psychological health |
| **Fully functioning person** | Rogers's term for self-actualization; a person who has developed all facets of the self; always in the process of actualizing, never a finished state |
| **Person-centered therapy** | Rogers's approach to therapy in which the client (not therapist) is responsible for changing his or her personality; therapist provides unconditional positive regard and facilitates change |
| **Encounter groups** | A group therapy technique (8–15 people; 20–60 hours) in which people learn about their feelings and how they relate to one another; no formal structure; a facilitator creates an atmosphere for self-expression |
| **Q-sort technique** | A self-report technique for assessing aspects of the self-concept; clients sort statements into categories from most to least descriptive; developed by William Stephenson (1953) |
| **Perceived self** | How one actually sees oneself currently |
| **Ideal self** | The person one would most like to be |
| **Self-insight** | Acceptance of self and reality, plus a sense of responsibility for the self; found by Rogers's research to be the single most important predictor of behavior in delinquent children |
| **Maturation** | Genetically determined development of the body's parts and processes; brought to fruition by the actualizing tendency |
| **Experience Inventory** | A self-report questionnaire (Coan, 1972) assessing openness/receptivity to experience |
| **Experiencing Scale** | A measure of self-trust (Gendlin & Tomlinson, 1967); subjects' free talk is rated for degree to which feelings guide behavior |
| **Phenomenological approach** | Understanding personality from the individual's own viewpoint based on subjective experiences |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Rogers's single basic motivation**: The organism has ONE basic tendency — to actualize, maintain, and enhance the experiencing organism. This is the **actualizing tendency** (contrast: Freud has multiple drives; Murray has needs).

2. **Three names for Rogers's therapy**: Originally **non-directive therapy** → **client-centered therapy** → **person-centered therapy**. Rogers used **"client"** (not "patient") deliberately.

3. **Kell's finding (replicated by McNeil)**: Among all factors predicting delinquent behavior, **self-insight** — not family environment or social interactions — was the single most important predictor. This surprise finding redirected Rogers's entire career.

4. **Unconditional vs. conditional positive regard**: Unconditional = love given freely regardless of behavior (healthy development); Conditional = love contingent on behavior (leads to conditions of worth).

5. **Conditions of worth = Rogers's Freudian superego**: Formed when children internalize parents' approval/disapproval standards; children believe they are worthy only under parent-defined conditions; inhibits full actualization.

6. **Incongruence**: Discrepancy between self-concept and experience = source of anxiety and maladjustment. The greater the incongruence, the greater the anxiety, depression, and poor adjustment.

7. **Fully functioning person — all 7 characteristics** (Table 10.1):
   - Awareness of all experience (open to positive AND negative feelings)
   - Freshness of appreciation / living fully in every moment
   - Trust in one's own organism
   - Freedom of choice without constraints
   - Creativity and spontaneity
   - Continual need to grow
   - State of **actualizing** (not actualized) — "a direction, not a destination"

8. **Q-sort technique developed by William Stephenson (1953)** — NOT Rogers. Mrs. Oak's pre-therapy r: **+.36**; post-therapy r: **+.79**. Butler & Haigh (25 clients): pre-therapy r: **.01**; post-therapy r: **+.31**.

9. **Encounter groups**: Groups of **8–15 people** meeting **20–60 hours** over several sessions; NO formal structure or agenda; facilitators (not leaders); Faith et al. (1995): results comparable to traditional therapies; larger, more frequent groups = better outcomes.

10. **Rogers's methodological innovation**: Introduced **recording and filming of therapy sessions** — "a microscope with which to examine the molecules of personality change" (1974, p. 120). Before this, only therapist's reconstructed notes existed.

11. **World War II impact**: Post-WWII demand led to rapid adoption of person-centered therapy. More than **400 college counseling centers** established under the **Veterans Administration** post-WWII. The therapy was valued for being "simple, informal, and brief, and requiring little training."

12. **Organismic valuing process and culture**: Positive self-regard may be less prevalent in **collectivist cultures** (e.g., Japan) than in individualistic cultures (e.g., US) — Heine et al. (1999).

13. **Rogers on human nature**: Strongly **optimistic** and **free will** oriented; humans not controlled by instinctive forces or first 5 years of life (direct contrast with Freud); prominent role for environment over heredity; present feelings more vital than childhood events.

14. **Positive self-regard is reciprocal** in nature: when people receive positive regard and develop positive self-regard, they in turn provide positive regard to others.

15. **Key criticisms**: Ignored unconscious forces; relied solely on subjective self-reports which clients may distort; approach cannot capture non-conscious influences; despite calling himself a non-scientist, Rogers used quantitative techniques (Q-sort, correlation coefficients) to analyze therapy sessions.
