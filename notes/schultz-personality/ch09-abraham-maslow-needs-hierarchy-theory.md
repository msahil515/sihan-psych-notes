# Ch 9: Abraham Maslow: Needs-Hierarchy Theory

Abraham Maslow, founder and spiritual leader of the humanistic psychology movement, proposed a hierarchy of five innate needs that motivate and direct human behavior, culminating in **self-actualization** — the fullest realization of personal potential. Unlike Freud (who studied pathology) or the behaviorists (who studied observable behavior only), Maslow studied the healthiest, most creative, and most fulfilled individuals to determine the upper limits of human potential. His theory is optimistic, humanistic, and rooted in the conviction that human nature is fundamentally good.

---

## The Life of Maslow (1908–1970)

### A Miserable Childhood

- Born **1908** in **Brooklyn, New York**; the **oldest of seven children**.
- Parents were **immigrants** with little education and extremely poor circumstances.
- Maslow's own statement: "with my childhood, it's a wonder I'm not psychotic" (Hall, 1968).
- Described his family as "miserable" and his mother as "a horrible creature" (Hoffman, 1996).
- Father: aloof, periodically abandoned the family; Maslow described him as loving "whiskey and women and fighting."
- Mother: showed open favoritism toward younger siblings; **never showed affection** toward Maslow; punished him harshly; threatened divine retribution for misbehavior.
- Killed stray kittens Maslow brought home by bashing their heads against a wall — an act Maslow never forgave.
- When his mother died, Maslow **refused to attend her funeral**.
- His antipathy toward his mother profoundly shaped his life philosophy and psychology: "The whole thrust of my life-philosophy … has its roots in a hatred for and revulsion against everything she stood for" (Hoffman, 1988).

### Alone in the World

- Felt ugly due to a prominent nose; had a scrawny build.
- Father publicly mocked his appearance at a family gathering: "Isn't Abe the ugliest kid you've ever seen?"
- Self-described: "I was all alone in the world … a very profound feeling that somehow I was wrong … one big aching inferiority complex."
- Attempted to compensate through **athletic achievement** — a living example of **Alfred Adler's concept of compensation for inferiority feelings**.

### Finding a New Style of Life

- Turned to books when athletics failed; the library became his refuge.
- Remembered arriving at the library before it opened and at school an hour early.
- Accepted to **City College of New York**; initially failed a course and was on academic probation.
- Briefly studied **law** at his father's request; quit after two weeks.

### Becoming a Behaviorist

- Married his cousin **Bertha** (age 20 and 19 respectively) at the **University of Wisconsin**, where both enrolled.
- Marriage provided a sense of belonging and direction. Said his life had little meaning until he married Bertha and began studies at Wisconsin.
- At Cornell had enrolled in a psychology course and found it "awful and bloodless … nothing to do with people."
- At Wisconsin, encountered the **behavioral psychology of John B. Watson** and became enraptured, believing behaviorism could solve the world's problems.
- Training included experimental work on **dominance and sexual behavior in primates**.

### From Monkeys to Self-Actualization

- Key influences that shifted his thinking away from behaviorism:
  1. **Onset of World War II** — resolved to develop a psychology addressing the highest human ideals.
  2. **Birth of his first child** — felt "small and weak and feeble before all this … anyone who had a baby couldn't be a behaviorist."
- Received **Ph.D. from the University of Wisconsin in 1934**.
- Postdoctoral fellowship under **E. L. Thorndike** at **Columbia University**.
- Taught at **Brooklyn College** (until 1951).
- Thorndike administered intelligence tests; Maslow scored an **IQ of 195** (within the genius range).
- In New York in the late 1930s/early 1940s, met **emigrant intellectuals fleeing Nazi Germany** including **Karen Horney** and **Alfred Adler**; was "tremendously excited" by Adler's theories.
- Also met **Gestalt psychologist Max Wertheimer** and **American anthropologist Ruth Benedict** — his admiration for them kindled his ideas about self-actualization.

#### A Parade Changes His Life

- **1941**: Witnessed a parade shortly after Japan's **Pearl Harbor attack** on the U.S. naval base.
- Resolved to devote himself to a psychology dealing with the highest human ideals — to show people are capable of better behavior than prejudice, hatred, and aggression.

#### Becoming Famous

- Taught at **Brandeis University** from **1951 to 1969**.
- A foundation grant allowed him to move to **California** to work on a humanistic philosophy of politics, economics, and ethics.
- Elected **president of the American Psychological Association in 1967**.
- Developed ailments: stomach disorders, insomnia, depression, heart disease.
- Died **1970** of a massive heart attack while **jogging around his swimming pool** (exercise recommended by his cardiologist).

## Personality Development: The Hierarchy of Needs

Maslow proposed a **hierarchy of five innate needs** that activate and direct human behavior (1968, 1970b). The needs are:
1. Physiological
2. Safety
3. Belongingness and Love
4. Esteem
5. Self-Actualization

### Key Terminology

- **Hierarchy of five innate needs**: An arrangement of innate needs, from strongest to weakest, that activates and directs behavior.
- **Instinctoid needs**: Maslow's term for the innate needs; they have a **hereditary component** but the behaviors used to satisfy them are **learned** (subject to variation).
- Needs are arranged **strongest at the bottom, weakest at the top**.
- **Lower needs must be at least partially satisfied before higher needs become influential.**
- Only **one need dominates** personality at any given time.
- The hierarchy can be **reversed** by circumstances (e.g., economic recession can reinstate physiological/safety priority).

### Characteristics of Needs

1. **Strength is inversely related to position**: Lower needs are stronger, more potent, and more urgent; higher needs are weaker.
2. **Higher needs appear later in life**:
   - Physiological and safety needs: **infancy**
   - Belongingness and esteem needs: **adolescence**
   - Self-actualization need: **midlife**
3. **Higher needs can be postponed**: Failure to satisfy a higher need does not produce a crisis; failure to satisfy a lower need **does** produce a crisis.
4. **Deficit (Deficiency) needs**: Lower needs — failure to satisfy them produces a **deficit or lack** in the individual. Also called **D-needs**.
5. **Growth (Being) needs**: Higher needs — satisfaction contributes to personal growth, health, happiness, contentment, fulfillment, and longevity. Also called **B-needs**.
6. **Greater external circumstances required for higher needs**: E.g., self-actualization requires greater freedom of expression than safety needs.
7. **Partial satisfaction principle**: A need does not have to be fully satisfied before the next becomes important. Maslow's hypothetical example:
   - Physiological: 85% satisfied
   - Safety: 70% satisfied
   - Belongingness/love: 50% satisfied
   - Esteem: 40% satisfied
   - Self-actualization: 10% satisfied

---

### Physiological Needs

- The **most basic and powerful needs**: food, water, sex (note: sex is a **physiological** need, not a love need).
- In affluent industrialized cultures, these are rarely a concern for middle-class individuals.
- A need that is **gratified** no longer motivates behavior.
- Have **greater impact as motivating forces** in cultures where basic survival is an everyday concern.

---

### Safety Needs

- Include: **stability, security, freedom from fear and anxiety, and order**.
- Particularly visible in **infants and children**: react immediately and visibly to threats to security.
- Adults have learned to inhibit reactions to dangerous situations.
- Children's **preference for structure, routine, and predictability** reflects safety needs.
- Too much permissiveness in childhood leads to anxiety and insecurity (threatens safety needs).
- **Neurotic and insecure adults**: still dominated by safety needs; compulsively avoid new experiences, organize their world predictably.
- Most normal adults have satisfied safety needs, but preferences for insurance, savings, stable jobs reflect residual safety motivation.
- Safety needs are **important drives for infants and neurotic adults** (not overwhelmingly for healthy normal adults).

---

### Belongingness and Love Needs

- Emerge once physiological and safety needs are reasonably satisfied.
- Expressed through **close relationships** (friend, lover, mate) or **social group membership**.
- Also maintained through **social media** connections.
- **Maslow did not equate love with sex** (sex = physiological need; love = distinct, higher need).
- Failure to satisfy the love need is described as a **fundamental cause of emotional maladjustment**.

---

### Esteem Needs

- **Two forms**:
  1. **Self-esteem**: Feelings of self-worth, respect from oneself.
  2. **Esteem from others**: Status, recognition, social success.
- Satisfaction of self-esteem: feelings of **confidence, strength, worth, and adequacy** → greater competence and productivity.
- Lack of self-esteem: feelings of **inferiority, helplessness, and discouragement**.

---

### The Self-Actualization Need

- The **highest need** in the hierarchy.
- **Self-actualization**: The fullest development of the self; maximum realization and fulfillment of potentials, talents, and abilities.
- Even if all other needs are satisfied, failure to self-actualize leads to **restlessness, frustration, and discontent**.
- Maslow: "A musician must make music, an artist must paint, a poet must write … to be ultimately at peace."
- Not limited to creative superstars: "A first-rate soup is more creative than a second-rate painting … cooking or parenthood or making a home could be creative, while poetry need not be" (1987, p. 159).
- **Everyone**, regardless of occupation or interests, is capable of maximizing personal abilities.

#### Conditions for Achieving Self-Actualization

1. Must be **free of constraints** imposed by society and by oneself.
2. Must **not be distracted** by lower-order needs.
3. Must be **secure in self-image** and relationships; able to love and be loved.
4. Must have **realistic knowledge of strengths and weaknesses**, virtues and vices.

#### Achieving Self-Actualization in Non-Traditional Ways

- Exceptions to the standard hierarchy exist:
  - People who **fast until death** for their beliefs (deny physiological/safety needs for self-actualization).
  - **Religious figures** who abandon worldly goods (satisfy self-actualization while frustrating lower needs).
  - **Artists** who imperil health and security for their work.
  - Common reversal: placing **esteem above love** — believing love can only be satisfied after self-confidence is established.

---

### Cognitive Needs

- Maslow later proposed a **second set of innate needs**: the **cognitive needs** — to **know** and to **understand**.
- Exist **outside** the original five-need hierarchy.
- **Need to know is stronger than the need to understand**; must be at least partially satisfied before the need to understand emerges.
- Appear in **late infancy and early childhood** as natural curiosity.
- Do **not have to be taught**, but parents and teachers can **inhibit** spontaneous curiosity.
- Failure to satisfy cognitive needs: **harmful**, hampers full development of personality.
- **Knowing and understanding are basic** to interacting with the environment in an emotionally healthy, mature way.
- **It is impossible to become self-actualizing** without meeting the needs to know and to understand.

#### Evidence for Cognitive Needs (Maslow, 1970b)

| Evidence Type | Finding |
|---|---|
| Laboratory studies | Animals explore/manipulate environment for no reward other than curiosity |
| Historical evidence | People have sought knowledge at risk of their lives (placing it above safety needs) |
| Clinical observation | Emotionally healthy adults attracted to mysterious/unexplained events |
| Clinical practice | Emotionally healthy adults complained of boredom in "stupid jobs"; improved with more challenging activities |

## The Study of Self-Actualizers

### Metamotivation

- Self-actualizers differ from others in their **basic motivation**.
- Maslow proposed **metamotivation** (also called **B-motivation** or **Being-motivation**) for self-actualizers.
  - **Metamotivation**: The motivation of self-actualizers, which involves **maximizing personal potential** rather than striving for a particular goal object.
  - The prefix **meta-** means "after" or "beyond" — goes beyond traditional psychology's idea of motivation.
- Self-actualizers are **not motivated to strive for a particular goal**; they are **developing from within**.

#### D-Motivation vs. B-Motivation

| Feature | D-Motivation (Deficiency) | B-Motivation (Being / Metamotivation) |
|---|---|---|
| Who | Non-self-actualizers | Self-actualizers |
| Nature | Striving for something specific to make up for a lack | Developing from within; fulfilling potential |
| Goal | Specific goal object (e.g., food for hunger) | No specific goal object |
| Tension | Seeks to **reduce** tension | Seeks to **increase** tension (for stimulating, challenging experiences) |
| Applies to | Physiological, safety, love, esteem needs | State of "being" — spontaneous, natural, joyful |

- D-motivation applies to physiological, safety, belongingness/love, and esteem needs.
- Self-actualizers function **beyond striving for specific goal objects** to satisfy a deficit; they are in a state of **"being."**

### Metaneeds

- **Metaneeds**: States of **growth or being** toward which self-actualizers evolve (e.g., goodness, uniqueness, perfection) — states of being rather than specific goal objects.
- Failure to satisfy metaneeds produces **metapathology**: a thwarting of self-development that prevents self-actualizers from expressing, using, and fulfilling their potential.
- Those experiencing metapathology may feel **helpless and depressed**, unable to pinpoint the source or identify an alleviating goal.

#### Maslow's Metaneeds and Corresponding Metapathologies (Table 9.1)

| Metaneed | Metapathology (if unmet) |
|---|---|
| Truth | Mistrust, cynicism, skepticism |
| Goodness | Hatred, repulsion, disgust, reliance only on self |
| Beauty | Vulgarity, restlessness, loss of taste, bleakness |
| Unity, Wholeness | Disintegration |
| Dichotomy-transcendence | Black/white thinking, either/or thinking, simplistic view of life |
| Aliveness, Process | Deadness, robotizing, feeling totally determined, loss of emotion and zest |
| Uniqueness | Loss of individuality, feeling interchangeable or anonymous |
| Perfection | Hopelessness, nothing to work for |
| Necessity | Chaos, unpredictability |
| Completion, Finality | Incompleteness, hopelessness, cessation of striving |
| Justice | Anger, cynicism, mistrust, lawlessness, total selfishness |
| Order | Insecurity, wariness, loss of safety and predictability |
| Simplicity | Over-complexity, confusion, bewilderment, loss of orientation |
| Richness, Totality, Comprehensiveness | Depression, uneasiness, loss of interest in the world |
| Effortlessness | Fatigue, strain, clumsiness, awkwardness, stiffness |
| Playfulness | Grimness, depression, paranoid humorlessness, loss of zest |
| Self-sufficiency | Responsibility given to others |
| Meaningfulness | Meaninglessness, despair, senselessness of life |

*Source: Adapted from The Farther Reaches of Human Nature, A. H. Maslow, 1971.*

### Characteristics of Self-Actualizers

Maslow estimated self-actualizers constitute **1 percent or less** of the population. He first observed these qualities in **Ruth Benedict** and **Max Wertheimer**.

| Characteristic | Description |
|---|---|
| **Efficient perception of reality** | Perceive the world and people clearly, objectively, without prejudgments or preconceptions |
| **Acceptance of self, others, and nature** | Accept own strengths/weaknesses without distortion or guilt; accept others' weaknesses too |
| **Spontaneity, simplicity, and naturalness** | Open, direct, natural; rarely hide feelings; feel secure enough to be themselves |
| **Focus on problems outside themselves** | Have a sense of mission/commitment; dedication to a cause or vocation is a **requirement** for self-actualization |
| **Sense of detachment and need for privacy** | Can experience isolation without harm; need solitude more than average; autonomous, do not crave social support |
| **Freshness of appreciation** | Perceive the world with freshness, wonder, and awe; enjoy recurrent experiences as if for the first time |
| **Peak experiences** | Know moments of intense ecstasy (not unlike religious experiences) during which the self is transcended; feel supremely powerful, confident, decisive |
| **Social interest** | (Adopted from Alfred Adler) Sympathy and empathy for all humanity; desire to help others even when irritated by them |
| **Profound interpersonal relations** | Deep, lasting (though not large in number) friendships; often attract admirers/disciples (usually one-sided) |
| **Creativeness** | Highly creative; inventive and original; flexible, spontaneous; willing to make mistakes and learn from them |
| **Resistance to enculturation** | Autonomous, independent, self-sufficient; resist social/cultural pressures without openly rebelling |

- Self-actualizers have **human flaws**: can be rude, even ruthless; experience doubts, conflicts, tension — but less frequently and less intensely than others.

---

### Failure to Become Self-Actualizing

- Self-actualization is the **weakest need** in the hierarchy (highest position = weakest) → easily inhibited.
- Less than **1%** of the population reaches this state.

#### Reasons for Failure

1. **Hostile or rejecting parents**: Impair satisfaction of love and esteem needs → self-actualization need may never emerge.
2. **Poor economic conditions**: Make physiological and safety needs primary; self-actualization assumes less importance.
3. **Inadequate education and improper child-rearing**:
   - Sex-role training (e.g., boys taught to inhibit tenderness) blocks full development.
   - Overprotection (not permitting new behaviors, ideas, skills) → inhibited adults.
   - **Excessive permissiveness** is equally harmful: leads to anxiety and insecurity, undermining safety needs.
   - **Ideal balance**: permissiveness **within limits of capacity to cope**, with guidance.
4. **Childhood deficits in love and security**:
   - Sufficient love in the **first two years** is prerequisite for self-actualization.
   - Comparison to **Erik Erikson's** emphasis on development of trust in early childhood and **Karen Horney's** ideas on the childhood need for security.

#### The Jonah Complex

- **Jonah complex**: The fear that maximizing our potential will lead to a situation with which we will be unable to cope.
- Based on the biblical tale of **Jonah** (called by God to prophesy, afraid of the task, tried to run away, eventually had to accept his fate).
- We may simultaneously **fear and be thrilled** by our own possibilities — but too often the fear takes precedence.
- Self-actualization requires **courage** — it takes effort, discipline, and self-control.
- Self-actualizers **constantly test themselves** by abandoning secure routines and familiar behaviors and attitudes.

## Questions about Human Nature

| Dimension | Maslow's Position |
|---|---|
| Free will vs. determinism | Strong **free will**: we choose how to satisfy needs and actualize potential; ultimately responsible for our own development |
| Nature vs. nurture | **Interaction**: needs are innate (hereditary); behaviors to satisfy them are **learned** (environmental) |
| Uniqueness vs. universality | Implicit emphasis on **uniqueness** of personality; universal needs but varied behaviors |
| Early vs. later experiences | Childhood important but we are **not victims** of early experiences |
| Optimism vs. pessimism | **Optimistic and humanistic** |
| Human nature | Basically **good, decent, and kind** |
| Evil | Not an inherited trait; result of **inappropriate environment**; some individuals are evil beyond reclamation |

## Assessment in Maslow's Theory

- Maslow's work began not as a formal research program but as an effort to satisfy **personal curiosity** about **Ruth Benedict** and **Max Wertheimer**.
- First research subjects: **college students** — found only **1 out of 3,000** could be described as self-actualizing.
- Concluded characteristics are **not yet developed in young people**; moved to studying **middle-aged and older** individuals — still found less than 1%.
- Self-actualizers identified included:
  - **Certain or highly probable cases**
  - **Partial cases**
  - **Potential cases**
- Historical figures studied: **Thomas Jefferson, Albert Einstein, George Washington Carver, Harriet Tubman, Eleanor Roosevelt**.

#### Assessment Methods Used by Maslow

| Method | Used for |
|---|---|
| Biographical analysis of written records | Historical figures |
| Interviews | Living subjects |
| Free association | Living subjects |
| Projective tests | Living subjects |
| Indirect observation | Used because subjects were self-conscious when questioned directly |

- Maslow himself acknowledged: "By ordinary standards of laboratory research, this simply was not research at all" (1971, p. 42).
- Considered his program **pilot studies only**; expected others to confirm his theory.

### The Personal Orientation Inventory (POI)

- Developed by psychologist **Everett Shostrom** (1964, 1974).
- A **self-report questionnaire** consisting of **150 pairs of statements**; respondents select which of each pair is more applicable to them.
- Designed to measure **self-actualization**.
- Scored on **2 major scales** and **10 subscales**:

| Major Scale | Description |
|---|---|
| **Time competence** | Degree to which one lives **in the present** |
| **Inner directedness** | How much one depends on **oneself** (rather than others) for judgments and values |

#### Sample POI Items (Table 9.3)

| Column A | Column B |
|---|---|
| I do what others expect of me. | I feel free to not do what others expect of me. |
| I must justify my actions in pursuit of my interests. | I need not justify my actions in pursuit of my interests. |
| I live by the rules and standards of society. | I do not always need to live by the rules and standards of society. |
| Reasons are needed to justify my feelings. | Reasons are not needed to justify my feelings. |
| I will continue to grow only by setting a high, socially approved goal. | I will continue to grow best by being myself. |
| People should always control their anger. | People should express honestly felt anger. |

---

### The Smartphone Basic Needs Scale

- Developed by **Kang & Jung (2014)**.
- A **20-item self-report inventory** to assess the degree to which **smartphone use can satisfy Maslow's needs**.
- Developed using college students in the **United States and South Korea**.
- Reported high levels of **validity and reliability**.
- Example items (for **belongingness need**):
  - "By using my Smartphone, I can…"
    1. Get closer to important people around me
    2. Meet nice people
    3. Develop relationships with others
    4. Get along with people well
    5. Work and communicate together

## Research on Maslow's Theory

Maslow did not use case studies, experimental methods, or correlational methods in his own research. External researchers, however, have tested aspects of his theory.

### The Hierarchy of Needs

> **KEY STUDY**: Williams & Page (1989)
> - **Sample**: Male and female college students
> - **Finding**: Satisfaction of safety, belongingness, and esteem needs was **negatively related to neuroticism and depression**. Esteem needs were **stronger than belongingness needs**. Less concern with safety needs (as expected among college-attending individuals).

> **KEY STUDY**: Lester (1990) — Need Satisfaction Inventory
> - **Sample**: College students
> - **Method**: Correlated Need Satisfaction Inventory scores with Eysenck Personality Inventory scores
> - **Finding**: Higher need satisfaction = **lower neuroticism**.

> **KEY STUDY**: Graham & Balloun (1973)
> - **Sample**: Representative general population
> - **Method**: Elaborate test of the hierarchy
> - **Finding**: Supported the **order of the five needs**; concern about each need **increased from lowest to highest need** (physiological needs = little concern; self-actualization = greatest interest).

> **KEY STUDY**: Noltemeyer, Bush, Patton, & Bergen (2012)
> - **Sample**: Economically disadvantaged youth in Midwestern USA
> - **Finding**: Primarily focused on **safety needs**; little concern for higher needs. Consistent with Maslow's theory.

> **KEY STUDY**: Tay & Diener (2011)
> - **Sample**: 40,000+ people in **123 countries**
> - **Finding**: Supported the view that basic physiological and safety needs must be satisfied before people can achieve **fulfillment and happiness**.

> **KEY STUDY**: Taormina & Gao (2013); Ivtzan et al. (2013)
> - **Finding**: More basic needs satisfied = greater chances higher needs also satisfied; people **older than 36** are far more likely to achieve self-actualization than younger people.

---

### The Belongingness Need

> **KEY STUDY**: Gardner, Pickett, & Brewer (2000)
> - **Method**: College students led to believe they were in an Internet chat room; then **excluded and rejected** by perceived other participants (no responses to messages).
> - **Task**: Read a diary by a college student; recall activities from the diary.
> - **Finding**: Students whose belongingness need had been **thwarted** recalled **significantly more social events** from the diary than those whose need had been satisfied.
> - **Conclusion**: Failure to satisfy the need to belong can influence **memory**, specifically affecting the type of events recalled.

> **KEY STUDY**: Smith & Williams (2004)
> - **Method**: College students led to believe they had been **excluded or rejected** by Internet chat room members.
> - **Finding**: Reported **lower levels of self-esteem, control, and belongingness** than non-ostracized students — negative feelings developed within **only 8 minutes** of being informed of exclusion.

> **KEY STUDY**: Collisson (2013); Seehusen et al. (2013)
> - **Populations**: Netherlands and United States
> - **Finding**: People high in need to belong **projected belongingness need onto others** (perceived others as also high in belongingness need); need to belong triggers **nostalgia** for the past (where they perceived greater belonging).

> **KEY STUDY**: Begen & Turner-Cobb (2012)
> - **Sample**: American teenagers
> - **Finding**: Teenagers who felt a sense of **belonging** were **physically healthier** than those who did not express a sense of belonging.

### Self-Esteem

#### High Self-Esteem — Research Findings

| Study | Finding |
|---|---|
| Ellis & Taylor (1983) — College job applicants | High self-esteem students received **more job offers** and were rated more favorably by recruiters |
| Shamir (1986) | High self-esteem people cope **more effectively** with job loss |
| Campbell, Rudich, & Sedikides (2002) | High self-esteem people perceive themselves higher in intellectual skills, agreeableness, morality, and extraversion |
| Bowker (2006); Pyszczynski et al. (2004) — USA and Canada | High self-esteem young adults more likely to participate in **school sports** and have lower anxiety and defensive behaviors |
| Noser & Zeigler-Hill (2014) — Women college students | **Unstable** self-esteem strongly predicted desire to become **famous** |

#### Low Self-Esteem — Research Findings

- Large-scale multinational research (USA, Iceland, Canada, China, Norway, New Zealand) found low self-esteem linked to:
  - Anxiety, depression, smoking addiction
  - School dropout rates, criminal convictions
  - Financial problems
  - Strong emotional reactions to negative outcomes
  - Difficulties at work
- Research in Canada and China: low self-esteem linked to more **physical health problems** and more **anxiety about death**.
- College students with low self-esteem: more social problems of adjustment.
- Adolescents (Seidah & Bouffard, 2007 — Canada, grades 7, 9, 11): More than one-third reported **perceived physical appearance** determined their self-esteem level; more concern with appearance = lower self-esteem; **no gender differences**.
- People with low self-esteem who perceived deliberate exclusion from a group: reported **significantly greater feelings of rejection** (Nezlek et al., 1997).
- Failure to achieve self-esteem goals (grades, job success) → increased anger, shame, sadness, worthlessness (Crocker & Park, 2004).
- Low self-esteem college students: **spent more time on Facebook** and social networking sites (Kalpidou et al., 2011; Mehdizadeh, 2010; Vogel et al., 2014).
- College students placed far greater value on **self-esteem boosting activities** (praise, high grades) than on eating favorite food, drinking alcohol, time with friends, paycheck, or sex (Bushman et al., 2011; Salamon, 2011).

#### Effects of Self-Esteem on Political Behavior

- Belgium and USA: Among older people (average age 71), **conservative political beliefs correlated with high self-esteem** (Van Hiel & Brebels, 2011).
- U.S. 2008 presidential primaries and election: People more likely to vote for candidates perceived as having **greater self-esteem** (Ziegler-Hill & Myers, 2009).

#### Stability of Self-Esteem over the Lifespan

- Self-esteem **increases** during adolescence and adulthood, **peaks at approximately age 60**, then **declines** (Gentile et al., 2010; Orth & Robins, 2014; Orth et al., 2010).
- Taiwan study: Self-esteem increased through childhood, adolescence, early adulthood; changed very little after **age 30** (Huang, 2010b).
- Germany: Being **married** and high **subjective well-being** linked to high self-esteem (Wagner et al., 2014).

#### Ethnic and Cultural Differences in Self-Esteem

| Population | Finding |
|---|---|
| Black teenagers vs. White teenagers | Black teenagers report **higher self-esteem**; self-esteem increases for Blacks throughout adulthood but declines more steeply after age 60 |
| Black women college students | Higher self-esteem among those who **strongly identify with Black culture** (Eaton et al., 2010) |
| Black college students feeling incompetent/inadequate | High stress and **low self-esteem** (Peteet et al., 2014) |
| 50+ countries (college students) | Students in **Japan** consistently scored the **lowest in self-esteem** (Schmitt & Allik, 2005; Tafarodi et al., 2011; Yamaguchi et al., 2007) |
| Asian Americans in USA | Generally lower self-esteem than European Americans; those with greater social connections in dorms scored higher (Fong & Mashek, 2014) |
| Mexican-American teenage boys | High self-esteem correlated with delinquency rates (Caldwell et al., 2006; Swenson & Prelow, 2005) |
| Mexican immigrants to USA | Greater pressure to adopt American culture = lower self-esteem (Kim et al., 2014) |
| Turkish and Moroccan immigrants in Netherlands | Stronger ethnic identity = greater self-esteem (Verkuyten, 2009) |
| Puerto Rican immigrants in USA | Stronger ethnic identity = greater self-esteem (Lopez, 2008) |

#### Self-Actualization (POI Research)

- Higher self-actualization scores on the POI are **positively related** to: emotional health, creativity, well-being following therapy, academic achievement, autonomy, racial tolerance.
- **Negatively related** to: alcoholism, institutionalization for mental disturbances, neuroticism, depression, hypochondriasis.
- POI research on women (ages 19–55): confirmed self-actualization occurs **gradually over the lifespan**.

#### Peak Experiences (Research)

> **KEY STUDY**: Hoffman, Kaneshiro, & Compton (2012)
> - **Sample**: Americans ages 40–65
> - **Finding**: Three most frequently reported peak experiences:
>   1. Joyful experiences with **other people**
>   2. A **sense of achievement**
>   3. **Personal growth**

> Cross-cultural comparison:
> - Mainland Chinese: more experiences involving **serenity**
> - Hong Kong Chinese: more involving **interpersonal joy and external achievement**
> - Brazil and Portugal: most frequent peak experiences involved achieving a **developmental landmark**

## Self-Determination Theory

- **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** is a closely related contemporary framework that elaborates on intrinsic motivation and basic psychological needs.
- Developed by **Edward Deci and Richard Ryan**.
- Proposes three **basic psychological needs** that must be satisfied for well-being and optimal functioning:
  1. **Autonomy**: The need to feel that one's actions are self-chosen and self-directed.
  2. **Competence**: The need to feel effective and capable in one's environment.
  3. **Relatedness**: The need to feel connected to others.

### Intrinsic Motivation

- **Intrinsic motivation**: Engaging in an activity for its own sake, because it is inherently interesting or enjoyable — not for external rewards.
- **Extrinsic motivation**: Engaging in an activity because of external rewards or to avoid punishment.
- SDT holds that **intrinsic motivation is supported** when basic needs for autonomy and competence are met.
- **Overjustification effect**: When people are given external rewards for intrinsically motivated activities, their **intrinsic motivation can decrease**.
- SDT resonates with Maslow's concept of **metamotivation** — acting out of being rather than deficiency.

> **KEY STUDY Context**: Research in SDT has shown that environments supporting autonomy (rather than controlling behavior) foster greater intrinsic motivation, well-being, and psychological health — consistent with Maslow's emphasis on freedom from constraints as necessary for self-actualization.

## Reflections on Maslow's Theory

### Contributions

- **Founded humanistic psychology** as an alternative to behaviorism and psychoanalysis.
- Shifted focus from pathology to **psychological health and human potential**.
- The **hierarchy of needs** has had enormous influence in psychology, education, business, and management (e.g., used in organizational motivation theories).
- Pioneered the study of **positive personality characteristics**, opening the field later formalized as **positive psychology**.
- Introduced concepts of **peak experiences**, **metaneeds**, **metamotivation**, and the **Jonah complex**.
- The **self-actualization** concept inspired widespread research and application.

### Criticisms

| Criticism | Detail |
|---|---|
| Lack of scientific rigor | Maslow himself admitted: "By ordinary standards of laboratory research, this simply was not research at all." No experimental controls, no random sampling, no blind assessment. |
| Circular reasoning | Self-actualizers are defined by their characteristics; those characteristics are then used as evidence of self-actualization. |
| Sampling bias | Studied only a select few historical figures and acquaintances; sample was not representative. |
| Vague definitions | "Self-actualization" and related concepts are difficult to define and operationalize precisely. |
| Cultural bias | Theory developed primarily from Western, individualistic perspective; hierarchy may not apply universally. |
| Order of hierarchy questioned | Research has not always confirmed the strict sequential order of needs; needs can be satisfied simultaneously or in different orders. |
| Elitist | Less than 1% of the population reaches self-actualization — raises questions about who gets to be the judge. |
| Inadequate account of development | Maslow acknowledged childhood importance but did not articulate a full developmental stage theory. |

## Chapter Summary

- Maslow, founder of humanistic psychology, rejected behaviorism and psychoanalysis in favor of studying the healthiest, most creative individuals.
- Proposed a **hierarchy of five innate needs** (instinctoid) in order from strongest to weakest: physiological, safety, belongingness/love, esteem, self-actualization.
- Lower (deficit/deficiency) needs must be at least partially satisfied before higher (growth/being) needs emerge.
- Additionally proposed **cognitive needs** (to know and to understand) that exist outside the hierarchy.
- **Self-actualizers** (less than 1% of population) are characterized by 12 key traits including: efficient perception of reality, acceptance, spontaneity, social interest, peak experiences, creativeness, and resistance to enculturation.
- Self-actualizers operate via **metamotivation (B-motivation)** rather than D-motivation; they evolve toward **metaneeds** and can experience **metapathology** when metaneeds are unmet.
- **Failure to self-actualize** is caused by: weak nature of the highest need, hostile/rejecting parents, poor economic conditions, improper child-rearing, and the **Jonah complex**.
- Assessment tools include the **POI (Shostrom, 1964)** and the **Smartphone Basic Needs Scale (Kang & Jung, 2014)**.
- Research broadly supports the hierarchy, belongingness need, and self-esteem components.
- **Self-Determination Theory** (Deci & Ryan) extends Maslow's ideas with three basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness.
- Maslow's theory is optimistic, humanistic, and views human nature as basically good.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Hierarchy of five innate needs** | Maslow's arrangement of innate needs (physiological, safety, belongingness/love, esteem, self-actualization) from strongest to weakest, that activates and directs behavior |
| **Instinctoid needs** | Maslow's term for innate needs that have a hereditary component but are satisfied via learned behaviors |
| **Deficit (Deficiency) needs / D-needs** | Lower-order needs; failure to satisfy them produces a deficit or lack in the individual; includes physiological, safety, love, and esteem needs |
| **Growth (Being) needs / B-needs** | Higher-order needs (especially self-actualization); their satisfaction contributes to personal growth, health, and fulfillment |
| **Physiological needs** | The most basic level: food, water, sex — bodily survival requirements |
| **Safety needs** | Needs for stability, security, order, and freedom from fear and anxiety |
| **Belongingness and love needs** | Needs for affectionate relations, connection, and acceptance by others |
| **Esteem needs** | Need for self-respect and respect from others; includes self-worth and external status/recognition |
| **Self-actualization** | The highest need; the fullest development and realization of one's potentials, talents, and abilities |
| **Cognitive needs** | Innate needs to know and to understand; exist outside the five-need hierarchy; appear in late infancy |
| **Metamotivation (B-motivation)** | The motivation of self-actualizers; involves maximizing personal potential rather than striving for a specific goal object; beyond traditional motivation |
| **D-motivation (Deficiency motivation)** | Motivation of non-self-actualizers; striving for something specific to make up for a lack or deficiency |
| **Metaneeds** | States of growth or being (e.g., truth, goodness, beauty, justice, order) toward which self-actualizers evolve |
| **Metapathology** | Pathology resulting from failure to satisfy metaneeds; thwarts full development of the personality |
| **Peak experience** | A moment of intense ecstasy, similar to a religious or mystical experience, during which the self is transcended; person feels supremely powerful and decisive |
| **Jonah complex** | The fear that maximizing our potential will lead to a situation with which we will be unable to cope; a barrier to self-actualization |
| **Personal Orientation Inventory (POI)** | A 150-item self-report questionnaire developed by Everett Shostrom (1964) to measure self-actualization; scored on time competence and inner directedness |
| **Time competence** | POI major scale measuring the degree to which one lives in the present |
| **Inner directedness** | POI major scale assessing how much one depends on oneself rather than others for judgments and values |
| **Smartphone Basic Needs Scale** | 20-item self-report inventory developed by Kang & Jung (2014) to assess how smartphone use satisfies Maslow's needs |
| **Social interest** | Concept adopted from Alfred Adler; sympathy and empathy that self-actualizers have for all humanity |
| **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** | Theory by Deci and Ryan proposing three basic psychological needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) essential for well-being |
| **Intrinsic motivation** | Engaging in activity for its own sake (inherently interesting/enjoyable), not for external reward |
| **Extrinsic motivation** | Engaging in activity for external rewards or to avoid punishment |
| **Overjustification effect** | Phenomenon where providing external rewards for intrinsically motivated activity decreases intrinsic motivation |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Maslow's hierarchy order** (bottom to top): Physiological → Safety → Belongingness and Love → Esteem → Self-Actualization. Lower needs are stronger and must be at least partially satisfied before higher needs emerge.

2. **Instinctoid needs**: Maslow's term; needs are innate (hereditary component) but behaviors to satisfy them are **learned**.

3. **Deficit needs vs. Growth needs**: Deficit needs (lower four) — failure produces a crisis; growth needs (self-actualization) — failure does not produce a crisis but impairs personal development.

4. **Cognitive needs** (to know and to understand) exist **outside** the five-need hierarchy; the need to know is **stronger** than the need to understand.

5. **Metamotivation (B-motivation)** applies to self-actualizers; they are not reducing deficits but developing from within, seeking to **increase tension** through stimulating experiences; contrasted with **D-motivation** (Deficiency motivation) of non-self-actualizers.

6. **Metaneeds** are states of being (e.g., truth, beauty, justice, order) toward which self-actualizers evolve; failure to satisfy them produces **metapathology**.

7. **Self-actualizers constitute less than 1% of the population**; Maslow found only 1 out of 3,000 college students qualified.

8. **Peak experience**: intense ecstasy where the self is transcended, similar to a religious/mystical experience; characteristic of self-actualizers.

9. **Jonah complex**: fear of one's own greatness; fear that maximizing potential will lead to unmanageable situations; named after the biblical Jonah; a key reason people fail to self-actualize.

10. **Personal Orientation Inventory (POI)**: developed by **Everett Shostrom** (1964); 150-item forced-choice questionnaire; two major scales: **time competence** and **inner directedness**.

11. **Social interest**: borrowed from **Alfred Adler**; self-actualizers show empathy and sympathy for all humanity.

12. **Maslow's model of human nature**: free will; optimistic; human nature basically good; evil is environmental not innate; interaction of heredity (innate needs) and environment (learned satisfying behaviors).

13. **Self-Determination Theory** (Deci & Ryan): three basic needs — **autonomy, competence, relatedness**; related to Maslow's metamotivation concept.

14. **Tay & Diener (2011)**: study of 40,000+ people in 123 countries supporting Maslow's view that basic physiological and safety needs must be satisfied before fulfillment and happiness can be achieved.

15. **Conditions for self-actualization**: freedom from constraints, not distracted by lower-order needs, secure in self-image and relationships, realistic knowledge of strengths and weaknesses.
