# Ch 6: Erik Erikson: Identity Theory

Erikson extended Freud's psychoanalytic framework across the entire life span, proposing eight
psychosocial stages each centered on a crisis requiring adaptive or maladaptive resolution. He
elevated the ego to an independent structure, recognized cultural and historical forces as
co-determinants of personality, and introduced the concept of the **identity crisis** as a universal
developmental turning point. His theory is optimistic, developmental, and partially deterministic.

---

## The Life of Erikson (1902–1994)

### Personal Identity Crises

- Born 1902 in **Frankfurt, Germany**.
- Mother: Danish, from a wealthy Jewish family; her first husband disappeared within hours of
  the wedding.
- Erikson was conceived by another man; mother was sent to Germany to give birth out of wedlock.
- Stepfather: **Dr. Theodore Homburger**, the infant's own pediatrician.
- Erikson did not know Homburger was not his biological father for several years; grew up unsure
  of his name and psychological identity.
- Kept the surname Homburger until **age 37** when he became a U.S. citizen and changed his
  name to **Erik Homburger Erikson**.
- Identity crises at school: rejected by German classmates (family was Jewish) and by Jewish
  classmates (tall, blond, Aryan features).
- Earned mediocre grades; showed talent for art.
- His daughter later wrote that he "suffered terribly from the sense that his real father had
  abandoned him" and "struggled with a depressive tendency all his life" (Bloland, 2005).

### Dropping Out of Society

- After high school, Erikson dropped out of conventional society, traveled extensively in
  Europe, reading, writing in a notebook, and observing life.
- Described himself as "morbidly sensitive and neurotic, even close to psychotic."
- Studied at two art schools; had work exhibited at an art gallery in Munich.
- Each time left formal training to continue his wandering — a search for identity.
- Later reflected: "No doubt, my best friends will insist that I needed to name this crisis and
  to see it in everybody else in order to really come to terms with it in myself"
  (Erikson, 1975, pp. 25–26).
- A biographer noted that what Erikson "saw and felt happening to himself … became the
  'research' that enabled a flow of ideas, articles, books" (Friedman, 1999, p. 16).

### Becoming a Freudian

- At **age 25** received an offer to teach at a small school in Vienna established for children
  of Sigmund Freud's patients and friends.
- Admitted he was partly drawn to Freud because of his search for a father figure.
- Trained in psychoanalysis; analyzed by **Anna Freud** (not Sigmund Freud).
  - Sessions: almost daily for **3 years**, at a fee of **$7 a month**.
  - Anna Freud's interest in child psychoanalysis, plus Erikson's own teaching, heightened
    his awareness of social influences on personality and child development.
- After completing his program, became a member of the **Vienna Psychoanalytic Institute**.

### Three Marriages and a Stable Foundation

- Met **Joan Serson** (Canadian-born artist and dancer, previously analyzed by a Freud disciple)
  at a **masquerade ball in Vienna in 1929**.
- Initially refused to marry her when she became pregnant (fear of commitment; parents'
  disapproval of a non-Jewish daughter-in-law).
- Friends persuaded him that refusing would repeat the abandonment pattern of his own
  biological father.
- Married Joan in **three separate ceremonies**: Jewish, Protestant, and civil.
- Joan abandoned her own career to become Erikson's lifelong intellectual partner and editor.
- Erikson's half-sister commented that "he would have been nothing without Joan."

### Coming to America

- In **1933**, recognizing the growing Nazi menace, the Eriksons immigrated first to Denmark,
  then to the **United States**, settling in **Boston**.
- Established a private psychoanalytic practice specializing in **children**.
- Worked at a guidance center for emotionally disturbed delinquents and at
  **Massachusetts General Hospital**.
- Began graduate work at **Harvard** intending to get a Ph.D. in psychology; **failed his
  first course and dropped out**.
- In **1936** (with no academic degree), invited to the **Institute of Human Relations at Yale**:
  taught in the medical school and continued psychoanalytic work with children.
- Collaborated with a Yale anthropologist on a study of **Sioux Indians (South Dakota)**
  child-rearing practices, reinforcing his belief in cultural influences on childhood.
- Continued work at the **Institute of Human Development, University of California at Berkeley**.
- Sought patients from diverse cultures, including those considered normal (not just disturbed).

### Honors and Accolades — but No Identity

- Observations of American Indians revealed psychological symptoms of **alienation from
  cultural traditions** and lack of clear self-identity — initially called **identity confusion**.
- Similar condition observed in **emotionally disturbed veterans after World War II**: not
  suffering from repressed conflicts but from **confusion of identity** due to traumatic
  experiences and being uprooted from their culture.
- **1950**: joined the **Austen Riggs Center**, Stockbridge, Massachusetts — treatment facility
  for emotionally disturbed adolescents.
- **1960**: returned to Harvard to teach; retired **1970**.
- Published a book on old age at age **84** (Vital Involvement in Old Age, 1986).
- Despite accolades, his daughter wrote that "it was still a source of shame to this celebrated
  man that he had been an illegitimate child" (Bloland, 2005, p. 51).
- Died **1994**.
- Notable cultural recognition: picture appeared on covers of **Newsweek** and the **New York
  Times Magazine** — unusual for a personality theorist.
- Achieved this without ever earning a university degree in any subject.

## Psychosocial Stages of Personality Development

### Overview and Freud Comparison

- Erikson divided personality growth into **eight psychosocial stages** spanning the entire
  life span.
- The **first four** are similar to Freud's oral, anal, phallic, and latency stages.
- Key difference: Erikson emphasized **psychosocial correlates**; Freud focused on
  **biological factors**.

### Three Ways Erikson Extended Freud

1. Personality **continues to develop over the entire life span** (not just childhood).
2. **Greater emphasis on the ego** as an independent part of personality, not dependent
   on or subservient to the id.
3. Recognized the **impact of cultural and historical forces** on personality — not governed
   entirely by innate biological factors from childhood.

### The Role of Genetics and the Environment

- **Epigenetic principle of maturation**: developmental process is governed by inherited
  (genetic) forces.
  - Prefix *epi* means "upon"; development depends on genetic factors.
  - Social and environmental forces control how genetically predetermined stages are realized.
  - Personality development is affected by **both biological and social factors**.

### Conflicts and Crises

- Human development involves a series of **personal conflicts**.
- Potential for these conflicts exists at birth as **innate predispositions**; each becomes
  prominent at a different stage as the environment demands certain adaptations.
- Each confrontation with the environment is called a **crisis**: a shift in perspective
  requiring refocusing of instinctual energy.
- Each developmental stage has its own **turning point** (crisis).
- Response to crisis: **maladaptive (negative)** or **adaptive (positive)** way.
- Only when a conflict is resolved can the personality continue its normal developmental
  sequence and acquire the strength to confront the next stage.
- Unresolved conflict makes later adaptation harder, but a successful outcome is still possible.

### Adaptive Coping

- The ego must incorporate **both maladaptive and adaptive ways of coping**.
- Example: In infancy, both trust AND some degree of mistrust are needed.
  - Total trust = vulnerability to deception and manipulation.
  - Ideal: **primarily the positive/adaptive attitude balanced by some of the negative**.
- Only then can the crisis be considered satisfactorily resolved.

## Basic Strengths

- Each of the eight psychosocial stages provides an opportunity to develop **basic strengths**
  (also called **virtues**).
- Strengths emerge once the crisis has been satisfactorily resolved.
- **Interdependent**: one strength cannot develop until the strength associated with the
  previous stage has been confirmed.

### Complete Table of Stages, Ages, Adaptive vs. Maladaptive, and Basic Strengths

| Stage | Ages | Adaptive vs. Maladaptive | Basic Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oral-sensory | Birth–1 | Trust vs. Mistrust | Hope |
| Muscular-anal | 1–3 | Autonomy vs. Doubt, Shame | Will |
| Locomotor-genital | 3–5 | Initiative vs. Guilt | Purpose |
| Latency | 6–11 | Industriousness vs. Inferiority | Competence |
| Adolescence | 12–18 | Identity cohesion vs. Role confusion | Fidelity |
| Young adulthood | 18–35 | Intimacy vs. Isolation | Love |
| Adulthood | 35–55 | Generativity vs. Stagnation | Care |
| Maturity/old age | 55+ | Ego integrity vs. Despair | Wisdom |

### Stage 1: Trust versus Mistrust (Oral-sensory, Birth–1)

- Parallels Freud's **oral stage**.
- Infant is totally dependent on mother/primary caregiver for survival, security, and affection.
- The mouth is of vital importance: infant "lives through, and loves with, [the] mouth"
  (Erikson, 1959, p. 57).
- Relationship between infant and world is not exclusively biological — also **social**.
- Interaction with the **mother** determines whether trust or mistrust is incorporated into
  personality.

#### It's Up to the Mother
- If mother responds appropriately to physical needs and provides ample affection, love, and
  security → infant develops sense of **trust**.
- Trust leads to expectation of "**consistency, continuity, and sameness**" from people and
  environments (Erikson, 1950, p. 247).
- This expectation provides the beginning of **ego identity**.
- If mother is rejecting, inattentive, or inconsistent → infant develops **mistrust**
  (suspicious, fearful, anxious).
- Erikson argued that a new mother who resumes a job and leaves the infant in day care
  risks promoting mistrust.

#### Trust Can Be Lost Later in Life
- Pattern of trust/mistrust set in infancy, but can be re-activated at later stages.
- An ideal infant–mother relationship → high trust, but this can be destroyed if the mother
  dies or leaves home.
- Childhood mistrust can be altered later in life through a loving and patient teacher or friend.

#### Basic Strength: Hope
- **Hope**: the belief that our desires will be satisfied; a persistent feeling of confidence
  despite temporary setbacks.

---

### Stage 2: Autonomy versus Doubt and Shame (Muscular-anal, 1–3)

- Parallels Freud's **anal stage**.
- Children rapidly develop physical and mental abilities: walk, climb, push, pull, hold on,
  let go, communicate.
- Children take pride in skills and want to do as much as possible for themselves.
- Most important ability: **holding on and letting go** (prototypes for reacting to later
  conflicts in behaviors and attitudes).
  - Holding on: can be loving OR hostile.
  - Letting go: can be destructive rage OR relaxed passivity.

#### Choice
- For the first time, children can exercise some choice and experience the power of their
  **autonomous will**.
- Key question: how much will society (parents) allow children to express themselves?

#### The Toilet Training Crisis
- Major parent–child crisis: **toilet training** — the first instance society attempts to
  regulate an instinctual need.
- Child taught to hold on and let go only at appropriate times and places.
- If parents force toilet training, show impatience and anger → child develops **feelings of
  self-doubt and shame**.
- Conflict is more **psychosocial than biological**, even though the anal region is the focus.

#### Basic Strength: Will
- **Will**: determination to exercise freedom of choice and self-restraint in the face of
  society's demands.

---

### Stage 3: Initiative versus Guilt (Locomotor-genital, 3–5)

- Parallels Freud's **phallic stage**.
- Motor and mental abilities continue to develop; children accomplish more on their own.
- Strong desire to take the initiative in many activities.

#### The Oedipal Relationship
- Initiatives may include fantasies: desire to possess the parent of the opposite sex, rivalry
  with parent of the same sex.
- If parents punish/inhibit these displays of initiative → **persistent guilt feelings** affecting
  self-directed activities throughout life.
- In the Oedipal relationship, the child inevitably fails. If parents guide with **love and
  understanding**, child acquires awareness of permissible behavior.
- Initiative can be channeled toward realistic and socially sanctioned goals → preparation for
  adult responsibility and morality (in Freudian terms: the **superego**).

#### Basic Strength: Purpose
- **Purpose**: the courage to envision and pursue goals.

---

### Stage 4: Industriousness versus Inferiority (Latency, 6–11)

- Corresponds to Freud's **latency period**.
- Child begins school; exposed to new social influences.
- Develops good work and study habits = **industriousness** (primarily to get praise and
  satisfaction from completing tasks).

#### Developing New Skills
- Growing powers of deductive reasoning and ability to play by rules → deliberate refinement
  of skills.
- Erikson (reflecting sex stereotypes of his era): boys build tree houses, model airplanes;
  girls cook and sew.
- Children making serious attempts to complete a task by **concentrated attention, diligence,
  and persistence**.
- "The basic skills of technology are developed as the child becomes ready to handle the
  utensils, the tools, and the weapons used by the big people" (Erikson, 1959, p. 83).
- Parental and teacher attitudes are crucial: scolding/ridiculing → **inferiority and
  inadequacy**; praise/reinforcement → **competence**.

#### Basic Strength: Competence
- **Competence**: the exertion of skill and intelligence in pursuing and completing tasks.

#### The Final Four Developmental Stages (Key Distinction)
- Outcome of crises in the **first four stages** depends mostly on others (what is done TO
  children).
- In the **last four stages**, individuals have increasing control over their environment —
  consciously choosing friends, colleges, careers, spouses, leisure activities.
- But these choices are affected by the personality characteristics formed during birth to
  adolescence.

---

### Stage 5: Identity Cohesion versus Role Confusion (Adolescence, 12–18)

- The **most famous** of Erikson's stages; the crisis of forming the **basic ego identity**.
- Adolescent forms a **self-image**: integration of ideas about themselves and about what
  others think of them.
- If resolved satisfactorily → consistent and congruent picture of self.
- Adolescence = a **psychological moratorium**: a necessary hiatus between childhood and
  adulthood to play different roles and live with different self-images.

#### The Identity Crisis
- **Identity crisis**: failure to achieve ego identity during adolescence.
- Those who fail exhibit **role confusion**: do not know who/what they are, where they
  belong, or where they want to go.
- May withdraw from normal life sequence (education, job, marriage) or seek a
  **negative identity** (crime, drugs).
- A negative identity is preferable to **no identity at all**, though not as satisfactory as a
  positive identity.
- Strong impact of **peer groups** on ego identity development in adolescence.
- Excessive association with **fanatical groups and cults**, or obsessive identification with
  popular culture icons, can restrict the developing ego.

#### Basic Strength: Fidelity
- **Fidelity**: sincerity, genuineness, and a sense of duty in relationships with others.
- Emerges from a cohesive ego identity.

---

### Stage 6: Intimacy versus Isolation (Young adulthood, 18–35)

- Erikson considered this a **longer stage** than previous ones.
- Individual establishes independence from parents and quasi-parental institutions (e.g., college).
- Undertakes productive work; establishes intimate relationships (close friendships and
  sexual unions).

#### Caring and Commitment
- **Intimacy** is not restricted to sexual relationships; also encompasses **caring and
  commitment**.
- Can display these emotions openly without self-protective mechanisms and without fear of
  losing self-identity.
- "We can merge our identity with someone else's without submerging or losing it."
- People unable to establish intimacies → **feelings of isolation**: avoid social contacts,
  reject others, may become aggressive, prefer to be alone (fear intimacy as a threat to ego
  identity).

#### Basic Strength: Love
- **Love**: a mutual devotion in a shared identity, the fusing of oneself with another person.
- Erikson considered love to be the **greatest of all human virtues**.

---

### Stage 7: Generativity versus Stagnation (Adulthood, 35–55)

- Need to be actively involved in **teaching and guiding the next generation**.
- Concern extends beyond the immediate family → future generations and the kind of society
  in which they will live.
- **One need not be a parent** to display generativity; having children does not automatically
  satisfy the urge.
- All institutions (business, government, social service, academic) provide opportunities for
  generativity (being a mentor, teacher, or guide to younger people).
- When middle-aged people cannot find an outlet for generativity → **stagnation, boredom,
  and interpersonal impoverishment** (Erikson, 1968, p. 138).
  - Similar to **Jung's description of the midlife crisis**.
  - These people may regress to **pseudo-intimacy**, indulging themselves in childlike ways.
  - May become physical or psychological invalids due to absorption with their own needs.

#### Basic Strength: Care
- **Care**: a broad concern for others, manifested in the need to teach — not only to help
  others but also to fulfill one's own identity.

---

### Stage 8: Ego Integrity versus Despair (Maturity/Old Age, 55+)

- Major endeavors are at or nearing completion.
- We examine and reflect on life, taking its final measure.
- **Ego integrity**: looking back with a sense of fulfillment and satisfaction, believing we
  have coped with life's victories and failures; accepting one's place and one's past.
- **Despair**: reviewing life with frustration, anger about missed opportunities, regret about
  mistakes that cannot be rectified; becoming disgusted with self, contemptuous of others,
  and bitter.

#### Erikson's Own Final Stage
- At age 84, Erikson reported results of a long-term study of **29 people in their 80s** on
  whom life-history data had been collected since **1928**: *Vital Involvement in Old Age*
  (Erikson, Erikson, & Kivnick, 1986).
- Prescription for ego integrity: older people must **remain active, vital participants in
  life** — grandparenting, returning to school, developing new skills.
- "Much of the despair [of older people] is in fact a continuing sense of stagnation."
- **Generativity** (Stage 7) may be the most important factor contributing to ego integrity
  in Stage 8.

#### Basic Strength: Wisdom
- **Wisdom**: a detached concern with the whole of life, conveyed to succeeding generations
  in an integration of experience — best described by the word **heritage**.

## Basic Weaknesses

- Just as basic strengths arise at each stage, so may **basic weaknesses**.
- Adaptive and maladaptive ways of coping are incorporated into the ego in a creative balance.
- The ego should primarily contain the adaptive attitude but also some of the negative.

### Maladaptive and Malignant Conditions

- In **unbalanced development**, the ego consists solely of one attitude (either adaptive or
  maladaptive): this is called **maldevelopment**.
- When only the **positive/adaptive** tendency is present → condition is **"maladaptive"**
  → can lead to **neuroses**.
- When only the **negative** tendency is present → condition is **"malignant"** → can
  lead to **psychoses**.
- Erikson believed both conditions can be corrected through **psychotherapy**.
- **Maladaptations** (less severe): can also be relieved through re-adaptation, environmental
  changes, supportive social relationships, or successful adaptation at a later stage.

## Questions about Human Nature

- Erikson's delineation of basic human strengths presents an **optimistic view of human
  nature**.
- Not everyone succeeds in attaining hope, purpose, wisdom, etc., but **all have the
  potential** — nothing in our nature prevents it.
- We need not inevitably suffer conflict, anxiety, and neurosis due to instinctual biological forces.
- Every stage offers the possibility of a **positive outcome**.
- Even failure at one stage (maladaptive response or basic weakness) → **hope for change
  at a later stage**.
- We have the potential to **consciously direct and shape our growth** throughout our lives.
- We are **not exclusively products of childhood experiences**.
- Theory is **only partially deterministic**:
  - First four stages: experiences are largely beyond our control (parents, teachers, peers).
  - Last four stages: more opportunity to exercise **free will**.
- Personality is affected more by **learning and experience** than by heredity.
- Psychosocial experiences (not instinctual biological forces) are the **greater determinant**.
- **Ultimate goal**: develop a positive ego identity incorporating all basic strengths.

## Assessment in Erikson's Theory

- Erikson agreed with some of Freud's theoretical formulations but **differed from Freud in
  methods of assessing personality**.

### Rejection of the Psychoanalytic Couch
- Erikson questioned the usefulness and safety of some Freudian techniques, beginning with
  the **psychoanalytic couch**.
- Problems with the couch: could lead to sadistic exploitation, create an illusion of
  objectivity, overemphasize unconscious material, engender impersonality.
- Erikson preferred **therapist and patient seated facing each other in comfortable chairs**
  (to promote equality and personal relationship).
- Used **free association** occasionally but **rarely analyzed dreams** (called this technique
  "wasteful and harmful").
- Assessment techniques should be selected and modified to fit the **unique requirements of
  the individual patient**.
- Developed personality theory primarily from **play therapy, anthropological studies, and
  psychohistorical analysis**.

### Play Therapy
- Primary method for work with emotionally disturbed children and in research on normal
  children and adolescents.
- Provided a variety of toys and **observed how children interacted with them**.
- Form and intensity of play revealed aspects of personality that might not be manifested
  verbally (due to children's limited verbal expression).

### Psychohistorical Analysis
- Erikson's most **unusual assessment technique**.
- Essentially **biographical studies** using his life-span theory framework.
- Studied significant political, religious, and literary figures: **Gandhi, Martin Luther, George
  Bernard Shaw**.
- Typically focuses on a **significant crisis** — an episode representing a major life theme
  uniting past, present, and future.
- Used **"disciplined subjectivity"**: Erikson adopted the subject's viewpoint as his own to
  assess life events through that person's eyes.

### Psychological Tests (Developed by Others Based on Erikson's Theory)

| Test | Authors | Purpose | Items/Details |
|---|---|---|---|

## Research on Erikson's Theory

- Erikson's primary research method: **case study**.
  - Weakness: difficulty duplicating and verifying case material.
  - Benefit: yields insights into personality development and helps resolve patients' problems.

### Play Constructions

> **KEY STUDY: Erikson's Play Constructions (1968)**
> - **Sample**: Boys and girls, ages **10 to 12**.
> - **Method**: Asked to construct a scene from an imaginary movie using dolls, toy animals,
>   toy cars, and wooden blocks.
> - **Findings**:
>   - **Girls**: built static, peaceful scenes with low, enclosed structures; intruders (animal or
>     male figures, never female) tried to force their way into interiors.
>   - **Boys**: focused on exteriors, action, and height; tall towers, cars and people in motion.
> - **Erikson's interpretation** (orthodox Freudian): "Sexual differences in the organization of
>   a play space seem to parallel the morphology of genital differentiation itself: in the male,
>   an external organ, erectable and intrusive … in the female, internal organs, with vestibular
>   access, leading to a statically expectant ova" (Erikson, 1968, p. 271).
> - **Criticism**: Suggests women are victims of their anatomy; personality determined by
>   absence of a penis.
> - **Erikson's concession**: Differences could also result from **societal sex-role training**.

#### Gender Preferences for Toys (Later Research)
- Wong & Hines (2014): Boys aged 2–4 played more with toy trains; girls chose dolls.
- Dinella, Devita, & Weisgram (2013): College men attracted to a "boy's toy" (model fighter
  jet) only when painted blue; rejected when painted pink.
- Joiner (1998): Boys (10–11) chose software labeled "Pirates"; girls chose "Princesses."
- Fathers reinforce gender-based play more than mothers; teach/reinforce gender-typed behaviors.
- Fathers encourage **passive, compliant behaviors** in girls and **assertive, aggressive
  behaviors** in boys (Quiery, 1998).

### Trust and Security

> **KEY STUDIES: Trust and Later Social Functioning**
> - Jacobson & Wille (1986); Londersville & Main (1981); Sroufe, Fox, & Pancake (1983).
> - Infants aged **12–18 months** with strong emotional bond to mother (presumed high trust):
>   when observed **3 years later**, showed higher social and emotional functioning.
> - High-trust children: more curious, sociable, popular, more likely to be leaders, greater
>   sensitivity to needs of others.
> - Low-trust children: more socially and emotionally withdrawn, reluctant to play, less
>   curious, less forceful in pursuing goals.

> **KEY STUDY: Holocaust Survivors**
> - Suedfeld et al. (2005): Survivors interviewed **30–40 years after WWII** (i.e., 1975–1985).
> - Had dealt successfully with all of Erikson's psychosocial stages **except the first
>   (Trust vs. Mistrust)** — their worldview included significantly more mistrust than trust.
> - BUT: their ability to cope with later developmental crises confirms Erikson's notion that
>   **positive events at later stages can counteract negative early experiences**.

### The Psychosocial Stages

> **KEY STUDY: Children's Stories and Stages (Ciaccio, 1971)**
> - Children aged 4, 8, and 11 were asked to make up stories based on test pictures.
> - Stories analyzed for which psychosocial stage they reflected.
> - Results supported Erikson's theory: 4-year-olds' stories concerned autonomy (just completed
>   stage); older children's stories reflected their respective developmental stages.

> **KEY STUDY: Vera Brittain Psychohistorical Analysis (Peterson & Stewart, 1990)**
> - Analyzed diaries, letters, and novels of **Vera Brittain (1893–1970)**, British feminist
>   and writer, from age 21 into middle age.
> - Initial concern: ego identity → changed over time to intimacy → then generativity.
> - Exactly as Erikson's theory predicted.

> **KEY STUDY: Adaptive vs. Maladaptive Development (Constantinople, 1969)**
> - Used the **Inventory of Psychosocial Development** to assess adaptive/maladaptive
>   development in Erikson's first six stages.
> - Found a **significant relationship between happiness and adaptive development** at each
>   stage.

> **KEY STUDY: Maladaptive Development and Alienation (Reimanis, 1974)**
> - Found a **high correlation between maladaptive development** in the first six stages
>   and a **sense of alienation and uprootedness**.

> **KEY STUDY: Identity Crisis and Prior Stages (Waterman, Buebel, & Waterman, 1970)**
> - Adolescents who developed trust, autonomy, initiative, and industriousness (adaptive coping)
>   in stages 1–4 displayed **high identity cohesion** (not role confusion).
> - Adolescents with role confusion had **not resolved** their earlier stage crises adaptively.

> **KEY STUDY: Canadian Men Across Ages (Beaumont & Zukanovic, 2005)**
> - Three groups of men in Canada: ages 19–25, 35–55, and 65–87.
> - Self-report measures of identity, self-worth, and psychological distress.
> - Younger men had highest levels of distress; older men had lowest.
> - Confirmed Erikson's view: "the older the individual, the better one is able to cope with
>   life's challenges" due to resolution of earlier psychological dilemmas.

> **KEY STUDY: Memory Recall and Developmental Stages (Conway & Holmes, 2004)**
> - British adults aged 62–89 asked to recall memories from earlier times.
> - Memories of first decade: trust, autonomy, initiative, industry.
> - Second decade (11–20): identity issues.
> - Young adulthood: intimacy.
> - Confirmed that recollections of each period centered on situations Erikson identified as
>   crucial to that stage.

### Adolescent Development: Marcia's Identity Statuses

Erikson's work on adolescent identity led to extensive research identifying **five psychosocial
types/statuses** for adolescence (Marcia, 1966, 1980).

#### 1. Identity Achievement
- **Definition**: Adolescents **committed to occupational and ideological choices** they have
  deliberated and decided.
- Characteristics:
  - Stable, concerned with realistic goals, able to cope with changing environmental demands.
  - Perform better on difficult tasks than those experiencing role confusion.
  - Major in more difficult subjects (engineering, physical sciences) in college
    (Marcia & Friedman, 1970).
  - Male and female teens achieving identity earlier were more likely to have stable intimate
    romantic relationships in their twenties (Beyers & Seiffge-Krenke, 2010).
  - Less likely to engage in binge drinking, illegal drug use, and high-risk sexual behaviors
    (Schwartz et al., 2010).
  - Correlates highly with self-esteem, positive coping, psychological well-being, stronger
    sense of self across American, Chinese, and Turkish students (multiple studies, 2007–2014).
  - Large-scale meta-analysis (>120 studies): identity achievement rose over late adolescence
    and young adulthood (Kroger, Martinussen, & Marcia, 2010).
  - Parents who provided direction and control in a **loving and caring way** (neither too
    permissive nor too authoritarian) → adolescents more likely to achieve identity
    (Berzonsky, 2004; Mohammadi, 2013 [Iranian college students]).
- Most psychologically and socially **mature** identity status.

#### 2. Moratorium
- **Definition**: People still **undergoing their identity crisis**; occupational and ideological
  commitments are vague.
- Characteristics:
  - Ambivalent toward authority figures (alternately rebelling and needing guidance).
  - Behavior ranges from indecisive to active and creative.
  - Score **high in anxiety** (Blustein et al., 1989; Lillevoll et al., 2013; Podd et al., 1968).
  - Tend to daydream, believe in supernatural phenomena, and enjoy behaving childishly
    (Bilsker & Marcia, 1991).

#### 3. Foreclosure
- **Definition**: Adolescents who have **not yet experienced an identity crisis** but express
  commitment to an occupation and ideology — commitments **determined by parents**, not
  by their own deliberate choice.
- Characteristics:
  - Tend to be **rigid and authoritarian**, have difficulty coping with change (Marcia, 1967).
  - Achievement-oriented, but focus energy toward **external rather than internal goals**
    (Stephen, Fraser, & Marcia, 1992).

#### 4. Identity Diffusion
- **Definition**: No occupational or ideological commitments; **may not have experienced an
  identity crisis**.
- Characteristics:
  - Chosen lifestyle may actively reject any kind of commitments.
  - Extreme form: **aimless drifting and wandering** (as Erikson did in his late teens).
  - Distant relationships with parents (seen as indifferent and rejecting) (Waterman, 1982).
  - Lower psychological adjustment and subjective well-being; higher unstable self-image.
  - More likely to engage in impulsive and self-destructive behavior, show excessive need for
    attention, have grandiose fantasies (Crawford et al., 2004; Luyckx et al., 2005;
    Vleioras & Bosma, 2005) — in Greek, Belgian, and U.S. adolescents.

#### 5. Alienated Achievement
- **Definition**: Adolescents who have **experienced an identity crisis**, have no occupational
  goal, and cling to beliefs **critical of the social and economic system**.
- Strong commitment to anti-system rationale precludes any career that would entangle them
  in the system they oppose.
- As students: tend to be **cerebral, philosophical, and cynical** (Marcia & Friedman, 1970;
  Orlofsky, Marcia, & Lesser, 1973).

#### Order of Statuses from Least to Most Resolved
Identity diffusion → Foreclosure → Moratorium → Identity Achievement
(Four of the five statuses, in this order, represent increasingly successful resolutions.)

> **KEY STUDY: Ego Strength and Identity Status (Bourne, 1978a, 1978b)**
> - Erikson predicted that people with integrated ego identity would have greater ego strength
>   than those farther from resolving identity.
> - Supported by research on college men.

> **KEY STUDY: Extracurricular Activities and Fidelity (Markstrom et al., 2005)**
> - High school students more involved in extracurricular and volunteer activities were higher
>   in the ego strength of **fidelity** than those less involved.

> **KEY STUDY: Physical Activity and Ego Identity (Fukama & Mizuochi, 2011; Jones et al., 2014)**
> - More time devoted to physical activity and sports → stronger sense of ego identity in both
>   Japanese and American students.

### Sex Differences in Ego Identity
- Men: tendency toward **separation and detachment** from others (Mellor, 1989).
- Women: tendency toward **connection and attachment** to others.
- Male identity focuses on **individual competence and knowledge**.
- Female identity more centered on **relating to others**; women depend heavily on social
  relationships when establishing identity (Curry, 1998).
- Dutch data: **female adolescents form an identity at an earlier age** than male adolescents;
  most males achieve some identity level by end of teen years (Klimstra et al., 2010).

### Three Key Elements of Adolescent Stress
1. **Conflict with parents**: forceful resistance to adult authority.
2. **Mood disruption**: volatile emotional life, mood swings, episodes of depression.
3. **Risky behaviors**: reckless, rule-breaking, antisocial behavior.

> **KEY STUDY: Adolescent Diary Study (Jensen-Campbell & Graziano, 2000)**
> - 155 adolescents kept diaries of daily interactions over a 2-week period.
> - **31% of interactions** involved conflicts with other people.
> - Conflicts with **parents** were more important and emotionally intense than conflicts with
>   peers.

> **KEY STUDY: Childhood Distress and Adolescent Problems (Steinberg & Morris, 2001)**
> - Tracking individuals from childhood to adolescence found that many who experienced
>   depression/emotional problems during teen years had also suffered psychological distress
>   as children.
> - Difficulties in adolescence do **not necessarily arise because of adolescence** itself.

### Virtual Identity
- Computer games and social media offer adolescents a high-tech opportunity to try different
  roles (exactly what Erikson said was necessary at this stage).
- Examples: **Second Life (SL)**, Dungeons and Dragons — role-playing games allowing
  young people to take on fictional personas.
- **Multi-user dungeons (MUDs)**: virtual places shared by multiple computer users
  simultaneously; allow interaction and building personal virtual worlds.

### Gender and Ego Identity
- Traditional gender stereotyping in toys and play persists more than 50 years after
  Erikson's play construction research.
- Most children still prefer gender-based toys: boys — trucks, soldiers, guns; girls — dolls,
  jewelry, toy kitchen implements.

### Generativity (Research)
- Loyola Generativity Scale (McAdams & de St. Aubin, 1992): 20-item self-report measuring
  level of generativity or stagnation in adulthood.
- Sample items measure passing knowledge to others, believing one has made a difference,
  keeping commitments to people and activities.

### Maturity
- Erikson's *Vital Involvement in Old Age* (1986): prescription for ego integrity requires
  remaining active and vital — not just reflecting on the past.

## Reflections on Erikson's Theory

### Contributions and Criticisms

**Contributions:**
- Extended Freud's theory to the entire life span.
- Elevated the ego to independence.
- Introduced the concept of the **identity crisis** — widely influential in developmental
  psychology and general culture.
- Recognized cultural and historical forces in personality development.
- Provided the framework for Marcia's identity statuses research.
- Works were bestsellers; unusual prominence for a personality theorist.

**Criticisms:**
- Play constructions study criticized for sexist interpretation (women as victims of anatomy).
- Primary research method (case study) lacks rigor — difficult to replicate and verify.
- Never earned a university degree; some question the academic foundation.
- Did not adequately address **gender differences** in ego identity formation.
- Stages may be **culturally biased** (formulated primarily on Western, middle-class experience).
- Critics noted Erikson "publicly defined himself as a loyal Freudian, even as he departed
  substantially from orthodox psychoanalytic theory" (Anderson & Friedman, 1997).

### Recognition and Influence
- Books were bestsellers.
- Picture appeared on covers of **Newsweek** and the **New York Times Magazine**.
- Achieved prominence without ever earning a university degree.
- Work had a profound influence on both psychoanalysis and general culture.
- Coined concepts now part of everyday language: "identity crisis," "life cycle."
- Quote on Erikson's role: "Psychoanalysis is always the starting point" (quoted in
  Keniston, 1983, p. 29).

## Chapter Summary

- Erikson extended Freud's theory across the entire life span with **eight psychosocial stages**.
- Each stage involves a **crisis** that must be resolved adaptively or maladaptively.
- The **epigenetic principle** states that development is governed by inherited forces shaped
  by social/environmental experience.
- Satisfactory resolution of each crisis generates a **basic strength (virtue)**.
- Unsatisfactory resolution leads to **basic weaknesses**.
- **Maldevelopment** (only adaptive or only maladaptive coping) can lead to neuroses or
  psychoses.
- Assessment methods: play therapy, psychohistorical analysis, psychological tests developed
  by others.
- Erikson's view of human nature is **optimistic and partially deterministic**.
- The **identity crisis** in adolescence is the central concept, elaborated by Marcia's
  five identity statuses.
- Research broadly supports Erikson's proposed sequence of psychosocial development.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Psychosocial stages of development** | Erikson's eight successive stages encompassing the life span; at each stage we must cope with a crisis in either an adaptive or a maladaptive way |
| **Epigenetic principle of maturation** | The idea that human development is governed by a sequence of stages that depend on genetic/hereditary factors, shaped by environmental forces |
| **Crisis** | The turning point faced at each developmental stage; requires a shift in perspective and refocusing of energy |
| **Basic strengths (virtues)** | Motivating characteristics and beliefs that derive from satisfactory resolution of the crisis at each developmental stage |
| **Basic weaknesses** | Motivating characteristics that derive from unsatisfactory resolution of developmental crises |
| **Maldevelopment** | A condition that occurs when the ego consists solely of one way of coping (either only adaptive or only maladaptive) |
| **Ego identity** | The self-image formed during adolescence that integrates ideas of what we are and what we want to be |
| **Identity crisis** | The failure to achieve ego identity during adolescence; results in role confusion |
| **Psychological moratorium** | Erikson's term for adolescence as a hiatus between childhood and adulthood — time to try different roles |
| **Play therapy** | Assessment/research technique in which children's interactions with toys are observed to reveal personality aspects |
| **Play constructions** | Personality assessment technique for children in which structures assembled from dolls, blocks, and other toys are analyzed |
| **Psychohistorical analysis** | Application of Erikson's life-span theory and psychoanalytic principles to study historical figures using disciplined subjectivity |
| **Disciplined subjectivity** | Erikson's psychohistorical method of adopting the subject's viewpoint to assess life events through that person's eyes |
| **Hope** | Basic strength of Stage 1; belief that desires will be satisfied despite temporary setbacks |
| **Will** | Basic strength of Stage 2; determination to exercise freedom of choice and self-restraint |
| **Purpose** | Basic strength of Stage 3; courage to envision and pursue goals |
| **Competence** | Basic strength of Stage 4; exertion of skill and intelligence in pursuing and completing tasks |
| **Fidelity** | Basic strength of Stage 5; sincerity, genuineness, and sense of duty in relationships |
| **Love** | Basic strength of Stage 6; mutual devotion in a shared identity; greatest of all human virtues (Erikson) |
| **Care** | Basic strength of Stage 7; broad concern for others, manifested in the need to teach |
| **Wisdom** | Basic strength of Stage 8; detached concern with the whole of life, expressed as heritage to succeeding generations |
| **Ego integrity** | Acceptance of one's place and one's past; looking back with fulfillment and satisfaction |
| **Despair** | Looking back with frustration, regret, bitterness about what might have been |
| **Generativity** | Concern with teaching and guiding the next generation; extends beyond one's own family to society at large |
| **Stagnation** | Failure to find an outlet for generativity; leads to boredom, interpersonal impoverishment, possible pseudo-intimacy |
| **Pseudo-intimacy** | Regressive, childlike self-indulgence seen when middle-aged people fail to achieve generativity |
| **Identity achievement** | Marcia's status: adolescent committed to occupational/ideological choices after deliberation |
| **Moratorium** | Marcia's status: still in identity crisis; vague commitments, high anxiety |
| **Foreclosure** | Marcia's status: committed without having experienced a crisis; commitments typically determined by parents |
| **Identity diffusion** | Marcia's status: no occupational/ideological commitments; may not have experienced a crisis |
| **Alienated achievement** | Marcia's status: experienced crisis, no occupational goal, critical of social/economic system |
| **Loyola Generativity Scale** | 20-item self-report scale by McAdams & de St. Aubin (1992) to measure generativity vs. stagnation |
| **Ego Identity Process Questionnaire** | 32-item measure of exploration and commitment in adolescents (Balistreri et al., 1995) |
| **Ego-Identity Scale** | Dignan (1965) instrument to measure ego identity development in adolescence |
| **Multi-user dungeons (MUDs)** | Virtual spaces shared by multiple computer users simultaneously; enable identity role-playing |
| **Identity confusion** | Erikson's initial term for lack of a clear self-image or self-identity; seen in American Indians and WWII veterans |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Erikson's three extensions of Freud**: (a) life-span development, (b) ego as independent of id, (c) cultural/historical forces — all three are exam-ready enumeration targets.

2. **Epigenetic principle of maturation**: development governed by genetic forces; the prefix *epi* means "upon"; social/environmental forces shape how stages are realized.

3. **Eight stages with exact ages, conflict pairs, and basic strengths** (memorize Table 6.1): this is the single highest-yield structure in the chapter for MCQs.

4. **Maldevelopment distinction**: "maladaptive" (only positive coping) → neurosis; "malignant" (only negative coping) → psychosis — an often-tested conceptual pair.

5. **Identity crisis vs. psychological moratorium**: identity crisis = failure to achieve ego identity = role confusion; moratorium = normal adolescent hiatus to try different roles.

6. **Marcia's five identity statuses** (1966, 1980): identity achievement, moratorium, foreclosure, identity diffusion, alienated achievement — definitions and characteristics of each.

7. **Order of Marcia's statuses from least to most resolved**: identity diffusion → foreclosure → moratorium → identity achievement (alienated achievement is separate).

8. **Basic strengths in order**: Hope, Will, Purpose, Competence, Fidelity, Love, Care, Wisdom — associate each with its stage and conflict.

9. **Psychohistorical analysis / disciplined subjectivity**: Erikson's unique method; applied to Gandhi, Martin Luther, George Bernard Shaw.

10. **Loyola Generativity Scale** (McAdams & de St. Aubin, 1992): **20 items**, self-report, measures generativity vs. stagnation in adulthood.

11. **Ego Identity Process Questionnaire** (Balistreri et al., 1995): **32 items**, measures exploration and commitment in adolescents.

12. **Play constructions study**: boys (10–12) built tall, action-oriented, exterior scenes; girls built low, enclosed, interior scenes — Erikson interpreted this in Freudian anatomical terms; criticized as sexist.

13. **Generativity and ego integrity link**: from *Vital Involvement in Old Age* (1986, study of 29 people in their 80s): generativity in Stage 7 is the most important factor contributing to ego integrity in Stage 8; "much of the despair [of older people] is in fact a continuing sense of stagnation."

14. **Foreclosure vs. identity diffusion**: Foreclosure = commitment present but not self-chosen (parental imposition), no prior identity crisis; Diffusion = no commitment AND possibly no crisis — a common MCQ trap.

15. **Erikson's biography as theory**: trained by Anna Freud (not Sigmund); never earned a university degree; changed name to Erik Homburger Erikson at age 37; identity confusion initially named condition seen in American Indians and WWII veterans.
