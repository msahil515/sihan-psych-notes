# Ch 6: Erik Erikson: Identity Theory

Erikson extended Freud across the entire life span with eight psychosocial stages, each centered on a crisis requiring adaptive or maladaptive resolution. He elevated the ego to an independent structure, recognized cultural and historical forces, and introduced the **identity crisis** as a universal developmental turning point. His theory is optimistic, developmental, and partially deterministic.

---

## The Life of Erikson (1902–1994)

### Personal Identity Crises
- Born 1902 in **Frankfurt, Germany**; mother Danish, from a wealthy Jewish family.
- Conceived by another man; mother sent to Germany to give birth out of wedlock. Stepfather: **Dr. Theodore Homburger**, the infant's own pediatrician.
- Didn't know Homburger wasn't his biological father for years; grew up unsure of his identity. Kept the surname Homburger until **age 37** (U.S. citizenship), then became **Erik Homburger Erikson**.
- School identity crises: rejected by German classmates (Jewish family) and Jewish classmates (tall, blond, Aryan features). Mediocre grades; talent for art.
- His daughter: he "suffered terribly from the sense that his real father had abandoned him" and struggled with depression (Bloland, 2005).

### Dropping Out of Society
- After high school he wandered Europe, reading and observing; described himself as "morbidly sensitive and neurotic." His own identity search "became the 'research'" behind his ideas (Friedman, 1999).

### Becoming a Freudian
- At **25** offered a teaching post in Vienna at a school for children of Freud's patients (partly drawn by a search for a father figure).
- Trained in psychoanalysis; **analyzed by Anna Freud** (not Sigmund) — almost daily for **3 years** at **$7/month**. This heightened his awareness of social influences. Became a member of the **Vienna Psychoanalytic Institute**.

### Three Marriages and a Stable Foundation
- Met **Joan Serson** (artist/dancer) at a Vienna masquerade ball in **1929**; initially refused marriage when she became pregnant, then married her in **three ceremonies** (Jewish, Protestant, civil). Joan became his lifelong intellectual partner/editor ("he would have been nothing without Joan").

### Coming to America
- In **1933** the Eriksons emigrated (Nazi threat), settling in **Boston**; he ran a child psychoanalytic practice and worked at **Massachusetts General Hospital**.
- Began a **Harvard** Ph.D., **failed his first course and dropped out**. In **1936** (no degree) invited to **Yale's Institute of Human Relations**.
- Studied **Sioux Indian (South Dakota)** child-rearing, reinforcing his belief in cultural influences; later at **UC Berkeley's Institute of Human Development**.

### Honors but No Identity
- Observed **identity confusion** (alienation from cultural traditions) in American Indians and in **WWII veterans** (confusion of identity, not repressed conflict).
- **1950**: joined the **Austen Riggs Center** (disturbed adolescents). **1960**: returned to Harvard; retired **1970**. Published *Vital Involvement in Old Age* at **84**.
- Despite fame, "it was still a source of shame...that he had been an illegitimate child" (Bloland, 2005). Died **1994**. Appeared on covers of **Newsweek** and the **New York Times Magazine** — never earned a university degree.

## Psychosocial Stages of Personality Development

### Overview and Freud Comparison
- **Eight psychosocial stages** across the life span. The **first four** parallel Freud's oral, anal, phallic, latency stages. Key difference: Erikson emphasized **psychosocial correlates**; Freud, biological factors.

### Three Ways Erikson Extended Freud
1. Personality **develops over the entire life span**.
2. **Greater emphasis on the ego** as independent of the id.
3. Recognized **cultural and historical forces**.

### Genetics and Environment
- **Epigenetic principle of maturation**: development is governed by inherited (genetic) forces (*epi* = "upon"), with social/environmental forces controlling how predetermined stages are realized. Both biological and social factors matter.

### Conflicts and Crises
- Development involves a series of **conflicts**, present at birth as innate predispositions, each prominent at a different stage.
- Each confrontation is a **crisis** (turning point) resolved **maladaptively (negative)** or **adaptively (positive)**. Resolution lets development continue and builds strength for the next stage; unresolved conflict makes later adaptation harder but not impossible.

### Adaptive Coping
- The ego incorporates **both adaptive and maladaptive** coping. Example: infancy needs trust AND some mistrust (total trust = vulnerability). Ideal = **primarily positive, balanced by some negative**.

## Basic Strengths

- Each stage offers a **basic strength (virtue)** once the crisis is resolved. **Interdependent**: each requires the prior stage's strength.

| Stage | Ages | Adaptive vs. Maladaptive | Basic Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oral-sensory | Birth–1 | Trust vs. Mistrust | Hope |
| Muscular-anal | 1–3 | Autonomy vs. Doubt, Shame | Will |
| Locomotor-genital | 3–5 | Initiative vs. Guilt | Purpose |
| Latency | 6–11 | Industriousness vs. Inferiority | Competence |
| Adolescence | 12–18 | Identity cohesion vs. Role confusion | Fidelity |
| Young adulthood | 18–35 | Intimacy vs. Isolation | Love |
| Adulthood | 35–55 | Generativity vs. Stagnation | Care |
| Maturity/old age | 55+ | Ego integrity vs. Despair | Wisdom |

### Stage 1: Trust vs. Mistrust (Birth–1)
- Parallels the **oral stage**; infant totally dependent on the mother; relationship is biological AND **social**.
- Appropriate care + affection → **trust** → expectation of "consistency, continuity, sameness" → beginning of **ego identity**. Rejecting/inconsistent care → **mistrust** (suspicious, anxious).
- Trust/mistrust set in infancy but **can change later** (loss of a parent destroys trust; a loving teacher can restore it).
- **Basic strength: Hope** — belief that desires will be satisfied despite setbacks.

### Stage 2: Autonomy vs. Doubt and Shame (1–3)
- Parallels the **anal stage**; children develop abilities and want to do things themselves. Key ability: **holding on and letting go** (prototypes for later reactions; each can be loving or hostile).
- First exercise of the **autonomous will**. Crisis: **toilet training** (first social regulation of an instinct). Forced training/impatience → **self-doubt and shame**. More **psychosocial than biological**.
- **Basic strength: Will** — determination to exercise free choice and self-restraint.

### Stage 3: Initiative vs. Guilt (3–5)
- Parallels the **phallic stage**; strong desire to take **initiative**.
- Includes Oedipal fantasies (desire for opposite-sex parent, rivalry with same-sex). Punishing initiative → **persistent guilt**. With loving guidance, initiative channels into socially sanctioned goals → preparation for adult morality (the **superego**).
- **Basic strength: Purpose** — courage to envision and pursue goals.

### Stage 4: Industriousness vs. Inferiority (6–11)
- Corresponds to **latency**; child starts school and develops work/study habits (**industriousness**) for praise and task satisfaction.
- Develops deductive reasoning and skill refinement. Parent/teacher attitudes are crucial: ridicule → **inferiority/inadequacy**; praise → **competence**.
- **Basic strength: Competence** — exerting skill and intelligence to complete tasks.
- **Key distinction**: outcomes in the **first four stages** depend mostly on others (done TO children); in the **last four** individuals increasingly **choose** (friends, careers, spouses), though shaped by earlier personality.

### Stage 5: Identity Cohesion vs. Role Confusion (12–18)
- The **most famous** stage; crisis of forming **basic ego identity** (self-image integrating one's own and others' views).
- Adolescence = a **psychological moratorium**: a hiatus to try different roles.
- **Identity crisis**: failure to achieve ego identity → **role confusion** (don't know who they are); may withdraw or seek a **negative identity** (crime, drugs) — preferable to **no identity**. **Peer groups** strongly influence identity; fanatical groups/cults can restrict the ego.
- **Basic strength: Fidelity** — sincerity, genuineness, sense of duty.

### Stage 6: Intimacy vs. Isolation (18–35)
- A **longer stage**; establish independence, productive work, and intimate relationships.
- **Intimacy** = caring and commitment (not just sexual): "We can merge our identity with someone else's without...losing it." Failure → **isolation** (avoids contact, may become aggressive).
- **Basic strength: Love** — mutual devotion in a shared identity; the **greatest of all human virtues**.

### Stage 7: Generativity vs. Stagnation (35–55)
- Need to **teach and guide the next generation** and care about future society. **Need not be a parent**; all institutions provide outlets (mentor, teacher).
- No outlet → **stagnation, boredom, interpersonal impoverishment** (similar to **Jung's midlife crisis**); may regress to **pseudo-intimacy**.
- **Basic strength: Care** — broad concern for others, manifested in the need to teach.

### Stage 8: Ego Integrity vs. Despair (55+)
- Reflect on life and take its measure. **Ego integrity** = fulfillment, acceptance of one's place and past. **Despair** = frustration, regret, bitterness.
- At 84 Erikson reported a study of **29 people in their 80s** (data since 1928): *Vital Involvement in Old Age* (Erikson, Erikson, & Kivnick, 1986). Prescription: remain **active, vital participants**; "much of the despair...is in fact a continuing sense of stagnation." **Generativity (Stage 7)** may be the most important factor for ego integrity.
- **Basic strength: Wisdom** — detached concern with the whole of life, conveyed as **heritage**.

## Basic Weaknesses

- Each stage may produce **basic weaknesses**. The ego balances adaptive and maladaptive coping (primarily adaptive, with some negative).

### Maladaptive and Malignant Conditions
- **Maldevelopment**: the ego consists solely of one attitude.
  - Only the **positive/adaptive** tendency → **"maladaptive"** → can lead to **neuroses**.
  - Only the **negative** tendency → **"malignant"** → can lead to **psychoses**.
- Both correctable via **psychotherapy**; milder maladaptations also via re-adaptation, environmental change, or later successful adaptation.

| Stage | Way of Coping | Maladaptive | Malignant |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oral-sensory | Trust / Mistrust | Sensory maladjustment | Withdrawal |
| Muscular-anal | Autonomy / Doubt, shame | Shameless willfulness | Compulsion |
| Locomotor-genital | Initiative / Guilt | Ruthlessness | Inhibition |
| Latency | Industriousness / Inferiority | Narrow virtuosity | Inertia |
| Adolescence | Identity cohesion / Role confusion | Fanaticism | Repudiation |
| Young adulthood | Intimacy / Isolation | Promiscuity | Exclusivity |
| Adulthood | Generativity / Stagnation | Overextension | Rejectivity |
| Maturity/old age | Ego integrity / Despair | Presumption | Disdain |

*Source: Vital Involvement in Old Age (Erikson, Erikson, & Kivnick, 1986).*

## Questions about Human Nature

- **Optimistic**: all have the potential for hope, purpose, wisdom; no inevitable conflict or neurosis. Every stage offers a positive outcome; failure at one stage leaves **hope for change later**.
- We can **consciously shape our growth**; we are **not exclusively products of childhood**.
- **Partially deterministic**: first four stages largely beyond our control; last four allow **free will**. Personality is affected more by **learning/experience** than heredity; psychosocial experiences are the greater determinant. **Ultimate goal**: a positive ego identity incorporating all basic strengths.

## Assessment in Erikson's Theory

### Rejection of the Couch
- Questioned the **psychoanalytic couch** (sadistic exploitation, false objectivity, impersonality). Preferred **patient and therapist facing each other in chairs**. Used **free association** occasionally but **rarely analyzed dreams** ("wasteful and harmful"). Tailored techniques to the individual.
- Built his theory mainly from **play therapy, anthropological studies, and psychohistorical analysis**.

### Play Therapy
- For disturbed and normal children: observe how children interact with toys; play reveals personality not expressed verbally.

### Psychohistorical Analysis
- His most **unusual technique**: biographical studies via his life-span framework, of figures like **Gandhi, Martin Luther, George Bernard Shaw**, focused on a **significant crisis**. Used **"disciplined subjectivity"** (adopting the subject's viewpoint).

### Tests (Developed by Others)

| Test | Authors | Purpose | Details |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ego-Identity Scale | Dignan (1965) | Ego identity in adolescence | — |
| Ego Identity Process Questionnaire | Balistreri et al. (1995) | Exploration & commitment | 32 items |
| Loyola Generativity Scale | McAdams & de St. Aubin (1992) | Generativity vs. stagnation | 20-item self-report |

## Research on Erikson's Theory

- Primary method: **case study** (hard to verify, but yields insight).

### Play Constructions
> **KEY STUDY: Erikson (1968)** — boys/girls **ages 10–12** built scenes from toys.
> - **Girls**: static, peaceful, low enclosed structures; intruders forced into interiors.
> - **Boys**: exteriors, action, height; tall towers, motion.
> - **Erikson** (Freudian): play space parallels genital morphology (intrusive male / internal female).
> - **Criticism**: women as victims of anatomy. **Concession**: differences could reflect **societal sex-role training**.

**Later toy research**: boys 2–4 prefer trains, girls dolls (Wong & Hines, 2014); a fighter jet appealed to men only painted blue, not pink (Dinella et al., 2013); boys chose "Pirates," girls "Princesses" software (Joiner, 1998). Fathers reinforce gender-typed play (passivity in girls, assertiveness in boys; Quiery, 1998).

### Trust and Security
> **KEY STUDIES** (Jacobson & Wille, 1986; Londersville & Main, 1981; Sroufe et al., 1983): infants **12–18 months** with strong maternal bonds (high trust), observed **3 years later**, were more curious, sociable, popular, leader-like. Low-trust children were withdrawn, less curious.

> **KEY STUDY: Holocaust Survivors (Suedfeld et al., 2005)** — interviewed 30–40 years post-WWII; coped with all stages **except Stage 1 (Trust)** (more mistrust than trust), yet coped with later crises — confirming **positive later events can counteract negative early experiences**.

### The Psychosocial Stages
> **Ciaccio (1971)**: children's stories (ages 4, 8, 11) reflected their respective stage themes.
> **Peterson & Stewart (1990)**: psychohistorical analysis of **Vera Brittain** showed identity → intimacy → generativity, as predicted.
> **Constantinople (1969)**: the Inventory of Psychosocial Development showed a **significant happiness–adaptive development** relationship at each stage.
> **Reimanis (1974)**: maladaptive development in stages 1–6 correlated with **alienation/uprootedness**.
> **Waterman et al. (1970)**: adaptive coping in stages 1–4 → **high identity cohesion**; role confusion = unresolved earlier crises.
> **Beaumont & Zukanovic (2005)**: Canadian men (19–25, 35–55, 65–87) — younger had most distress, older least; "the older the individual, the better one is able to cope."
> **Conway & Holmes (2004)**: British adults 62–89 recalled memories matching each period's stage themes.

### Marcia's Identity Statuses (1966, 1980)

**1. Identity Achievement** — **committed** to occupational/ideological choices after deliberation. Most mature status: stable, realistic goals, better on hard tasks; major in harder subjects; more stable romantic relationships (Beyers & Seiffge-Krenke, 2010); less binge drinking/drug use (Schwartz et al., 2010); correlates with self-esteem and well-being across cultures; rises over late adolescence (meta-analysis >120 studies, Kroger et al., 2010); fostered by loving, balanced parenting (Berzonsky, 2004).

**2. Moratorium** — **still in crisis**; vague commitments; ambivalent toward authority; **high anxiety** (Blustein et al., 1989); daydreaming, childish behavior (Bilsker & Marcia, 1991).

**3. Foreclosure** — **no crisis yet** but committed to occupation/ideology **determined by parents**. Rigid, authoritarian; achievement toward **external goals** (Stephen et al., 1992).

**4. Identity Diffusion** — **no commitments**, may not have had a crisis. Extreme = **aimless drifting** (as Erikson did); distant parents; lower adjustment; impulsive/self-destructive behavior (Crawford et al., 2004; Luyckx et al., 2005).

**5. Alienated Achievement** — **experienced a crisis**, no occupational goal, beliefs **critical of the social/economic system**; cerebral, philosophical, cynical.

**Order from least to most resolved**: Identity diffusion → Foreclosure → Moratorium → Identity Achievement.

> **Bourne (1978)**: integrated ego identity → greater ego strength. **Markstrom et al. (2005)**: more extracurricular involvement → higher **fidelity**. **Fukama & Mizuochi (2011); Jones et al. (2014)**: more physical activity → stronger ego identity (Japanese and American students).

### Sex Differences in Ego Identity
- Men: **separation/detachment**, identity via **individual competence**. Women: **connection/attachment**, identity via **relating to others** (Mellor, 1989; Curry, 1998). Dutch data: **females form identity earlier** than males (Klimstra et al., 2010).

### Adolescent Stress — Three Elements
1. **Conflict with parents**; 2. **Mood disruption**; 3. **Risky behaviors**.

> **Jensen-Campbell & Graziano (2000)**: 155 adolescents' diaries — **31% of interactions** involved conflict; **parent** conflicts more intense than peer conflicts.
> **Steinberg & Morris (2001)**: many with teen depression had childhood distress — difficulties don't necessarily arise from adolescence itself.

### Virtual Identity
- Games/social media let adolescents try roles (e.g., **Second Life**, Dungeons & Dragons). **Multi-user dungeons (MUDs)**: shared virtual spaces for identity role-play.

### Gender, Generativity, Maturity
- Gender-typed toy preference persists 50+ years after Erikson's study. **Loyola Generativity Scale** (20 items) measures generativity vs. stagnation. *Vital Involvement in Old Age* (1986): ego integrity requires remaining active, not just reflecting.
- **Racial/ethnic identity** and **gender preference identity** are addressed in the chapter (examining how minority status, discrimination, and LGBTQ+ identity intersect with the stages).

## Reflections on Erikson's Theory

### Contributions
- Extended Freud to the whole life span; elevated the ego; introduced the **identity crisis**; recognized cultural/historical forces; framed Marcia's research; bestselling, culturally prominent.

### Criticisms
- Sexist play-construction interpretation; case study lacks rigor; never earned a degree; inadequate on **gender differences**; possibly **culturally biased** (Western, middle-class); "publicly defined himself as a loyal Freudian" while departing from it (Anderson & Friedman, 1997).

### Recognition
- Coined everyday terms ("identity crisis," "life cycle"); profound influence on psychoanalysis and culture; "Psychoanalysis is always the starting point."

## Chapter Summary

- **Eight psychosocial stages** across the life span, each with a **crisis** resolved adaptively/maladaptively.
- **Epigenetic principle**: development governed by inherited forces shaped by experience.
- Resolution → a **basic strength (virtue)**; failure → **basic weaknesses**.
- **Maldevelopment** (only one coping mode) → neuroses or psychoses.
- Assessment: play therapy, psychohistorical analysis, others' tests.
- View of human nature: **optimistic and partially deterministic**.
- The **identity crisis** is central, elaborated by **Marcia's five statuses**.
- Research broadly supports the proposed sequence.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Three extensions of Freud**: life-span development; ego independent of id; cultural/historical forces.

2. **Epigenetic principle**: development governed by genetic forces (*epi* = "upon"); environment shapes realization.

3. **Eight stages with ages, conflict pairs, basic strengths** (the highest-yield structure for MCQs).

4. **Maldevelopment**: "maladaptive" (only positive) → neurosis; "malignant" (only negative) → psychosis.

5. **Identity crisis vs. moratorium**: crisis = failure to achieve identity = role confusion; moratorium = normal hiatus to try roles.

6. **Marcia's five statuses**: identity achievement, moratorium, foreclosure, diffusion, alienated achievement.

7. **Order least → most resolved**: diffusion → foreclosure → moratorium → achievement.

8. **Basic strengths in order**: Hope, Will, Purpose, Competence, Fidelity, Love, Care, Wisdom.

9. **Psychohistorical analysis / disciplined subjectivity**: applied to Gandhi, Martin Luther, G.B. Shaw.

10. **Loyola Generativity Scale** (McAdams & de St. Aubin, 1992): **20 items**.

11. **Ego Identity Process Questionnaire** (Balistreri et al., 1995): **32 items**.

12. **Play constructions**: boys (10–12) = tall, exterior, action; girls = low, enclosed, interior; Freudian anatomical interpretation; criticized as sexist.

13. **Generativity → ego integrity** (Vital Involvement in Old Age, 1986; 29 people in their 80s): Stage 7 generativity is the key factor for Stage 8 integrity; despair = "continuing sense of stagnation."

14. **Foreclosure vs. diffusion**: foreclosure = commitment but not self-chosen (parental), no crisis; diffusion = no commitment and possibly no crisis.

15. **Erikson's biography**: trained by Anna Freud (not Sigmund); no university degree; changed name at 37; "identity confusion" first named in American Indians and WWII veterans.
