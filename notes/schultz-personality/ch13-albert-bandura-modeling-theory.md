# Ch 13: Albert Bandura: Modeling Theory

Bandura's **social-learning theory** (also called **social-cognitive theory**) proposes that behavior is learned primarily through observation and imitation of models rather than through direct reinforcement alone. Unlike Skinner, Bandura emphasized the role of **cognitive processes** as mediators between stimulus and response and insisted that most human behavior is acquired in a social context. His theory represents a **less extreme form of behaviorism** that bridges strict behaviorism and cognitive psychology.

---

## The Life of Bandura (1925–)

### Background and Education
- Born **1925** in Alberta, Canada, in a small town; high school had only **2 teachers and 20 students**
- Parents were **immigrants from Poland** who strongly emphasized education
- Summer after high school: worked in the **Yukon Territory** filling holes in the **Alaska Highway** — observed psychopathology of everyday life among fugitives, debtors, and probation-evaders
- Enrolled in psychology at the **University of British Columbia** almost by accident (carpool logistics with engineering/pre-med students)
- **Ph.D. in 1952 from the University of Iowa**
- One year at **Wichita, Kansas Guidance Center**, then joined **Stanford University** faculty
- Elected **president of the American Psychological Association in 1973** — only 21 years after his Ph.D.
- Received the **APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award in 1980**
- Received the **American Psychological Foundation's Gold Medal Award for Life Achievement in 2006**

### Intellectual Context
- When Bandura began his career, **Skinner's behaviorism had a "stranglehold" on psychology** (Bandura, 2011)
- Launching observational learning research was considered risky for an unknown young psychologist

---

## Modeling: The Basis of Observational Learning

### Core Premise
- **Learning can occur through observation or example**, not only through direct reinforcement
- Criticized **operant conditioning** (trial-and-error) as **inefficient and potentially dangerous** for skills like swimming or driving
- Most human behavior is learned through **example**, either intentionally or accidentally

### Key Definitions
- **Observational learning**: Learning new responses by observing the behavior of other people
- **Vicarious reinforcement**: Learning or strengthening a behavior by observing the behavior of others and the consequences of that behavior, rather than experiencing reinforcement or consequences directly
- **Modeling**: A behavior-modification technique that involves observing the behavior of others (the models) and participating with them in performing the desired behavior

### Contrast with Skinner
| Feature | Skinner | Bandura |
|---|---|---|
| Primary learning mechanism | Direct reinforcement | Observation/vicarious reinforcement |
| Role of cognition | Denied/ignored | Central mediator |
| Subject population | Individual animals | Humans in social interaction |
| Social context | Irrelevant | Fundamental |
| Controls behavior | Reinforcers | Models |

---

### KEY STUDY: Bobo Doll Experiment

> **Researcher:** Albert Bandura, Ross, & Ross (1963)  
> **Method:** Preschool children observed an adult model physically and verbally attacking an inflatable **Bobo doll** (~4 feet tall). Model shouted "Sock him in the nose!" and "Throw him in the air!" Children then left alone with the doll; compared to a no-model control group.  
> **Finding:** Experimental group was **twice as aggressive** as control group. Intensity of aggressive behavior was **equal whether the model was seen live, on television, or as a cartoon character**.

### KEY STUDY: Parents and Children's Aggression (Bandura & Walters, 1963)

> **Researcher:** Bandura & Walters (1963)  
> **Method:** Compared parents of highly aggressive children vs. parents of inhibited children.  
> **Finding:** Parents of inhibited children were inhibited; parents of aggressive children were aggressive. Children had modeled their behavior on parental examples.

### Verbal Modeling
- **Verbal modeling**: Using verbal instructions to induce behaviors (e.g., teaching driving)
- Often supplemented by behavioral demonstrations

## Disinhibition

- **Disinhibition**: The weakening of inhibitions or constraints by observing the behavior of a model
- Example: Crowd members starting a riot, discarding inhibitions because others are doing so

### KEY STUDY: Sexual Disinhibition (Walters, Bowen, & Parke, 1963)

> **Researcher:** Walters, Bowen, & Parke (1963)  
> **Method:** Male undergraduates watched an erotic film; a moving spot of light represented eye movements of a prior "model." For half, the spot focused on erotic parts (uninhibited model); for the other half, the spot stayed in the background (inhibited model). Subjects' own eye movements then recorded.  
> **Finding:** Subjects matched their model. Those with an "uninhibited" model looked directly at erotic parts; those with an "inhibited" model spent more time on background. Modeling determined not only what subjects did but also **what they looked at and perceived**.

### Trolling as Online Disinhibition
- **Trolling**: Posting inflammatory, derogatory, or hateful messages online — an online form of disinhibition
- Research: frequent video gamers most into trolling were **younger and male** (Thacker & Griffiths, 2012)
- Motivations: deliberate revenge, boredom, or amusement
- Disinhibition effect **declines with age**: strong at 18-19, greatly reduced at 22-25 (Vaidya et al., 2010)

## The Effects of Society's Models

- Much behavior — good/bad, normal/abnormal — is learned by imitating others
- Deviant individuals have followed models society considers undesirable (not a different learning process)
- Non-rational fears are acquired through modeling (e.g., child learns fear of thunderstorms from fearful parents)
- Key contrast: **In Skinner's system, reinforcers control behavior; for Bandura, models control behavior**

## Characteristics of the Modeling Situation

Bandura identified **three factors** influencing modeling:

### 1. Characteristics of the Models

**Similarity to Observer**
- More influenced by someone similar to us
- Live model > filmed child > animated cartoon (decreasing similarity = decreasing imitation); even cartoon produced significantly more behavior than no-model control

**Age and Sex**
- More likely to model behavior of person of **same sex** and **same age**
- Peers who have successfully solved our problems are highly influential

**Status and Prestige**
- High-status models elicit more imitation (e.g., pedestrians more likely to jaywalk behind a well-dressed person)
- TV commercials exploit high-status/prestige models (athletes, celebrities)

**Type of Behavior**
- **Highly complex behaviors** not imitated as quickly as simpler behaviors
- **Hostile and aggressive behaviors** tend to be strongly imitated, especially by children
- Infants as young as **16 months** can imitate tool use, but only **if previously shown the purpose of the tool** (Esseilly et al., 2013)

**Size and Weight of Models**
- Students at schools where older students were overweight gained more weight than students at schools with normal-weight older students (Leatherdale & Papadakis, 2011)

### 2. Characteristics of the Observers

**Age of Observers**
- **Infancy**: modeling limited to immediate imitation (lacks imaginal and verbal representational systems for delayed imitation); modeled behavior must be within the infant's sensorimotor range
- **By age 2**: sufficient attentional, retention, and production processes for delayed imitation
- Age progression of reinforcers:
  - Younger children: physical stimuli (food, affection, punishment)
  - Older children: associate physical reinforcers with approval/disapproval from significant models
  - Eventually: rewards/punishments become self-administered

**Attributes of Observers**
- People **low in self-confidence and self-esteem** are much more likely to imitate a model
- A person previously reinforced for imitating is more susceptible to model influence

### 3. Reward Consequences Associated with the Behaviors

- Reward consequences can **override** the impact of model and observer characteristics

### KEY STUDY: Reward/Punishment and Aggression (Bandura, 1965)

> **Researcher:** Bandura (1965)  
> **Method:** Children watched Bobo doll model either receive praise/candy (reward) or verbal/physical punishment for aggression.  
> **Finding:** Children who observed punishment displayed **significantly less aggression** toward the Bobo doll than children who saw the model reinforced.

## The Processes of Observational Learning

Bandura identified **four related mechanisms** governing observational learning:

### TABLE: Four Processes of Observational Learning

| Process | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Attentional** | Paying sufficient attention to model; perceiving accurately enough to imitate | Staying awake in driver's education class |
| **Retention** | Remembering behavior via mental images and verbal descriptions for later imitation | Taking notes on driving lecture/video |
| **Production** | Translating mental images/verbal codes into overt behavior through practice and feedback | Practicing shifting gears with an instructor |
| **Incentive/Motivational** | Perceiving that the model's behavior leads to reward; expecting similar consequences | Expecting to earn a driver's license after mastering skills |

### 1. Attentional Processes
- Observational learning will not occur without attention to the model
- Variables influencing attention: age, status, sex, similarity; celebrity/expert models attract more attention; personal relevance; behavior with positive or negative (not neutral) consequences
- Observers with more highly developed cognitive abilities attend more carefully

### 2. Retention Processes
- Must encode observed behavior as symbolic representations
- **Two representational systems**:
  1. **Imaginal system**: Forming vivid, easily retrievable mental images while observing (used as basis for later imitation)
  2. **Verbal system**: Verbal coding of observed behavior; can be rehearsed silently; provides hints/cues during later performance
- Together, images and verbal symbols allow storage and rehearsal of observed situations

### 3. Production Processes
- Translating imaginal/verbal representations into **overt behavior** (= practice)
- Even with good attention and retention, performance may be clumsy at first (especially for complex skills)
- **Practice and feedback** are required for smooth performance

### 4. Incentive and Motivational Processes
- Without incentive or motivation, behavior will not be performed even if it can be
- Incentives also influence attention and retention
- **Anticipation of reinforcement**: seeing a model rewarded creates expectation that our same behavior will yield similar results — reinforcement is experienced **vicariously** during observation
- **Reinforcement is not always required for learning**: loud sounds, bright lights, exciting videos capture attention without prior reinforcement
- Children imitate TV/video game models regardless of promised rewards
- When reinforcement occurs, it can be: given by another person, experienced vicariously, or **self-administered**

## Self-Reinforcement and Self-Efficacy

The **self** in Bandura's theory is a set of cognitive processes and structures concerned with thought and perception — not a psychic agent that causes behavior.

### Self-Reinforcement

- **Self-reinforcement**: Administering rewards or punishments to oneself for meeting, exceeding, or falling short of one's own expectations or standards
- Particularly important for older children and adults
- Types:
  - **Tangible**: buying oneself new gym shoes, a car
  - **Emotional**: pride or satisfaction from a job well done
- Self-administered punishment: shame, guilt, depression for falling short
- Conceptually similar to **conscience** or **superego** but Bandura denies it is the same
- Regulated by **internal standards of performance**: subjective criteria against which we evaluate behavior
- When achievement no longer challenges, the standard is raised; failure may lead to lowering the standard

**Unrealistic Performance Standards**
- Modeled from unusually talented/successful models — leads to repeated failure
- Self-punishment: feelings of worthlessness and depression
- Can lead to: alcohol/drug abuse, retreat into fantasy
- Initial standards learned from **parents and teachers** (increasingly from online sources: celebrity blogs, social media)

### Self-Efficacy, or "Believing You Can"

- **Self-efficacy**: Feelings of adequacy, efficiency, and competence in coping with life (Bandura)
- Defined as "the power of believing you can" (Maddux, 2002)
- Also described as **perception of control over one's life**
- Meeting performance standards enhances self-efficacy; failure reduces it (Bandura, 2012, 2013)

#### Low vs. High Self-Efficacy

| Low Self-Efficacy | High Self-Efficacy |
|---|---|
| Feels helpless, unable to exercise control | Believes they can deal effectively with events |
| Views effort as futile | Expects to succeed; perseveres at tasks |
| Quickly gives up when obstacles encountered | Often performs at high level |
| May not even attempt to cope | Greater confidence; little self-doubt |
| Destroys motivation, lowers aspirations | Views difficulties as challenges, not threats |
| Interferes with cognitive abilities | Actively seeks novel situations |
| Adversely affects physical health | Reduces fear of failure; raises aspirations; improves problem-solving |

#### Four Sources of Information about Self-Efficacy

**1. Performance Attainment** *(most influential)*
- Previous successes = direct evidence of mastery and competence; raise self-efficacy
- Prior failures (especially repeated childhood failures) = lower self-efficacy
- Positive feedback raises perceived competence; negative feedback lowers it
- Tai Chi training (6 months) in older adults = significant self-efficacy increases (Li et al., 2001)
- 16-hour self-defense course in female college students = higher self-efficacy in physical competence, coping, and assertiveness (Weitlauf et al., 2001)

**2. Vicarious Experiences**
- Seeing similar people succeed: "If they can do it, so can I"
- Seeing similar people fail: "If they can't do it, neither can I"

**3. Verbal Persuasion**
- Simply reminding people they have the ability to achieve
- Most **common** of the four sources; used by parents, teachers, coaches, therapists
- Must be **realistic** to be effective

**4. Physiological and Emotional Arousal**
- Greater calm and composure = higher self-efficacy
- Higher arousal (fear, anxiety, tension) = lower self-efficacy

#### Ways of Increasing Self-Efficacy (Bandura's Four Methods)
1. Expose to **success experiences** by arranging reachable goals (boosts performance attainment)
2. Expose to **appropriate successful models** (enhances vicarious success experiences)
3. Provide **verbal persuasion** to believe in ability to succeed
4. Strengthen **physiological health** through proper diet, stress reduction, and exercise

## Developmental Stages of Self-Efficacy

### Childhood
- Infants begin developing self-efficacy by exercising influence over physical and social environments
- Ideal parents: responsive to activities, provide stimulating surroundings, allow freedom to explore

**Gender Differences in High Self-Efficacy Development (Schneewind, 1995):**
- High self-efficacy **men** (as children): warm relationships with **fathers**; mothers were more demanding/achievement-oriented
- High self-efficacy **women** (as children): **fathers** pressured for high achievement

**Reduction of Parental Influence:**
- Significance diminishes as child's world expands
- Like **Adler**, Bandura considered **birth order** important:
  - First-born/only children have different bases for self-efficacy judgments than later-born children
  - Same-sex siblings are more competitive than opposite-sex siblings
- Among peers, those most experienced/successful at tasks serve as high-efficacy models

**Teachers and Schools:**
- Teachers influence self-efficacy through cognitive ability and problem-solving development
- Children often rate competence in terms of teachers' evaluations
- **Ability groupings** undermine self-efficacy in students assigned to lower groups
- **Grading on a curve** dooms poor achievers to average or low grades

### Adolescence
- New demands: awareness of sex, choice of college and career; new competency establishment
- Success at this stage typically depends on the level of self-efficacy established during **childhood**

### Adulthood

Bandura divided adulthood into **two periods**:

**Young Adulthood:**
- New adjustments: marriage, parenthood, establishing a career
- High self-efficacy women as parents = promote self-efficacy in children; less subject to despondency/emotional strain (Olioff & Aboud, 1991; Teti & Gelfand, 1991)
- High self-efficacy mothers working outside home experienced significantly less strain from work-family conflicts (Bandura, 1995)

**Middle Years:**
- Reevaluation of career, family, social lives; confronting limitations; redefining goals
- Must reassess skills and find new opportunities for enhancing self-efficacy

### Old Age
- Declining mental and physical abilities, retirement, social withdrawal
- Lowering of self-efficacy can become a **self-fulfilling prophecy**
  - Example: reduced confidence about sexual performance leads to reduction in sexual activity
- **Self-efficacy is the single most important factor in determining success or failure throughout the entire life span** (Bandura)

## Behavior Modification

Bandura developed **three forms** of behavior therapy:
1. **Modeling**
2. **Guided participation**
3. **Covert modeling**

### Fears and Phobias

### KEY STUDY: Dog Phobia in Children (Bandura, Grusec, & Menlove, 1967)

> **Researcher:** Bandura, Grusec, & Menlove (1967)  
> **Method:** Children afraid of dogs observed a same-age model making progressively bolder movements toward a dog (petting through playpen bars, then entering pen and playing).  
> **Finding:** Observers' fear of dogs was **considerably reduced**.

### KEY STUDY: Snake Phobia in Adults (Bandura, Blanchard, & Ritter, 1969)

> **Researcher:** Bandura, Blanchard, & Ritter (1969)  
> **Method:** Adults with intense snake phobia watched a film of models making progressively closer contact with a snake (plastic snakes → touching live snake → snake crawling over body). Subjects could stop the film when too threatening.  
> **Finding:** Subjects gradually overcame their fear. Classic demonstration of film-based modeling therapy.

### Guided Participation
- **Guided participation**: Watch live model (through observation window) + then participate with the model
- Snake phobia procedure:
  1. Watch model handle snake
  2. Enter room; observe at close range
  3. Touch middle of snake (wearing gloves) while model holds head and tail
  4. Eventually touch snake without gloves

### Covert Modeling
- **Covert modeling**: Subjects instructed to **imagine** a model coping with a feared situation; no actual model observed
- Effective for snake phobias and social inhibitions

### Advantages of Modeling Therapy
- Complex behaviors seen as a whole
- Extraneous behaviors can be edited out
- Same videos reused with many patients and multiple therapists simultaneously
- Can be used with groups (saves time and money)
- Effective for: phobias, obsessive-compulsive disorders, sexual dysfunction
- Positive effects reported to last for years

### Anxiety Treatment

**Fear of Medical Treatment — KEY STUDY (Melamed & Siegel, 1975)**
> **Researcher:** Melamed & Siegel (1975)  
> **Method:** Children scheduled for first surgery: (1) watched film of a boy coping well with hospital experience; (2) control group watched film of boy taking a trip.  
> **Finding:** Modeling film reduced anxiety; those who saw hospital film also had **fewer behavior problems after hospitalization** than control group.

**Test Anxiety — KEY STUDY (Sarason, 1975)**
> **Researcher:** Sarason (1975)  
> **Method:** College students divided by test anxiety level. Three conditions: (1) model discussed test anxiety + coping mechanisms; (2) model discussed test anxiety but not coping; (3) model talked about other college activities. All subjects recalled nonsense syllables.  
> **Finding:** High-anxiety subjects exposed to the **coping mechanisms** model performed **significantly better** on recall test than high-anxiety subjects in other conditions.

### Ethical Issues in Behavior Modification

Criticisms: exploits people, manipulates/controls against their will.

Bandura's counter-arguments:
1. Does **not** occur without client **awareness** — self-awareness and self-regulation are vital
2. Clients themselves **decide what to change** — not controlled by anyone else
3. Client-therapist relationship is a **contract between two consenting individuals**
4. Modeling techniques actually **increase personal freedom** (constraints of phobias/compulsions are removed)

## Questions about Human Nature

- **Reciprocal determinism**: The idea that behavior is controlled by the individual (through cognitive processes) AND by the environment (through external social stimulus events) — they are **mutually determinant**
- People are neither "powerless objects controlled by environmental forces" nor "free agents who can become whatever they choose"
- "Both people and their environments are **reciprocal determinants** of each other" (Bandura, 1977, p. vii)

## Assessment in Bandura's Theory

- Focused on **overt behavior** (like Skinner) but also accepted cognitive variables
- Did NOT use: free association, dream analysis, projective techniques
- Assessment methods:
  - Direct observation of behavior
  - Self-report inventories
  - Physiological measurements
  - Quantitative assessment of behavioral and cognitive variables
  - Behavioral-avoidance tests (subjects self-rate expected task completion)
  - Personality inventories (e.g., for test anxiety)

## Research on Bandura's Theory

### Self-Efficacy: Age and Gender Differences
- On average, **men score higher** than women in self-efficacy
- Gender differences **peak during the 20s**, then decline in later years
- For both sexes: self-efficacy **increases through childhood and early adulthood**, **peaks in middle age**, then **declines after age 60** (Gecas, 1989; Lachman, 1985)

### Memory Self-Efficacy
- Dutch adults (avg. age 66): those who believed their memory was getting worse performed significantly worse on memory tests **6 years later** (Valentijn et al., 2006)
- Meta-analysis of 100+ studies confirms: **low memory self-efficacy** directly affects memory test performance (Beaudoin & Desrichard, 2011)

### Role of Parental Self-Efficacy
- Italian study: adolescents with high parental self-efficacy parents had higher own self-efficacy, fewer behavioral problems, less anxiety, better school performance, more open communication with parents (Steca et al., 2011)
- African-American teenage boys: high parental support/control/self-efficacy = higher teen self-efficacy; low parental self-efficacy = greater substance abuse and delinquency (Nebbitt, 2009)
- American young adults: **tight parental control** = **lower** self-efficacy in children (Givertz & Segrin, 2014)
- Malaysian teenagers: **father involvement** as important as mother involvement for self-efficacy and happiness (Yap & Baharudin, 2015)

### Physical Appearance and Self-Efficacy (Andreoletti, Zebrowitz, & Lachman, 2001)
- Adults ages 25-76: physical appearance had **greater effect on control beliefs** than self-esteem or health
- **"Baby-faceness"** (round face, large eyes, small nose bridge, small chin): strongly related to **low control beliefs** in young and middle adulthood
- People rated less attractive: lower feelings of control in job and social situations
- **Shorter people**: lower feelings of control in young adulthood

### Academic Performance
- Significant positive relationship between self-efficacy and academic performance
- Teachers with high self-efficacy create more opportunities for student achievement
- Student self-efficacy positively related to: motivation, effort, aspiration, persistence
- German classroom study: high self-efficacy teachers rated more effective by students; positive student ratings in turn raised teachers' self-efficacy (Holzberger, Philipp, & Kunter, 2013) — bidirectional relationship

## Reflections on Bandura's Theory

### Strengths
- Based on **rigorous laboratory research** with normal people in social interaction
- Well-controlled experiments; precise measurement; large subject groups; statistical analysis
- Highly applicable: phobias, anxiety, education, health behavior, media effects
- Bridges strict behaviorism and cognitive psychology

### The Limited-Domain Approach
- Bandura's theory is a **limited-domain approach** to personality
- Focuses on specific, measurable constructs (self-efficacy, observational learning, self-reinforcement) rather than an all-encompassing personality theory
- Contrasts with grand theories (Freud, Jung): narrower in scope, but better supported by empirical research
- Characteristic of the modern cognitive-behavioral tradition

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Bobo doll experiment (Bandura, Ross, & Ross, 1963)**: children showed twice the aggression of controls; effect was equal whether the model was live, on TV, or an animated cartoon — all three media equivalent.

2. **Four processes of observational learning** (in order): attentional, retention, production, incentive/motivational — know what each involves and an example for each.

3. **Four sources of self-efficacy information** (know all four): performance attainment (MOST INFLUENTIAL), vicarious experiences, verbal persuasion (MOST COMMON), physiological/emotional arousal.

4. **Disinhibition**: weakening of inhibitions by observing a model; Walters, Bowen & Parke (1963) demonstrated it even in perceptual responses (eye movements, not just overt behavior).

5. **Reciprocal determinism**: neither pure free will nor environmental determinism; person, behavior, and environment are **mutually** determinant — Bandura's answer to the free will question.

6. **Three behavior therapy forms**: modeling (including film-based), guided participation (watch live model + then participate), covert modeling (imagine model — no actual model).

7. **Self-efficacy across the lifespan**: increases through childhood and early adulthood, **peaks in middle age**, **declines after age 60**; gender differences peak in the **20s** (men score higher on average).

8. **Performance attainment is the single most influential source** of self-efficacy; verbal persuasion is the most **common** source.

9. **Self-efficacy is the single most important factor** in determining success or failure throughout the entire life span (Bandura's own claim).

10. **Bandura on self-reinforcement**: it is similar to but not the same as conscience or superego; involves setting internal standards and rewarding/punishing oneself for meeting or failing to meet them.

11. **Characteristics of the modeling situation — three factors**: (1) characteristics of models, (2) characteristics of observers, (3) reward consequences associated with behaviors.

12. In childhood, **high self-efficacy men** had warm father relationships and demanding mothers; **high self-efficacy women** had achievement-demanding fathers (Schneewind, 1995).

13. **Verbal persuasion** must be **realistic** to be effective — the most common but not the most powerful source of self-efficacy.

14. Bandura's ethical defense of behavior modification: increases personal freedom, requires client awareness and consent, clients choose their own treatment goals.

15. Bandura's key criticism of Skinner: used **individual animal subjects** rather than humans in social interaction — most people do not live in social isolation, making Skinner's data irrelevant to real-world behavior.
