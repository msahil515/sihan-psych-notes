# Ch 13: Albert Bandura: Modeling Theory

Bandura's **social-learning theory** (social-cognitive theory): behavior is learned primarily through observation and imitation of models, not direct reinforcement alone. Unlike Skinner, he emphasized **cognitive processes** mediating stimulus and response, and held that most behavior is acquired socially. A **less extreme behaviorism** bridging strict behaviorism and cognitive psychology.

---

## The Life of Bandura (1925–)

- Born **1925** in Alberta, Canada (tiny high school: 2 teachers, 20 students); **Polish immigrant** parents who valued education.
- Worked on the **Alaska Highway** in the Yukon — observed everyday psychopathology.
- **Ph.D. 1952, University of Iowa**; joined **Stanford**. **APA president 1973** (21 years after his Ph.D.); APA Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award (**1980**); Gold Medal for Life Achievement (**2006**).
- When he began, **Skinner's behaviorism had a "stranglehold"** on psychology; studying observational learning was risky.

---

## Modeling: The Basis of Observational Learning

- **Learning can occur through observation/example**, not just direct reinforcement. Operant trial-and-error is **inefficient and dangerous** for skills like swimming or driving.
- **Observational learning**: learning new responses by observing others.
- **Vicarious reinforcement**: learning/strengthening a behavior by observing others' behavior and its consequences, not experiencing them directly.
- **Modeling**: a behavior-modification technique — observing models and participating with them.

| Feature | Skinner | Bandura |
|---|---|---|
| Learning mechanism | Direct reinforcement | Observation/vicarious reinforcement |
| Cognition | Denied | Central mediator |
| Subjects | Individual animals | Humans in social interaction |
| Controls behavior | Reinforcers | Models |

> **KEY STUDY: Bobo Doll (Bandura, Ross, & Ross, 1963)** — preschoolers who watched an adult attack an inflatable **Bobo doll** were **twice as aggressive** as a no-model control; aggression was **equal whether the model was live, on TV, or a cartoon**.

> **KEY STUDY: Bandura & Walters (1963)** — parents of inhibited children were inhibited; parents of aggressive children were aggressive — children **modeled** parental behavior.

- **Verbal modeling**: using verbal instructions to induce behaviors (e.g., teaching driving).

---

## Disinhibition

- **Disinhibition**: weakening of inhibitions by observing a model (e.g., joining a riot).

> **KEY STUDY: Walters, Bowen, & Parke (1963)** — male undergraduates watched an erotic film with a light spot representing a prior model's eye movements; subjects matched their model (uninhibited model → looked at erotic parts). Modeling shaped **what subjects looked at and perceived**, not just overt behavior.

- **Trolling** = online disinhibition; frequent gamer-trolls were **younger and male** (Thacker & Griffiths, 2012). Disinhibition **declines with age** (strong at 18–19, reduced at 22–25; Vaidya et al., 2010).

---

## The Effects of Society's Models

- Much behavior (good/bad, normal/abnormal) is learned by imitation; deviance follows undesirable models (same learning process). Non-rational fears (e.g., of thunderstorms) are modeled. Key contrast: for Skinner, reinforcers control behavior; for Bandura, **models** do.

---

## Characteristics of the Modeling Situation

Three factors:

### 1. Characteristics of the Models
- **Similarity**: more influenced by similar models; live > filmed > cartoon (but even cartoon beats no model).
- **Age/sex**: model **same sex and age** more imitated.
- **Status/prestige**: high-status models elicit more imitation (jaywalking behind a well-dressed person; celebrity ads).
- **Type of behavior**: complex behaviors imitated less readily; **hostile/aggressive** behaviors strongly imitated, especially by children. Infants at **16 months** imitate tool use only if previously shown the tool's purpose (Esseilly et al., 2013).
- **Size/weight**: students gained more weight where older students were overweight (Leatherdale & Papadakis, 2011).

### 2. Characteristics of the Observers
- **Age**: infants limited to immediate imitation; by **age 2**, delayed imitation possible. Reinforcers shift from physical (food) → approval → self-administered.
- **Attributes**: people **low in self-confidence/self-esteem** imitate more; prior reinforcement for imitating increases susceptibility.

### 3. Reward Consequences
- Can **override** model and observer characteristics.

> **KEY STUDY: Bandura (1965)** — children who saw an aggressive Bobo model **punished** showed **significantly less aggression** than those who saw the model rewarded.

---

## The Processes of Observational Learning

Four mechanisms:

| Process | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Attentional** | Attending to and accurately perceiving the model | Staying awake in driver's ed |
| **Retention** | Remembering via mental images and verbal codes | Taking notes on the driving video |
| **Production** | Translating representations into behavior via practice/feedback | Practicing shifting gears |
| **Incentive/Motivational** | Expecting the modeled behavior to lead to reward | Expecting to earn a license |

- **Attentional**: influenced by age, status, sex, similarity, relevance, and non-neutral consequences.
- **Retention**: encode via the **imaginal** (mental images) and **verbal** (silent rehearsal) representational systems.
- **Production**: practice and feedback needed; performance is clumsy at first.
- **Incentive**: anticipating reinforcement (experienced **vicariously**) motivates performance, but **reinforcement is not always required** for learning; reinforcement can be given by others, vicarious, or **self-administered**.

## Self-Reinforcement and Self-Efficacy

The **self** = cognitive processes about thought/perception, not a psychic agent.

### Self-Reinforcement
- **Self-reinforcement**: rewarding/punishing oneself for meeting or missing one's own standards. **Tangible** (buying something) or **emotional** (pride); failure → shame, guilt, depression.
- Similar to **conscience/superego** (but Bandura denies they are the same); regulated by **internal standards of performance**, raised when met, sometimes lowered after failure.
- **Unrealistic standards** (modeled from exceptional people) → repeated failure, worthlessness, depression, possible substance abuse. Standards learned from parents/teachers (increasingly online/social media).

### Self-Efficacy
- **Self-efficacy**: feelings of adequacy, efficiency, competence in coping with life ("the power of believing you can"; Maddux, 2002); a perception of **control**. Meeting standards raises it; failure lowers it.

| Low Self-Efficacy | High Self-Efficacy |
|---|---|
| Feels helpless; effort futile | Believes they can deal effectively |
| Gives up quickly | Perseveres, performs at a high level |
| Lowers aspirations; harms health | Views difficulties as challenges; raises aspirations |

**Four sources of self-efficacy information:**
1. **Performance attainment** *(most influential)* — prior successes raise it; repeated failures lower it.
2. **Vicarious experiences** — "if they can, so can I."
3. **Verbal persuasion** *(most common)* — reminding people of their ability; must be **realistic**.
4. **Physiological/emotional arousal** — calm = higher; fear/tension = lower.

**Increasing self-efficacy** (Bandura's four methods): arrange success experiences; provide successful models; verbal persuasion; strengthen physiological health (diet, stress reduction, exercise).

---

## Developmental Stages of Self-Efficacy

### Childhood
- Develops by influencing the environment; ideal parents are responsive and allow exploration.
- **Gender (Schneewind, 1995)**: high-efficacy **men** had warm relationships with **fathers** and demanding mothers; high-efficacy **women** had **fathers** who pressured for achievement.
- Like **Adler**, Bandura emphasized **birth order**. **Teachers/schools**: ability groupings and grading on a curve **undermine** self-efficacy in lower-ranked students.

### Adolescence
- New demands (sex, college, career); success depends on **childhood** self-efficacy.

### Adulthood
- **Young adulthood**: marriage, parenthood, career; high-efficacy mothers promote children's self-efficacy and feel less work-family strain.
- **Middle years**: reevaluate and find new efficacy sources.

### Old Age
- Declining abilities, retirement; lowered self-efficacy can become a **self-fulfilling prophecy**. **Self-efficacy is the single most important factor in success or failure throughout the life span.**

## Behavior Modification

Three forms: **modeling**, **guided participation**, **covert modeling**.

> **KEY STUDY: Dog Phobia (Bandura, Grusec, & Menlove, 1967)** — children watched a same-age model make progressively bolder moves toward a dog; their fear was **considerably reduced**.

> **KEY STUDY: Snake Phobia (Bandura, Blanchard, & Ritter, 1969)** — adults watched a film of models making progressively closer contact with a snake; they gradually overcame the fear. Classic film-based modeling therapy.

- **Guided participation**: watch a live model, then participate (snake phobia: watch → observe close → touch with gloves → touch without gloves).
- **Covert modeling**: **imagine** a model coping; no actual model observed.
- **Advantages**: complex behaviors shown whole, reusable videos, group use, lasting effects; works for phobias, OCD, sexual dysfunction.

> **KEY STUDY: Melamed & Siegel (1975)** — children who watched a film of a boy coping with surgery had **less anxiety and fewer post-hospitalization behavior problems** than controls.

> **KEY STUDY: Sarason (1975)** — high-test-anxiety students exposed to a model demonstrating **coping mechanisms** recalled significantly better than those whose model lacked coping content.

**Ethics**: Bandura argued modeling requires client **awareness** and consent, clients **choose their goals**, and it **increases personal freedom** (removing phobia/compulsion constraints).

---

## Questions about Human Nature

- **Reciprocal determinism**: behavior is controlled by the individual (cognitive processes) AND the environment — they are **mutually determinant**. People are neither powerless objects nor wholly free agents.

---

## Assessment in Bandura's Theory

- Focused on **overt behavior** (like Skinner) but accepted cognitive variables. No free association, dream analysis, or projective tests. Methods: direct observation, self-report inventories, physiological measures, behavioral-avoidance tests, personality inventories.

---

## Research on Bandura's Theory

- **Age/gender**: **men score higher** on average; gender differences peak in the **20s**; self-efficacy increases through childhood/early adulthood, **peaks in middle age**, declines after **60** (Gecas, 1989).
- **Memory self-efficacy**: believing one's memory is declining predicted worse performance **6 years later** (Valentijn et al., 2006); a 100+ study meta-analysis confirms low memory self-efficacy harms performance (Beaudoin & Desrichard, 2011).
- **Parental self-efficacy**: high parental self-efficacy → higher teen self-efficacy, fewer problems (Steca et al., 2011); **tight parental control → lower** self-efficacy (Givertz & Segrin, 2014); **father involvement** matters as much as mother's (Yap & Baharudin, 2015).
- **Appearance (Andreoletti et al., 2001)**: appearance had a greater effect on control beliefs than self-esteem or health; **"baby-faceness"** and shorter stature linked to **lower control beliefs**.
- **Academics**: self-efficacy positively relates to academic performance, motivation, persistence; teacher and student self-efficacy are **bidirectionally** related (Holzberger et al., 2013).

---

## Reflections on Bandura's Theory

### Strengths
- Based on **rigorous lab research** with normal people in social interaction; well-controlled, statistical; highly applicable (phobias, education, health, media); bridges behaviorism and cognitive psychology.

### The Limited-Domain Approach
- A **limited-domain approach**: focuses on specific, measurable constructs (self-efficacy, observational learning) rather than a grand theory; narrower than Freud/Jung but better empirically supported.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Bobo doll (Bandura, Ross, & Ross, 1963)**: twice the aggression of controls; **live, TV, and cartoon models equally effective**.

2. **Four processes of observational learning** (in order): attentional, retention, production, incentive/motivational.

3. **Four sources of self-efficacy**: performance attainment (**MOST INFLUENTIAL**), vicarious experiences, verbal persuasion (**MOST COMMON**), physiological/emotional arousal.

4. **Disinhibition**: Walters, Bowen & Parke (1963) showed it even in perception (eye movements).

5. **Reciprocal determinism**: person, behavior, environment mutually determinant — Bandura's answer to free will.

6. **Three behavior therapy forms**: modeling, guided participation, covert modeling (imagine only).

7. **Self-efficacy lifespan**: rises through childhood/early adulthood, peaks in **middle age**, declines after **60**; gender gap peaks in the **20s**.

8. **Performance attainment = most influential** source; verbal persuasion = most **common**.

9. **Self-efficacy is the single most important factor** in lifelong success/failure.

10. **Self-reinforcement**: like but not identical to conscience/superego; uses internal standards.

11. **Three modeling-situation factors**: model characteristics, observer characteristics, reward consequences.

12. **Schneewind (1995)**: high-efficacy men had warm fathers; high-efficacy women had achievement-demanding fathers.

13. **Verbal persuasion** must be **realistic**.

14. **Ethical defense**: increases freedom; requires awareness/consent; clients choose goals.

15. **Criticism of Skinner**: used individual animals, not humans in social interaction.
