# Ch 8: Raymond Cattell, Hans Eysenck, The Five-Factor Theory, HEXACO, and the Dark Triad

Covers the genetics/trait approach: Cattell's 16-source-trait model, Eysenck's three-dimension PEN model, the Big Five/NEO model (McCrae & Costa), Ashton & Lee's HEXACO six-factor model, and Paulhus & Williams's Dark Triad. Running theme: the primacy of heredity in shaping traits, assessed via twin and adoption studies across cultures.

---

## Predicting Behavior

- Cattell's goal: **predict** behavior in a given situation. *"Personality is that which permits a prediction of what a person will do in a given situation"* (Cattell, 1950).
- Unlike clinical theorists (Freud, Adler), he studied **normal people** with no intent to treat; believed modifying personality before understanding it was unwise.

### Factor Analysis
- Hallmark method: **factor analysis** — a statistical procedure correlating every pair of measures to identify common underlying **factors**. Highly correlated measures are combined into one factor.

> **KEY TERM — Factor Analysis:** A statistical technique based on correlations between measures, explained via underlying factors.

- The factors derived are called **traits** — the mental elements of personality and the basis for prediction.

---

## The Life of Cattell (1905–1998)

- Born in **Staffordshire, England**. At **9**, WWI wounded soldiers near his home made him serious about life's brevity; lifelong work intensity.
- Age 16: **University of London** (physics/chemistry), honours in 3 years. Switched to psychology in **1924** (only six psychology professorships existed in England).
- Graduate study under **Charles E. Spearman** (developer of factor analysis for mental abilities); Ph.D. **1929**. Resolved to apply factor analysis to **personality structure**.
- Post-doctorate hardship; **Edward L. Thorndike** invited him to Columbia. Then Clark University, **Harvard** (1941, with **Gordon Allport** and William Sheldon), and the **University of Illinois** (research professor). Published **500+ articles, 43 books**. Died in Honolulu at **92** (1998), a year after the APA **Gold Medal for Life Achievement** (1997).

## Cattell's Approach to Personality Traits

- **Traits** (Cattell): *relatively permanent reaction tendencies that are the basic structural units of personality*, derived by factor analysis.

### Common vs. Unique Traits

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Common traits** | Possessed by everyone to some degree (similar heredity + culture) | Intelligence, extraversion |
| **Unique traits** | Shared by few; evident in interests/attitudes | Interest in genealogy, martial arts |

### Ability, Temperament, Dynamic Traits

| Type | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Ability traits** | How efficiently one works toward a goal | Intelligence |
| **Temperament traits** | General behavioral style and emotional tone | Assertiveness, irritability |
| **Dynamic traits** | Driving forces; motivations, interests | Ergs, sentiments |

### Surface vs. Source Traits
- **Surface traits**: correlate but do not form a single factor (no single source); less stable. E.g., anxiety + indecision + irrational fear → "neuroticism."
- **Source traits**: unitary, stable, permanent factors from factor analysis; the true building blocks (analogous to atoms).

### Constitutional vs. Environmental-Mold Traits

| Type | Origin | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Constitutional** | Biological/physiological conditions | Talkativeness; slurred speech from alcohol |
| **Environmental-mold** | Social/physical environment; learned | Officer vs. jazz musician patterns |

## Source Traits: The Basic Factors of Personality

- After 20+ years, Cattell (1965) identified **16 source traits**, assessed by the **Sixteen Personality Factor (16 PF) Questionnaire**.

| Factor | Low | High |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| **A** | Reserved, detached | Outgoing, warmhearted |
| **B** | Low intelligence | High intelligence |
| **C** | Low ego strength, unstable | High ego strength, stable |
| **E** | Submissive, meek | Dominant, assertive |
| **F** | Serious, sober | Happy-go-lucky, cheerful |
| **G** | Expedient, low superego | Conscientious, high superego |
| **H** | Timid, shy | Bold, adventurous |
| **I** | Tough-minded, self-reliant | Tender-minded, sensitive |
| **L** | Trusting, accepting | Suspicious, jealous |
| **M** | Practical, down-to-earth | Imaginative, absentminded |
| **N** | Forthright, naive | Shrewd, worldly |
| **O** | Self-assured, secure | Apprehensive, insecure |
| **Q1** | Conservative, traditional | Radical, experimenting |
| **Q2** | Group-dependent | Self-sufficient, independent |
| **Q3** | Uncontrolled, lax | Controlled, compulsive |
| **Q4** | Relaxed, tranquil | Tense, driven |

> **MCQ TIP:** **Q1–Q4** emerged only from questionnaire (Q-data). **Factor B = Intelligence.** Test for ages **16+**, ~**40 languages**.

## Dynamic Traits: The Motivating Forces

- **Dynamic traits** concern **motivation** — the driving forces of behavior.
- **Ergs** (Greek *ergon* = energy): constitutional dynamic source traits; innate biological drives (sex, gregariousness, curiosity, fear, hunger).
- **Sentiments**: environmental-mold dynamic source traits; learned attitudes/interests (career, religious). The **self-sentiment** organizes all others.
- **Attitudes**: specific manifestations of ergs/sentiments; the most surface-level dynamic structure.
- **Dynamic lattice (subsidiation chain)**: attitudes subsidiate sentiments → sentiments subsidiate ergs (innate drives at the top).

---

## Heredity and Environment

- Compared twins/siblings reared together vs. apart.
- **Factor B (Intelligence)** and **Factor H (Timidity/Boldness)**: each ~**80%** genetic.
- Overall: **one-third genetic, two-thirds social/environmental**.

## Stages of Personality Development

| Stage | Age | Key Features |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| **Infancy** | 0–6 | **Major formative period**; weaning, toilet training; ego/superego, security; incorporates some Freudian ideas |
| **Childhood** | 6–14 | Few problems; independence from parents; peer identification |
| **Adolescence** | 14–23 | Troublesome; emotional disorders, delinquency; conflicts over independence and sex |
| **Maturity** | 23–50 | Productive; career/marriage/family; less flexible; emotional stability rises |
| **Late Maturity** | 50–65 | Physical/social changes; reexamination of values (like Jung's midlife) |
| **Old Age** | 65+ | Adjusting to losses, retirement, loneliness |

> **MCQ TIP:** Cattell's Adolescence = **14–23** (not 12–18); Infancy (0–6) = major formative period.

## Questions about Human Nature

- Behavior must be **lawful/orderly** for prediction; leans toward **determinism**. No ultimate goal pulls behavior.
- Acknowledged early life but did not see childhood as permanently determining personality.
- **Nature + nurture** (constitutional = nature; environmental-mold = nurture). Moderate on uniqueness (common + unique traits).

---

## Assessment in Cattell's Theory

- **L-data (Life records)**: observers' ratings of overt behaviors in **naturalistic settings** (absences, grades, conscientiousness).
- **Q-data (Questionnaires)**: self-report ratings. Limitations: superficial self-awareness; **deliberate falsification**.
- **T-data (Tests)**: "objective" tests where the person doesn't know what's measured — hard to fake (Rorschach, TAT, word-association). Caveat: most psychologists call these **subjective**.
- **16 PF Test**: 16 source traits, ages **16+**, ~**40 languages**; used for research, clinical diagnosis, predicting job success; variants for children/clinical purposes.

## Research on Cattell's Theory

| Approach | Description | Cattell's View |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Bivariate** | Lab: one IV, one DV | Rigorous but too restrictive |
| **Clinical** | Case studies, dreams, free association | Subjective, "fuzzy" |
| **Multivariate** | Factor analysis (R = group, P = single subject) | **Preferred** |

> **KEY STUDY 1 — Heredity (Cattell, 1982):** N=3,000 males 12–18. Primarily **hereditary**: F, I, Q3. Primarily **environmental**: E, G, Q4.

> **KEY STUDY 2 — Marital Stability (Cattell & Nesselroade, 1967):** **Stable marriages = similar traits; unstable = highly different traits.**

> **KEY STUDY 3 — Faking:** Taken honestly then favorably → significant differences; **16 PF can be faked** (Simon, 2007).

> **KEY STUDY 4 — Translation:** Tshivenda translation invalidated items (Van Eeden & Mantsha, 2007); Greek and Turkish translations were valid.

### Criticisms
- **Subjectivity** at multiple stages: choice of variables, factor-analytic technique, and especially **naming factors**. Other researchers struggle to **replicate** the 16 source traits.
- **Legacy**: the **father of the personality trait approach**; launched genetic-influence research.

## Behavioral Genetics

- **Behavioral genetics**: study of the relationship between genetic factors and personality traits. Allport and Cattell were among the first to argue heredity ranks with environment. Methods: identical (monozygotic) vs. fraternal (dizygotic) twins; adoption studies.

## Hans Eysenck (1916–1997)

- Born in **Berlin**; emigrated to England in **1934** (Hitler). Wanted physics; reluctantly took psychology.
- Published **79 books** and **1,097 articles**; at death the world's **most-cited psychologist** (Farley, 2000). Worked at the **University of London's Maudsley Hospital / Institute of Psychiatry**.
- Tools: **Eysenck Personality Inventory (EPI)**, Eysenck Personality Profiler, Maudsley Medical Questionnaire, **Maudsley Personality Inventory (MPI)**.

### The PEN Model
- **EPI** jointly developed with **Sybil Eysenck** over **12 years** and **20 factor analyses**.

| Dimension | Poles | Core Traits |
|-----------|-------|------------|
| **E — Extraversion** | vs. Introversion | Sociable, lively, assertive, sensation-seeking, dominant |
| **N — Neuroticism** | vs. Emotional Stability | Anxious, depressed, guilty, low self-esteem, tense, moody |
| **P — Psychoticism** | vs. Impulse Control | Aggressive, cold, egocentric, impulsive, antisocial, creative, tough-minded |

> **MCQ TIP:** Eysenck = **PEN model**. E and N recognized since the ancient Greeks; appear on nearly every assessment device. EPI used in ~**40 countries**.

- **Stability**: traits remain stable across the life span (introverted child → introverted adult; Ganiban et al., 2008).
- **Intelligence**: ~**80% inherited** (Eysenck & Eysenck, 1985); not a personality dimension but an important influence.

### Extraversion
- Sociable, impulsive, assertive; experience **more pleasant emotions** and are **happier** than introverts.
- **Cortical arousal theory**: extraverts have **LOWER** baseline cortical arousal → **seek stimulation**; introverts have **HIGHER** arousal → avoid it, are more sensitive to low-level stimuli, and have **lower pain thresholds**. Genetically based.

### Neuroticism
- Anxious, tense, moody, low self-esteem; greater activity in the **sympathetic branch of the autonomic nervous system**, which **overreacts** to mild stressors. Largely **inherited**.

> **Research:** Lower N + higher E → more satisfaction (Scollon & Diener, 2006); high-N workers excel in **fast-paced, stressful** environments (Smillie et al., 2006); high N → lower verbal abilities (Chamorro-Premuzic et al., 2006); high N in midlife → cognitive impairments 25 years later (Crowe et al., 2006).

### Psychoticism
- Aggressive, antisocial, tough-minded, cold; more **alcohol/drug abuse and violent crime** (Boduszek et al., 2013); paradoxically can be **highly creative**.
- Large genetic component; high-P scorers had **more authoritarian/controlling parents** (Heaven & Ciarrochi, 2006).
- **Men score higher** on P; high E, N, and P may predict crime; Chinese research links crime to high P and N (Huo-Liang, 2006).

### Primary Role of Heredity
- All three primarily hereditary (strongest for E and N). **Twin studies**: identical > fraternal in similarity, even reared apart. **Adoption studies**: children resemble **biological** parents more. Three dimensions found in **35+ nations**.

## McCrae & Costa: The Five-Factor Model

- Eysenck had **too few** (3); Cattell **too many** (16). **Robert McCrae** (1949–) and **Paul Costa** (1942–) at the **Gerontology Research Center, NIH (Baltimore)** identified five "**robust**"/**Big Five** factors in the **1980s** — "a turning point" in the field.

| Factor | Descriptors |
|--------|----------------|
| **N — Neuroticism** | Worried, insecure, nervous |
| **E — Extraversion** | Sociable, talkative, affectionate |
| **O — Openness** | Original, creative, daring |
| **A — Agreeableness** | Good-natured, trusting, courteous |
| **C — Conscientiousness** | Careful, reliable, organized |

> **MCQ TIP:** Acronym **OCEAN** / CANOE. Test = **NEO Personality Inventory** (from N, E, O).

### Measuring the Factors
- Confirmed via self-ratings, objective tests, observers' reports. **NEO-PI** is the **most frequently used** Big Five instrument; can be distorted by self-presentation.

### Origin and Overlap
- **Stronger hereditary** component: N, E, O, C. **Agreeableness** has a stronger **environmental** component.
- McCrae-Costa **E and N** overlap with Eysenck's; **A and C** may represent the low end of Eysenck's **Psychoticism**. **Openness** correlates with **intelligence**; **Agreeableness** correlates with **Adler's social interest**.

### Cross-Cultural Consistency
- Found across Eastern and Western cultures — "a common human structure of personality" (McCrae & Costa, 1997); in **50+ nations**.

> **KEY EXCEPTION:** No Big Five in an isolated, illiterate **Bolivian** tribe (Gurven et al., 2013); measured countries tend to be urban, literate, educated.

**Most-valued factor by culture:** Japan = **Conscientiousness**; Hong Kong/India = **Agreeableness**; Singapore = **Emotional Stability**; Venezuela/Australia = **Extraversion** (+ Agreeableness in Australia). Europeans/Americans higher in E and O, lower in A vs. Asians/Africans.

> **KEY STUDY — Schmitt et al. (2008):** 55 countries — **women scored higher** on N, E, A, C; differences greatest in **prosperous, egalitarian** nations.

### Stability and Change
- Found in children and adults. **Costa & McCrae (1988)**: high stability over 6 years; after ~**age 30**, traits become relatively fixed.
- With age: **A and C increase**, **N decreases** — found universally (suggesting a biological program).

### Correlates
- **Emotional**: N → negative affect; E → positive affect; O → emotional intensity.
- **Behavioral**: **C = best single predictor of job performance**; A → prosocial behavior; O → creativity; E → leadership; N → vulnerability to psychopathology.
- **Self vs. others**: we see ourselves as more neurotic and open; we see others as more conscientious (Allik et al., 2010).

## Ashton & Lee: HEXACO — The Six-Factor Model

| Letter | Factor | Descriptors |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| **H** | **Honesty-Humility** | Sincere, honest, modest, fair, greedless |
| **E** | **Emotionality** | Emotional, sentimental, fearful, anxious |
| **X** | **Extraversion** | Outgoing, lively, sociable |
| **A** | **Agreeableness** | Patient, tolerant, mild |
| **C** | **Conscientiousness** | Organized, diligent, careful |
| **O** | **Openness** | Intellectual, creative, unconventional |

> **MCQ TIP:** HEXACO's unique addition = **Honesty-Humility (H)**. Its **Emotionality (E)** differs from Big Five Neuroticism (includes empathy/attachment).

- Cross-linguistic studies (Korean, Dutch, Hungarian) yielded **six factors**, with **Honesty-Humility** stable. Relevant to **ethics, integrity, dark traits**.
- **HEXACO-PI-R**: 60 or 100 items; self- and observer-report. **Low H** predicts manipulation/exploitation — linked to the **Dark Triad**.

## Paulhus & Williams: The Dark Triad

- Proposed by **Delroy Paulhus & Kevin Williams (2002)**: three socially aversive, **subclinical** traits.

| Trait | Characteristics |
|-------|---------------------|
| **Narcissism** | Grandiosity, entitlement, dominance, need for admiration |
| **Machiavellianism** | Manipulation, exploitation, cynicism, strategic self-interest, deception |
| **Psychopathy** | Impulsivity, thrill-seeking, low empathy/anxiety, callousness, antisocial behavior |

> **MCQ TIP:** Coined by **Paulhus & Williams (2002)**; all three **subclinical**, sharing **callousness and low empathy**; all linked to **low Agreeableness** and **low Honesty-Humility**.

### Differences

| Feature | Narcissism | Machiavellianism | Psychopathy |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|------------|
| Core drive | Admiration | Strategic self-interest | Impulsive thrill-seeking |
| Impulsivity | Low | Low (planned) | **High** |
| Long-term planning | Present | **High** | Low |

### Assessment

| Measure | Author(s) | Items |
|---------|----------|-------|
| **Dirty Dozen** | Jonason & Webster (2010) | 12 |
| **Short Dark Triad (SD3)** | Jones & Paulhus (2014) | 27 |
| **Narcissistic Personality Inventory (NPI)** | Raskin & Hall (1979) | 40 |
| **MACH-IV** | Christie & Geis (1970) | 20 |
| **Levenson Self-Report Psychopathy Scale** | Levenson et al. (1995) | 26 |

### Correlates
- All three: short-term mating, more partners, **workplace deviance**, aggression, cheating.
- Narcissism → leadership emergence, overconfidence; Machiavellianism → success in unregulated environments; **Psychopathy → greatest risk for criminal behavior**, substance abuse.
- **Men score higher** on all three (especially psychopathy), cross-culturally.

## Personality Traits and the Internet

- **Extraverts**: more active online, more friends, more self-disclosure. **Introverts**: may compensate via online anonymity. **Neurotics**: heavy users; escape/regulation; more **problematic Internet use**.
- Big Five: **Conscientiousness** negatively predicts problematic use; **Openness** → learning/novelty; **Agreeableness** → prosocial, less cyberbullying; **Narcissism** → self-promoting posts/selfies.

> **KEY STUDY — Trolling (Buckels, Trapnell, & Paulhus, 2014):** online trolling strongly correlated with all three Dark Triad traits, especially **psychopathy** and sadism.

## Reflections on the Trait Approach

### Strengths
1. Rigorous/empirical (factor analysis). 2. Predictive power. 3. Cross-cultural validity. 4. Strong hereditary evidence (twins/adoption). 5. Practical tools (16 PF, NEO-PI, EPI).

### Criticisms
1. **Situationism (Mischel, 1968)** — the person-situation debate. 2. Descriptive, not explanatory. 3. Subjectivity in factor analysis. 4. Cattell's 16 factors not replicated. 5. Oversimplification. 6. Cultural bias (Western, urban, literate; Gurven et al., 2013). 7. Limited developmental account.

### Interactionist Perspective
- Most modern researchers: behavior is determined by **both traits and situations**. Traits predict best in **weak situations**; situations dominate in **strong situations**.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **16 PF**: 16 source traits; ages **16+**; ~**40 languages**; predicts **marital stability** (Cattell & Nesselroade, 1967); can be **faked** (Simon, 2007).

2. **Cattell's three data types**: **L-data** (naturalistic observer ratings), **Q-data** (self-report, falsifiable), **T-data** (faking-resistant).

3. **Heredity split**: ~**1/3 heredity, 2/3 environment** overall; Factors B and H each ~**80% genetic**.

4. **Six stages**: Infancy (0–6, **major formative**), Childhood (6–14), Adolescence (14–23), Maturity (23–50), Late Maturity (50–65), Old Age (65+).

5. **PEN model**: Psychoticism, Extraversion, Neuroticism; E and N known since the Greeks; EPI in ~40 countries.

6. **Cortical arousal theory**: extraverts **lower** arousal → seek stimulation; introverts **higher** → avoid; introverts have **lower pain thresholds**.

7. **Eysenck genetics**: ~**80% of intelligence inherited**; identical > fraternal twins; adopted children resemble biological parents; three dimensions in **35+ nations**.

8. **Big Five (OCEAN)**: McCrae & Costa (1980s); **NEO-PI**; **50+ nations**; NOT found in an illiterate Bolivian tribe (Gurven et al., 2013).

9. **Big Five heredity**: N, E, O, C stronger hereditary; **Agreeableness** stronger environmental.

10. **Gender (Schmitt et al., 2008, 55 countries)**: women higher on N, E, A, C; greatest in prosperous/egalitarian nations.

11. **HEXACO** (Ashton & Lee): six factors; unique = **Honesty-Humility**; HEXACO-PI-R; from cross-linguistic analysis.

12. **Dark Triad** (Paulhus & Williams, 2002): Narcissism, Machiavellianism, Psychopathy; **subclinical**; men higher; **SD3** (27) / **Dirty Dozen** (12).

13. **Conscientiousness** = **best single predictor of job performance**; Japan values it most; Australia values extraversion/agreeableness most.

14. **Trolling and Dark Triad** (Buckels et al., 2014): strongly correlated, especially psychopathy and sadism.

15. **Marital stability** (Cattell & Nesselroade, 1967): stable = similar traits; unstable = highly different traits.
