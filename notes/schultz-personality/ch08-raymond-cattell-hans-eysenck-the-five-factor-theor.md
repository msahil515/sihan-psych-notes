# Ch 8: Raymond Cattell, Hans Eysenck, The Five-Factor Theory, HEXACO, and the Dark Triad

**Chapter Overview:** This chapter covers the genetics/trait approach to personality, tracing factor-analytic methods from Cattell's 16-source-trait model through Eysenck's three-dimension PEN model, the Big Five/NEO model of McCrae and Costa, Ashton and Lee's HEXACO six-factor model, and Paulhus and Williams's Dark Triad. A running theme is the primacy of heredity in shaping personality traits, assessed via twin and adoption studies across cultures.

---

## Predicting Behavior

- Cattell's defining goal: **predict** how a person will behave in response to a given stimulus situation.
- Famous definition: *"Personality is that which permits a prediction of what a person will do in a given situation."* (Cattell, 1950, p. 2)
- Contrast with clinical theorists (Freud, Adler, etc.) who studied disturbed patients seeking change; Cattell studied **normal people** with no intent to treat.
- Believed modifying personality before fully understanding it was unwise.

### A Scientific Approach

- Rigorously empirical: over **50 kinds of measurements** could be taken from a single subject.
- Described as rivaled by no other theory in comprehensiveness and adherence to empirical evidence (Horn, 2001).

### Factor Analysis

- Hallmark of Cattell's method: **factor analysis** — a statistical procedure assessing the correlation between every possible pair of measurements from a group of subjects to identify common underlying factors.
- If two measures show a high correlation they are assumed to measure related aspects and are combined into a single **factor**.
- Example: if anxiety and introversion scales correlate highly, both may measure the same underlying characteristic.

> **KEY TERM — Factor Analysis:** A statistical technique based on correlations between several measures, which may be explained in terms of underlying factors.

### Personality Traits

- The factors derived via factor analysis are called **traits**: the mental elements of personality.
- Only by knowing a person's traits can we predict behavior in a given situation.

## The Life of Cattell (1905–1998)

### An Older Brother and Wounded Soldiers

- Born in **Staffordshire, England**; high-standards but permissive parents.
- Age **9**: England entered World War I; nearby mansion became a hospital, trainloads of wounded soldiers made him unusually serious — awareness of the "brevity of life and the need to accomplish while one might."
- Lifelong work intensity and fierce competitiveness with an older brother.

### Psychology: A Bad Choice

- Age 16: enrolled at **University of London** for physics and chemistry; graduated with honours in 3 years.
- Decided social problems required study of the human mind; chose psychology in **1924** — only six academic professorships in psychology existed in all of England.
- Graduate studies under **Charles E. Spearman** (developer of factor analysis for mental abilities).
- Ph.D. awarded **1929** from the University of London.
- Resolved to apply factor analysis to the **structure of personality** (Spearman had used it only for mental abilities).

### A Time of Hardship

- Post-doctorate: few jobs, chronic digestive disorders, wife left him.
- Forced focus on practical problems: "Those years made me as canny and distrustful as a squirrel who has known a long winter."

### Success in America

- **Edward L. Thorndike** invited Cattell to Columbia University.
- Professorship at **Clark University** (Worcester, MA), then **Harvard** (1941) — colleagues included **Gordon Allport** and **William Sheldon**.
- Age 40: settled at **University of Illinois** as research professor.
- Published **500+ articles** and **43 books**.
- Later joined graduate faculty at **University of Hawaii**.
- Died in Honolulu, age **92** (1998), one year after receiving the **Gold Medal Award for Life Achievement in Psychological Science** from APA (1997).

## Cattell's Approach to Personality Traits

- **Traits** (Cattell): *relatively permanent reaction tendencies that are the basic structural units of the personality*, derived by factor analysis.
- Classified traits in multiple ways.

### Common Traits and Unique Traits

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Common traits** | Possessed by everyone to some degree; universal due to similar heredity and social pressures within a culture | Intelligence, extraversion, gregariousness |
| **Unique traits** | Aspects shared by few; especially evident in interests and attitudes | Interest in genealogy, Civil War battles, Chinese martial arts |

### Ability, Temperament, and Dynamic Traits

| Type | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Ability traits** | How efficiently one works toward a goal | Intelligence |
| **Temperament traits** | General behavioral style and emotional tone | Assertiveness, easygoingness, irritability |
| **Dynamic traits** | Driving forces; motivations, interests, ambitions | Ergs, sentiments |

### Surface Traits and Source Traits

- **Surface traits**: correlate with one another but do NOT constitute a single factor because not determined by a single source.
  - Example: anxiety + indecision + irrational fear combine to form the surface trait "neuroticism."
  - Less stable, less permanent, less important for describing personality.
- **Source traits**: unitary, stable, permanent personality factors derived via factor analysis; each gives rise to some aspect of behavior.
  - Analogous to atoms in the physical world — the true building blocks.

### Constitutional Traits and Environmental-Mold Traits

Source traits classified by origin:

| Type | Origin | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Constitutional traits** | Biological/physiological conditions (not necessarily innate) | Talkativeness, slurred speech from alcohol |
| **Environmental-mold traits** | Social and physical environments; learned characteristics | Inner-city vs. upper-class upbringing patterns; military officer vs. jazz musician |

## Source Traits: The Basic Factors of Personality

- After 20+ years of factor-analytic research, Cattell (1965) identified **16 source traits** as the basic factors of personality.
- Assessed by the **Sixteen Personality Factor (16 PF) Questionnaire**.

### Cattell's 16 Source Traits

| Factor | Low Scorers | High Scorers |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| **A** | Reserved, aloof, detached | Outgoing, warmhearted, easygoing |
| **B** | Low in intelligence | High in intelligence |
| **C** | Low ego strength, easily upset, emotionally unstable | High ego strength, calm, emotionally stable |
| **E** | Submissive, obedient, docile, meek | Dominant, assertive, forceful |
| **F** | Serious, sober, depressed, worrying | Happy-go-lucky, enthusiastic, cheerful |
| **G** | Expedient, low in superego | Conscientious, high in superego |
| **H** | Timid, shy, aloof, restrained | Bold, adventurous |
| **I** | Tough-minded, self-reliant, demanding | Tender-minded, sensitive, dependent |
| **L** | Trusting, understanding, accepting | Suspicious, jealous, withdrawn |
| **M** | Practical, down-to-earth, detail-oriented | Imaginative, absentminded |
| **N** | Forthright, naive, unpretentious | Shrewd, worldly, insightful |
| **O** | Self-assured, secure, complacent | Apprehensive, insecure, self-reproaching |
| **Q1** | Conservative, holds traditional values, dislikes change | Radical, liberal, experimenting, embraces change |
| **Q2** | Group-dependent, prefers to follow others | Self-sufficient, resourceful, independent |
| **Q3** | Uncontrolled, lax, impulsive | Controlled, compulsive, exacting |
| **Q4** | Relaxed, tranquil, composed | Tense, driven, fretful |

> **MCQ TIP:** Factors **Q1–Q4** emerged only from questionnaire (Q-data). **Factor B = Intelligence.** The test is for ages **16+**, translated into ~**40 languages**.

- Additional **temperament traits** identified later: excitability, zest, self-discipline, politeness, self-assurance (Cattell, 1973; Cattell & Kline, 1977).

## Dynamic Traits: The Motivating Forces

- **Dynamic traits** concern **motivation** — the driving forces of behavior.
- A personality theory without dynamic forces is like describing an engine without mentioning fuel.

### Ergs

- **Ergs** (from Greek *ergon* = energy): constitutional dynamic source traits; innate, biologically based drives.
- Examples: sex, gregariousness, parental protectiveness, curiosity, fear, self-assertion, narcissism, pugnacity, acquisitiveness, hunger, appeal, rest.

### Sentiments

- **Sentiments**: environmental-mold dynamic source traits; learned attitudes and interests formed through culture and social experience.
- Examples: career sentiment, religious sentiment, self-sentiment.
- Most important: the **self-sentiment** — organizes and integrates all other attitudes and sentiments.

### Attitudes

- **Attitudes**: specific manifestations of ergs and sentiments; interest in and a course of action toward an object or situation.
- Most surface-level dynamic structure.

### The Dynamic Lattice (Subsidiation Chain)

- Cattell organized dynamic traits into a **dynamic lattice**:
  - Attitudes subsidiate (feed into) sentiments; sentiments subsidiate ergs.
  - Top level: ergs (innate drives); mid level: sentiments (learned); bottom level: specific attitudes.

## The Influences of Heredity and Environment

- Cattell investigated heredity vs. environment by comparing:
  - Twins reared together vs. apart.
  - Non-twin siblings reared together vs. apart.

### Key Findings

- **Factor B (Intelligence)**: ~**80%** genetically determined.
- **Factor H (Timidity vs. Boldness)**: ~**80%** genetically determined.
- Overall: **one-third of personality is genetically based; two-thirds determined by social and environmental influences**.

## Stages of Personality Development

Cattell proposed **six stages** spanning the entire life span:

| Stage | Age | Key Features |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| **Infancy** | Birth to 6 | Major formative period; weaning, toilet training; formation of ego, superego, social attitudes; feelings of security/insecurity; attitudes toward authority; possible tendency to neuroticism. Cattell incorporated some Freudian ideas (oral/anal conflicts matter). |
| **Childhood** | 6–14 | Few psychological problems; beginning independence from parents; increasing identification with peers |
| **Adolescence** | 14–23 | Troublesome and stressful; emotional disorders and delinquency possible; conflicts about independence, self-assertion, and sex |
| **Maturity** | 23–50 | Productive and satisfying; career, marriage, family; personality less flexible; emotional stability increases; little change in interests and attitudes |
| **Late Maturity** | 50–65 | Physical, social, and psychological changes; health/vigor decline; reexamination of values; search for new self (similar to Jung's midlife period) |
| **Old Age** | 65+ | Adjustments to losses: death of friends/relatives, retirement, loss of status in youth-worshipping culture, loneliness and insecurity |

> **MCQ TIP:** Adolescence in Cattell = **14–23** (not 12–18). Infancy (0–6) = **major formative period**. Maturity = 23–50.

## Questions about Human Nature

- Behavior must be **lawful and orderly** for prediction to work — little room for spontaneity.
- **Free will vs. determinism**: leans toward **determinism**.
- No ultimate goal drives behavior (no self-actualization, no psychosexual conflicts pulling/pushing behavior).
- Acknowledged early life events but did NOT believe childhood forces permanently determine personality.
- **Nature vs. nurture**: accepted both (constitutional traits = nature; environmental-mold traits = nurture).
- **Uniqueness vs. universality**: moderate — common traits apply to everyone; unique traits describe the individual.
- Personal view evolved from early optimism about solving social problems to later belief that human nature and society had regressed.

## Assessment in Cattell's Theory

Three primary techniques:

### Life Records (L-data)

- **L-data**: observers' ratings of specific behaviors in real-life naturalistic settings (classroom, office).
- Examples: frequency of work absences, grades, job conscientiousness, emotional stability, sociability.
- Key: involves **overt behaviors observable in naturalistic settings** (not lab situations).

### Questionnaires (Q-data)

- **Q-data**: self-report ratings (subjects rate themselves on characteristics, attitudes, interests).
- Limitations:
  1. Superficial self-awareness may yield inaccurate answers.
  2. Subjects may **deliberately falsify** responses to appear favorable.
- Cattell warned Q-data must not automatically be assumed accurate.

### Personality Tests (T-data)

- **T-data**: "objective" tests where person responds without knowing what aspect is being evaluated — hard to fake.
- Examples Cattell used: Rorschach, Thematic Apperception Test (TAT), word-association test.
- **Caveat**: Most psychologists call these **subjective** tests due to scoring/interpretation biases — Cattell's label "objective" is misleading to most psychologists.

### The 16 PF Test

- Assesses **16 major source traits**; intended for ages **16+**.
- Scores 16 scales objectively; computerized scoring available.
- Translated into ~**40 languages**.
- Uses: research, clinical diagnosis, predicting job success.
- Variations exist for children, adolescents, and specific clinical purposes (anxiety, depression, neuroticism; marriage counseling; executive performance evaluation).

## Research on Cattell's Theory

### Three Approaches Cattell Described

| Approach | Description | Cattell's Evaluation |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Bivariate** | Standard lab: manipulate IV, measure DV; one variable at a time (also called univariate) | Scientific and rigorous but too restrictive; cannot replicate significant emotional experiences |
| **Clinical** | Case studies, dream analysis, free association | Highly subjective; yields no verifiable/quantifiable data; "fuzzy" |
| **Multivariate** | Factor analysis (Cattell's choice); R-technique (group) and P-technique (single subject) | Preferred — yields specific, comprehensive data |

### Key Research Findings

> **KEY STUDY 1 — Heredity of Source Traits (Cattell, 1982):**
> N = 3,000 males ages 12–18; 16 PF Test.
> Primarily **hereditary**: Factor F (serious vs. happy-go-lucky), Factor I (tough-minded vs. tender-minded), Factor Q3 (uncontrolled vs. controlled).
> Primarily **environmental**: Factor E (submissive vs. dominant), Factor G (expedient vs. conscientious), Factor Q4 (relaxed vs. tense).

> **KEY STUDY 2 — Marital Stability (Cattell & Nesselroade, 1967):**
> Married couples classified as stable (no dissolution steps taken) vs. unstable.
> Finding: Marital stability predicted by 16 PF scores. **Stable marriages = similar personality traits; unstable marriages = highly different traits.**

> **KEY STUDY 3 — Faking the 16 PF:**
> College students, prisoners, substance abusers took 16 PF twice: honestly, then favorably.
> Finding: Significant differences between sets — **the 16 PF can be faked** (Simon, 2007).

> **KEY STUDY 4 — Cultural Translation:**
> South Africa: Tshivenda translation changed item meanings so much responses were invalid (Van Eeden & Mantsha, 2007). Greek and Turkish translations proved valid.

### Summary of 16 PF Research Highlights

- Can **predict marital stability**.
- Can be **faked** to present oneself favorably.
- Usable in many cultures; **literal translation sometimes impossible**.
- Some source traits are primarily **inherited**; others primarily **environmental**.
- Used for research, clinical diagnosis, and **predicting job success**.

## Reflections on Cattell's Theory

### Criticisms

- **Subjectivity** may enter at multiple stages:
  1. Choice of tests/variables to measure.
  2. Choice of factor-analytic technique and significance level.
  3. **Naming/labeling of factors** (ambiguous names may misrepresent the factor's nature).
- Other researchers have difficulty **replicating** Cattell's 16 basic source traits.

### Legacy

- Considered the **father of the personality trait approach** and one of the most influential 20th-century psychologists.
- The trait approach and investigation of genetic influences he initiated continue to fascinate contemporary researchers.

---

## Behavioral Genetics

- **Behavioral genetics**: the study of the relationship between genetic/hereditary factors and personality traits.
- Allport and Cattell were among the first to argue inherited factors rank in importance with environmental factors.
- Methods: identical (monozygotic) vs. fraternal (dizygotic) twin comparisons; adoption studies.

## Hans Eysenck (1916–1997)

### Resigned to Psychology

- Born in **Berlin**; immigrated to England in **1934** after Hitler came to power.
- Wanted to study physics at University of London; lacked the required background.
- Enrolled in psychology reluctantly: "I was told there was always psychology. 'What on earth is that?'..." (1980, p. 156).
- When asked if he ever regretted his career choice: "Often," but said he was resigned to it.

### A Prolific Record

- Published **79 books** (including popular science books) and **1,097 journal articles**.
- At time of death: the world's **most frequently cited psychologist** (Farley, 2000).
- Wife destroyed all personal and professional papers after his death (Harris, 2011).
- Career spent primarily at the **University of London's Maudsley Hospital and Institute of Psychiatry**.
- Assessment tools developed:
  - **Eysenck Personality Inventory (EPI)**
  - **Eysenck Personality Profiler (EPP)**
  - **Maudsley Medical Questionnaire**
  - **Maudsley Personality Inventory (MPI)**

---

## The Dimensions of Personality

### A Joint Effort

- **EPI** jointly developed by Hans and **Sybil Eysenck** (Ph.D., University of London).
- Required **12 years** of joint research and **20 factor analyses** (Eysenck & Eysenck, 1963).
- Hans credited it largely to Sybil's "skill, patience, and endurance."

### Three Dimensions of Personality (PEN Model)

| Dimension | Poles | Core Traits |
|-----------|-------|------------|
| **E — Extraversion** | Extraversion vs. Introversion | Sociable, lively, active, assertive, sensation-seeking, carefree, dominant, venturesome |
| **N — Neuroticism** | Neuroticism vs. Emotional Stability | Anxious, depressed, guilt feelings, low self-esteem, tense, irrational, shy, moody |
| **P — Psychoticism** | Psychoticism vs. Impulse Control (superego functioning) | Aggressive, cold, egocentric, impersonal, impulsive, antisocial, creative, tough-minded |

> **MCQ TIP:** Eysenck's model = **PEN model**. E and N have been recognized as basic personality elements since ancient Greek philosophers. Eysenck (1997) noted these dimensions appear on nearly every personality assessment device ever developed. EPI used successfully in translated forms in ~**40 countries**.

### Stability over Time

- Traits and dimensions remain **stable throughout the life span** despite changing social/environmental experiences.
- Introverted child tends to remain introverted through adolescence and adulthood (Ganiban et al., 2008).
- Studies in England and Scandinavia confirm stability of E and N (Billstedt et al., 2014; Gale et al., 2013).

### The Role of Intelligence

- Intelligence not listed as a personality dimension but considered an important influence.
- ~**80% of intelligence inherited**; only 20% from social/environmental forces (Eysenck & Eysenck, 1985).
- IQ 120 person likely has more complex, multidimensional personality than IQ 80 person.

## Extraversion

### Characteristics

- Oriented toward the outside world; prefer company of others.
- Sociable, impulsive, adventurous, assertive, dominant.
- Experience **more pleasant emotions** and are **happier** than introverts (Fisher & Francis, 2013; Holder & Klassen, 2010; Lucas & Fujita, 2000).
- Extraverted businessmen perform better on difficult tasks than introverted ones (Campbell et al., 2011).

### Biological Basis — Cortical Arousal Theory

- **Extraverts have a LOWER baseline cortical arousal** than introverts.
- Low arousal → actively **seek** excitement and stimulation.
- **Introverts have HIGHER cortical arousal** → shy away from additional stimulation.
- Introverts show **greater sensitivity to low-level stimuli** and have **lower pain thresholds**.
- These differences are **genetically based**.

---

## Neuroticism

### Characteristics

- Anxious, depressed, tense, irrational, moody; low self-esteem; prone to guilt feelings.
- Greater activity in brain areas controlling the **sympathetic branch of the autonomic nervous system**.
- Sympathetic nervous system **overreacts** even to mild stressors → chronic hypersensitivity → heightened emotionality.
- Neuroticism is largely **inherited** per Eysenck.

### Key Research

> **Scollon & Diener (2006):** 2-year US study (ages 16–70). Satisfaction from work and social relationships associated with **lower N and higher E**.

> **Smillie et al. (2006):** Australia. High-N workers **outperformed** low-N workers in **fast-paced, stressful environments**.

> **Chamorro-Premuzic et al. (2006):** England. High N associated with **lower verbal abilities**.

> **Crowe et al. (2006):** Sweden. High N in middle age predicted **cognitive impairments 25 years later**.

---

## Psychoticism

### Characteristics

- Aggressive, antisocial, tough-minded, cold, egocentric.
- Cruel, hostile, insensitive to others; lower emotional well-being.
- Greater problems with **alcohol, drug abuse, and violent criminal behavior** (Boduszek et al., 2013).
- Paradoxically, can be **highly creative**.

### Heredity and Environment

- Large genetic component; also: high-P scorers had **more authoritarian and controlling parents** (Heaven & Ciarrochi, 2006).

### Gender and Crime

- **Men score higher** on P than women — possible link to male hormones.
- High scores on all three (E, N, P) may predict criminal behavior (modest support; Eysenck & Gudjonsson, 1989).
- Chinese research: significant positive correlation between criminal behavior and **high P and high N** (Huo-Liang, 2006).

---

## The Primary Role of Heredity

- All three dimensions primarily hereditary; genetic component strongest for **E and N** vs. P.
- **Twin studies**: identical twins more alike in personality than fraternal twins, even reared apart.
- **Adoption studies**: adopted children's personalities more closely resemble **biological** than adoptive parents even without contact.
- **Cross-cultural evidence**: three dimensions found consistently in **35+ nations** (USA, England, Australia, Japan, China, Nigeria, Sweden; Bouchard, 1985; Eaves et al., 1989; Tellegen et al., 1988; Hur, 2009).

## Robert McCrae and Paul Costa: The Five-Factor Model

### Background

- Some felt **Eysenck had too few dimensions (3)** and **Cattell had too many factors (16)**.
- **Robert McCrae** (1949–) and **Paul Costa** (1942–) at the **Gerontology Research Center, National Institutes of Health (NIH), Baltimore, Maryland**.
- Research beginning in the **1980s** identified five "**robust**" or **Big Five** factors (McCrae & Costa, 1985b, 1987).
- Described as "a turning point in the history of personality psychology" (McCrae, 2011, p. 210).

### The Five Factors (OCEAN)

| Factor | Key Descriptors |
|--------|----------------|
| **N — Neuroticism** | Worried, insecure, nervous, highly strung |
| **E — Extraversion** | Sociable, talkative, fun-loving, affectionate |
| **O — Openness** | Original, independent, creative, daring |
| **A — Agreeableness** | Good-natured, softhearted, trusting, courteous |
| **C — Conscientiousness** | Careful, reliable, hardworking, organized |

> **MCQ TIP:** Acronym = **OCEAN** or **CANOE**. The personality test = **NEO Personality Inventory** (named from Neuroticism, Extraversion, Openness).

### Measuring the Five Factors

- Confirmed through self-ratings, objective tests, and observers' reports.
- Test: **NEO Personality Inventory (NEO-PI)** and its revised forms.
- Shorter version for Internet research (Buchanan, Johnson, & Goldberg, 2005).
- Adjective checklists: one with **100 adjectives**, another with **40 adjectives**.
- **NEO remains the most frequently used** Big Five instrument.
- Results can be **distorted** by subjects wanting to appear well-adjusted.

### Origin and Overlap of Factors

- **Four factors with stronger hereditary component**: N, E, O, C.
- **Agreeableness** has stronger **environmental** component (Bergeman et al., 1993; Pedersen et al., 1988).
- McCrae-Costa **E and N** overlap with Eysenck's E and N.
- McCrae-Costa **Agreeableness and Conscientiousness** may represent the low end of Eysenck's **Psychoticism** (impulse control) dimension.
- **Openness** shows high positive correlation with **intelligence**.
- **Agreeableness** correlates with **Adler's concept of social interest**.

### Cross-Cultural Consistency

- Five factors consistently observed in **Eastern and Western cultures** — supports genetic component.
- Represent "a **common human structure of personality** that transcends cultural differences" (McCrae & Costa, 1997, p. 515).
- Found in **50+ nations** including UK, Germany, Portugal, Czech Republic, Turkey, Israel, China, Korea, Japan, France, Philippines, Russia, India, Denmark, Italy, Lebanon, Canada, Romania, USA.

> **KEY EXCEPTION:** No evidence of Big Five in an **isolated, largely illiterate tribal group in Bolivia** (Gurven et al., 2013). Countries where Big Five measured tend to be **urban, literate, well-educated** societies.

### Relative Value of Factors Across Cultures

| Culture | Most Valued Factor(s) |
|---------|----------------------|
| Australia | Extraversion and Agreeableness |
| **Japan** | **Conscientiousness** |
| Hong Kong, India | **Agreeableness** |
| Singapore | **Emotional Stability** (low Neuroticism) |
| Venezuela | **Extraversion** |
| Chile, Finland, Germany, Netherlands, Norway, Turkey, USA | No single factor dominant |
| Europeans/Americans vs. Asians/Africans | Higher E and O; lower A |

### Gender Differences

> **KEY STUDY — Schmitt et al. (2008):** 55 countries.
> Finding: **Women scored higher** than men on N, E, A, and C.
> Differences most pronounced in **prosperous and egalitarian nations** where women had greater education/employment opportunity.

### How People View Themselves and Others

- People see themselves as **more neurotic and more open** to experience than others see them.
- People see **others as higher in conscientiousness** than themselves (Allik et al., 2010).

### Stability of Factors over Time

- Five factors found in **children and adults**.
- **Longitudinal study (Costa & McCrae, 1988):** Same people over 6 years — **high stability** for all five. High-A children remained high-A as adults.
- Finnish twin study (ages 18–59): high stability for E and N across men and women.
- After approximately **age 30**, personality traits become relatively fixed ("plaster set").

### Predicting Changes over Time

- Young people: higher N, lower A and C than older people.
- **Agreeableness and Conscientiousness increase with age**; **Neuroticism decreases with age**.
- Changes found **universally** across cultures — suggests biologically programmed developmental process.

### Emotional Correlates

- **N** → negative affect (anxiety, depression, anger).
- **E** → positive affect (joy, enthusiasm).
- **O** → emotional intensity (open people experience emotions more intensely, both positive and negative).

### Behavioral Correlates

- **C** = best single predictor of **job performance** across occupations.
- **A** predicts prosocial behavior.
- **O** predicts creativity and interest in novel experiences.
- **E** predicts leadership emergence and social dominance.
- **N** predicts vulnerability to psychopathology.

## Michael Ashton and Kibeom Lee: HEXACO — The Six-Factor Model

- Proposed by **Michael Ashton** and **Kibeom Lee**.
- **HEXACO**: extends/refines the Big Five by adding a sixth factor.

### The Six HEXACO Factors

| Letter | Factor | Key Descriptors |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| **H** | **Honesty-Humility** | Sincere, honest, faithful, modest, fair-minded, greedless |
| **E** | **Emotionality** | Emotional, oversensitive, sentimental, fearful, anxious |
| **X** | **Extraversion** | Outgoing, lively, cheerful, sociable, talkative |
| **A** | **Agreeableness** | Patient, tolerant, peaceful, mild, agreeable |
| **C** | **Conscientiousness** | Organized, diligent, perfectionistic, thorough, careful |
| **O** | **Openness to Experience** | Intellectual, creative, unconventional, innovative |

> **MCQ TIP:** The unique addition in HEXACO vs. Big Five is **Honesty-Humility (H)**. HEXACO's **Emotionality (E)** differs from Big Five Neuroticism — it includes empathy/attachment components, not just emotional instability.

### Why Six Factors?

- Cross-linguistic personality studies in many non-English/non-European languages (Korean, Dutch, Hungarian, etc.) consistently produced **six factors, not five**.
- Big Five's Agreeableness and Neuroticism reorganized across languages, with **Honesty-Humility** emerging as a stable sixth factor.
- HEXACO particularly relevant for understanding **ethical behavior, integrity, and dark personality traits**.

### Assessing the Six Factors

- **HEXACO Personality Inventory-Revised (HEXACO-PI-R)**: primary assessment tool; 60 or 100 items; self-report and observer-report versions.
- Validated across many cultures and languages.
- **Low H (Honesty-Humility)** predicts manipulation, greediness, exploitation — directly related to **Dark Triad traits**.

## Delroy Paulhus and Kevin Williams: The Dark Triad of Personality

- Proposed by **Delroy Paulhus** and **Kevin Williams** (**2002**).
- **The Dark Triad**: a cluster of three socially aversive but **subclinical** (non-pathological) personality traits sharing overlapping features.

### The Three Components

| Trait | Core Characteristics |
|-------|---------------------|
| **Narcissism** | Grandiosity, entitlement, dominance, superiority, self-focus, need for admiration |
| **Machiavellianism** | Manipulation, exploitation of others, cynical worldview, strategic self-interest, lack of morality, deception |
| **Psychopathy** | Impulsivity, thrill-seeking, low empathy and anxiety, callousness, antisocial behavior |

> **MCQ TIP:** Dark Triad coined by **Paulhus & Williams (2002)**. All three are **subclinical** — they exist on a continuum in the general population, not just in psychiatric patients. All three share **callousness** and **low empathy**.

### Shared Features

- All three involve callousness and lack of empathy.
- All three associated with interpersonal manipulation and social exploitation.
- They co-occur — high scores on one predict higher scores on others.
- All three associated with **low Agreeableness** (Big Five) and **low Honesty-Humility** (HEXACO).

### Differences Among the Three

| Feature | Narcissism | Machiavellianism | Psychopathy |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|------------|
| Core drive | Self-aggrandizement, admiration | Strategic self-interest | Impulsive thrill-seeking |
| Empathy | Low but not absent | Strategically withheld | Constitutionally absent |
| Impulsivity | Low | Low (strategic/planned) | **High** |
| Long-term planning | Present | **High** (calculated) | Low |
| Emotional tone | Positive self-view | Cold, cynical | Flat, callous |

### Assessing the Dark Triad

| Measure | Author(s) | Items | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|-------|
| **Dirty Dozen** | Jonason & Webster (2010) | 12 (4 per trait) | Rapid screening measure |
| **Short Dark Triad (SD3)** | Jones & Paulhus (2014) | 27 (9 per trait) | More valid and reliable |
| **Narcissistic Personality Inventory (NPI)** | Raskin & Hall (1979) | 40 | Gold standard for narcissism |
| **MACH-IV** | Christie & Geis (1970) | 20 | Standard Machiavellianism scale |
| **Levenson Self-Report Psychopathy Scale** | Levenson et al. (1995) | 26 | Subclinical psychopathy |

### Behavioral Correlates

- All three traits associated with:
  - **Short-term mating strategies**; more sexual partners.
  - **Workplace deviance**: counterproductive work behavior, bullying, harassment.
  - **Aggressive behavior** and interpersonal conflicts.
  - **Deceit and cheating** in academic/professional settings.
- **Narcissism** specifically:
  - Leadership emergence (initially perceived as confident).
  - Some creativity; overconfidence and inflated self-assessment.
- **Machiavellianism** specifically:
  - Success in competitive, unregulated environments.
  - Greater use of ingratiation and impression management.
- **Psychopathy** specifically:
  - Greatest risk for **criminal behavior**.
  - Substance abuse; thrill-seeking; risky behavior.

### Gender

- **Men score significantly higher** than women on all three Dark Triad traits, especially psychopathy.
- These gender differences are cross-cultural.

## Personality Traits and the Internet

### Eysenck's Dimensions and Online Behavior

- **Extraverts**: more active on social networking sites; more online friends; more self-disclosure.
- **Introverts**: may use Internet to compensate for face-to-face deficits; feel more comfortable expressing themselves online due to anonymity.
- **Neurotics**: heavy Internet users; use for escape and emotional regulation; more likely to develop **problematic Internet use / Internet addiction**; compulsive checking of email and social media.

### Big Five and Online Behavior

- **Conscientiousness** negatively predicts problematic Internet use.
- **Openness** positively predicts Internet use for learning, creativity, and seeking novelty.
- **Agreeableness** associated with prosocial online behavior and less cyberbullying.
- **Extraversion** associated with larger social network size and more frequent posting.
- **Narcissism**: frequent self-promoting posts, selfies, seeking validation through likes/comments.

### Dark Triad and Online Behavior

- High **narcissism**: heavy selfie-posting, frequent status updates, seeking online validation.
- High **psychopathy and Machiavellianism**: associated with **trolling** (deliberate online provocation and harassment).

> **KEY STUDY — Trolling and Dark Triad (Buckels, Trapnell, & Paulhus, 2014):**
> Strong positive correlation between **online trolling** and all three Dark Triad traits, especially **psychopathy** and sadism.

## Reflections on the Trait Approach

### Strengths

1. **Rigorous and empirical**: grounded in factor analysis and data.
2. **Predictive power**: stable traits allow behavioral prediction across situations.
3. **Cross-cultural validity**: Big Five and PEN model found across diverse cultures.
4. **Hereditary evidence**: twin and adoption studies strongly support genetic basis.
5. **Practical applications**: 16 PF, NEO-PI, EPI widely used in clinical, organizational, and research settings.

### Criticisms

1. **Situationism (Mischel, 1968)**: behavior more influenced by situations than stable traits — the **person-situation debate**.
2. **Descriptive, not explanatory**: traits label behavior but do not explain its cause.
3. **Subjectivity in factor analysis**: researcher judgment at multiple stages.
4. **Replication problems**: Cattell's 16 factors not consistently replicated.
5. **Oversimplification**: reducing personality to a handful of factors may miss individual richness.
6. **Cultural bias**: Big Five developed in Western, urban, literate cultures; not applicable to isolated or illiterate groups (Gurven et al., 2013).
7. **Limited developmental account**: does not fully explain how traits develop or change.

### The Interactionist Perspective

- Most modern researchers: **interactionist** position — behavior determined by both **traits** (person variables) AND **situations**.
- Traits predict behavior best in **weak situations** (ambiguous, few social demands); situations dominate in **strong situations** (clear social norms and demands).

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Factor analysis** | Statistical technique assessing correlations between measures to identify underlying common factors |
| **Traits** (Cattell) | Relatively permanent reaction tendencies that are the basic structural units of personality, derived via factor analysis |
| **Common traits** | Traits possessed in some degree by all persons within a culture |
| **Unique traits** | Traits possessed by one or a few persons; evident in interests and attitudes |
| **Ability traits** | Traits describing skills and how efficiently one works toward goals |
| **Temperament traits** | Traits describing general behavioral style in responding to the environment |
| **Dynamic traits** | Traits describing motivations, interests, and ambitions — the driving forces |
| **Surface traits** | Characteristics that correlate but do not form a single factor; composed of multiple elements; less stable |
| **Source traits** | Stable, permanent, unitary personality factors derived by factor analysis; basic elements of personality |
| **Constitutional traits** | Source traits with biological/physiological origins |
| **Environmental-mold traits** | Source traits learned from social and environmental interactions |
| **16 PF Test** | Cattell's Sixteen Personality Factor Questionnaire measuring 16 major source traits; for ages 16+ |
| **L-data** | Life-record data: observer ratings of overt behaviors in naturalistic settings |
| **Q-data** | Self-report questionnaire ratings of characteristics, attitudes, and interests |
| **T-data** | Data from personality tests resistant to faking (subjects do not know what is being measured) |
| **Bivariate approach** | Standard lab experimental method studying one IV and one DV at a time (also called univariate) |
| **R technique** | Factor analysis of large amounts of data collected from a group of people |
| **P technique** | Factor analysis of large amounts of data collected from a single person over time |
| **Multivariate approach** | Cattell's preferred method; uses factor analysis to study multiple variables simultaneously |
| **Ergs** | Constitutional dynamic source traits; innate, biologically based drives (sex, fear, curiosity, etc.) |
| **Sentiments** | Environmental-mold dynamic source traits; learned attitudes formed through culture and social experience |
| **Self-sentiment** | The most important sentiment; organizes and integrates all other attitudes and sentiments |
| **Dynamic lattice** | Cattell's hierarchical organization: specific attitudes subsidiate sentiments, which subsidiate ergs |
| **Subsidiation** | Cattell's term for the way attitudes feed into sentiments, which feed into ergs |
| **Behavioral genetics** | Study of the relationship between genetic/hereditary factors and personality traits |
| **PEN model** | Eysenck's three-dimension model: Psychoticism, Extraversion, Neuroticism |
| **Eysenck Personality Inventory (EPI)** | Eysenck's questionnaire measuring E, N, and P; developed over 12 years with wife Sybil |
| **Cortical arousal theory** | Eysenck's explanation: extraverts have lower baseline cortical arousal and seek stimulation; introverts have higher arousal and avoid it |
| **Maudsley Personality Inventory (MPI)** | Earlier Eysenck personality questionnaire measuring E and N |
| **Big Five / Five-Factor Model** | McCrae and Costa's five robust personality factors: OCEAN |
| **NEO Personality Inventory** | McCrae and Costa's test measuring the Big Five (named from N, E, O) |
| **OCEAN** | Acronym for Big Five: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism |
| **HEXACO** | Ashton and Lee's six-factor model adding Honesty-Humility to the Big Five structure |
| **Honesty-Humility** | HEXACO's unique sixth factor: sincerity, fairness, modesty, greedlessness |
| **HEXACO-PI-R** | HEXACO Personality Inventory-Revised; 60 or 100 items; self-report and observer-report versions |
| **Dark Triad** | Paulhus and Williams's (2002) constellation: Narcissism, Machiavellianism, Psychopathy |
| **Narcissism** | Dark Triad trait: grandiosity, entitlement, dominance, need for admiration |
| **Machiavellianism** | Dark Triad trait: manipulation, exploitation, cynicism, strategic self-interest |
| **Psychopathy** (subclinical) | Dark Triad trait: impulsivity, callousness, low empathy, antisocial behavior |
| **Short Dark Triad (SD3)** | 27-item measure of Dark Triad (Jones & Paulhus, 2014) |
| **Dirty Dozen** | 12-item rapid Dark Triad measure (Jonason & Webster, 2010) |
| **Person-situation debate** | Mischel's (1968) challenge to trait theory arguing situations determine behavior more than stable traits |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Cattell's 16 PF Test** assesses 16 source traits; used with people **16+ years**; translated into ~**40 languages**; can predict **marital stability** (Cattell & Nesselroade, 1967); can be **faked** (Simon, 2007); used for research, clinical diagnosis, predicting job success.

2. **Cattell's three assessment data types**: **L-data** (observer ratings in naturalistic settings), **Q-data** (self-report questionnaires; can be falsified), **T-data** (tests resistant to faking; subjects unaware what is being measured).

3. **Cattell's heredity-environment split**: approximately **one-third heredity, two-thirds environment** for personality overall. Intelligence (Factor B) and timidity/boldness (Factor H) are each ~**80% genetic**.

4. **Cattell's six developmental stages**: Infancy (0–6, **major formative period**), Childhood (6–14), Adolescence (14–23, most troublesome), Maturity (23–50), Late Maturity (50–65, similar to Jung's midlife), Old Age (65+).

5. **Eysenck's PEN model**: **Psychoticism, Extraversion, Neuroticism** — three superfactors. E and N recognized since ancient Greek philosophers. EPI used in ~40 countries. Model also called **three-dimensions theory**.

6. **Cortical arousal theory** (Eysenck): Extraverts = **lower** baseline cortical arousal → **seek stimulation**. Introverts = **higher** baseline arousal → **avoid stimulation**. Introverts have **lower pain thresholds** and greater sensitivity to low-level stimuli.

7. **Eysenck's genetics**: ~**80% of intelligence inherited**. Identical twins more alike in personality than fraternal twins even reared apart. Adopted children resemble **biological** parents more than adoptive parents. Three dimensions found in **35+ nations**.

8. **Big Five (OCEAN)**: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism. Proposed by **McCrae and Costa** (1980s). Measured by **NEO Personality Inventory**. Found in **50+ nations**. **NOT** found in isolated illiterate Bolivian tribal group (Gurven et al., 2013).

9. **Hereditary/environmental split in Big Five**: N, E, O, C = stronger **hereditary** component; **Agreeableness** = stronger **environmental** component.

10. **Gender and Big Five**: Women score higher than men on **N, E, A, and C** across 55 countries (Schmitt et al., 2008); differences most pronounced in **prosperous, egalitarian nations**.

11. **HEXACO** (Ashton & Lee): Six factors; unique addition is **Honesty-Humility (H)**. Low H predicts manipulation and relates to Dark Triad. Assessed by **HEXACO-PI-R**. Emerged from cross-linguistic factor analysis.

12. **Dark Triad** (Paulhus & Williams, 2002): **Narcissism, Machiavellianism, Psychopathy** — all **subclinical**, share callousness and low empathy. Men score higher on all three. Assessed by **SD3** (27 items; Jones & Paulhus, 2014) or **Dirty Dozen** (12 items; Jonason & Webster, 2010).

13. **Conscientiousness** = the **best single predictor of job performance** across occupations in the Big Five framework. Japan values **conscientiousness** most; Australia values **extraversion and agreeableness** most.

14. **Trolling and Dark Triad** (Buckels, Trapnell, & Paulhus, 2014): Online trolling strongly positively correlated with Dark Triad traits, especially **psychopathy** and sadism.

15. **Marital stability study** (Cattell & Nesselroade, 1967): Stable marriages = **similar personality traits** between partners; unstable marriages = **highly different personality traits**.
