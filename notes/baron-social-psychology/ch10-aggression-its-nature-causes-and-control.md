# 10 Aggression: Its Nature, Causes, and Control

This chapter covers the nature, causes, and control of aggression: biological, drive, social-learning, and cognitive-affective theories; social, cultural, personality, and situational determinants; bullying and cyberbullying; and reduction strategies.

---

## Perspectives on Aggression

### Definition
- **Aggression**: behavior directed at harming another living being who is motivated to avoid such treatment.
  - Two key elements: (1) intentional harm; (2) the target wants to avoid it.
  - Includes verbal, physical, direct, indirect, and modern forms (cyberbullying, online humiliation).

### Contemporary Context
- >40% of teenagers engage in **sexting**; ~15% of teenage boys send nude photos of ex-girlfriends after breakups.

---

## Biological Factors: Are We Programmed to Aggress?

### Instinct Theories
- **Freud**: aggression stems from **thanatos** (death instinct), redirected outward.
- **Konrad Lorenz** (ethologist; 1966, 1974): aggression springs from an **inherited fighting instinct**; ensures strongest males pass on genes.

### Why Social Psychologists Initially Rejected Instinct Views
1. Human aggression is too varied to be genetically fixed.
2. Frequency varies dramatically across societies.

### The Evolutionary Perspective
- Males competed for mates; successful aggression against rivals increased reproductive success, possibly producing a tendency for males to aggress against **other males** (not females, who reject publicly aggressive males; Griskevicius et al., 2007).
- **Griskevicius et al. (2009)**: activating mating motivation increased male aggression against other men, especially with only males watching, NOT with females present.

> **KEY POINT**: Genetic influence does NOT make aggression inevitable; it means a potential exists.

---

## Drive Theories

- **Drive theories of aggression**: aggression stems from external conditions arousing the motive to harm (Berkowitz, 1989; Feshbach, 1984). The motive is externally elicited; can be triggered by frustration, provocation, or even a weapon present (Anderson, 1998).

### The Frustration-Aggression Hypothesis
- **Dollard, Doob, Miller, Mowrer, and Sears (1939)**.
- **Frustration**: anything preventing us from reaching goals.
- **Two assertions**: (1) frustration ALWAYS leads to aggression; (2) aggression ALWAYS stems from frustration.
- **Current view**: both are too extreme. Frustration is a strong determinant mainly when perceived as **illegitimate/unjustified** (Folger & Baron, 1996).

---

## Modern Theories

### Social Learning Perspective
- **Bandura (1997; 1963)**: aggression is **acquired** through experience, not innate.
- Learned via (1) **direct experience** (reinforcement/punishment) and (2) **observational learning** (watching models).
- People learn: how to harm, who are "appropriate" targets, what justifies retaliation, when aggression is approved.

### General Aggression Model (GAM)
- **Anderson and Bushman (2002)**; most comprehensive theory; incorporates cognition.

**Input variables, two categories:**
1. **Situational**: frustration, provocation, aggressive models, discomfort/negative affect (heat, pain).
2. **Person factors**: irritability, pro-violence beliefs, **hostile attributional bias**, Type A pattern, aggression skills.

**Three internal states they act through:** **arousal**, **affective states** (hostility), **cognitions** (hostile thoughts/scripts).

**Appraisal/decision:** **thoughtful action** (restraint) vs. **impulsive action** (overt aggression); restraining factors include police presence and threat of retaliation.

- **Bushman & Anderson (2002):** repeated exposure strengthens aggression **knowledge structures** (beliefs, schemas, scripts), more easily activated, "priming" aggression.

## Basic Sources: Frustration and Provocation

### Frustration
- One of many factors; strongest when perceived as **unjustified/illegitimate**.

### Direct Provocation
- **Provocation**: actions perceived as stemming from malicious intent; one of the STRONGEST causes. We reciprocate, more so when we are sure the other **intended** harm.

**Forms (strongest first):**
1. **Condescension** (Harris, 1993): extremely powerful.
2. **Harsh, unjustified criticism** (Baron, 1993b).
3. **Teasing**: provoking statements about flaws (Kowalski, 2001); more hostile attribution → more aggression (Campos et al., 2007).
4. **Threats to status/image**: Griskevicius et al. (2009): 48.3% of men and 45.3% of women cited status/reputation as the main cause of their most recent aggressive act.

## EMOTIONS AND AGGRESSION: Does Arousal Play a Role?

- Aggression does NOT always involve strong emotion (grudge-holders, professional assassins).
- **Two dimensions of mood**: positive-negative (valence) and activation (arousal).

### Excitation Transfer Theory
- **Dolf Zillmann (1983, 1988, 1994)**: physiological arousal dissipates slowly; residual arousal from Situation A intensifies reactions in unrelated Situation B.
- **Conditions for transfer:** the person is (1) **unaware** of residual arousal, or (2) **misattributes** it to the current situation.
- Illustration: a near-miss accident (arousal) → minor provocation later → disproportionate rage.
- Support: arousal from competition (Christy, Gelfand, & Hartmann, 1971), exercise (Zillmann, 1979), and music (Rogers & Ketcher, 1979) increased aggression.

## Social Causes: Social Exclusion and Media Violence

### Social Exclusion
- A powerful aggression trigger (Leary, Twenge, & Quinlivan, 2006); creates a self-perpetuating cycle.
- **Mechanism is cognitive, NOT emotional distress**: rejection activates a **hostile cognitive mind-set** (perceiving ambiguous actions as hostile, aggression as appropriate).

> **KEY STUDY: DeWall, Twenge, Gitter, & Baumeister (2009)**
> - **Study 1:** rejected (vs. benign-non-cooperation) participants completed ambiguous word fragments more aggressively ("r_pe" → rape vs. ripe).
> - **Study 2:** those told they would be alone in the future rated an ambiguous stranger as more hostile.
> - **Study 3:** the exclusion condition gave more harmful job evaluations of a stranger.
> - Exclusion → hostile cognitive bias → aggression, even toward uninvolved others.

### Media Violence

**Expert consensus (Anderson, Berkowitz et al., 2004, U.S. Senate):** exposure significantly increases aggression; effects are short- AND long-term; magnitude is large (comparable to important medical effects).

> **KEY STUDY: Bandura's Bobo Doll Studies (Bandura, Ross, & Ross, 1963)**
> - Nursery school children watched a "TV program" where an adult either aggressively attacked an inflated clown (**Bobo doll**) (punching, mallet, kicking) or sat quietly.
> - Children who saw the aggressive model imitated those specific acts; the nonaggressive-model group did not.
> - Children learned new aggressive behaviors AND that aggression is acceptable (social learning).

**Longitudinal:** Huesmann & Eron (1984, 1986): childhood TV/film violence → higher adult aggression and violent-crime arrests; replicated cross-culturally. Also found for violent music lyrics (Anderson, Carnagey, & Eubanks, 2003).

**Violent video games meta-analysis (Anderson et al., 2010):**
- Increases aggressive **cognitions**, **affect**, **behavior**.
- Decreases **empathy** and **prosocial behavior**.
- Found in Eastern and Western cultures, short- and long-term. "Content matters."

**Why so popular? (Przybylski, Ryan, & Rigby, 2009)** based on **cognitive evaluation theory** (Ryan & Deci, 2000, 2007): popularity is due to satisfying needs for **autonomy and competence/mastery**, NOT violent content. High trait aggressives preferred violent games, but the difference vanished when mastery was held constant.

**Mechanisms:**
1. **Hostile expectation bias** (Bushman & Anderson, 2002): expecting others to behave aggressively.
2. **Desensitization**: Krahe et al. (2011): lower skin conductance to violent films in frequent viewers.

> **KEY STUDY: Bartholow, Bushman, & Sestir (2006), Neural Desensitization**
> - Measured **P300** (event-related potential indexing emotional processing) while participants viewed neutral and violent images.
> - Frequent violent-game players showed **smaller P300 responses** to violent images; degree of desensitization predicted subsequent aggression.

## Cultural Factors

### Cultures of Honor
- **Cultures of honor**: strong norms that aggression is appropriate (sometimes required) in response to insults to one's honor.
- **Origins (Cohen & Nisbett, 1994, 1997):** arose where wealth was easily stolen (cattle, slaves); demonstrating forceful responses deterred theft.
- Persist today (Vandello & Cohen, 2003). **Timmerman (2007):** white pitchers more likely to hit batters after honor insults.

### Sexual Jealousy
- **Sexual jealousy**: fear that one's partner is/may be unfaithful; especially threatening to male honor (Baker, Gregware, & Cassidy, 1999); a powerful aggression motivator (Kaighobadi et al., 2009).
- U.S.: 20% of nonfatal violence against women is by intimate partners (~600,000/year; BJS, 2003); 30% of female homicide victims killed by intimate partners (BJS, 2007).
- Evolutionary: males who detected infidelity maintained paternity certainty → jealousy-related mate-retention behaviors.

### The Male Gender Role
- Norms prescribe males be dominant, tough, willing to use aggression; failing to respond may seem unmasculine.

## Personality, Gender, and Aggression

### Personality Factors
- **Type A behavior pattern**: competitiveness, time urgency, hostility; more aggression under provocation/competition.
- **Trait aggressiveness (irritability)**: lower thresholds across situations.
- **Hostile attributional bias**: perceiving hostile intent in ambiguous actions; strong predictor, especially in children; activated by exclusion (DeWall et al., 2009).
- **Narcissism** (inflated self-view): heightened aggression when self-image is threatened (**ego threat**); secure self-esteem does NOT have this effect (Baumeister).

### Gender and Aggression
- Gender differences are NOT as large as believed (Hawley et al., 2007).

| Type | Who Uses More |
|------|--------------|
| Physical (direct, overt) | Men |
| Relational/indirect (rumors, exclusion) | Women as often or more |

- **Relational aggression** (social aggression): damages relationships without physical confrontation. Evolutionary and socialization explanations.

## Situational Determinants: Heat and Alcohol

### Temperature
- **Heat hypothesis**: high temperatures increase aggression; higher temperatures → higher violent crime (Anderson, 1987, 1989).
- **Negative affect escape model** (Anderson, 1989): at very HIGH temperatures, the desire to escape may override aggression (curvilinear/inverted-U).
- Global warming implication: rising temperatures may raise violence.

### Alcohol
- A powerful situational determinant; many violent crimes involve intoxication.
- **Alcohol myopia** (Steele & Josephs, 1990): alcohol narrows attention to the most salient cues (typically provocative ones) while inhibitory cues are ignored → more aggression.
- Also: reduced thoughtful processing and self-awareness lower inhibitions.

## Bullying

- **Bullying**: repeated victimization of specific individuals through aggression. Three elements: **intentional harm**, **repetition**, **power imbalance**.

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Physical | Hitting, kicking, property damage |
| Verbal | Name-calling, threats |
| Relational/Social | Exclusion, rumors |
| Cyberbullying | Through digital media |

### Why People Bully
- Instrumental gain, dominance/status, coping with negative emotions, revenge, enjoyment, peer norms.
- **Reactive aggression**: angry response to provocation. **Proactive aggression**: deliberate, cold, goal-directed. Bullies typically use **proactive** aggression.

### Characteristics
- **Bullies**: high aggression, pro-violence attitudes, low empathy; NOT necessarily low self-esteem (often narcissistic); small supportive peer group.
- **Victims**, two subtypes: **passive/submissive** (anxious, don't retaliate) and **provocative/bully-victims** (anxious AND aggressive). Common: physically weaker, anxious, isolated, low self-esteem, lack peer support.
- **Consequences**: depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, academic decline; severe cases include self-harm and suicide (Jessica Logan case).

### Reducing Bullying
- Multi-component school programs: awareness campaigns, clear rules, increased supervision, social-skills/assertiveness training, **bystander training** (peer disapproval is one of the strongest inhibitors; intervention stops bullying within seconds), counseling for bullies.
- **Olweus Bullying Prevention Program** (Dan Olweus, pioneer): school-wide, documented effectiveness.

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Cyberbullying

- **Cyberbullying**: bullying through electronic media.

| Feature | Cyberbullying Specific |
|---------|----------------------|
| Reach | 24/7; victims can't escape, even at home |
| Audience | Potentially unlimited |
| Anonymity | Bully anonymous → lower inhibitions |
| Permanence | Digital content persists |
| Power basis | Not physical strength; anyone with access |
| Bully's feedback | Can't see victim's suffering → reduced empathy |

- **Forms**: harassment, flaming, outing, exclusion, impersonation, cyberstalking, sexting-related bullying.
- **Legal consequences**: **Phillip Alpert** (sent ex's nude photo → child pornography conviction, sex offender registry to age 43); **Jessica Logan** (peers taunted her after nude photos circulated; she took her own life).
- **Prevention**: digital citizenship education, blocking/reporting, legal deterrence, not responding and reporting to adults.

## Prevention and Control of Aggression

### Punishment
- Two views: **just desserts** (retributive) and **deterrence**.
- Effective only when **swift**, **certain**, and **sufficiently severe**, conditions rarely met in reality.
- May **increase** aggression: models aggression, increases arousal/anger; physical punishment of children predicts increased childhood aggression.

### Self-Regulation
- **Self-regulation**: controlling impulses, including aggression; draws on a limited resource (**ego strength**).
- **Ego depletion** (Baumeister): prior self-control depletes the resource, impairing subsequent restraint.
- Self-regulatory capacity can be **strengthened through practice** (like a muscle); mindfulness supports thoughtful appraisal.

### Catharsis: Does "Blowing Off Steam" Help?
- **Catharsis hypothesis** (Freudian): expressing aggression reduces the drive to aggress.
- **Empirically FALSE**: venting (punching pillows, etc.) typically INCREASES aggression.
- Why it fails: keeps aggressive thoughts active, lowers inhibitions, arousal misattributed as anger, rehearsal effect.

> **CRITICAL EXAM POINT**: catharsis is empirically false; venting increases aggression. Classic MCQ trap.

### Bolstering Self-Esteem
- Ego threats trigger aggression; secure self-views and **self-affirmation** reduce it.
- **Caveat**: **narcissism** + ego threat = increased aggression. The goal is genuine, secure self-esteem, not narcissism (Baumeister, Bushman).

### Other Strategies
- **Apologies/mitigating information** reduce retaliation.
- **Incompatible responses** (humor, empathy, mild positive affect) inhibit aggression.
- **Nonaggressive models** demonstrate calm responses.
- **Cognitive reappraisal** and **attributional retraining** (changing hostile attributional bias) reduce aggression, especially in children with conduct problems.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Definition of aggression**: intentional harm + the target wants to avoid it. Accidents don't qualify.
2. **Frustration-aggression hypothesis** (Dollard et al., 1939, all five authors): both original claims now incorrect; frustration matters most when unjustified (Folger & Baron, 1996).
3. **GAM** (Anderson & Bushman, 2002): situational + person inputs act through arousal, affect, cognition.
4. **Excitation transfer theory (Zillmann, 1983)**: residual arousal from A intensifies B; misattribution of arousal.
5. **Bobo doll (Bandura, Ross & Ross, 1963)**: children imitated specific aggressive acts; observational learning.
6. **Social exclusion mechanism (DeWall et al., 2009)**: COGNITIVE (hostile cognitive bias), not emotional distress; aggression even toward strangers.
7. **Media violence consensus (Anderson, Berkowitz et al., 2004)**: real, short- and long-term, large effects; hostile expectation bias and desensitization.
8. **Video games (Anderson et al., 2010)**: increase aggressive cognition/affect/behavior, decrease empathy/prosocial. Popularity = autonomy/competence (Przybylski et al., 2009).
9. **P300 desensitization (Bartholow et al., 2006)**: smaller P300 to violent images in frequent players; predicts aggression.
10. **Cultures of honor (Cohen & Nisbett)**: arose where wealth was easily stolen; sexual jealousy linked; 20% of nonfatal violence against women, 30% of female homicides by intimate partners.
11. **Catharsis is EMPIRICALLY FALSE**: venting increases aggression (classic MCQ trap).
12. **Alcohol myopia (Steele & Josephs, 1990)**: narrows attention to provocative cues; ignores inhibitory cues.
13. **Cyberbullying vs. traditional**: anonymity, unlimited audience, 24/7 reach, permanence, no physical power, reduced empathy.
14. **Gender and aggression**: men more physical; women relational/indirect as much or more; differences smaller than believed. Narcissism + ego threat = aggression.
15. **Self-regulation and ego depletion**: limited, depletable resource; strengthenable through practice.
