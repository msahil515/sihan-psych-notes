# 10 Aggression: Its Nature, Causes, and Control

Chapter Overview: This chapter examines the nature, causes, and control of human aggression — behavior directed at harming others. It surveys biological, drive-based, social-learning, and modern cognitive-affective theories; explores social, cultural, personality, and situational determinants; covers bullying and cyberbullying; and reviews evidence-based strategies for reducing aggression.

---

## Perspectives on Aggression: In Search of the Roots of Violence

### Definition

- **Aggression**: Behavior directed toward the goal of harming another living being who is motivated to avoid such treatment.
  - Key elements: (1) intentional infliction of harm; (2) the target wants to avoid the treatment.
  - Encompasses verbal, physical, direct, and indirect forms.
  - Modern forms include cyberbullying, sexting revenge, smear campaigns, online humiliation.

### Contemporary Context

- >40% of teenagers involved in **sexting** (sending explicit sexual photos over the Web/cell).
- ~15% of teenage boys send nude photos of ex-girlfriends to others after breakups.
- Many observers believe we live in an era where humiliating others is more socially acceptable (e.g., American Idol ridicule, PeopleofWalmart.com).

## The Role of Biological Factors: Are We Programmed to Aggress?

### Instinct Theories

#### Freud's Thanatos (Death Instinct)
- **Sigmund Freud**: Aggression stems from **thanatos** — a powerful death wish/instinct.
- Initially aimed at self-destruction, then redirected outward toward others.

#### Lorenz's Ethological View
- **Konrad Lorenz** (Nobel Prize-winning ethologist; 1966, 1974): Aggression springs from an **inherited fighting instinct**.
- Evolutionary function: ensures only the strongest males obtain mates and pass genes to the next generation.

### Why Social Psychologists Initially Rejected Instinct Views

1. Human aggression encompasses an enormous range of behaviors (from social exclusion to overt violence) — too varied to be genetically fixed.
2. Frequency of aggression varies dramatically across societies — inconsistent with a universal genetic determinant.

### The Evolutionary Perspective: A More Nuanced Biological View

- Contemporary evolutionary reasoning (accepted by some social psychologists):
  - In evolutionary past, males needed to compete with rivals for mates.
  - Successful aggression against male rivals increased reproductive success.
  - This may have produced a genetically influenced tendency for males to aggress against **other males**.
  - Males would NOT be expected to aggress against females (females reject publicly aggressive males as mates, per Griskevicius et al., 2007).
  - Females might aggress equally or more against males than other females.

#### Supportive Research
- Males more aggressive toward other males than toward females (domestic violence is an exception).
- **Griskevicius et al. (2009)**: Activating mating motivation in men (by having them read about meeting an attractive woman) increased aggression against other men — especially when only other males were watching; NOT when females were present.
- Conclusion: Biological factors may play a role via the status-reproductive success link.

> **KEY POINT**: Genetic influence on aggression does NOT mean it is inevitable — it means a potential exists, generated partly by biological factors.

## Drive Theories: The Motive to Harm Others

### Core Idea

- **Drive theories of aggression**: Theories suggesting aggression stems from external conditions that arouse the motive to harm or injure others.
- Major contributors: Berkowitz (1989), Feshbach (1984).
- Contrast with instinct theories: the motive is externally (not internally) elicited.
- The aggressive drive can be triggered by frustration, provocations from others, or even the presence of a weapon in the room (Anderson, 1998).

### The Frustration-Aggression Hypothesis

- Proposed by **Dollard, Doob, Miller, Mowrer, and Sears (1939)**.
- **Frustration**: Anything that prevents us from reaching goals we are seeking.
- **Two sweeping assertions**:
  1. Frustration ALWAYS leads to some form of aggression.
  2. Aggression ALWAYS stems from frustration.
- **Current view**: Both assertions are now considered too extreme and misleading.
  - People respond to frustration with sadness, depression, or direct coping — not just aggression.
  - Not all aggression stems from frustration; many other causes exist.
  - Frustration IS a strong determinant when perceived as **illegitimate or unjustified** (Folger & Baron, 1996).

> **EXAM NOTE**: Dollard et al. (1939) — all five authors. This is the most famous drive theory. Both its original assertions are now considered incorrect.

## Modern Theories of Aggression: The Social Learning Perspective and the General Aggression Model

### Social Learning Perspective

- **Albert Bandura (1997)**; early work 1963.
- Core idea: Humans are NOT born with aggressive responses; they are **acquired** through experience.
- Aggressive behaviors are learned through:
  1. **Direct experience** (reinforcement or punishment of one's own aggressive acts).
  2. **Observational learning** (watching social models — live people or media characters — behave aggressively).

- What individuals learn through these processes:
  1. Various ways of seeking to harm others.
  2. Which people or groups are "appropriate" targets for aggression.
  3. What actions by others justify retaliation or vengeance.
  4. What situations or contexts permit or even approve aggression.

### General Aggression Model (GAM)

- **Anderson and Bushman (2002)**.
- Most comprehensive modern theory of human aggression.
- Builds on social learning perspective; fully incorporates cognitive processes.

#### Input Variables: Two Categories

**1. Situational Factors:**
- Frustration
- Provocation (insult, criticism)
- Exposure to aggressive models (real or media)
- Any source of discomfort/negative affect (high temperatures, pain, dull lectures)

**2. Person Factors (Individual Differences):**
- High irritability / negative affectivity
- Attitudes and beliefs about violence (believing it is acceptable)
- **Hostile attributional bias** (perceiving hostile intent in ambiguous behavior)
- Type A behavior pattern
- Pro-aggression values
- Specific aggression-related skills

#### Three Internal States Through Which Input Variables Act

1. **Arousal** — increase in physiological excitement
2. **Affective states** — hostile feelings, angry facial expressions
3. **Cognitions** — hostile thoughts, aggression-related beliefs and scripts

#### Appraisal and Decision Mechanism

- After internal state changes, the person engages in:
  - **Thoughtful action** — restraining anger after careful appraisal
  - **Impulsive action** — leading to overt aggression

- Restraining factors: presence of police, perceived threat of target's retaliation.

#### How Repeated Exposure Primes Aggression (Bushman & Anderson, 2002 Extension)

- Repeated exposure to aggression (real-life or media) strengthens **knowledge structures** related to aggression (beliefs, attitudes, schemas, scripts).
- Stronger knowledge structures are more easily activated by situational or person variables.
- Result: individuals are truly "primed" for aggression.

> **GAM vs. Frustration-Aggression Hypothesis**: GAM is far more comprehensive, incorporating the role of cognition, affect, arousal, and both situational and personal variables.

## Causes of Human Aggression: Social, Cultural, Personal, and Situational

---

## Basic Sources of Aggression: Frustration and Provocation

### Frustration

- One of many factors, not the only or most important cause.
- A strong elicitor when perceived as **unjustified or illegitimate**.
- Example: student receiving unexplained poor grade may feel thwarted, develop hostile thoughts, and seek revenge against professor.

### Direct Provocation

- **Provocation**: Actions by others that tend to trigger aggression in the recipient, often perceived as stemming from malicious intent.
- One of the STRONGEST causes of human aggression.
- We tend to reciprocate — often returning as much or more aggression than received.
- Reciprocation is stronger when we are certain the other person **intended** to harm us.

#### Forms of Provocation (Strongest First)

1. **Condescension** — arrogance, disdain (Harris, 1993): extremely powerful.
2. **Harsh and unjustified criticism** — personal attacks (Baron, 1993b).
3. **Teasing**: Provoking statements calling attention to flaws/imperfections; may be somewhat playful (Kowalski, 2001).
   - More attribution to hostile motives → more aggressive response (Campos et al., 2007).
4. **Threats to status or public image**:
   - Griskevicius et al. (2009): 48.3% of men and 45.3% of women cited status/reputation concerns as main cause of their most recent aggressive act.

## EMOTIONS AND AGGRESSION: Does Arousal Play a Role?

### Key Insight: Aggression Does NOT Always Involve Strong Emotion

- Counter-examples:
  - A person nursing a grudge may wait calmly before retaliating ("Revenge is best served cold").
  - Professional assassins kill without anger or emotional involvement.

### Two Dimensions of Mood

- **Positive-negative** (valence): happy to sad
- **Activation** (arousal level): low to high

### Excitation Transfer Theory

- **Dolf Zillmann (1983, 1988, 1994)**.
- **Core principle**: Physiological arousal dissipates slowly. Residual arousal from Situation A can persist and intensify emotional reactions in unrelated Situation B.

#### Conditions for Transfer

- Most likely when:
  1. Person is relatively **unaware** of persisting residual arousal (small elevations are hard to notice).
  2. Person is aware of arousal but **misattributes it** to the current situation (rather than the original source).

#### Classic Illustration

- Near-miss traffic accident (high arousal) → drive to airport → security line slowdown (minor provocation) → residual arousal intensifies anger → disproportionate rage.

#### Experimental Support

- Arousal from competitive games (Christy, Gelfand, & Hartmann, 1971), exercise (Zillmann, 1979), and certain music (Rogers & Ketcher, 1979) all increased subsequent aggression in experiments.

> **EXAM NOTE**: Excitation transfer theory = Zillmann. Key mechanism = misattribution of persisting arousal from a prior situation.

## Social Causes of Aggression: Social Exclusion and Exposure to Media Violence

### Social Exclusion

- Social rejection is a **powerful trigger for aggression** (Leary, Twenge, & Quinlivan, 2006).
- Can create a self-perpetuating cycle: rejection → aggression → more rejection.

#### Mechanism: NOT Emotional Distress — It Is Hostile Cognitive Bias

- Studies testing whether emotional distress mediates the rejection-aggression link have NOT confirmed it.
- The correct mechanism: rejection activates a **hostile cognitive mind-set** (hostile cognitive bias).
  - Ambiguous actions of others are perceived as hostile.
  - Aggression is perceived as common and appropriate.

#### KEY STUDY: DeWall, Twenge, Gitter, & Baumeister (2009)

**Study 1 — Word Fragments:**
- IV: Active rejection by partner vs. benign reason for non-cooperation.
- DV: Completion of ambiguous word fragments (e.g., "r_pe" → rape vs. ripe).
- Finding: Rejected participants completed words more aggressively.

**Study 2 — Hostile Bias:**
- IV: Told future would be spent alone (exclusion) vs. in rich social relationships.
- Task: Rated ambiguous stranger's behavior using hostility-related adjectives.
- Finding: Those in exclusion condition rated stranger's behavior as more hostile.

**Study 3 — Aggression:**
- IV: Same as Study 2.
- DV: Rated stranger's suitability for a needed job (negative rating = harm the stranger).
- Finding: Exclusion condition gave lower (more harmful) evaluations.

**Overall Conclusion**: Social exclusion → hostile cognitive bias → aggression (not just toward excluders but toward others as well).

### Media Violence

#### Expert Consensus (Anderson, Berkowitz et al., 2004 — U.S. Senate Testimony)

1. Exposure to violent TV, movies, video games, and music **significantly increases** likelihood of aggressive behavior.
2. Effects are both **short-term and long-term**.
3. **Magnitude is large** — at least as large as medical effects considered important by physicians (e.g., aspirin on heart attacks).

#### Bandura's Bobo Doll Studies (1963) — Classic Evidence

- **Researchers**: Bandura, Ross, & Ross (1963a, 1963b).
- **Participants**: Nursery school-age children.
- **Procedure**:
  - Condition A: Children watched a "TV program" in which an adult model aggressively attacked an inflated plastic clown (**Bobo doll**) — sitting on it, punching it in the nose, hitting it with a toy mallet, kicking it around the room.
  - Condition B: Adult model sat quietly; no aggression.
  - Children then placed in room with same toys for 20 minutes; behavior observed.
- **Finding**: Children who saw the aggressive model strongly imitated those specific aggressive behaviors. Children who saw the nonaggressive model did not.
- **Conclusion**: Children learned new ways of aggressing AND that aggression is acceptable behavior from observing the model — consistent with social learning.

#### Longitudinal Research

- Huesmann & Eron (1984, 1986): Greater TV/film violence viewing in childhood → higher aggression as teenagers/adults (higher arrest rates for violent crimes).
- Cross-cultural replication: Australia, Finland, Israel, Poland, South Africa (Botha, 1990).
- Effects also found for: violent news programs, violent music lyrics (Anderson, Carnagey, & Eubanks, 2003), violent video games.

#### Violent Video Games — Meta-Analysis (Anderson et al., 2010)

Increases:
1. Aggressive **cognitions** (thoughts about harming others)
2. Aggressive **affect** (hostility, anger, desire for revenge)
3. Aggressive **behavior** (overt)

Decreases:
1. **Empathy** for others
2. **Prosocial behavior**

- Effects found in Eastern AND Western cultures.
- Found in both short-term lab AND long-term longitudinal studies.
- Long-term effects: lasting increases in aggressive cognitions, affect, and behavior.
- Conclusion (Anderson et al., 2010): "Video games are neither inherently good nor inherently bad. But people learn. And content matters."

#### Why Are Violent Video Games So Popular? (Przybylski, Ryan, & Rigby, 2009)

- Theoretical basis: **Cognitive evaluation theory** (Ryan & Deci, 2000, 2007).
- Popular assumption: People like violent games for their violent content. — **INCORRECT.**
- Actual finding: Popularity is due to games satisfying needs for **autonomy and competence/mastery**, NOT violent content.
- Measures of autonomy and competence satisfaction predicted enjoyment, absorption, and desire for a sequel; violent content did NOT.
- High trait aggressives DID prefer violent games — but when mastery/competence held constant, this difference disappeared.
- Implication: Non-violent games providing autonomy/competence could be equally popular without the harm.

#### Mechanisms: Why Does Media Violence Increase Aggression?

**1. GAM / Hostile Expectation Bias (Bushman & Anderson, 2002)**:
- Repeated exposure creates a **hostile expectation bias** — strong expectation that others will behave aggressively.
- People perceive provocations everywhere, even when they don't exist.

**2. Desensitization**:
- Frequent exposure reduces emotional sensitivity to violence.
- Krahe et al. (2011): skin conductance lower in frequent media-violence viewers when watching violent films — demonstrating reduced arousal/emotional response.

**3. Neural Desensitization — Neuroscience Evidence (Bartholow, Bushman, & Sestir, 2006)**:
- Participants reported past violent vs. nonviolent video game history.
- Viewed neutral images (man on bicycle) and violent images (person with gun to another's head).
- Measured **P300 activity** (event-related brain potential indicating extent emotional stimuli are being processed/categorized).
- Finding: Frequent violent video game players showed **smaller P300 responses** to violent images — evidence of neural desensitization.
- Degree of desensitization predicted likelihood of subsequent aggression.

**Summary of Mechanisms**:
1. **Desensitization** — violence seems "normal," not extraordinary.
2. **Strengthened knowledge structures** — beliefs, expectations, schemas, and scripts related to aggression are more accessible, more easily activated.

## Cultural Factors in Aggression: "Cultures of Honor," Sexual Jealousy, and the Male Gender Role

### Cultures of Honor

- **Cultures of honor**: Cultures with strong norms indicating that aggression is an appropriate and sometimes required response to insults to one's honor.

#### Origins (Cohen & Nisbett, 1994, 1997)

- Developed in geographic areas where wealth was concentrated in easily stolen assets (cattle; historically also slaves).
- To deter theft and affronts, individuals needed to demonstrate they would respond forcefully.
- Result: Norms condoning violence in response to insults emerged and became widely accepted.

#### Research Evidence

- Such norms are still alive today (Vandello & Cohen, 2003).
- **Timmerman (2007)**: White baseball pitchers more likely to hit opposing batters after their honor was insulted (after an opponent hit a home run, or after one of their own teammates was hit by a pitched ball).

### Sexual Jealousy

- **Sexual jealousy**: Suspicion or fear that one's partner is or might be unfaithful.
- In cultures of honor, female infidelity is seen as especially threatening to male honor (Baker, Gregware, & Cassidy, 1999).
- Powerful motivator of aggression (Kaighobadi et al., 2009; Kaighobadi, Starratt, Schackelford, & Popp, 2008).

#### U.S. Statistics

- 20% of nonfatal violence against women is perpetrated by intimate partners (~600,000 assaults/year; Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2003).
- 30% of female homicide victims are killed by intimate partners (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2007).

#### Evolutionary Explanation

- Males who could detect/prevent infidelity maintained certainty of paternity (not investing in another male's offspring).
- Led to evolution of jealousy-related mate-retention behaviors, including intimidation and violence.

### The Male Gender Role

- Cultural norms prescribe that males should be dominant, tough, and willing to use aggression.
- Failure to respond aggressively to provocation may be seen as unmasculine.
- The male gender role is itself a cultural prescription for aggression in specific contexts.

## Personality, Gender, and Aggression

### Personality Factors

#### Type A Behavior Pattern

- **Type A behavior pattern**: Intense competitiveness, time urgency, hostile orientation toward others.
- Type A individuals more likely to aggress, especially in situations involving provocation or competition.
- Included as a person-factor variable in GAM.

#### Trait Aggressiveness (Irritability)

- Some individuals have lower thresholds for aggression across situations.
- High trait aggressiveness is a dispositional variable in GAM.

#### Hostile Attributional Bias

- **Hostile attributional bias**: Tendency to perceive hostile intent in others' ambiguous actions.
- People with this bias interpret neutral or ambiguous behavior as attacks.
- Strong predictor of aggressive behavior, especially in children and adolescents.
- Also activated by social exclusion (DeWall et al., 2009).

#### Narcissism and Aggression

- **Narcissism** (inflated, unrealistic self-view) is associated with heightened aggression when self-image is threatened (**ego threat**).
- Baumeister & colleagues: Narcissism + ego threat = dramatically increased aggression.
- Note: HIGH narcissism + ego threat = aggression; genuine secure self-esteem does NOT have this effect.

### Gender and Aggression

#### Common Belief vs. Reality

- Men appear much more aggressive than women — but gender differences in aggression are NOT as large as commonly believed (Hawley et al., 2007).

#### Type of Aggression Matters

| Type | Who Uses More |
|------|--------------|
| Physical aggression (direct, overt) | Men |
| Relational/indirect aggression (rumors, exclusion) | Women as often or more |

- **Relational aggression** = also called **social aggression** — damages social relationships without physical confrontation.

#### Explanation

- Evolutionary: Males competed directly with rivals; females competed indirectly.
- Socialization: Boys socialized toward direct aggression; girls toward indirect forms.

## Situational Determinants of Aggression: The Effects of Heat and Alcohol

### Temperature and Aggression

#### The Heat Hypothesis

- **Heat hypothesis**: High ambient temperatures increase aggression.
- Higher temperatures associated with higher violent crime rates (Anderson, 1987, 1989).
- More aggressive behaviors in baseball (pitchers hitting batters) on hot days.
- Laboratory studies confirm: discomfort from heat increases aggression.

#### Negative Affect Escape Model (Anderson, 1989)

- At very HIGH temperatures, the desire to ESCAPE the discomfort may eventually override the drive to aggress — resulting in a curvilinear (inverted-U) relationship.

#### Global Warming Implication

- If global temperatures continue to rise, rates of interpersonal violence may also rise.

### Alcohol and Aggression

#### The Link

- Alcohol is one of the most powerful situational determinants of aggression.
- Large proportion of violent crimes involve intoxicated perpetrators.

#### Key Theory: Alcohol Myopia (Steele & Josephs, 1990)

- **Alcohol myopia**: Alcohol narrows attention to the most immediate, salient cues in the environment.
- In conflict situations, provocative cues are most salient → alcohol-intoxicated people focus on them.
- Inhibitory cues (consequences of aggression, social norms against it) are IGNORED.
- Result: Intoxicated people aggress more readily.

#### Additional Mechanisms

- **Reduced thoughtful processing**: Alcohol impairs higher-level cognitive appraisal of situations and consequences.
- **Reduced self-awareness and self-monitoring**: Lowers inhibitions against aggression.

## Bullying: Singling Out Others for Repeated Abuse

### Definition

- **Bullying**: Repeated victimization of specific individuals by one or more persons through aggression.
- Three defining elements:
  1. **Intentional harm**
  2. **Repetition** over time
  3. **Power imbalance** (bully has more power than victim)

### Types of Bullying

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Physical | Hitting, kicking, theft/destruction of property |
| Verbal | Name-calling, insults, teasing, threats |
| Relational/Social | Deliberate exclusion, spreading rumors, social manipulation |
| Cyberbullying | Bullying through digital/electronic media |

---

## Why Do People Engage in Bullying?

### Motivations

1. **Instrumental gain** — resources, money, possessions.
2. **Dominance and status** — establishing/maintaining power in peer group.
3. **Coping with negative emotions** — managing own frustration, anger, inadequacy.
4. **Revenge or retaliation** — perceiving oneself as retaliating for a wrong.
5. **Entertainment/enjoyment** — some bullies report enjoying the act.
6. **Peer norms** — peer groups that condone bullying increase its frequency.

### Reactive vs. Proactive Aggression

- **Reactive aggression**: Angry, emotional response to a real or perceived provocation (retaliation).
- **Proactive aggression**: Deliberate, goal-directed, cold aggression without immediate provocation; aimed at gaining resources or dominance.
- Bullies typically rely on **proactive aggression** — calculated and premeditated.

---

## The Characteristics of Bullies and Victims

### Characteristics of Bullies

- Higher than average general aggression.
- Positive attitudes toward violence; little empathy for victims.
- Higher than average social intelligence in some cases (skilled manipulators).
- NOT necessarily low in self-esteem — many have inflated/narcissistic self-views.
- Parents may model/use aggressive disciplinary practices.
- Often have a small supportive peer group.

### Characteristics of Victims

**Two main subtypes:**

1. **Passive/submissive victims**: Anxious, submissive behavior; do not retaliate; few friends.
2. **Provocative victims / Bully-victims**: Combine anxious AND aggressive reactions; may provoke others but lack the strength to defend themselves.

**Common characteristics across victim types:**
- Physically weaker, more anxious, socially isolated.
- Low self-esteem; negative self-views.
- Lack peer support.
- Often perceived as "different" (appearance, interests, weight).

### Consequences for Victims

- Depression, anxiety, reduced self-esteem.
- Academic underperformance.
- Social isolation and loneliness.
- Long-term mental health impact.
- In severe cases: school avoidance, self-harm, suicide (e.g., Jessica Logan case).

---

## Reducing the Occurrence of Bullying: Some Positive Steps

### Effective Multi-Component School Programs

1. **Awareness campaigns**: Educating staff, students, and parents about bullying and its consequences.
2. **Clear rules and policies**: Explicit school-wide norms that bullying is unacceptable.
3. **Increased supervision**: Monitoring common bullying locations (playgrounds, hallways, bathrooms).
4. **Social skills training for victims**: Assertiveness, help-seeking skills.
5. **Bystander training**: Teaching peers to intervene — peer disapproval of bullying is one of the strongest inhibitors; bystander intervention typically stops bullying within seconds.
6. **Counseling for bullies**: Addressing underlying emotional and social issues.

### Olweus Bullying Prevention Program

- Developed by **Dan Olweus** — the pioneer of bullying research.
- Comprehensive, school-wide program with documented effectiveness.
- Components: school conference day, increased supervision, parental involvement, clear consequences for bullying.

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Cyberbullying

### Definition

- **Cyberbullying**: Bullying that occurs through electronic media — text messages, email, social media platforms, websites — rather than face-to-face.

### How Cyberbullying Differs from Traditional Bullying

| Feature | Cyberbullying Specific |
|---------|----------------------|
| Reach | 24/7 access; victims cannot escape, even at home |
| Audience | Potentially unlimited (thousands of viewers) |
| Anonymity | Bully can act anonymously → lower inhibitions |
| Permanence | Digital content persists; hard to fully remove |
| Power basis | Not based on physical strength; anyone with digital access can bully |
| Bully's feedback | Bully cannot directly see victim's suffering → reduced empathy |

### Forms of Cyberbullying

- **Harassment**: Repeatedly sending offensive messages.
- **Flaming**: Hostile, angry online exchanges.
- **Outing**: Sharing private or embarrassing information without consent.
- **Exclusion**: Deliberately excluding from online groups.
- **Impersonation**: Pretending to be the victim online.
- **Cyberstalking**: Repeated threats or harassment via digital means.
- **Sexting-related bullying**: Distributing intimate images without consent.

### Legal Consequences

- **Phillip Alpert case**: Sent nude photo of ex-girlfriend → convicted of sending child pornography → 5 years probation → sex offender registry until age 43 → expelled from college → restricted travel.
- **Jessica Logan case**: Ex-boyfriend sent nude photos → peers taunted her as "slut and whore" both in person and online → she took her own life. Her case illustrates the potentially fatal consequences of cyberbullying.

### Prevention Strategies

- Education about digital citizenship.
- Blocking/reporting features on platforms.
- Legal deterrence.
- Teaching victims not to respond and to report to trusted adults.

## The Prevention and Control of Aggression: Some Useful Techniques

---

## Punishment: Just Desserts or Deterrence?

### Two Views

1. **Just desserts (retributive)**: People who aggress deserve punishment — a matter of justice.
2. **Deterrence**: Punishment prevents future aggression by that individual and deters others.

### Conditions for Punishment to Be Effective

Punishment reduces aggression when it is:
1. **Swift** — occurs immediately after the aggressive act.
2. **Certain** — the aggressor knows punishment will definitely follow.
3. **Sufficiently severe** — strong enough to deter.

### Limitations

- Rarely swift, certain, and severe enough in the real world.
- May **increase** aggression:
  - Models aggressive behavior (aggressive response to aggression).
  - Increases arousal and anger in the punished individual.
  - Physical punishment of children by parents models that aggression resolves conflicts.
- Research consistently shows physical punishment of children is associated with **increased childhood aggression**.

---

## Self-Regulation: Internal Mechanisms for Controlling Aggression

### Ego Depletion

- **Self-regulation**: Ability to control impulses, including aggressive ones.
- Self-control draws on a limited resource (**ego strength**).
- **Ego depletion**: When prior exertion of self-control depletes this resource, subsequent self-control (including restraint of aggression) is impaired.
- Baumeister and colleagues documented ego depletion extensively.

### Strengthening Self-Regulation

- Self-regulatory capacity can be **strengthened through practice** (like a muscle).
- Regular exercises in self-control increase capacity over time.
- Implications: Interventions that build self-control may reduce aggression.

### Role of Mindfulness

- Mindfulness (present-moment awareness without judgment) supports thoughtful appraisal of provocations before responding impulsively.

---

## Catharsis: Does "Blowing Off Steam" Really Help?

### The Catharsis Hypothesis

- **Catharsis hypothesis**: Expressing aggressive impulses (verbally or physically) reduces the drive to engage in further aggression.
- Origin: Freudian theory — releasing "pent-up" aggression provides relief.

### Empirical Evidence

- Research does NOT support the catharsis hypothesis.
- Expressing aggression typically **INCREASES** subsequent aggression.
- Even "harmless" venting (punching a pillow, hitting a punching bag) tends to maintain or increase aggression rather than reduce it.

### Why Catharsis Fails

1. Expressing aggression keeps aggressive thoughts active and accessible.
2. Venting lowers inhibitions against aggression.
3. Physical arousal from venting may be misattributed to anger (via excitation transfer).
4. **Rehearsal effect**: Practicing aggression makes it more likely to occur again.

> **CRITICAL EXAM POINT**: The catharsis hypothesis is empirically FALSE. The intuitive answer ("venting helps") is wrong. Venting anger increases rather than reduces subsequent aggression.

---

## Reducing Aggression by Bolstering Self-Esteem

### The Self-Esteem Link

- Threats to self-esteem are powerful triggers of aggression.
- If ego threats increase aggression, then bolstering self-esteem should reduce it.

### Evidence

- People with positive, secure self-views respond less aggressively to provocations.
- **Self-affirmation** (reflecting on one's important values and characteristics) reduces the impact of ego threats and therefore reduces aggression.

### The Narcissism Caveat

- Narcissism (unrealistically inflated self-esteem) is associated with INCREASED aggression when the ego is threatened.
- The goal is **genuine, secure self-esteem**, NOT narcissism.
- Baumeister, Bushman and colleagues: Narcissism + ego threat = heightened aggression.

---

## Other Strategies for Reducing Aggression

### Apologies and Mitigating Information

- Sincere apology or mitigating explanation from the provocateur reduces retaliation.
- "I'm sorry, I had terrible news this morning" reduces the likelihood of aggression.

### Incompatible Responses

- Responses incompatible with anger (mild positive affect, humor, empathy) inhibit aggression.
- Inducing humor has been shown to reduce aggressive responding.

### Nonaggressive Models

- Observing people who remain calm and handle frustration non-aggressively serves as a model for nonviolent responses.

### Cognitive Reappraisal and Attributional Retraining

- Reappraising provocation ("maybe they didn't mean it") reduces anger and aggression.
- **Attributional retraining**: Changing hostile attributional bias — effective especially in children with conduct problems.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Definition of aggression** must include BOTH components: intentional harm + the target is motivated to avoid the treatment. Accidents and harm without intent do not qualify as aggression.

2. **Frustration-aggression hypothesis** (Dollard et al., 1939 — all five authors): Both of its original claims are now considered incorrect. Frustration is only strongly linked to aggression when perceived as unjustified or illegitimate (Folger & Baron, 1996).

3. **GAM input variables** split into SITUATIONAL (frustration, provocation, aggressive models, discomfort/heat) and PERSON factors (hostile attributional bias, Type A, irritability, pro-aggression beliefs). They act through arousal, affect, and cognition. Proposed by Anderson & Bushman (2002).

4. **Excitation transfer theory (Zillmann, 1983)**: Residual arousal from Situation A intensifies emotional reactions in unrelated Situation B. Most likely when person is unaware of, or misattributes, residual arousal. This can explain why arousal from traffic, exercise, or competitive games subsequently increases aggression.

5. **Bobo doll studies** (Bandura, Ross & Ross, 1963): Children imitated adult aggressive model's specific actions against an inflated clown (punching, hitting with mallet, kicking). Classic demonstration of observational learning of aggression. The model's behavior was presented as a "TV program."

6. **Social exclusion mechanism** (DeWall et al., 2009): The pathway is COGNITIVE (hostile cognitive mind-set/hostile attributional bias), NOT simply emotional distress. Rejection leads people to perceive neutral actions as hostile and to act aggressively even against uninvolved strangers.

7. **Media violence expert consensus** (Anderson, Berkowitz et al., 2004): Effects are real, both short-term and long-term, and large in magnitude (comparable to important medical effects). Mechanisms: hostile expectation bias AND desensitization.

8. **Violent video games meta-analysis** (Anderson et al., 2010): Increases aggressive cognitions, affect, and behavior; decreases empathy and prosocial behavior. People prefer these games for **autonomy and competence** — NOT violent content (Przybylski et al., 2009, based on cognitive evaluation theory of Ryan & Deci).

9. **P300 evidence of desensitization** (Bartholow, Bushman & Sestir, 2006): Frequent violent video game players showed smaller P300 brain responses to violent images, demonstrating neural desensitization. Degree of desensitization predicted subsequent aggression.

10. **Cultures of honor** (Cohen & Nisbett, 1994, 1997): Arose where wealth (cattle, slaves) was easily stolen. Sexual jealousy is particularly linked to cultures of honor; 20% of nonfatal violence against women and 30% of female homicides involve intimate partners (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2003, 2007).

11. **Catharsis hypothesis is EMPIRICALLY FALSE**: Venting anger (hitting pillows, punching bags, yelling) typically INCREASES rather than decreases subsequent aggression. The intuitive answer is wrong. This is a classic MCQ trap.

12. **Alcohol myopia** (Steele & Josephs, 1990): Alcohol narrows attention to most salient (typically provocative) cues; inhibitory cues are ignored — explaining why alcohol dramatically increases aggression.

13. **Cyberbullying vs. traditional bullying**: Key differences — anonymity, unlimited audience, 24/7 reach, permanence of digital content, no physical power requirement, bully cannot see victim's immediate suffering (reducing empathy/inhibition).

14. **Gender and aggression**: Men use more physical (direct) aggression; women use relational/indirect aggression as much as or more than men. Overall gender differences are NOT as large as commonly believed (Hawley et al., 2007). Narcissism + ego threat = aggression (Baumeister).

15. **Self-regulation and ego depletion**: Self-control draws on a limited, depletable resource. When depleted, aggressive impulses are harder to restrain. Self-regulatory capacity can be strengthened through practice, like a muscle.
