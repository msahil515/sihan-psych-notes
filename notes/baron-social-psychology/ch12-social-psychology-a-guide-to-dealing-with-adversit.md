# 12 Social Psychology: A Guide to Dealing with Adversity and Achieving a Happy Life

This capstone chapter applies social-psychological knowledge to well-being across five domains: overcoming loneliness and relationship failure, managing health threats (obesity, stress), improving legal-system fairness, and attaining happiness.

---

## Basic Causes of Social Adversity and Coping

- Life mixes positive events and adversities. Social psychology offers strategies for **flourishing** (well-being beyond mere absence of illness). Key sources addressed: loneliness, relationship failure, obesity, stress.

---

## Loneliness

### Definition
- **Loneliness** = the unpleasant emotional/cognitive state of desiring close relationships but having fewer and less satisfying ones than desired (Archibald, Bartholomew, & Marx, 1995). **Involuntary loneliness** is distinguished from preferred solitude (Burger, 1995).
- Universal across cultures (Rokach & Neto, 2000); affects psychological and physical health and life expectancy.

### Consequences
- Lonely people spend leisure time alone, have mostly casual acquaintances (Berg & McQuinn, 1989), feel left out (Bell, 1993). Even **one friend** reduces these reactions in children (Asher & Paquette, 2003).
- Associated negatives: depression, anxiety, pessimism, self-blame, shyness (Anderson et al., 1994). Lonely people are perceived as maladjusted (Lau & Gruen, 1992) and have poor health and lower life expectancy (Cacioppo, Hawkley, & Berntson, 2003).

> **KEY STUDY: Jetten, Haslam, Pugliese, Tonks, & Haslam (2010)**
> - First-year students studied longitudinally. The more groups students belonged to before university, the LESS likely they were to become depressed during the transition. Group membership buffered stress.

> **KEY STUDY: Jetten, Haslam, & Haslam (2011)**
> - In older adults, belonging to (or even believing they belonged to) more groups predicted better self-reported health. Connectedness affects health at all ages.

### Why Are Some People Lonely?
**1. Genetic factors.**
> **KEY STUDY: McGuire & Clifford (2000)**
> - Behavioral genetics of loneliness in children 9-14 (biological/adoptive siblings, identical/fraternal twins).
> - Identical twins more similar than fraternal → loneliness is **partly heritable**; adoptive siblings more similar than random → environment also matters. No single "loneliness gene."

**2. Attachment style.** Fearful-avoidant and dismissive styles fear/avoid intimacy (Duggan & Brennan, 1994); insecure attachment links to social anxiety and loneliness (Vertue, 2003); preoccupied style correlates with less satisfying relationships.

**3. Lack of early social learning.** Preschool/early peer interaction predicts being better liked later (Erwin & Letchford, 2003). Without it, children develop self-defeating behaviors (avoidance, aggression) → rejection → loneliness, persisting into adulthood (Asendorpf, 1992).

**Summary model (Figure 12.2):** predisposing factors → dispositional loneliness → emotional (depression, fear of intimacy), cognitive (pessimism, self-blame, mistrust), and behavioral (shyness, avoidance, aggression) effects.

### Reducing Loneliness
- Lonely people are aware of difficulties but not why (Duck, Pond, & Leatham, 1994). They can acquire **social skills**: accurate social perception, impression management, emotional regulation, adapting to new situations (Ferris, Davidson, & Perrewe, 2006).
- **Interventions:** (1) **videotape feedback** (viewing one's own behavior, guided by experts; Hope, Holt, & Heimberg, 1995); (2) **cognitive therapy** (changing thoughts, building realistic expectations).

## Shattering and Building Relationships

### What Makes Long-Term Relationships Happy?
- In the U.S., >50% of marriages end in divorce/separation, yet unmarried respondents estimate only a **10%** chance for themselves (Fowers et al., 2001): **optimism bias**.
- **Commitment**: desire to maintain a relationship despite opposing forces (Frank & Branstatter, 2002); commitment from positive rewards beats commitment from fear of loneliness.

**Similarity and assumed similarity:**
- Similarity predicts marital success (Terman & Buttenwieser, 1935). Caspi, Herbener, & Ozer (1992): couples surviving 20 years are highly similar and stay similar.
- **Assumed similarity**: happy partners believe they are MORE similar than they are (Byrne & Blaylock, 1963); higher in dating than married couples (romantic illusions). Both actual and assumed similarity boost satisfaction.

**Dispositional factors:**
- **Narcissism** (superiority, admiration-seeking, low empathy): less commitment (Campbell & Foster, 2002); two narcissists rarely happy (an exception to the similarity rule).
- **Attachment**: preoccupied/fearful-avoidant → less satisfying; secure/dismissing → more satisfying (Murray et al., 2001).

> **KEY STUDY: Lee, Rogge, & Reis (2010), Implicit Attitudes**
> - A word-sorting reaction-time task measured implicit feelings toward a partner.
> - More **positive implicit attitudes** → lower breakup likelihood; implicit feelings predicted outcomes **better than explicit self-reports**.

### Why Relationships Fail
- The gap between idealized and actual similarity becomes apparent (Sillars et al., 1994); minor flaws grow intolerable (Felmlee, 1995). **98.8%** of couples report disagreements (McGonagle, Kessler, & Schilling, 1992). Tension between independence and closeness (Baxter, 1990).

> **KEY STUDY: Murray, Griffin, Rose, & Bellavia (2006), Contingent Love**
> - 173 couples kept 21-day diaries. People **low in self-esteem** felt less accepted and more rejected by partners on days of professional failure; high-SE people did not. Failures "spill over" for low-SE individuals.

### Building Stronger Relationships
> **KEY STUDY: Turan & Vicary (2010)**
> - People better at recognizing relationship-building actions were more satisfied; high attachment anxiety slows this learning.

> **KEY STUDY: McNulty (2010), Positive vs. Constructive**
> - Couples who **always** expressed positivity and always forgave showed LARGER satisfaction declines, especially facing serious problems. A **balance** of positivity and constructive problem-solving is optimal.

**Communal vs. exchange (Clark et al., 2010):**
- **Communal approach**: meeting the partner's needs without balancing benefits.
- **Exchange approach**: balancing benefits via reciprocity.
- Communal norm is the ideal but fades over time; shift toward exchange → lower satisfaction (an early warning sign).

> **KEY STUDY: Miller, Caughlin, & Huston (2003), Positive Illusions**
> - 168 couples over ~13 years. **Positive illusions** (perceiving the partner as more agreeable than actual behavior warrants) → more love and SMALLER declines over 13 years; "the cement of lasting relationships."
- May act as self-fulfilling prophecies; but balance is needed (McNulty, 2010).

- Health enables happiness; social factors play a major role. Focus: **obesity** and **stress**.

## Obesity

### Definitions and Prevalence
- **Obesity**: exceeding ideal weight by ≥ **20%**, or **BMI ≥ 30**. ~**66%** of Americans exceed ideal weight; U.S. leads obesity globally at **30.6%**; Japan/South Korea lowest at **3.2%**.

### Biological Factors
- **"Feast-or-famine" inheritance**: efficient calorie storage was adaptive for ancestors, problematic now.

### Environmental (Social) Factors
1. **Portion size** has grown (Coke 8 oz → 32 oz; McDonald's burgers 250 → 440-540 calories; Ritzer, 2011).
2. **Reduced walking/activity** (crime fears, cars, malls, school buses).
3. **Media campaigns** for high-calorie foods.
4. **Decline of sit-down meals** → all-day snacking, weaker regulation.
5. **Socioeconomic**: healthy food costs more → obesity more common in disadvantaged groups.
6. **Social norms**: "thin is in," yet obesity rises; **NAAFA** counters anti-fat stigma.

- **Baumeister, Heatherton, & Tice (1994)**: obesity relates to **self-control**.
- **Interventions**: Michelle Obama's campaign; restaurant calorie-posting.

### SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Online Weight Loss
> **KEY STUDY: Internet Weight Loss (J. Medical Internet Research)**
> - After a qualifying phase (lost ≥ 9 lbs), participants were assigned to **self-directed**, **monthly counselor meetings**, or **Internet-based** programs.
> - Those who **visited the Internet site regularly regained the LEAST weight**. Works via specific goals, information, and frequent feedback. Caveat: sites lack oversight.

## Stress

### Definition
- **Stress** = our response to events that disrupt or threaten our physical/psychological functioning (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984).

### Sources
- **Major stressful life events** (death, divorce) and **daily hassles** (low intensity, high frequency).

### Effects
- High stress → much higher illness risk (Cohen, 1998; Holmes & Rahe, 1967).
- **Holmes & Rahe (1967):** developed the **Social Readjustment Rating Scale (SRRS)**.

### Social Support
- **Social support**: emotional, informational, and material resources from others; buffers stress. Group membership buffers stress (Jetten et al., 2010).
- **Types:** emotional (empathy, caring), informational (advice), instrumental (tangible aid).

### Social Tactics for Reducing Stress
- Seeking support, **cognitive reappraisal** (challenge vs. threat), prosocial behavior, positive emotions.
- **"Tend and befriend"** (Taylor, 2002): under stress, women (and some men) tend offspring and affiliate rather than fight-or-flight; partly mediated by **oxytocin** (enhanced by estrogen).

## Making the Legal System Fairer

The legal system's assumptions about behavior are often incorrect. Two foci: social influence processes and prejudice/stereotypes.

### Eyewitness Testimony
- **Eyewitness testimony**: appears credible but is **highly fallible**.
- **Weapon focus effect**: a weapon draws attention from the perpetrator's face, reducing accuracy.
- **Cross-race effect (own-race bias)**: better recognition of own-race faces; more cross-race errors → wrongful convictions.

### Police Lineups
- **Sequential lineup**: suspects shown one at a time (yes/no each). **Simultaneous lineup**: all at once (relative judgment).
- **Wells (1993)**: sequential lineups reduce false identifications by discouraging relative judgment.
- **Blind administration** (administrator doesn't know the suspect) prevents cueing; **foils/fillers** should match the witness description.

### Confessions
- **False confessions**: admissions for crimes one didn't commit.
  - **Voluntary** (no external pressure), **compliant** (to end interrogation), **internalized** (comes to believe it).
- **Reid Technique** (psychological pressure, minimization, maximization), criticized for false confessions; **PEACE Model** (UK; Planning, Engage/Explain, Account, Closure, Evaluate) is less coercive. Sleep deprivation increases false confessions.

### Jury Decision-Making
- **Jury nullification**: verdict contrary to evidence (disagreement with the law). Initial majority usually prevails (**majority-wins**).
- **Verdict-driven** (early vote, then build arguments) vs. **evidence-driven** (review evidence first; more thorough).
- **Death-qualified juries** (anti-death-penalty people excluded) are more **conviction-prone**.

### Inadmissible Evidence
- Jurors often **cannot** ignore inadmissible evidence; a **backfire effect** can increase focus on it (**ironic processes of mental control**; Wegner, 1994).

### Prejudice and Stereotypes in Law
- **Attractiveness bias**: attractive defendants get lighter sentences (Stewart, 1980; Downs & Lyons, 1991).
- **Race bias**: minorities get harsher sentences, especially with White victims.

> **KEY STUDY: Eberhardt, Davies, Purdie-Vaughns, & Johnson (2004)**
> - In actual Philadelphia cases (Black defendant, White victim), the more "stereotypically Black" the appearance (Afrocentric features), the more likely a **death sentence**. Implicit racial bias in legal decisions.

**Reducing bias:** blind lineup administration, sequential lineups, jury instructions, jury diversity, recording interrogations, expert witnesses on eyewitness fallibility.

## Personal Happiness

- **Subjective well-being (SWB)** = the scientific construct of happiness: **life satisfaction** (cognitive), high **positive affect**, low **negative affect**.

### How Happy Are People?
- Most report being **moderately happy** (above the midpoint); the **positivity offset** means slightly more positive than negative affect even in neutral situations (Diener & Diener, 1996). True even amid adversity (resilience).
- **Hedonic adaptation (hedonic treadmill)**: people return to a stable happiness level after life changes.

### Factors Influencing Happiness
- **Social relationships**: among the most consistent predictors; quality > quantity.
- **Work/goals**: progress toward meaningful goals matters; **flow** (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990); autonomy/competence/relatedness (Self-Determination Theory; Deci & Ryan).
- **Health**: positively correlated; people adapt to chronic illness.
- **Religion/spirituality**: provides support, purpose, meaning.
- **Personal characteristics**: dispositional optimism (Scheier & Carver, 1985), self-esteem, extraversion (higher), neuroticism (lower happiness), internal locus of control.
- **Genetics**: **set-point theory** (Lykken & Tellegen, 1996); ~**50%** of variance is genetic.

### Wealth and Happiness
- **Easterlin Paradox** (1974): within a country wealthier people are happier, but national wealth growth doesn't raise average happiness.
- Money matters most at low incomes; beyond ~**$75,000/year** (Kahneman & Deaton, 2010), emotional well-being shows diminishing returns (life satisfaction keeps rising).
- **Why wealth disappoints**: adaptation, social comparison, shifting aspirations, opportunity costs.
- **Experiential purchases** (travel, concerts) yield more lasting happiness than **material purchases** (Van Boven & Gilovich, 2003).

### Having vs. Wanting
- **Affective forecasting**: predicting future feelings. **Impact bias** (Wilson & Gilbert, 2003): overestimating intensity AND duration of future emotional reactions.
- **Focalism**: focusing too narrowly on the event. **Immune neglect**: failing to anticipate the **psychological immune system** (rationalizing, reframing).
- **Adaptation level theory** (Helson, 1964): happiness is relative to a shifting standard; the hedonic treadmill prevents satisfaction.

> **KEY STUDY: Emmons & McCullough (2003), Gratitude**
> - Participants wrote weekly about gratitude, hassles, or neutral events.
> - The gratitude group reported higher positive affect, life satisfaction, fewer physical complaints, and more prosocial behavior.

### The Benefits of Happiness
- Happy people live longer, have stronger immune systems (Cohen et al., 2003), lower cardiovascular risk, are more creative, prosocial, better liked, and perform better at work.

> **KEY STUDY: Danner, Snowdon, & Friesen (2001), "Nun Study"**
> - Nuns who expressed more positive emotion in their 20s essays lived **7-10 years longer**; lifestyle was controlled (nuns live similarly).

- **Broaden-and-Build Theory** (Fredrickson, 1998, 2001): positive emotions **broaden** thought-action repertoires and **build** enduring personal resources; an **upward spiral**. Negative emotions narrow repertoires.

### Can We Increase Happiness?
- **"Happiness pie"** (Lyubomirsky, Sheldon, & Schkade, 2005): **set point ~50%**, **circumstances ~10%**, **intentional activities ~40%**. Circumstances barely matter; intentional activities give significant control.

**Evidence-based strategies:**
1. Gratitude exercises (Emmons & McCullough, 2003).
2. Acts of kindness (five in one day, once weekly, raised well-being for 6 weeks).

> **KEY STUDY: Dunn, Aknin, & Norton (2008), Spending on Others**
> - Those who spent a windfall on others reported greater happiness than those who spent on themselves. Prosocial spending increases well-being.

3. Savoring positive experiences. 4. Pursuing meaningful goals. 5. Mindfulness. 6. Exercise. 7. Social connection. 8. Avoiding negative comparisons. 9. Expressing optimism ("best possible self").

- **Variety matters**: concentrating five kindnesses on one day beat spreading them out (preserves novelty; prevents adaptation).
- **Person-activity fit**: strategies work best matched to one's strengths and interests (Lyubomirsky et al., 2005).

### EMOTIONS AND HAPPINESS: Can You Be Too Happy?
- **Gruber, Mauss, & Tamir (2011)**: extreme happiness has costs: excessive risk-taking, reduced motivation, maladaptive timing, mania (bipolar symptom). Moderate negative emotions are adaptive (signal problems, promote careful thinking, enhance empathy).
- **Oishi, Diener, & Lucas (2007)**: moderate (not maximal) positive affect predicts better academic performance and creativity.
- **Emodiversity** (Quoidbach et al., 2014): a rich variety of emotions predicts better health than predominantly one emotion.
- **Conclusion**: optimal happiness is **moderately high**, not maximal; emotional flexibility matters most.

## Summary and Integrative Themes
1. Social psychology has **direct practical applications** to adversity and happiness.
2. **Loneliness** has genetic, attachment, and social-learning roots; reduced by social skills training.
3. **Relationship success** depends on similarity, positive (but not excessive) illusions, communal norms, commitment, and balanced realism.
4. **Health** (obesity, stress) has social determinants (norms, media, support).
5. The **legal system** is vulnerable to biases (eyewitness fallibility, false confessions, juror prejudice) mitigable by reforms.
6. **Happiness** is less about wealth/circumstances than believed; intentional activities offer control.
7. **Extreme happiness** can backfire; moderate positive affect with emotional diversity is optimal.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Loneliness** (Archibald et al., 1995): desiring more/better relationships than one has; involuntary vs. voluntary.
2. **Jetten et al. (2010)**: pre-university group membership predicted less depression after arrival; groups buffer stress.
3. **McGuire & Clifford (2000)**: loneliness is partly **heritable** (twins); no single gene.
4. **Lee, Rogge, & Reis (2010)**: implicit attitudes predict breakup better than explicit self-reports.
5. **McNulty (2010)**: "always positive" couples show GREATER satisfaction decline; balance is optimal.
6. **Communal vs. exchange (Clark et al., 2010)**: communal predicts satisfaction; shift to exchange is a warning sign.
7. **Positive illusions (Miller et al., 2003)**: predict more love and smaller decline over 13 years; "cement" of relationships.
8. **Obesity**: BMI ≥ 30 or ≥ 20% over ideal; ~66% of Americans above ideal, ~30.6% obese (global #1).
9. **Happiness "pie" (Lyubomirsky et al., 2005)**: set point ~50%, circumstances ~10%, intentional activities ~40%.
10. **Impact bias (Wilson & Gilbert, 2003)**: overestimating intensity AND duration of future emotions.
11. **Danner et al. (2001) "Nun Study"**: positive early-life emotion → 7-10 years longer life.
12. **Broaden-and-Build (Fredrickson)**: positive emotions broaden repertoires and build resources (upward spiral).
13. **Easterlin Paradox**: within countries richer = happier, but national wealth growth doesn't raise average happiness.
14. **Sequential vs. simultaneous lineup (Wells, 1993)**: sequential reduces false IDs by preventing relative judgment.
15. **"Too Happy" paradox (Gruber et al., 2011; Oishi et al., 2007)**: extreme happiness → risk-taking, reduced motivation, poorer academics; moderate high happiness is optimal.
