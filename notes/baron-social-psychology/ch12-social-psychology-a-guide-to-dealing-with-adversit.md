# 12 Social Psychology: A Guide to Dealing with Adversity and Achieving a Happy Life

Chapter 12 serves as the integrative capstone of Baron & Branscombe, demonstrating how social-psychological knowledge applies directly to human well-being. It covers five major applied domains: overcoming loneliness and relationship failure, managing health threats (obesity and stress), improving the fairness of the legal system, and attaining personal happiness — with particular attention to what research reveals versus what common sense assumes.

---

## Some Basic Causes of Social Adversity — and Coping with Them

- Life is a "mixed bag" of positive events and adversities (setbacks, disappointments, obstacles, defeats).
- Social psychology offers scientifically grounded strategies for handling adversity and achieving **flourishing** — a state of psychological and social well-being beyond mere absence of illness.
- Key sources of social adversity addressed: loneliness, relationship failure, obesity, stress.
- Quote anchoring the chapter: *"Difficulty…is the nurse of greatness"* — William Cullen Bryant (1842).

## Loneliness: Life Without Relationships

### Definition and Nature of Loneliness

- **Loneliness** = the unpleasant emotional and cognitive state based on desiring close relationships but being unable to attain them; specifically, having fewer and less satisfying relationships than an individual desires (Archibald, Bartholomew, & Marx, 1995).
- Also described as **involuntary loneliness**: distinguished from voluntary social isolation (preference for solitude; Burger, 1995).
- Loneliness is **universal**: documented across many cultures (Goodwin, Cook, & Young, 2001; Rokach & Neto, 2000; Shams, 2001).
- Not merely subjective discomfort — affects psychological and **physical health** and **life expectancy**.

### The Consequences of Being Lonely

Lonely individuals tend to:
- Spend leisure time in **solitary activities**.
- Have few important connections; mostly casual acquaintances (Berg & McQuinn, 1989).
- Feel **left out** and perceive little in common with others (Bell, 1993).
- Even **one friend** is sufficient to reduce such reactions in children (Asher & Paquette, 2003).

**Negative feelings associated with loneliness:**
- Depression, anxiety, unhappiness, dissatisfaction, pessimism about the future, self-blame, shyness (Anderson, Miller, Riger, Dill, & Sedikides, 1994; Jackson, Soderlind, & Weiss, 2000; Jones, Carpenter, & Quintana, 1985).

**Social perception of lonely people:**
- Perceived by others as **maladjusted** — people to be avoided (Lau & Gruen, 1992; Rotenberg & Kmill, 1992).

**Health consequences:**
- Associated with **poor health** and **lower life expectancy** (Cacioppo, Hawkley, & Berntson, 2003; Hawkley, Burleson, Berntson, & Cacioppo, 2003).

> **KEY STUDY — Jetten et al. (2010):**
> - **Researchers:** Jetten, Haslam, Pugliese, Tonks, & Haslam
> - **Method:** Studied first-year university students longitudinally — before they arrived on campus and after. Measured depression and group memberships.
> - **Finding:** The more groups students belonged to before university, the **less likely they were to become depressed** during the high-stress freshman transition. Group membership buffered against stress.

> **KEY STUDY — Jetten, Haslam, & Haslam (2011):**
> - **Participants:** Older adults.
> - **Finding:** Among older people, belonging to more groups — or even **believing** they belonged to more groups — was associated with **better self-reported health**.
> - Conclusion: Social connectedness (group membership) is a powerful determinant of health at all ages.

### Why Are Some People Lonely?

Three primary predisposing factors:

#### 1. Genetic Factors

> **KEY STUDY — McGuire & Clifford (2000):**
> - **Design:** Behavioral genetic study of loneliness in children aged 9–14. Participants: biological siblings, unrelated adoptive siblings, identical twins, fraternal twins.
> - **Findings:**
>   - Identical twins more similar in loneliness than fraternal twins → genetic similarity associated with loneliness similarity.
>   - Unrelated adoptive siblings more similar than random pairs → environmental factors also matter.
>   - Conclusion: Loneliness is **partly heritable**, but no single "loneliness gene" — relevant genes may affect depression or hostility, which then drive interpersonal rejection.

#### 2. Attachment Style

- **Fearful-avoidant** or **dismissive attachment styles** (see Ch. 7): individuals fear intimacy and avoid establishing relationships (Duggan & Brennan, 1994; Sherman & Thelen, 1996).
- Insecure attachment (generally) is associated with **social anxiety** and loneliness (Vertue, 2003).
- **Preoccupied attachment style** also correlates with less satisfying relationships.

#### 3. Lack of Early Social Learning Experiences

- Children who attended **preschool** or had early peer interactions are liked better in elementary school (Erwin & Letchford, 2003).
- Without peer interaction, children may develop **self-defeating behaviors**: avoidance, verbal aggression (teasing), physical aggression → peer **rejection** → seeds of loneliness (Johnson, Poteat, & Ironsmith, 1991; Ray, Cohen, Secrist, & Duncan, 1997).
- Without intervention, interpersonal difficulties **persist** through adolescence into adulthood (Asendorpf, 1992; Hall-Elston & Mullins, 1999).

**Summary model of loneliness (Figure 12.2):**
- Predisposing factors → Dispositional loneliness → Emotional, cognitive, and behavioral effects
  - **Emotional effects:** Depression, anxiety, unhappiness, fear of intimacy, feeling unappreciated
  - **Cognitive effects:** Pessimism, self-blame, mistrust of others, perceived lack of reciprocity
  - **Behavioral effects:** Shyness, avoidance of relationships, interpersonal aggression, teasing

### Reducing Loneliness

- Lonely individuals are typically **aware** of their social difficulties but not why they occur (Duck, Pond, & Leatham, 1994; Furr & Funder, 1998; Meleshko & Alden, 1993).
- Cannot change genes or early history, but can acquire **social skills**.

**Social skills** include:
1. Accurate **social perception** (reading others' thoughts and feelings)
2. Effective **impression management** (making good first impressions)
3. **Emotional regulation**
4. Ability to **adapt to new social situations** (Ferris, Davidson, & Perrewe, 2006)

**Intervention methods:**
1. **Videotape feedback**: Having individuals interact with strangers, then view tapes of their own behavior. Many are unaware of turn-offs (e.g., commands vs. requests, interrupting). Guided by experts in social skills (Hope, Holt, & Heimberg, 1995).
2. **Cognitive therapy**: Changing how individuals think about others; developing realistic expectations (e.g., recognizing that people are sensitive to criticism).

- Key message from Jetten et al. (2009): *"We are social animals who live in groups. For humans, membership in groups is an indispensable part of who we are and what we need to lead rich and fulfilling lives."*

## The Shattering — and Building — of Relationships

### What Makes Long-Term Relationships Happy?

- Most people marry at some point; in the U.S. and many countries, **more than 50% of marriages end in divorce or separation**.
- Yet unmarried respondents estimate only a **10% chance of divorce** for themselves (Fowers, Lyons, Montel, & Shaked, 2001) — illustrating **optimism bias**.
- **Commitment** is crucial: a strong desire to maintain a relationship regardless of forces acting against it (Frank & Branstatter, 2002).
  - Commitment based on **fear of loneliness** is less effective than commitment based on **positive rewards** of the continuing relationship.

#### Similarity and Assumed Similarity

- **Similarity** has been linked to long-term relationship success since classic studies in the 1930s (Terman & Buttenwieser, 1935a, 1935b).
- **Longitudinal research** (Caspi, Herbener, & Ozer, 1992): Couples whose relationships survive 20 years are highly similar in attitudes, values, and goals — and remain similar over time.
- **Assumed similarity**: In happy marriages, partners believe they are even MORE similar than they actually are (Byrne & Blaylock, 1963; Schul & Vinokur, 2000).
- Dating couples show **higher assumed similarity** than married couples → possibly due to **romantic illusions**.
- Both actual and assumed similarity contribute to **relationship satisfaction**.

#### Dispositional Factors

- **Narcissism**: Feeling superior, seeking admiration, lacking empathy (American Psychiatric Association, 1994). Narcissists report **less commitment** to relationships (Campbell & Foster, 2002). Two narcissists together → unlikely to have a happy relationship (Campbell, 1999). Exception to the similarity rule.
- Interpersonal behavior and **attachment style** predict success:
  - **Preoccupied** and **fearful-avoidant** styles → less satisfying relationships.
  - **Secure** or **dismissing** styles → more satisfying (Murray, Holmes, Griffin, Bellavia, & Rose, 2001).

#### Implicit Processes and Relationship Stability

> **KEY STUDY — Lee, Rogge, & Reis (2010):**
> - **Method:** Romantic partners provided their partner's first name and two partner-related words (pet name or key characteristic). Then completed a word-sorting task: press spacebar for "target" words. In one condition, positive words AND partner-related words were targets (bad words were distractors). In another condition, bad words AND partner-related words were targets.
> - **Logic:** If implicit feelings toward partner are positive, performance should be better when positive and partner-related words are targets, and worse when bad words are targets.
> - **Finding:** More **positive implicit attitudes** toward partner → **lower likelihood of relationship breakup**. Implicit feelings were **better predictors** of relationship outcome than **explicit self-reports** of relationship strength.
> - **Implication:** Subtle, subconscious feelings we cannot accurately report strongly predict relationship fate.

### Why Relationships Fail

#### Problems Between Partners

- No partner is perfect; over time, the gap between **idealized** and **actual** similarity becomes apparent (Sillars et al., 1994).
- Minor personality flaws become less tolerable over time (Felmlee, 1995; Pines, 1997).
- If initial attraction was based on uniqueness/difference → **disenchantment** likely (Felmlee, 1998).
- **98.8%** of married couples report disagreements; most report serious conflicts at least once per month (McGonagle, Kessler, & Schilling, 1992).
- Inevitable tension: desire for **independence** vs. need for **closeness** (Baxter, 1990).

#### Contingent Love — Self-Esteem Effects

> **KEY STUDY — Murray, Griffin, Rose, & Bellavia (2006):**
> - **Method:** 173 married/cohabiting couples completed self-esteem and relationship satisfaction measures. Kept **daily event diaries** for 21 days reporting personal successes, failures, felt rejection, and felt acceptance by spouse.
> - **Finding:** People **low in self-esteem** felt **less accepted and more rejected** by their partners on days when they experienced professional failures. People **high in self-esteem** did not show this pattern.
> - **Implication:** For low-SE individuals, job/school failures "spill over" into relationships, eroding relationship happiness.

### Building Stronger Relationships

#### Knowledge of Relationship-Building Behaviors

> **KEY STUDY — Turan & Vicary (2010):**
> - **Finding:** Individuals better at recognizing which actions build vs. undermine relationships were **more satisfied** in their relationships and better at choosing supportive partners.
> - **Role of attachment anxiety:** Those high in attachment anxiety (fearing rejection/abandonment) are **slower** to learn which actions help vs. harm relationships.

#### Being Positive vs. Being Constructive

> **KEY STUDY — McNulty (2010):**
> - **Finding:** Couples who **always** expressed positive expectancies, made positive attributions, and always forgave showed **larger declines in relationship satisfaction** over time relative to more balanced couples.
> - **"Honeymoon effect":** All couples show some satisfaction decline, but "always positive" couples show steeper decline — especially when facing serious problems.
> - **Reason:** The contrast between high expectations and reality becomes especially distressing.
> - **Recommendation:** A **balance** between positive focus and constructive problem-addressing is optimal. Even behaviors once considered relationship-destroying (blaming partner, withholding forgiveness) may be beneficial when couples face genuine problems.

#### Communal vs. Exchange Approach

- **Communal approach (norm)**: Each partner tries to meet the other's needs without seeking to balance benefits received (key term defined).
- **Exchange approach**: "I'll do things for you, but only if you give me equal benefit in return." Based on **social exchange** or **reciprocity**.
- **Research (Clark, Lemay, Graham, Pataki, & Finkel, 2010):** Most people perceive communal norm as ideal, but it often fades with time. Greater shift toward exchange → **lower relationship satisfaction**.
- The shift toward exchange is more likely in **securely attached** individuals than those with avoidant or anxious styles.
- **Key lesson:** Stay close to the communal ideal; shift to exchange perspective is an early warning sign that love is dying.

#### Positive Illusions in Relationships

- Most couples begin with inflated, idealized views of partners — more positive traits attributed than actually exist.

> **KEY LONGITUDINAL STUDY — Miller, Caughlin, & Huston (2003):**
> - **Design:** 168 couples studied across ~13 years (measures at 2 months, 1 year, 2 years, ~13 years of marriage). Face-to-face interviews measuring: (a) agreeable/disagreeable spouse behaviors (daily diaries in Phase 1), (b) trait ratings of partner agreeableness, (c) love.
> - **Positive illusions** measured as: degree to which partners perceived each other as more agreeable than actual behavior warranted.
> - **Finding:** Stronger positive illusions → more love reported and **smaller declines** in love over 13 years.
> - **Conclusion:** Positive illusions are "the cement of lasting relationships."

- Positive illusions may also function as **self-fulfilling prophecies** — communicating high expectations may encourage partners to live up to them.
- **Caveat (McNulty, 2010):** Avoid going too far in either direction — some balance is needed; couples facing severe problems benefit from directly addressing them.

- Good health is prerequisite for happiness and fulfillment.
- Many health variables are outside direct control (genetics, pathogens, environment), but **social factors** play a major role.
- Two main focus areas in this chapter: (1) **Obesity** and (2) **Stress**.

## Obesity: Why Its Roots Are Social as Well as Biological

### Definitions and Prevalence

- **Obesity**: Substantially exceeding one's ideal, healthy weight; clinically defined as exceeding ideal weight by at least **20%** or having a **Body Mass Index (BMI) of 30 or higher**.
- In the U.S.: approximately **66%** exceed their ideal weight.
- Global epidemic: U.S. leads worldwide obesity rankings at **30.6%** of population with BMI > 30; Japan and South Korea lowest at **3.2%** (Figure 12.7).

### Genetic/Biological Factors

- **"Feast-or-famine" evolutionary inheritance**: All humans have a tendency to store excess calories efficiently — advantageous for ancestors, problematic in calorie-abundant modern environments.

### Environmental (Social) Factors

1. **Portion size increase**: Over 30 years, restaurant portions have grown dramatically (Ritzer, 2011). Example: Coke grew from 8 oz to 32 oz; McDonald's hamburgers from 250 calories (small) to 440–540 calories (double cheeseburger/Big Mac).
2. **Reduced walking/physical activity**:
   - Fear of urban crime reduces walking.
   - Cars used for all trips.
   - Shopping malls concentrate stores with adjacent parking.
   - School buses stop at every house → children exercise less.
3. **Media campaigns**: Enticing TV, billboard, and magazine advertising for high-calorie foods increases caloric intake.
4. **Decline of sit-down family meals**: Families eat at different times and away from home → all-day snacking → body's regulatory mechanisms for eating become less effective.
5. **Socioeconomic factors**: Low-calorie healthy foods (fresh fruit/vegetables) are more expensive than high-calorie fast food → obesity more common in **lower socioeconomic/disadvantaged groups**.
6. **Social norms**: "Thin is in" yet obesity increases. Organizations like the **National Association to Advance Fat Acceptance (NAAFA)** counter anti-fat stigma.

### Research on Obesity and Self-Control

- **Baumeister, Heatherton, & Tice (1994)**: Obesity is related to **self-control** — the ability to do things known to be beneficial (sticking to diet) while avoiding harmful actions (overeating). Lessons applicable to weight control efforts.

### Social Interventions

- Michelle Obama's campaign: combat childhood obesity via healthier school food, increased exercise, restrictions on advertising high-calorie foods to children.
- Restaurant calorie-posting legislation: requiring calorie counts on menus as social influence technique.

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Can Internet Sites Help People Lose Weight?

- In the past, weight loss was a private matter (diet books in brown paper wrappers, secretive Weight Watchers membership).
- Now, growing willingness to seek help online due to normalization of being overweight.
- Internet weight-loss sites offer: advice on diet and exercise, personal logs for tracking weight/exercise/goals, comparative information ("How am I doing vs. others?"), feedback on progress.

> **KEY STUDY — Internet Weight Loss (Journal of Medical Internet Research):**
> - **Design:** Participants first completed a qualifying phase (diet + exercise information). Those who lost ≥ 9 pounds advanced to study. Then assigned to one of **three conditions**:
>   1. **Self-directed** (no outside help)
>   2. **Monthly meetings** with health counselor
>   3. **Internet-based** program (weight monitoring, diet/exercise info, bulletin board interactions)
> - **Finding:** Participants who **visited the Internet site regularly** regained the **least weight**. Self-directed and monthly-meeting groups regained more.
> - **Why it works** (consistent with behavioral change principles): specific goals, information on how to attain them, frequent feedback on progress toward them (Baumeister, Heatherton, & Tice, 1994 principles).
> - **Caveat:** Internet sites are largely **free of government oversight and safety controls** — must be approached with caution.

## Stress: Social Tactics for Reducing Its Harmful Effects

### Definition of Stress

- **Stress** = our response to events that disrupt, or threaten to disrupt, our physical or psychological functioning (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984; Taylor, 2002).

### Major Sources of Stress

- **Major stressful life events**: related to relationships with others (death of loved one, painful divorce).
- **Daily hassles**: Minor annoyances of daily life — low intensity but **high frequency**; compensate for low intensity through sheer number; also important contributors to total "stress quotient."

### Effects of Stress on Health

- People experiencing high stress are much more likely to become seriously ill (Cohen, 1998; Cohen & Janicki-Deverts, 2009; Holmes & Rahe, 1967).
- Stress contributes to a wide range of medical problems.

> **Classic Reference — Holmes & Rahe (1967):** Developed the **Social Readjustment Rating Scale (SRRS)** to quantify life stressors, linking life event changes to illness probability.

### Social Support as Stress Buffer

- **Social support**: Resources (emotional, informational, material) provided by others in times of need.
- Having a strong network of social support significantly reduces the negative effects of stress.
- Group membership buffers against stress (Jetten et al., 2010).
- **Types of social support:**
  1. **Emotional support**: Empathy, love, trust, caring.
  2. **Informational support**: Advice, guidance, suggestions.
  3. **Instrumental (tangible) support**: Direct assistance, material aid.

### Social Tactics for Reducing Stress

1. **Seeking social support**: Turning to others; especially important for women who exhibit a **"tend and befriend"** response pattern under stress (Taylor, 2002) rather than the classic "fight-or-flight."
2. **Reframing/Cognitive reappraisal**: Changing how stressors are interpreted (e.g., challenge vs. threat appraisal).
3. **Prosocial behavior**: Helping others as a means of reducing one's own stress.
4. **Positive emotions**: Generating positive affect helps undo the physiological effects of stress.

### Tend and Befriend Response (Taylor, 2002)

- **"Tend and befriend"**: Under stress, women (and some men) tend to offspring and affiliate with others for protection, rather than the male-typical "fight or flight."
- Proposed to have evolutionary underpinnings — tending protects offspring; befriending creates social networks.
- Mediated in part by **oxytocin**, which is enhanced by estrogen and may promote affiliative behavior.

## Making the Legal System More Open, Fair, and Effective: The Social Side of the Law

### Overview

- The legal system makes assumptions about human behavior that social psychological research shows are often **incorrect**.
- Two main focus areas:
  1. **Social influence processes** in the legal context (confessions, eyewitness testimony, jury decision-making).
  2. **Prejudice and stereotypes** influencing legal outcomes.

---

## Social Influence and the Legal System

### Eyewitness Testimony

- **Eyewitness testimony**: Accounts of witnessed events given in legal proceedings. Widely believed to be highly accurate but research shows it is **highly fallible**.
- **Weapon focus effect**: When a weapon is present, witnesses focus on it and away from the perpetrator's face → reduced accuracy in identification.
- **Cross-race effect (Other-race effect / Own-race bias)**: People are better at recognizing faces of their own racial group than those of other racial groups.
  - More errors in cross-race identification.
  - Leads to wrongful convictions.

### Police Lineups

- **Police lineup**: A procedure in which an eyewitness views a set of individuals (live or in photos) to identify a perpetrator.
- **Sequential lineup**: Suspects shown one at a time; witness makes a yes/no decision on each.
- **Simultaneous lineup**: All suspects shown at once; witness makes a relative judgment (who looks most like the person?).
- Research (Wells, 1993): **Sequential lineups** reduce false identifications relative to simultaneous lineups because they discourage **relative judgment** (choosing the person who looks most like the perpetrator, even if the actual perpetrator is absent).
- **Blind administration**: Lineup administrator should not know which person is the suspect → prevents unintentional cueing of the witness.
- **Foils/fillers**: Lineup members who are known to be innocent should match witness description of the perpetrator.

### Confessions and False Confessions

- Legal system assumes confessions are reliable indicators of guilt.
- **False confessions**: Admissions of guilt for crimes one did not commit.
  - **Voluntary false confessions**: Without external pressure (e.g., to protect someone).
  - **Compliant false confessions**: Person confesses to end an aversive interrogation situation.
  - **Internalized false confessions**: Person comes to believe they actually committed the crime.
- **Reid Technique**: Commonly used police interrogation method emphasizing psychological pressure, minimization (downplaying seriousness), and maximization (exaggerating consequences) — criticized for producing false confessions.
- **PEACE Model** (alternative): Planning and Preparation, Engage and Explain, Account, Closure, Evaluate — less coercive; used in UK.
- Role of **sleep deprivation** and stress in increasing false confession rates.

### Jury Decision-Making

- **Jury nullification**: When a jury returns a verdict contrary to the evidence, based on disagreement with the law or sympathy for the defendant.
- **Deliberation process**: Juries often begin deliberations with a vote; the initial majority view usually prevails (**majority wins rule**).
- **Evidence-driven vs. verdict-driven deliberation**:
  - **Verdict-driven**: Jury takes an early vote and builds arguments to support that verdict.
  - **Evidence-driven**: Jury reviews all evidence before taking a vote (associated with more thorough discussion).
- **Death-qualified juries**: Juries in capital cases from which people who oppose the death penalty have been excluded. These juries are more **conviction-prone** than typical juries.

### Inadmissible Evidence

- When judges instruct juries to **ignore** inadmissible evidence (e.g., inflammatory prior record), jurors often **cannot** comply with this instruction.
- **Backfire effect**: In some cases, telling jurors to ignore information makes them focus on it more (thought suppression rebound).
- **Ironic processes of mental control** (Wegner, 1994): Attempts to suppress thoughts can paradoxically increase their accessibility.

## The Influence of Prejudice and Stereotypes on the Legal System

### Defendant Characteristics

- **Attractiveness bias**: Physically attractive defendants receive lighter sentences than less attractive defendants for the same crime (e.g., Stewart, 1980; Downs & Lyons, 1991).
- **Race/ethnicity bias**: Members of minority groups (especially African Americans) receive harsher sentences, especially in crimes involving White victims.
- **"Black = Criminal" stereotype** (Eberhardt et al., 2004): The more a Black defendant's appearance matches racial stereotypes (Afrocentric features), the more likely death sentences are recommended when the victim is White.

> **KEY STUDY — Eberhardt, Davies, Purdie-Vaughns, & Johnson (2004):**
> - Examined actual criminal cases in Philadelphia where defendants were Black and convicted of murdering a White victim.
> - Found a significant correlation: the more "stereotypically Black" the defendant's physical appearance, the more likely they were to receive the **death sentence**.
> - Demonstrated implicit racial bias in legal decision-making.

### Stereotype Threat in the Courtroom

- **Stereotype threat**: Concern about confirming negative stereotypes about one's group, which can impair performance.
- Jurors from stereotyped groups may be influenced by awareness of how their group is perceived.

### Reducing Bias in the Legal System

1. **Blind lineup administration** — administrator doesn't know who the suspect is.
2. **Sequential rather than simultaneous lineups**.
3. **Jury instructions** — though imperfect, some do reduce bias.
4. **Diversity on juries** — more demographically diverse juries may reduce racial bias.
5. **Recording interrogations** — reduces coercive techniques and false confessions.
6. **Expert witness testimony** on eyewitness fallibility and psychological factors.

## Personal Happiness: What It Is, and How to Attain It

### Overview

- **Subjective well-being (SWB)** = the overall evaluation of one's life in terms of positive affect, negative affect, and life satisfaction — the scientific construct closest to "happiness."
- Components of SWB:
  1. **Life satisfaction** (cognitive component): Global evaluation of one's life as a whole.
  2. **High positive affect**: Frequent positive emotions.
  3. **Low negative affect**: Infrequent negative emotions.
- The chapter uses "happiness" and "personal happiness" to refer broadly to SWB.

---

## How Happy Are People Generally?

- Most people, when asked, report being **moderately happy** — above the midpoint of happiness scales.
- The **positivity offset**: Even in neutral situations, most people experience slightly more positive than negative affect.
- Surveys across many countries consistently show that **most people describe themselves as fairly happy** (Diener & Diener, 1996).
- This is true even for people with disabilities, chronic illness, or other adversities — suggesting humans are **more resilient** than commonly assumed.
- **Adaptation level** / **hedonic adaptation**: People adjust to changes in their circumstances (positive or negative) and return to a relatively stable level of happiness — the **hedonic treadmill**.

---

## Factors That Influence Happiness

### Social Relationships

- **Strong social relationships** are one of the most consistent predictors of happiness.
- Married people, on average, report greater happiness than unmarried people (though this gap has narrowed in recent decades).
- Quality of relationships matters more than quantity.

### Work and Goals

- **Progress toward meaningful goals** is more important for happiness than simply attaining them.
- **Flow** (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990): A state of complete absorption in a challenging activity that matches one's skill level — associated with high subjective well-being.
- Work that is meaningful and provides **autonomy, competence, and relatedness** (Self-Determination Theory; Deci & Ryan) promotes happiness.

### Health

- Physical health is positively correlated with happiness, though the relationship is complex.
- **Hedonic adaptation**: People with chronic illness adapt over time, often returning to near-baseline happiness levels.

### Religion/Spirituality

- Religious belief and participation correlate positively with happiness in many studies.
- May provide social support, sense of purpose, and meaning.

### Personal Characteristics

- **Dispositional optimism**: Tendency to expect positive outcomes (Scheier & Carver, 1985) — strongly correlated with higher subjective well-being.
- **Self-esteem**: Higher self-esteem associated with greater happiness.
- **Extraversion**: Extraverts consistently report higher happiness levels than introverts.
- **Neuroticism**: Higher neuroticism (emotional instability) strongly predicts **lower** happiness.
- **Sense of personal control** (locus of control): Internal locus correlates with greater happiness.

### Genetics

- **Set-point theory**: Each individual has a genetically determined baseline level of happiness to which they tend to return after positive or negative life events (Lykken & Tellegen, 1996).
- Twin studies: approximately **50%** of variance in happiness is attributable to genetic factors.

---

## Wealth: An Important Ingredient in Personal Happiness?

- Common sense suggests more money → more happiness. Research provides a nuanced answer.

### Income and Happiness

- **Easterlin Paradox** (Easterlin, 1974): Within a country, wealthier people are happier than poorer people, BUT as countries become wealthier over time, average happiness does NOT increase.
- Money matters most at lower income levels — moving out of poverty provides large happiness gains.
- Beyond a certain threshold (estimated at ~$75,000/year household income in U.S. by Kahneman & Deaton, 2010), additional income yields **diminishing returns** on emotional well-being.
- **Life satisfaction** (vs. daily emotional well-being) continues to rise with income beyond this threshold.

### Why Wealth Doesn't Buy as Much Happiness as Expected

1. **Adaptation**: People adapt to increased wealth; it no longer provides a "boost."
2. **Social comparison**: Wealth is relative — people compare to others at similar or higher income levels. If everyone gets richer, no one feels wealthier in relative terms.
3. **Shifting aspirations**: As income rises, so do expectations — the "hedonic treadmill."
4. **Opportunity costs of wealth**: More money often means less time for relationships, leisure, and other happiness-producing activities.

### The "Experience vs. Material Goods" Research

- **Experiential purchases** (vacations, concerts, meals) tend to produce more lasting happiness than **material purchases** (gadgets, clothing) — Van Boven & Gilovich (2003).
- Experiences are harder to compare unfavorably against others' purchases; they become part of identity; social connection during experiences.

## Is Happiness Having What You Want, or Wanting What You Have?

### Affective Forecasting and the "Impact Bias"

- **Affective forecasting**: Predicting how future events will make us feel.
- **Impact bias** (Wilson & Gilbert, 2003): People **overestimate** both the intensity and duration of their emotional reactions to future events (positive or negative).
- We are poor predictors of our own future happiness.
- **Focalism**: When imagining a future event, we focus too much on that event and ignore other factors in daily life that will moderate emotional reactions.
- **Immune neglect**: People fail to anticipate how effectively their **psychological immune system** (cognitive processes that rationalize, normalize, and frame events positively) will help them cope.

### Wanting vs. Having

- **Adaptation level theory** (Helson, 1964): Happiness is assessed relative to a personal standard (adaptation level) that shifts with experience.
- Continually desiring more (the "hedonic treadmill") prevents satisfaction.
- **Gratitude**: Recognizing and appreciating what one already has is associated with increased happiness (Emmons & McCullough, 2003).

> **KEY STUDY — Emmons & McCullough (2003):**
> - Participants randomly assigned to write weekly about: (a) things they were grateful for, (b) daily hassles, or (c) neutral life events.
> - Gratitude group reported **higher levels of positive affect, life satisfaction, fewer physical complaints**, and **more prosocial behavior**.
> - Demonstrates that deliberately cultivating gratitude produces measurable happiness benefits.

## The Benefits of Happiness

- Being happy is not merely pleasant — it confers **real, measurable advantages**.

### Physical Health Benefits

- Happy people live **longer** (Danner, Snowdon, & Friesen, 2001 — the "Nun Study").

> **KEY STUDY — Danner, Snowdon, & Friesen (2001) — "Nun Study":**
> - Analyzed personal essays written by nuns in their 20s (entering a convent in the 1930s–40s) for emotional content.
> - Nuns who expressed more positive emotions in their essays lived on average **7–10 years longer** than those who expressed fewer positive emotions.
> - Controls for socioeconomic status, lifestyle, diet (nuns live similarly), making it a powerful demonstration of the longevity–happiness link.

- Happy people have **stronger immune systems** (Cohen et al., 2003).
- Happy people have **lower cardiovascular disease risk**.
- Happy people recover more quickly from illness.

### Psychological and Social Benefits

- Happy people are **more creative** and show **broader cognitive scope** (Fredrickson's Broaden-and-Build Theory).
- Happy people are **more helpful and prosocial**.
- Happy people are **better liked** and have higher-quality relationships.
- Happy people perform better at work — higher productivity, creativity, and earnings.

### Broaden-and-Build Theory (Fredrickson, 1998, 2001)

- **Positive emotions broaden** one's **thought-action repertoires** — widening the array of thoughts, actions, and behaviors that come to mind.
- This broadening **builds enduring personal resources** — cognitive, social, psychological, and physical.
- Contrast with negative emotions, which **narrow** thought-action repertoires (focusing attention and behavior for immediate coping).
- **Upward spiral**: Positive emotions build resources that increase resilience, which generates more positive emotions.

## Can We Increase Personal Happiness?

### The "Happiness Pie" Model (Lyubomirsky, Sheldon, & Schkade, 2005)

- Total happiness variance can be attributed to three factors:
  1. **Set point (genetic baseline)**: ~50%
  2. **Life circumstances** (income, marital status, geography): ~10%
  3. **Intentional activities** (behaviors and practices we choose): ~40%
- Key finding: Life circumstances (which most people believe determine happiness) account for only ~10% of variance.
- Intentional activities account for **40%** — meaning **we have significant control** over our own happiness through our choices.

### Evidence-Based Strategies for Increasing Happiness

1. **Counting blessings / Gratitude exercises** (Emmons & McCullough, 2003): Writing about things one is grateful for increases happiness.
2. **Performing acts of kindness** (Lyubomirsky, Sheldon, & Schkade, 2005; Dunn, Aknin, & Norton, 2008): Performing **five acts of kindness in a single day** once per week increased well-being for 6 weeks.

> **KEY STUDY — Dunn, Aknin, & Norton (2008): "Spending Money on Others":**
> - Participants randomly assigned to spend a small windfall on themselves or on others.
> - Those who spent money **on others** reported **greater happiness**.
> - Demonstrated that **prosocial spending** increases well-being — counterintuitive to most people.

3. **Savoring positive experiences**: Deliberately attending to and appreciating positive events, preventing adaptation.
4. **Pursuing meaningful goals** rather than just pleasurable ones.
5. **Meditation / mindfulness practices**: Associated with reduced stress and increased positive affect.
6. **Physical exercise**: One of the most reliable short- and long-term mood elevators.
7. **Social connection**: Investing in relationships and social activities.
8. **Avoiding negative social comparisons**: Shifting focus away from how one compares to better-off others.
9. **Expressing optimism**: Writing about one's "best possible self" increases positive affect.

### Why Strategies Must Be Varied (Adaptation and Boredom)

- **Variety is important**: Performing the same kindness act repeatedly reduces its happiness benefit due to adaptation.
- Lyubomirsky et al. found that performing five acts of kindness on **one day per week** was more effective than spreading them across the week — concentration preserved novelty.
- Rotating between different happiness-boosting activities prevents adaptation to any single one.

### Person-Activity Fit

- Happiness strategies work best when they match the individual's **strengths, values, and interests** (Lyubomirsky et al., 2005).
- Example: An introverted person may benefit more from acts of kindness done in writing; an extrovert may benefit more from in-person helping.

## EMOTIONS AND PERSONAL HAPPINESS: Is It Possible to Be Too Happy?

- Common assumption: More happiness is always better. Research says: **not necessarily**.

### Evidence That Extreme Happiness Can Be Counterproductive

- **Gruber, Mauss, & Tamir (2011)**: Reviewed evidence showing that very high levels of positive affect or happiness can have costs:
  1. **Risk-taking**: Extremely happy people may take excessive risks because they fail to adequately appraise threats.
  2. **Reduced motivation**: Extreme happiness may reduce the drive to improve or work harder.
  3. **Maladaptive timing**: Feeling very positive emotions in situations that call for negative ones (e.g., feeling happy after a personal defeat) can be maladaptive.
  4. **Mania**: Extremely elevated mood is a symptom of **bipolar disorder** — pathological happiness.
  5. **Moderate negative emotions** (e.g., sadness, anxiety) serve important adaptive functions: they signal problems, motivate correction, promote careful thinking, and enhance empathy.

### Optimal Levels of Happiness

- Happiness is **not always better** at higher intensities for certain outcomes:
  - **Academic performance**: Moderate positive affect may produce better academic outcomes than very high positive affect (Oishi, Diener, & Lucas, 2007), because very happy students may not be sufficiently motivated to study hard.
  - **Creativity**: Moderate positive affect often facilitates creativity more than extreme positive affect.
  - **Vigilance**: Some negative affect promotes more careful, systematic information processing.

- **Emodiversity** (Quoidbach et al., 2014): Having a rich variety of emotional experiences (both positive AND negative) predicts better mental and physical health than experiencing predominantly one emotion (even if that emotion is positive).

### Conclusion on "Too Happy"

- The optimal level of happiness is **moderately high**, not maximal.
- Different outcomes require different emotional states; **flexibility** and **appropriate emotional response** to situations matter more than consistently high positive affect.
- Social psychology supports the conclusion that human emotional life is richest and most adaptive when it includes a **full range of emotions** experienced at appropriate times.

## Summary and Key Integrative Themes

1. Social psychology is not merely an academic endeavor — it has **direct practical applications** to human adversity and happiness.
2. **Loneliness** has genetic, attachment, and social learning roots; it is harmful to health and can be reduced through social skills training.
3. **Relationship success** depends on similarity, positive (but not excessive) illusions, communal norms, commitment, and balanced realism.
4. **Health** (obesity, stress) has significant social determinants, including social norms, media influence, and social support.
5. The **legal system** is vulnerable to social psychological biases (eyewitness fallibility, juror prejudice, false confessions) that can be mitigated by evidence-based reforms.
6. **Happiness** is less determined by wealth and circumstances than most believe; intentional activities (gratitude, kindness, savoring) offer meaningful control.
7. **Extreme happiness** can be counterproductive; moderate positive affect with emotional flexibility and diversity is optimal.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Loneliness** | The unpleasant emotional and cognitive state based on desiring close relationships but being unable to attain them; having fewer and less satisfying relationships than desired (Archibald, Bartholomew, & Marx, 1995) |
| **Involuntary loneliness** | Social isolation not by choice; distinguished from preferred solitude |
| **Flourishing** | State of optimal psychological and social functioning beyond mere absence of illness |
| **Assumed similarity** | In relationships, the belief that one's partner is more similar to oneself than is actually the case |
| **Communal approach (norm)** | Relationship principle: each partner tries to meet the other's needs without seeking to balance benefits received |
| **Exchange approach** | Relationship principle based on reciprocity: benefits provided must be balanced between partners |
| **Positive illusions** | In relationships, holding idealized, inflated beliefs about one's partner that exceed actual evidence |
| **Commitment** | A strong desire to maintain a relationship regardless of forces acting to end it |
| **Stress** | Our response to events that disrupt, or threaten to disrupt, our physical or psychological functioning (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984) |
| **Daily hassles** | Minor annoyances of daily life that contribute to cumulative stress load |
| **Obesity** | Condition of substantially exceeding ideal healthy weight; clinically defined as BMI ≥ 30 or exceeding ideal weight by ≥ 20% |
| **Body Mass Index (BMI)** | Weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared; BMI ≥ 30 = obese |
| **Social support** | Resources (emotional, informational, material) provided by others in times of need |
| **Tend and befriend** | Stress response pattern (especially in women) of nurturing offspring and seeking social affiliation, rather than fight-or-flight (Taylor, 2002) |
| **Eyewitness testimony** | Accounts of witnessed events given in legal proceedings; highly fallible despite appearing credible |
| **Weapon focus effect** | Tendency for eyewitnesses to focus on a weapon rather than the perpetrator's face, reducing identification accuracy |
| **Cross-race effect (own-race bias)** | Better recognition of faces from one's own racial group than other racial groups |
| **Sequential lineup** | Police procedure in which suspects are shown one at a time; reduces false identifications |
| **Simultaneous lineup** | Police procedure in which all suspects are shown at once; promotes relative judgment and false identifications |
| **False confession** | An admission of guilt for a crime one did not commit |
| **Compliant false confession** | False confession made to escape an aversive interrogation situation |
| **Internalized false confession** | False confession in which the person comes to actually believe they committed the crime |
| **Death-qualified jury** | Jury in a capital case from which anti-death-penalty people have been excluded; more conviction-prone |
| **Jury nullification** | Jury verdict contrary to evidence, based on disagreement with the law or sympathy for defendant |
| **Subjective well-being (SWB)** | Scientific measure of happiness comprising life satisfaction, high positive affect, and low negative affect |
| **Life satisfaction** | Cognitive component of SWB: a global evaluation of one's life as a whole |
| **Hedonic treadmill (hedonic adaptation)** | Tendency to return to a relatively stable level of happiness following positive or negative life events |
| **Set-point theory** | Theory that each individual has a genetically determined baseline happiness level to which they tend to return |
| **Affective forecasting** | Predicting how future events will make us feel |
| **Impact bias** | Tendency to overestimate both intensity and duration of emotional reactions to future events (Wilson & Gilbert, 2003) |
| **Focalism** | Focusing too narrowly on a future event and ignoring other daily factors when making affective forecasts |
| **Immune neglect** | Failure to anticipate how effectively the psychological immune system will help one cope with negative events |
| **Psychological immune system** | Cognitive processes that help people rationalize, normalize, and reframe adversity positively |
| **Easterlin Paradox** | Within a country, wealthier people are happier, but as countries become wealthier over time, average happiness does not rise |
| **Experiential purchase** | Spending on experiences (travel, concerts) which tends to yield more lasting happiness than material goods |
| **Broaden-and-Build Theory** | Fredrickson's theory that positive emotions broaden thought-action repertoires and build enduring personal resources |
| **Flow** | Csikszentmihalyi's concept: complete absorption in a challenging activity matching one's skill level; associated with high well-being |
| **Gratitude** | Recognizing and appreciating what one already has; associated with increased happiness and prosocial behavior |
| **Dispositional optimism** | Stable tendency to expect positive outcomes; strongly correlates with higher well-being (Scheier & Carver, 1985) |
| **Emodiversity** | Having a rich variety of both positive and negative emotional experiences; associated with better health (Quoidbach et al., 2014) |
| **Person-activity fit** | The principle that happiness-boosting strategies work best when matched to an individual's strengths and interests |
| **NAAFA** | National Association to Advance Fat Acceptance — organization countering anti-fat stigma |
| **Reid Technique** | Commonly used but criticized police interrogation method emphasizing psychological pressure, minimization, and maximization |
| **PEACE Model** | Less coercive police interrogation model used in UK: Planning & Preparation, Engage & Explain, Account, Closure, Evaluate |
| **Self-control** | Ability to do things known to be beneficial while avoiding actions known to be harmful (Baumeister et al., 1994); related to obesity |
| **Weapon focus effect** | Eyewitness attentional bias toward a weapon at the expense of perpetrator features |
| **Social Readjustment Rating Scale (SRRS)** | Scale developed by Holmes & Rahe (1967) quantifying stress from life events |
| **Stereotype threat** | Concern about confirming a negative stereotype about one's group, which can impair performance |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Definition of loneliness** (Archibald et al., 1995) = desiring more/better relationships than one has; involuntary vs. voluntary isolation distinction.

2. **Jetten et al. (2010) study** on first-year university students: group membership BEFORE university predicted LESS depression after arrival — group membership buffers stress. This is a highly specific MCQ-testable finding.

3. **McGuire & Clifford (2000) behavioral genetics study**: Identical twins more similar in loneliness than fraternal twins; loneliness is partly **heritable** (no single "loneliness gene").

4. **Lee, Rogge, & Reis (2010)**: **Implicit attitudes** toward partner (measured via word-sorting reaction time task) were **better predictors** of relationship breakup than **explicit self-reports** — a classic implicit-vs-explicit finding.

5. **McNulty (2010)**: Couples who are **always positive** (always express positive expectancies, always forgive) show **greater declines in relationship satisfaction** over time than balanced couples — "too much of a good thing" principle applies to relationships.

6. **Communal vs. exchange approach** (Clark et al., 2010): Communal approach (meeting partner's needs without balancing) predicts higher satisfaction; shift toward exchange is an early warning sign the relationship is deteriorating.

7. **Positive illusions** (Miller et al., 2003): Couples who hold positive illusions about each other (perceive partner as more agreeable than actual behavior warrants) show **less decline in love over 13 years** — positive illusions are "cement" of lasting relationships.

8. **Obesity BMI definition**: BMI ≥ 30, or exceeding ideal weight by ≥ 20%; U.S. has ~66% above ideal weight, ~30.6% obese (global rank #1).

9. **Happiness "pie" (Lyubomirsky et al., 2005)**: Set point = **50%**, life circumstances = **10%**, intentional activities = **40%** — the counterintuitive result that circumstances barely matter while intentional activities matter most.

10. **Impact bias** (Wilson & Gilbert, 2003): People overestimate intensity AND duration of emotional reactions to future events in affective forecasting.

11. **Danner, Snowdon, & Friesen (2001) "Nun Study"**: Positive emotions in early-life writing predicted **7–10 years longer life expectancy** — direct evidence linking positive affect to longevity.

12. **Broaden-and-Build Theory** (Fredrickson): Positive emotions broaden thought-action repertoires AND build enduring personal resources (cognitive, social, physical, psychological) — produces an upward spiral.

13. **Easterlin Paradox**: Within countries, richer people are happier, but national wealth increases do NOT increase average national happiness over time.

14. **Sequential vs. simultaneous lineup** (Wells, 1993): Sequential lineups reduce false identifications because they prevent the "relative judgment" strategy (choosing the person who looks MOST like the perpetrator even if the real one is absent).

15. **"Too Happy" paradox** (Gruber et al., 2011; Oishi et al., 2007): Extreme happiness is associated with increased risk-taking, reduced motivation, poorer academic performance, and maladaptive emotional responses — **moderate high** happiness (not maximum) is optimal. This is highly likely to appear as a "surprising finding" MCQ.
