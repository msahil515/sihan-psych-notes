# 3 Social Perception: Perceiving and Understanding Others

**Chapter Overview:** Social perception is the process through which we seek to know and understand other people. The chapter covers three major processes: (1) nonverbal communication — reading others' emotions through facial expressions, eye contact, body language, touch, and scent; (2) attribution — inferring the causes of others' behavior; (3) impression formation and impression management — how first impressions are formed and controlled. Accurate social perception is foundational to all social life.

---

## Nonverbal Communication: The Unspoken Language of Expressions, Gazes, Gestures, and Scents

**Social perception**: The process through which we seek to know and understand other people.

**Nonverbal communication**: Communication between individuals that does not involve the content of spoken language; relies on facial expressions, eye contact, and body language.

**Attribution**: The process through which we seek to identify the causes of others' behavior and so gain knowledge of their stable traits and dispositions.

**Impression formation**: The process through which we form impressions of others.

**Impression management (self-presentation)**: Efforts by individuals to produce favorable first impressions on others.

- Temporary factors (moods, emotions, fatigue, illness, drugs, biological processes such as the menstrual cycle) affect social behavior and thought.
- People are sometimes unwilling to reveal inner feelings — e.g., negotiators hide reactions; salespeople mask true feelings.
- Nonverbal behavior is relatively **irrepressible** (DePaulo et al., 2003) — difficult to control; inner feelings "leak out" even when people try to suppress them.

---

## Nonverbal Communication: The Basic Channels

Research identifies **five basic channels** of nonverbal communication:
1. Facial expressions
2. Eye contact (gaze/stare)
3. Body movements and posture
4. Touching
5. (Scent — treated separately below)

---

### Facial Expressions as Clues to Others' Emotions

- Roman orator **Cicero** (quoted): "The face is the image of the soul."
- **Five basic emotions** clearly represented on the human face from very early in life (Izard, 1991; Rozin, Lowery, & Ebert, 1994):
  1. Anger
  2. Fear
  3. Happiness
  4. Sadness
  5. Disgust
- **Surprise** has been proposed but evidence is mixed (Reisenzein, Bordgen, Holtbernd, & Matz, 2006) — NOT established as a sixth universally recognized basic facial emotion.
- The number of unique facial expressions is immense because emotions occur in combinations and vary in degree.

**Universality of Facial Expressions:**
- Early research: Ekman & Friesen (1975) — facial expressions are universal (recognized across cultures).
- Later: Effenbein & Ambady (2002) — largely confirmed universality.
- Shaver, Murdaya, & Fraley (2001): smiles, frowns, sadness signs recognized across many cultures.
- Dissenting: Russell (1994); Carroll & Russell (1996) — results not entirely consistent.
- Conclusion: facial expressions are unlike spoken languages — they need little translation across cultures.

> **KEY STUDY — Olympic Athletes (Matsumoto & Willingham, 2006):**
> - Photos of Olympic athletes taken at: match conclusion, medal ceremony, posing for photographers.
> - **Findings:**
>   - Gold medal winners: almost all smiled openly and clearly.
>   - Bronze medalists: most smiled (happy to have won any medal at all).
>   - Silver medalists: very few smiled; showed sadness instead.
>   - Explanation: silver medalists engaged in **counterfactual thinking** ("if only I had done X differently, I could have won gold"), creating self-induced unhappiness.
>   - When posing: gold and bronze medal winners showed **true (Duchenne) smiles**; silver medal winners showed **social smiling** (contextually required, not reflecting genuine happiness).
> - **Significance:** Facial expressions reliably reflect underlying emotional states; true smiles differ from performed social smiles.

- Close friends are better at reading each other's subtle nonverbal cues than strangers (Zhang & Parmley, 2011).

---

### Gazes and Stares: Eye Contact as a Nonverbal Cue

- Ancient poets: eyes are "windows to the soul."
- High level of gazing = interpreted as **liking or friendliness** (Kleinke, 1986).
- Avoidance of eye contact = unfriendly, disliking, or shy.

**Staring**: A form of eye contact in which one person continues to gaze steadily at another regardless of what the recipient does.
- Staring is interpreted as **anger or hostility** ("cold stare").
- People may quickly **terminate social interaction** with a staring person (Ellsworth & Carlsmith, 1973).
- People may **leave the scene entirely** (Greenbaum & Rosenfield, 1978).
- Road rage experts: drivers should **avoid eye contact** with traffic violators who are already in a high-excitability state (Bushman, 1998).

---

### Body Language: Gestures, Posture, and Movements

**Body language**: Cues provided by the position, posture, and movement of others' bodies or body parts.

- Frequent self-directed movements (touching, rubbing, scratching) suggest **emotional arousal**; greater frequency = higher nervousness.
- Phrases like "she adopted a threatening posture" / "he greeted her with open arms" reflect how postures signal emotional states.

> **KEY STUDY — Aronoff, Woike, & Hyman (1992) — Ballet Characters:**
> - **Method:** Identified threatening/dangerous ballet characters (Macbeth, Angel of Death, Lizzie Borden) vs. warm/sympathetic characters (Juliet, Romeo). Analyzed their actual dancing postures.
> - **Prediction:** Dangerous characters = diagonal/angular postures; warm characters = rounded postures.
> - **Finding:** Strongly confirmed — posture type reliably distinguished threatening from sympathetic characters.
> - **Significance:** Large-scale body movements/postures provide information about emotions and apparent traits.

**Emblems**: Body movements carrying specific meanings in a given culture (e.g., thumbs-up, OK sign). Culture-specific — the same gesture can be meaningless or offensive in another culture. Important warning: be careful using gestures while traveling.

---

### Touching: What Does It Convey?

Meaning of touch depends on:
- Who does the touching (friend, stranger, same/other gender)
- Nature of contact (brief/prolonged, gentle/rough, body part touched)
- Context (business, social setting, doctor's office)

Touch can suggest: affection, sexual interest, dominance, caring, or aggression.

When viewed as **appropriate**, touching often produces **positive reactions** (Alagna, Whitcher, & Fisher, 1979; Levav & Argo, 2010).

> **KEY STUDY — Chaplin, Phillips, Brown, Clanton, & Stein (2000) — Handshaking:**
> - Firmer, longer, more vigorous handshakes → higher ratings on **extraversion** and **openness to experience** + more favorable first impressions.
> - Handshaking IS a meaningful and revealing form of nonverbal communication.

> **KEY STUDY — Levav & Argo (2010) — Pat on the Arm:**
> - A light, comforting pat on the arm induced feelings of **security** among both men and women — BUT only when performed by a **woman**.
> - Feelings of security then led to greater **risk-taking** in an investment task.
> - Those touched on the shoulder by a female experimenter showed greater risk-taking than those not touched or those who only shook hands.

## Scent: Another Source of Nonverbal Social Information

**Paralinguistic cues**: Changes in tone or inflection of voice apart from word meaning — also a channel of nonverbal information.

**Olfactory/chemical cues as nonverbal information:**

> **KEY STUDY — Miller & Maner (2010) — T-shirt Ovulation Study:**
> - **Method:** Women wore clean T-shirts during ovulation (days 13–15 of menstrual cycle) and post-ovulation (days 20–22). Shirts sealed in bags, presented to men who smelled them.
> - Men's **testosterone** was measured after smelling each shirt type.
> - **Findings:**
>   - Men who smelled T-shirts worn by **ovulating women** showed significantly **higher testosterone levels** than those who smelled post-ovulation shirts or clean unworn shirts.
>   - Men **could NOT consciously report** detecting differences between the scents.
>   - Yet testosterone levels still differed significantly.
> - **Significance:** Sub-conscious olfactory cues about women's fertility cycle are transmitted and detected; scent is a real channel of nonverbal social information.

## Are Facial Expressions an Especially Important Source of Information About Others?

Three lines of evidence that facial expressions are **uniquely crucial** among nonverbal channels:

**1. Almost impossible to ignore:**
- Normally, viewing visual stimuli once reduces attention on subsequent viewing (habituation). This does NOT happen for facial expressions (Blagrove & Watson, 2010).
- **Negative facial expressions** are especially persistent: an angry face in an array of faces is spotted more quickly than neutral or smiling faces.

**2. We perceive more emotion in faces than is actually there:**
- If a person's neutral face resembles an emotional expression, observers perceive that emotion even when the person is not feeling it (Zebrowitz, Kikuchi, & Fellous, 2007).
- Male faces are seen as resembling **angry expressions** more than female faces.
- Black and Korean faces are seen as resembling **happiness or surprise** more than white faces.
- These perceptions occur even without any actual emotion on the part of the target.

**3. Facial expressions can generate emotions (Facial Feedback Hypothesis):**
- William James (1894): facial expressions are not only external signs of internal states — they can also trigger/influence internal emotional experiences.

---

## The Facial Feedback Hypothesis: Do We Show What We Feel and Feel What We Show?

**Facial feedback hypothesis** (Laird, 1984): There is a close, **bidirectional** link between facial expressions and internal feelings.
- Direction 1: Internal emotions → facial expressions (standard view).
- Direction 2: Facial expressions → feed back to brain → influence subjective emotional experience.
- Summary: **We don't just show what we feel — we also feel what we show.**

> **KEY STUDY — McCanne & Anderson (1987):**
> - **Method:** Female participants imagined positive events ("inherit $1 million") and negative events ("lose a close friendship") while enhancing or suppressing tension in specific facial muscles:
>   - Muscle 1: Active during smiling/viewing happy scenes.
>   - Muscle 2: Active during frowning/viewing unhappy scenes.
>   - No visible change in facial expression required.
> - **Findings:**
>   - Suppressing smiling muscle during positive scenes → reported **less enjoyment**.
>   - Suppressing frowning muscle during negative scenes → slight tendency for **less distress**.
>   - Participants also reported **less ability to imagine and experience scenes** when suppressing muscle activity.
> - **Limitation:** Instructions to tense/relax muscles may have directly influenced self-reports (demand characteristics).

> **KEY STUDY — Davis, Senghas, Brandt, & Ochsner (2010) — Botox Study:**
> - **Design:** Two groups received cosmetic injections:
>   - **Botox group**: Botox **paralyzes** facial muscles involved in expressions.
>   - **Restylane group**: Restylane fills in wrinkles WITHOUT paralyzing muscles.
>   - Participants rated emotional reactions to positive and negative video clips:
>     - 8 days **before** injections
>     - 14–24 days **after** injections
> - **Findings:** Botox group reported **weaker emotional reactions** to both:
>   - Negative clips: less negative feeling
>   - Positive clips: less positive feeling
>   - Compared to Restylane group.
> - **Significance:** Elegant design avoiding demand characteristics. Confirms that facial muscle feedback shapes emotional experience. "Let a smile be your umbrella" — has empirical support.

## Deception: Recognizing It Through Nonverbal Cues, and Its Effects on Social Relations

**Prevalence of lying:**
- Most people tell at least **one lie per day** (DePaulo & Kashy, 1998).
- Deception occurs in approximately **20% of social interactions**.
- A majority of strangers lie to each other at least once during a brief first encounter (Feldman, Forrest, & Happ, 2002; Tyler & Feldman, 2004).
- Reasons: avoid hurting feelings ("white lies"), conceal true reactions, avoid punishment, further personal goals.

**Accuracy at detecting deception:**
- Only **slightly better than chance** at detecting lies (Ekman, 2001; Malone & DePaulo, 2001).
- Reasons for poor detection:
  1. **Truth bias**: We tend to perceive others as truthful, so we don't actively look for deception cues (Ekman, 2001).
  2. **Desire to be polite**: Makes us reluctant to discover or report deception.
  3. **Lack of attention** to relevant nonverbal cues (Etcoff, Ekman, Magee, & Frank, 2000).
  4. **Consistency assumption**: We assume truthful people in one context are truthful in all contexts (O'Sullivan, 2003).
- Paul Ekman identified a rare group who detect lies reliably **more than 80% of the time** — a heterogeneous group, not tied to any profession (Coniff, 2004).

### Nonverbal Cues to Deception (DePaulo et al., 2003)

**1. Microexpressions**: Fleeting facial expressions lasting only a **few tenths of a second**. Appear very quickly after an emotion-provoking event; difficult to suppress; highly revealing of true feelings.

**2. Interchannel discrepancies**: Inconsistencies between nonverbal cues from different channels (**channel** = type of nonverbal cue, e.g., facial expressions, body movements). Liars often control face well but fail to control body movements, or avoid eye contact.

**3. Eye contact cues**: Liars often:
   - **Blink more often**
   - Show **more dilated pupils**
   - Show unusually **low** eye contact (nervousness) OR unusually **high** eye contact (faking honesty)

**4. Exaggerated facial expressions**: Liars may show overly broad smiles, excessive sorrow, or exaggerated regret (e.g., saying "no" with exaggerated regret signals the stated reason may be false).

### Verbal/Paralinguistic Cues to Deception

**Linguistic style**: Aspects of speech apart from the meaning of words.
- **Rising voice pitch** (especially under high motivation to lie).
- **Longer response latency** — take longer to begin answering.
- Greater tendency to **start sentences, stop, and restart** (speech disruptions).

### Effects of Deception on Social Relations

> **KEY STUDY — Tyler, Feldman, & Reichert (2006):**
> - When people discovered they had been lied to: reacted with **mistrust and disliking** toward the liar.
> - More lies = more disliking + less trust.
> - **Contagion of lying**: After being exposed to a liar, people became more willing to lie themselves — not just to the liar, but to **others** as well.
> - **Implication:** Lying creates a toxic social atmosphere; once begun in a relationship or group, it is hard to reverse and leads to serious decline in mutual trust.

## EMOTIONS AND SOCIAL PERCEPTION: Cultural Differences in Inferring Others' Emotions

**Core question:** Do emotions reside *inside* individuals or *between* people in relationships?

| Culture Type | View of Emotions | Source of Emotion Inference |
|---|---|---|
| **Individualistic** (e.g., White Americans) | Emotions arise inside the individual; they are personal | Facial expressions, body language, eye contact, nonverbal cues |
| **Collectivist** (e.g., Japanese) | Emotions arise from relations with others; not in isolation | Nonverbal cues + interpersonal relationships and others' reactions |

**Example:** American wins prize → "I'm happy because of my accomplishment." Japanese wins prize → "I'm happy because my parents and friends will be proud of me."

> **KEY STUDY — Uchida, Townsend, Markus, & Bersieker (2009):**
> - **Study 1:** Content analysis of emotion words used by American and Japanese Olympic athletes in media interviews.
>   - Japanese athletes used more emotion words when questions related to **relationships with others** (e.g., "What support has your family given you?").
> - **Study 2:** American and Japanese students shown photos of medal-winning athletes standing **alone** vs. **with teammates**. Asked to describe athletes' feelings.
>   - **Predicted and confirmed:**
>     - **Japanese students**: used more emotion words when athletes shown **with teammates**.
>     - **American students**: used more emotion words when athletes shown **alone**.
> - **Significance:** The extent to which nonverbal cues are used to infer others' feelings varies by cultural context. In collectivist cultures, relationships play a central role.

Support: Mesquita & Leu (2007) — cultural differences in emotion inference confirmed in many studies.

## Attribution: Understanding the Causes of Others' Behavior

**Attribution**: Efforts to understand the causes behind others' behavior (and sometimes our own).

- We want to know not just *what* people did, but *why* — this helps us predict future behavior and understand them.
- Motivated by the basic desire to understand cause-and-effect in the social world (Pittman, 1993; Van Overwalle, 1998).
- Knowing others' level of **self-control** affects judgments of trustworthiness and predictability (Righetti & Finkenauer, 2011).

---

## Theories of Attribution: Frameworks for Understanding How We Make Sense of the Social World

### Jones & Davis's Theory of Correspondent Inference (1965)

**Correspondent inference**: A theory describing how we use others' behavior as a basis for inferring their stable dispositions.

**Core problem:** People often act due to external pressures (role requirements, situational constraints), not because behavior reflects true traits. Example: waitress smiles because the job requires it, not because she is genuinely friendly.

**Three conditions for drawing correspondent (dispositional) inferences:**

1. **Freely chosen behavior**: Ignore behavior that was forced or role-required; consider only voluntarily chosen behavior.

2. **Noncommon effects**: Focus on actions producing effects that could be caused by **one specific factor only** (not multiple). Note: "noncommon" ≠ "uncommon" (infrequent).
   - Example: Friend marrying someone beautiful but difficult and poor → can infer he values beauty above personality/wealth (noncommon effect = physical attractiveness alone explains the choice).
   - Example: Friend marrying someone beautiful, kind, wealthy, and loving → cannot infer anything specific (too many possible reasons = common effects).

3. **Low social desirability**: Behavior that is out of the ordinary (unusual) reveals more about the person than normative behavior.

**Summary:** Correspondent inferences most likely when behavior is (1) freely chosen, (2) yields noncommon effects, (3) is low in social desirability.

Updated by Jones & McGillis (1976).

---

### Kelley's Theory of Causal Attribution — Covariation Model (1972)

**Kelley (1972; Kelley & Michela, 1980):** We first ask: Did behavior stem from **internal causes** (traits, motives, intentions) or **external causes** (social/physical world)?

**Three key informational dimensions:**

| Dimension | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Consensus** | Extent to which *other people* react to the same stimulus/event in the same way as the target person. |
| **Consistency** | Extent to which *this person* responds to the same stimulus/situation the same way across different occasions. |
| **Distinctiveness** | Extent to which *this person* responds in the same manner to *different* stimuli/events. High distinctiveness = person reacts specifically to THIS stimulus only. |

**Attribution rules:**

| Consensus | Consistency | Distinctiveness | Attribution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low | High | Low | **Internal** cause (person's trait) |
| High | High | High | **External** cause (something about the stimulus) |
| Low | High | High | **Combination** of internal + external |

**Classic Example (server flirting with customer):**
- External: Many other servers flirt with this customer (high consensus) + this server flirted before (high consistency) + this server doesn't flirt with others (high distinctiveness) → this customer is very attractive.
- Internal: No other servers flirt with customer (low consensus) + this server flirted before (high consistency) + this server flirts with many customers (low distinctiveness) → this server just likes to flirt.

---

### Other Dimensions of Causal Attribution (Weiner, 1985, 1993, 1995)

Three **independent** dimensions of causal attribution:

| Dimension | Description | Stable Example | Unstable Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Internal–External** | Cause inside vs. outside person | Personality (internal) | Task difficulty (external) |
| **Stable–Unstable** | Cause persists over time vs. changes | Chronic illness (stable) | Fatigue, mood (unstable) |
| **Controllable–Uncontrollable** | Person can change/influence cause or not | Effort (controllable) | Chronic disability (uncontrollable) |

All three dimensions are considered when understanding causes of behavior.

---

### Fate Attributions Versus Personal Choice

**Fate attribution**: Attributing events to forces outside one's understanding and control — events were "simply meant to be." Based on belief in **overdetermination** (so many causes combine, no single cause is essential).

> **KEY STUDY — Norenzayan & Lee (2010):**
> - **Question:** What leads people to attribute improbable events to fate vs. personal action?
> - **Hypotheses:** Fate belief linked to (a) religious belief in God, and (b) **belief in complex causality** (many causes combine → events are overdetermined → "meant to be").
> - **Method:** Christians vs. nonreligious; European heritage vs. East Asian heritage. Read stories of improbable events (e.g., finding a diamond ring while tying a shoelace); rated fate vs. chance.
> - **Findings:**
>   - Religious persons (both European and East Asian): **more fate attributions** than nonreligious.
>   - East Asians (both religious and nonreligious): **more fate attributions** than Europeans.
>   - **Mediation:** For Christians → fate belief mediated by belief in God. For East Asians → fate belief mediated by belief in causal complexity.

---

### Action Identification and the Attribution Process

**Action identification**: The level of interpretation placed on an action.
- **Low-level**: Focuses on the action itself ("she puts change in a jar").
- **High-level**: Focuses on ultimate goals/intentions ("she is saving for her education").

> **KEY STUDY — Kozak, Marsh, & Wegner (2006):**
> - Across several studies: Actions interpreted at **higher levels** → actors perceived as having **more complex motives, goals, and thought processes**.
> - Not just WHAT people do, but our INTERPRETATION shapes perceptions of them.

## Attribution: Some Basic Sources of Error

### The Correspondence Bias / Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)

**Correspondence bias** / **Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)**: The tendency to attribute others' behavior to **dispositional (internal) causes** and to **underestimate situational (external) causes**.

> **KEY STUDY — Jones & Harris (1967) — Castro Essay:**
> - Participants read essays strongly supporting Fidel Castro.
> - Even told the writer was **assigned** to write pro-Castro (had no choice), participants still rated the writer as having **pro-Castro attitudes**.
> - Classic demonstration of FAE: situational constraint ignored; dispositional attribution made anyway.

**Why FAE occurs:**
1. The actor is more **perceptually salient** than the situation (figure vs. background).
2. **Two-step model (Gilbert, 1989):** Step 1 = automatic dispositional inference (fast, automatic). Step 2 = cognitive adjustment for situation (requires effort and cognitive resources). When cognitively busy, stop at Step 1 → dispositional error.
3. Situations are often invisible to observers (Gilbert & Malone, 1995).

---

### The Actor–Observer Effect

**Actor–observer effect**: Tendency to attribute **own** behavior to situational causes, while attributing **others'** behavior to dispositional causes.

- As actor: the situation is what you see → emphasize external causes.
- As observer: the actor is the focal point → emphasize internal causes.
- Proposed: Jones & Nisbett (1972).
- Based on both **informational differences** (actors know more about their own situational history) and **perceptual salience** differences.

**Exception:** In domains where we take pride, we may also make dispositional attributions for our successes.

---

### The Self-Serving Bias

**Self-serving bias**: Tendency to attribute **successes** to internal causes (ability, effort) and **failures** to external causes (bad luck, others' unfairness, task difficulty).

- Protects and enhances self-esteem.
- Extensively documented by Zuckerman (1979).
- **Depressive realism**: Clinically depressed individuals show **less** self-serving bias and make more accurate attributions (Alloy & Abramson, 1979) — though this is debated.

---

### The False Consensus Effect

**False consensus effect**: Tendency to believe that **others share our opinions, behaviors, and reactions** to a greater extent than they actually do.

- We assume our own reactions are normal and typical (Ross, Greene, & House, 1977).
- Example: Junk food eaters think most others eat junk food; exercisers think most others exercise.

## Applications of Attribution Theory: Insights and Interventions

### Attribution and Achievement (Weiner's Model)

Weiner's attribution model applies the three dimensions to academic outcomes:

| Attribution | Dimension | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| "I failed because I have low ability" | Internal, Stable, Uncontrollable | Learned helplessness, giving up |
| "I failed because I didn't try hard enough" | Internal, Unstable, Controllable | Motivation to try harder |
| "I failed because the test was unfair" | External, Unstable, Uncontrollable | Anger, minimal behavior change |

### Attribution and Depression

**Depressive attributional style** (Abramson, Seligman, & Teasdale, 1978): Depressed individuals attribute failure to **internal, stable, global** causes ("I failed because I'm stupid, I'm always stupid, and I'm stupid at everything").

**Learned helplessness** (Seligman): Attributing bad outcomes to stable, uncontrollable causes → person stops trying.

### Attributional Retraining

- Teaching people to change dysfunctional attributions (e.g., from "I'm dumb" to "I didn't study enough") improves motivation.
- Wilson & Linville (1982, 1985): First-year university students told grades typically improve after freshman year (external, unstable attribution for poor performance) → showed better academic performance and lower dropout rates.

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Understanding Other People Through the Internet — Attribution and Computer-Mediated Communication

**Computer-mediated communication (CMC)**: Communication via the internet, social media, text, email, etc.

- Many nonverbal cues absent in CMC (no facial expressions visible, no eye contact, no vocal tone in text).
- Creates **attribution challenges**: infer others' traits, moods, intentions from limited cues.
- People fill in missing information using **existing schemas and stereotypes**, introducing systematic bias.
- **Online disinhibition effect**: People disclose more personal information or behave uninhibitedly online due to perceived reduced observation and judgment.
- **SIDE model** (Social Identity model of Deindividuation Effects): When personal identities are less salient online, group identities become more prominent → people act more in line with group norms and stereotypes.
- People form impressions of online others quickly using: profile pictures, username, writing style, and self-disclosure patterns.
- Lying is common in online dating profiles (height, weight, age); people apply offline-calibrated attributional corrections.
- Accuracy of online impression formation: Some traits (extraversion) judged reasonably accurately; others less so.

## Impression Formation and Impression Management: Combining Information About Others

**Impression formation**: Process through which we form impressions of others.
**Impression management (self-presentation)**: Efforts to produce favorable first impressions on others.

---

## The Beginnings of Research on First Impressions: Asch's Research on Central and Peripheral Traits

> **KEY STUDY — Asch (1946) — Warm/Cold Study:**
> - **Method:** Participants read a list of traits describing a hypothetical person:
>   - "intelligent, skillful, industrious, **warm**, determined, practical, cautious"
>   - vs. "intelligent, skillful, industrious, **cold**, determined, practical, cautious"
>   - Then rated the target's other traits and formed an overall impression.
> - **Findings:**
>   - Substituting "warm" for "cold" produced dramatically different overall impressions.
>   - "Warm" target rated as more generous, happy, good-natured, humane, sociable.
>   - "Cold" target rated as calculating, less generous, less humane.
>   - Comparison: substituting "polite" for "blunt" produced only minor differences.
> - **Conclusions:**
>   - **Central traits**: "Warm–cold" — exerts disproportionately large influence on overall impressions.
>   - **Peripheral traits**: "Polite–blunt" — smaller effects on overall impressions.

**Central traits**: Traits that have a disproportionately large impact on overall impressions.

**Peripheral traits**: Traits that have relatively small effects on overall impressions.

**Theoretical implications:**
- First impressions form an overall **Gestalt** (unified whole), not a simple sum of individual traits.
- **Halo effect**: A positive (or negative) overall impression colors all specific trait judgments.
- **Primacy effect**: In Asch's subsequent research, information presented **first** has greater impact than information presented later. List of traits presented in positive-to-negative order → more positive overall impression than negative-to-positive order.
- **Recency effect**: In some conditions (long delays between information sets), **recently presented** information can dominate.

---

## How Quickly Are First Impressions Formed — and Are They Accurate?

> **KEY STUDY — Willis & Todorov (2006) — 100 Milliseconds:**
> - **Method:** Participants shown faces for varying durations (100 ms to unlimited) and asked to judge trustworthiness, competence, likeability, aggressiveness, and attractiveness.
> - **Findings:**
>   - Judgments made after **100 milliseconds (1/10 of a second)** were highly correlated with judgments made with unlimited time.
>   - More exposure time increased **confidence** but not accuracy appreciably.
> - **Conclusion:** First impressions form nearly instantaneously — in 100 ms or less.

**Thin slices of behavior** (Ambady & Rosenthal): Brief observations of behavior (as short as a few seconds) yield surprisingly accurate predictions of real-world outcomes (teacher effectiveness, relationship quality).

**Accuracy of first impressions:**
- Reasonably accurate for **extraversion** and some observable traits.
- Less accurate for intelligence, emotional stability, and less visible traits.
- People are overconfident in their impressions.
- **Physical attractiveness bias** ("what is beautiful is good"): Attractive people are attributed more positive traits overall (Dion, Berscheid, & Walster, 1972).

## Implicit Personality Theories: Schemas That Shape First Impressions

**Implicit personality theories**: Personal beliefs and assumptions about which personality traits tend to co-occur in people; cognitive schemas about personality structure.

- Everyone has their own set of such beliefs (e.g., "warm people are also generous and happy").
- These beliefs lead us to **infer unobserved traits** from observed ones — filling in our mental picture.
- Implicit (people are often unaware of holding them) and can introduce **systematic bias**.
- Cultural variation: Implicit personality theories differ across cultures.
- Related to **stereotypes**: Group-based schemas lead us to expect certain traits in group members.
- Can produce **self-fulfilling prophecies**: Expectations about a person influence how we interact with them → they behave in ways that confirm the expectation (Snyder, Tanke, & Berscheid, 1977).

## Impression Management: Tactics for "Looking Good" to Others

Major impression management tactics (Baumeister, 1982; Jones & Pittman, 1982; Schlenker, 1980):

| Tactic | Description | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Ingratiation** | Flattery, agreement, doing favors, expressing liking | Being liked |
| **Self-promotion** | Describing own accomplishments and abilities | Appearing competent |
| **Intimidation** | Conveying capacity for aggression or threats | Appearing dangerous/powerful |
| **Exemplification** | Going beyond what is required; demonstrating moral dedication | Appearing morally superior |
| **Supplication** | Emphasizing own weaknesses to elicit help | Appearing helpless; eliciting nurturance |
| **Self-handicapping** | Creating obstacles to success in advance so failure can be attributed externally | Protecting self-esteem from failure |

**Self-enhancement**: Presenting oneself in a highly positive light.

**Other-enhancement**: Tactics focused on making the target feel good (subtype of ingratiation).

## Does Impression Management Work? Does It Really Boost Impressions of the People Using It?

**General finding:** Impression management tactics **often work** — when done skillfully and subtly, they produce more favorable impressions.

- **Ingratiation** effectively increases liking — especially when flattery targets genuine insecurities or important goals (Jones, 1964).
- **Self-promotion** is effective when claims are backed by evidence.
- **Gordon (1996)**: Meta-analysis confirmed ingratiation reliably increases liking and other positive outcomes.

**Conditions for effective IM:**
1. Tactics are **subtle**, not blatant.
2. Target is not highly motivated to form an accurate impression.
3. Impression manager is skilled and experienced.

**Conditions where IM backfires:**
1. Flattery so extreme it seems unrealistic.
2. Target is aware of ulterior motives.
3. Self-promoter's claims are easily refuted.
4. Transparency of IM tactics → perceived as insincere → negative impressions.

**Online IM:**
- Social media profiles are key arenas for impression management.
- Strategic curation of photos, status updates, and self-disclosures.
- Audiences increasingly sophisticated about the performative nature of online profiles.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Five basic emotions** clearly represented in facial expressions (Izard, 1991): anger, fear, happiness, sadness, disgust. Surprise is proposed but evidence is mixed — NOT a confirmed sixth universal. This is a frequent trick question.

2. **Facial feedback hypothesis** (Laird, 1984): facial expressions BOTH reflect AND influence internal emotions (bidirectional). Key studies: McCanne & Anderson (1987) — muscle suppression; Davis et al. (2010) — Botox paralyzes facial muscles → weaker emotional reactions to both positive and negative clips.

3. **Kelley's covariation model — three variables**: Consensus, Consistency, Distinctiveness. The pattern **Low consensus + High consistency + Low distinctiveness = INTERNAL attribution**. High C + High C + High D = EXTERNAL attribution. These combinations are MCQ favorites.

4. **Correspondent inference theory** (Jones & Davis, 1965): three conditions for inferring stable dispositions: (1) freely chosen, (2) produces noncommon effects, (3) low social desirability. "Noncommon effects" ≠ "uncommon" — distinguish these.

5. **Microexpressions** last only a **few tenths of a second**; key cue for detecting deception; difficult to suppress. This time duration is a common MCQ target.

6. **Asch (1946) — Warm/Cold Study**: "warm–cold" is a **central trait** (large effect on impressions); "polite–blunt" is a **peripheral trait** (small effect). The warm/cold manipulation produced dramatic differences in overall impressions.

7. **FAE / Correspondence Bias**: overattributing others' behavior to dispositions, underweighting situations. Classic study: Jones & Harris (1967) Castro essay — people attribute pro-Castro attitudes even when writer was assigned (no choice). Two-step model (Gilbert): automatic dispositional inference → requires cognitive effort to correct for situation.

8. **Actor–observer effect**: actors → situational attributions for own behavior; observers → dispositional attributions for actor's behavior. Proposed by Jones & Nisbett (1972).

9. **Self-serving bias**: successes → internal; failures → external. Contrast with **depressive realism** (depressed individuals show less self-serving bias).

10. **Miller & Maner (2010) — T-shirt study**: men's testosterone higher after smelling ovulating women's T-shirts; men could NOT consciously detect the scent difference. Demonstrates sub-conscious olfactory nonverbal cue.

11. **Willis & Todorov (2006)**: First impressions form in as little as **100 milliseconds** (1/10 of a second). A frequently tested specific figure.

12. **Cultural differences in emotion inference** (Uchida et al., 2009): Individualistic cultures (US) → emotions inside individual, inferred from nonverbal cues. Collectivist cultures (Japan) → emotions in relationships, inferred from interpersonal context. Japanese used more emotion words when athletes shown WITH teammates.

13. **Olympic athletes study** (Matsumoto & Willingham, 2006): gold and bronze medal winners smiled; silver medalists showed sadness. Gold/bronze = true Duchenne smiles; silver = social smiles. Explained by counterfactual thinking in silver medalists.

14. **Ingratiation**: impression management tactic aimed at being liked via flattery, agreement, favors. Must be **subtle** to work; obvious ingratiation backfires. Gordon (1996) meta-analysis confirmed its effectiveness.

15. **Self-handicapping**: creating obstacles before performance so failure can be attributed to the obstacle (external), not to one's ability (internal). Protects self-esteem from failure attribution.
