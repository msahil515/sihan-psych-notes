# 3 Social Perception: Perceiving and Understanding Others

**Overview:** Social perception is how we seek to know and understand others. Covers three major processes: (1) nonverbal communication (reading emotions via facial expressions, eye contact, body language, touch, scent); (2) attribution (inferring causes of behavior); (3) impression formation and management.

---

## Nonverbal Communication: Expressions, Gazes, Gestures, and Scents

**Social perception**: The process through which we seek to know and understand other people.

**Nonverbal communication**: Communication that does not involve the content of spoken language; relies on facial expressions, eye contact, and body language.

**Attribution**: The process through which we identify the causes of others' behavior to learn their stable traits and dispositions.

**Impression formation**: The process through which we form impressions of others.

**Impression management (self-presentation)**: Efforts to produce favorable first impressions on others.

- Temporary factors (moods, emotions, fatigue, illness, drugs, the menstrual cycle) affect social behavior and thought.
- People sometimes hide inner feelings (e.g., negotiators, salespeople).
- Nonverbal behavior is relatively **irrepressible** (DePaulo et al., 2003): inner feelings "leak out" even when suppressed.

---

## Nonverbal Communication: The Basic Channels

**Five basic channels:**
1. Facial expressions
2. Eye contact (gaze/stare)
3. Body movements and posture
4. Touching
5. Scent (treated separately below)

---

### Facial Expressions as Clues to Others' Emotions

- **Cicero**: "The face is the image of the soul."
- **Five basic emotions** represented on the face from early in life (Izard, 1991; Rozin, Lowery, & Ebert, 1994): anger, fear, happiness, sadness, disgust.
- **Surprise** proposed but evidence mixed (Reisenzein et al., 2006); NOT an established sixth universal facial emotion.
- The number of unique expressions is immense because emotions combine and vary in degree.

**Universality:**
- Ekman & Friesen (1975): facial expressions are universal; confirmed by Effenbein & Ambady (2002) and Shaver, Murdaya, & Fraley (2001).
- Dissenting: Russell (1994); Carroll & Russell (1996), results not entirely consistent.
- Conclusion: expressions need little translation across cultures.

> **KEY STUDY, Olympic Athletes (Matsumoto & Willingham, 2006):**
> - Photos of Olympic athletes at match end, medal ceremony, and posing.
> - **Findings:** Gold winners almost all smiled openly; bronze medalists mostly smiled (happy to win any medal); silver medalists rarely smiled, showing sadness, due to **counterfactual thinking** ("if only... I could have won gold").
> - When posing: gold and bronze showed **true (Duchenne) smiles**; silver showed **social smiling** (not genuine happiness).
> - **Significance:** Expressions reliably reflect emotional states; true smiles differ from social smiles.

- Close friends read each other's subtle cues better than strangers (Zhang & Parmley, 2011).

---

### Gazes and Stares: Eye Contact

- High gazing = **liking or friendliness** (Kleinke, 1986); avoiding eye contact = unfriendly or shy.

**Staring**: Eye contact in which one gazes steadily at another regardless of the recipient's response.
- Interpreted as **anger or hostility** ("cold stare").
- People may quickly **terminate interaction** with a starer (Ellsworth & Carlsmith, 1973) or **leave the scene** (Greenbaum & Rosenfield, 1978).
- Road rage: drivers should **avoid eye contact** with already-aroused violators (Bushman, 1998).

---

### Body Language: Gestures, Posture, Movements

**Body language**: Cues from the position, posture, and movement of bodies or body parts.

- Frequent self-directed movements (touching, rubbing, scratching) suggest **emotional arousal/nervousness**.

> **KEY STUDY, Aronoff, Woike, & Hyman (1992), Ballet Characters:**
> - **Method:** Analyzed postures of threatening characters (Macbeth, Angel of Death, Lizzie Borden) vs. warm characters (Juliet, Romeo).
> - **Finding:** Dangerous characters used diagonal/angular postures; warm characters used rounded postures.
> - **Significance:** Large-scale postures convey emotions and apparent traits.

**Emblems**: Body movements carrying specific meanings in a given culture (e.g., thumbs-up, OK sign). Culture-specific; a gesture can be offensive elsewhere.

---

### Touching: What Does It Convey?

Meaning of touch depends on who touches, the nature of contact, and the context. Touch can suggest affection, sexual interest, dominance, caring, or aggression. When **appropriate**, touching produces **positive reactions** (Alagna, Whitcher, & Fisher, 1979; Levav & Argo, 2010).

> **KEY STUDY, Chaplin et al. (2000), Handshaking:**
> - Firmer, longer, more vigorous handshakes → higher **extraversion** and **openness** ratings plus more favorable first impressions. Handshaking is meaningful nonverbal communication.

> **KEY STUDY, Levav & Argo (2010), Pat on the Arm:**
> - A light, comforting pat on the arm induced **security** in both men and women, but only when done by a **woman**. Security then led to greater **risk-taking** in an investment task.

---

## Scent: Another Source of Nonverbal Information

**Paralinguistic cues**: Changes in tone or inflection of voice apart from word meaning.

> **KEY STUDY, Miller & Maner (2010), T-shirt Ovulation Study:**
> - **Method:** Women wore clean T-shirts during ovulation (days 13–15) and post-ovulation (days 20–22). Men smelled the shirts; **testosterone** was measured.
> - **Findings:** Men who smelled **ovulating women's** shirts showed significantly **higher testosterone** than those smelling post-ovulation or clean shirts. Men could NOT consciously detect the scent difference.
> - **Significance:** Sub-conscious olfactory cues about fertility are transmitted and detected; scent is a real nonverbal channel.

---

## Are Facial Expressions Especially Important?

Three lines of evidence that facial expressions are **uniquely crucial**:

**1. Almost impossible to ignore:**
- Habituation (reduced attention on repeat viewing) does NOT occur for facial expressions (Blagrove & Watson, 2010).
- **Negative expressions** persist: an angry face in an array is spotted faster than neutral or smiling faces.

**2. We perceive more emotion than is there:**
- Neutral faces resembling an expression are perceived as showing that emotion (Zebrowitz, Kikuchi, & Fellous, 2007).
- Male faces resemble **angry** expressions more than female faces; Black and Korean faces resemble **happiness/surprise** more than white faces.

**3. Facial expressions can generate emotions:**
- William James (1894): expressions can trigger/influence internal emotional experience (precursor to facial feedback).

---

## The Facial Feedback Hypothesis

**Facial feedback hypothesis** (Laird, 1984): a close, **bidirectional** link between facial expressions and feelings.
- Direction 1: emotions → expressions.
- Direction 2: expressions → brain → influence subjective emotional experience.
- We don't just show what we feel; we also feel what we show.

> **KEY STUDY, McCanne & Anderson (1987):**
> - **Method:** Female participants imagined positive ("inherit $1 million") and negative ("lose a close friendship") events while enhancing or suppressing specific facial muscles (smiling muscle; frowning muscle), with no visible expression change.
> - **Findings:** Suppressing the smiling muscle during positive scenes → **less enjoyment**; suppressing the frowning muscle during negative scenes → slightly **less distress**. Participants also reported less ability to imagine scenes when suppressing muscles.
> - **Limitation:** Possible demand characteristics from the muscle instructions.

> **KEY STUDY, Davis, Senghas, Brandt, & Ochsner (2010), Botox Study:**
> - **Design:** **Botox group** (paralyzes facial muscles) vs. **Restylane group** (fills wrinkles without paralysis). Rated emotional reactions to clips 8 days before and 14–24 days after injections.
> - **Findings:** Botox group reported **weaker reactions** to both negative and positive clips than the Restylane group.
> - **Significance:** Elegant design avoiding demand characteristics; confirms facial muscle feedback shapes emotional experience.

## Deception: Recognizing It and Its Effects

**Prevalence of lying:**
- Most people tell at least **one lie per day** (DePaulo & Kashy, 1998).
- Deception occurs in ~**20% of social interactions**.
- Most strangers lie at least once in a brief first encounter (Feldman, Forrest, & Happ, 2002).
- Reasons: avoid hurting feelings, conceal reactions, avoid punishment, further goals.

**Detection accuracy:**
- Only **slightly better than chance** (Ekman, 2001; Malone & DePaulo, 2001). Reasons:
  1. **Truth bias**: we perceive others as truthful (Ekman, 2001).
  2. **Desire to be polite**.
  3. **Lack of attention** to relevant cues (Etcoff et al., 2000).
  4. **Consistency assumption**: assume truthful-in-one-context = truthful everywhere (O'Sullivan, 2003).
- Ekman identified a rare group detecting lies **>80%** of the time, not tied to any profession (Coniff, 2004).

### Nonverbal Cues to Deception (DePaulo et al., 2003)

1. **Microexpressions**: fleeting facial expressions lasting only a **few tenths of a second**; appear quickly, hard to suppress, highly revealing.
2. **Interchannel discrepancies**: inconsistencies between cues from different channels (a **channel** = a type of nonverbal cue). Liars control the face but not the body.
3. **Eye contact cues**: liars often **blink more**, show **more dilated pupils**, and unusually **low** (nervousness) or **high** (faking honesty) eye contact.
4. **Exaggerated facial expressions**: overly broad smiles, excessive sorrow or regret.

### Verbal/Paralinguistic Cues

**Linguistic style**: aspects of speech apart from word meaning.
- **Rising voice pitch**; **longer response latency**; more starting, stopping, and restarting (speech disruptions).

### Effects of Deception on Social Relations

> **KEY STUDY, Tyler, Feldman, & Reichert (2006):**
> - Discovering a lie → **mistrust and disliking** of the liar; more lies = more disliking, less trust.
> - **Contagion of lying**: after exposure to a liar, people became more willing to lie themselves, even to others.
> - **Implication:** lying creates a toxic atmosphere that is hard to reverse.

## EMOTIONS AND SOCIAL PERCEPTION: Cultural Differences

**Core question:** Do emotions reside *inside* individuals or *between* people?

| Culture Type | View of Emotions | Source of Inference |
|---|---|---|
| **Individualistic** (e.g., White Americans) | Emotions arise inside the individual | Facial expressions, body language, eye contact |
| **Collectivist** (e.g., Japanese) | Emotions arise from relations with others | Nonverbal cues + relationships and others' reactions |

**Example:** American: "I'm happy because of my accomplishment." Japanese: "I'm happy because my parents and friends will be proud."

> **KEY STUDY, Uchida, Townsend, Markus, & Bersieker (2009):**
> - **Study 1:** Japanese athletes used more emotion words when questions related to **relationships**.
> - **Study 2:** Students described feelings of medal-winning athletes shown **alone** vs. **with teammates**. **Japanese** used more emotion words with teammates; **Americans** used more when alone.
> - **Significance:** Use of nonverbal cues to infer feelings varies by culture; collectivist cultures center relationships.

Support: Mesquita & Leu (2007).

## Attribution: Understanding the Causes of Behavior

**Attribution**: Efforts to understand the causes of others' (and sometimes our own) behavior.

- We want to know *why*, to predict future behavior (Pittman, 1993; Van Overwalle, 1998).
- Knowing others' **self-control** affects judgments of trustworthiness (Righetti & Finkenauer, 2011).

---

## Theories of Attribution

### Jones & Davis's Correspondent Inference (1965)

**Correspondent inference**: how we use others' behavior to infer their stable dispositions.

**Core problem:** people often act due to external pressures, not true traits (e.g., a waitress smiles because the job requires it).

**Three conditions for dispositional inferences:**
1. **Freely chosen behavior** (ignore forced/role-required behavior).
2. **Noncommon effects**: actions whose effects could be caused by **one specific factor only**. ("Noncommon" ≠ "uncommon/infrequent.")
   - Example: a friend marrying someone beautiful but difficult and poor → infer he values beauty (noncommon effect).
   - Marrying someone beautiful, kind, wealthy, and loving → can't infer (too many possible reasons = common effects).
3. **Low social desirability**: unusual behavior reveals more than normative behavior.

Updated by Jones & McGillis (1976).

---

### Kelley's Covariation Model (1972)

First ask: did behavior stem from **internal causes** (traits, motives) or **external causes** (the situation)? (Kelley, 1972; Kelley & Michela, 1980)

| Dimension | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Consensus** | Extent to which *other people* react to the same stimulus the same way as the target |
| **Consistency** | Extent to which *this person* responds the same way across different occasions |
| **Distinctiveness** | Extent to which *this person* responds the same way to *different* stimuli. High = reacts to THIS stimulus only |

| Consensus | Consistency | Distinctiveness | Attribution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low | High | Low | **Internal** cause (person's trait) |
| High | High | High | **External** cause (the stimulus) |
| Low | High | High | **Combination** of internal + external |

**Example (server flirting):**
- External: many servers flirt with this customer (high consensus) + flirted before (high consistency) + doesn't flirt with others (high distinctiveness) → customer is attractive.
- Internal: no others flirt (low consensus) + flirted before (high consistency) + flirts with many (low distinctiveness) → this server just likes to flirt.

---

### Weiner's Dimensions (1985, 1993, 1995)

Three **independent** dimensions of causal attribution:

| Dimension | Description | Stable Example | Unstable Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Internal–External** | Cause inside vs. outside person | Personality (internal) | Task difficulty (external) |
| **Stable–Unstable** | Cause persists vs. changes | Chronic illness (stable) | Fatigue, mood (unstable) |
| **Controllable–Uncontrollable** | Person can change cause or not | Effort (controllable) | Chronic disability (uncontrollable) |

---

### Fate Attributions Versus Personal Choice

**Fate attribution**: attributing events to forces beyond one's control; events were "meant to be." Based on belief in **overdetermination** (many combined causes; no single essential cause).

> **KEY STUDY, Norenzayan & Lee (2010):**
> - **Hypotheses:** fate belief linked to (a) belief in God, and (b) **belief in complex causality**.
> - **Method:** Christians vs. nonreligious; European vs. East Asian heritage. Read stories of improbable events (e.g., finding a diamond ring while tying a shoelace); rated fate vs. chance.
> - **Findings:** Religious persons made **more fate attributions** than nonreligious; East Asians made **more** than Europeans.
> - **Mediation:** Christians → mediated by belief in God; East Asians → mediated by belief in causal complexity.

---

### Action Identification

**Action identification**: the level of interpretation placed on an action.
- **Low-level**: the action itself ("she puts change in a jar").
- **High-level**: ultimate goals ("she is saving for her education").

> **KEY STUDY, Kozak, Marsh, & Wegner (2006):**
> - Actions interpreted at **higher levels** → actors perceived as having **more complex motives and thought processes**. Interpretation, not just behavior, shapes perception.

---

## Attribution: Basic Sources of Error

### Correspondence Bias / Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)

**Correspondence bias / FAE**: tendency to attribute others' behavior to **dispositional** causes and underestimate **situational** causes.

> **KEY STUDY, Jones & Harris (1967), Castro Essay:**
> - Participants read pro-Castro essays. Even when told the writer was **assigned** the position (no choice), they still rated the writer as holding **pro-Castro attitudes**. Classic FAE.

**Why FAE occurs:**
1. The actor is more **perceptually salient** than the situation.
2. **Two-step model (Gilbert, 1989):** Step 1 = automatic dispositional inference; Step 2 = effortful situational adjustment. When cognitively busy, we stop at Step 1.
3. Situations are often invisible to observers (Gilbert & Malone, 1995).

---

### The Actor–Observer Effect

**Actor–observer effect**: attributing **own** behavior to situations, **others'** behavior to dispositions (Jones & Nisbett, 1972).
- As actor, the situation is salient; as observer, the actor is the focal point.
- Based on **informational** and **perceptual salience** differences.
- **Exception:** for sources of pride, we may make dispositional attributions for our own successes.

---

### The Self-Serving Bias

**Self-serving bias**: attributing **successes** to internal causes (ability, effort) and **failures** to external causes (luck, unfairness, difficulty); protects self-esteem (Zuckerman, 1979).
- **Depressive realism**: depressed individuals show **less** self-serving bias and more accurate attributions (Alloy & Abramson, 1979; debated).

---

### The False Consensus Effect

**False consensus effect**: believing **others share our opinions and behaviors** more than they actually do; we assume our reactions are typical (Ross, Greene, & House, 1977). Example: junk-food eaters think most others eat junk food.

---

## Applications of Attribution Theory

### Attribution and Achievement (Weiner)

| Attribution | Dimension | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| "I failed because I have low ability" | Internal, Stable, Uncontrollable | Learned helplessness, giving up |
| "I failed because I didn't try hard enough" | Internal, Unstable, Controllable | Motivation to try harder |
| "I failed because the test was unfair" | External, Unstable, Uncontrollable | Anger, minimal behavior change |

### Attribution and Depression

**Depressive attributional style** (Abramson, Seligman, & Teasdale, 1978): attributing failure to **internal, stable, global** causes ("I failed because I'm stupid, always, at everything").

**Learned helplessness** (Seligman): attributing bad outcomes to stable, uncontrollable causes → stop trying.

### Attributional Retraining

- Changing dysfunctional attributions (from "I'm dumb" to "I didn't study enough") improves motivation.
- Wilson & Linville (1982, 1985): first-year students told grades typically improve (external, unstable attribution) → better performance and lower dropout.

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Attribution and CMC

**Computer-mediated communication (CMC)**: communication via internet, social media, text, email.

- Many nonverbal cues are absent, creating **attribution challenges**.
- People fill gaps using **existing schemas and stereotypes**, introducing bias.
- **Online disinhibition effect**: people disclose more or act uninhibitedly online due to perceived reduced observation.
- **SIDE model** (Social Identity model of Deindividuation Effects): when personal identity is less salient online, group identity dominates → more norm/stereotype-driven behavior.
- Impressions form quickly from profile pictures, username, writing style, self-disclosure.
- Online dating profiles often lie (height, weight, age). Some traits (extraversion) judged reasonably accurately; others less so.

## Impression Formation and Impression Management

**Impression formation**: forming impressions of others.
**Impression management (self-presentation)**: producing favorable first impressions.

---

## Asch's Central and Peripheral Traits

> **KEY STUDY, Asch (1946), Warm/Cold Study:**
> - **Method:** Read trait lists: "intelligent, skillful, industrious, **warm**, determined, practical, cautious" vs. the same with "**cold**." Rated other traits and overall impression.
> - **Findings:** Swapping warm/cold produced dramatically different impressions ("warm" → generous, happy, sociable; "cold" → calculating, less humane). Swapping "polite" for "blunt" produced only minor differences.
> - **Conclusions:** "Warm–cold" is a **central trait**; "polite–blunt" is a **peripheral trait**.

**Central traits**: traits with disproportionately large impact on overall impressions.
**Peripheral traits**: traits with relatively small effects.

**Theoretical implications:**
- First impressions form an overall **Gestalt**, not a sum of traits.
- **Halo effect**: a positive (or negative) overall impression colors all specific trait judgments.
- **Primacy effect**: information presented **first** has greater impact (positive-to-negative order → more positive overall impression).
- **Recency effect**: with long delays, **recently presented** information can dominate.

---

## How Quickly Are First Impressions Formed?

> **KEY STUDY, Willis & Todorov (2006), 100 Milliseconds:**
> - **Method:** Faces shown for varying durations (100 ms to unlimited); judged trustworthiness, competence, likeability, aggressiveness, attractiveness.
> - **Findings:** Judgments after **100 ms** correlated highly with unlimited-time judgments. More time increased **confidence**, not accuracy.
> - **Conclusion:** First impressions form in 100 ms or less.

**Thin slices of behavior** (Ambady & Rosenthal): brief observations (a few seconds) yield surprisingly accurate predictions (teacher effectiveness, relationship quality).

**Accuracy:**
- Reasonably accurate for **extraversion**; less for intelligence, emotional stability, less-visible traits.
- People are overconfident.
- **Physical attractiveness bias** ("what is beautiful is good"): attractive people are attributed more positive traits (Dion, Berscheid, & Walster, 1972).

---

## Implicit Personality Theories

**Implicit personality theories**: beliefs about which personality traits tend to co-occur; cognitive schemas about personality.

- We **infer unobserved traits** from observed ones (e.g., "warm people are also generous").
- Usually implicit and can introduce **systematic bias**; vary across cultures.
- Related to **stereotypes** (group-based schemas).
- Can produce **self-fulfilling prophecies**: expectations shape interaction → target confirms the expectation (Snyder, Tanke, & Berscheid, 1977).

---

## Impression Management: Tactics

Major tactics (Baumeister, 1982; Jones & Pittman, 1982; Schlenker, 1980):

| Tactic | Description | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Ingratiation** | Flattery, agreement, favors, expressing liking | Being liked |
| **Self-promotion** | Describing own accomplishments and abilities | Appearing competent |
| **Intimidation** | Conveying capacity for aggression or threats | Appearing dangerous/powerful |
| **Exemplification** | Going beyond what is required; moral dedication | Appearing morally superior |
| **Supplication** | Emphasizing weaknesses to elicit help | Appearing helpless; eliciting nurturance |
| **Self-handicapping** | Creating obstacles in advance so failure is attributed externally | Protecting self-esteem |

**Self-enhancement**: presenting oneself very positively.
**Other-enhancement**: making the target feel good (a subtype of ingratiation).

---

## Does Impression Management Work?

**General finding:** tactics **often work** when done skillfully and subtly.
- **Ingratiation** increases liking, especially targeting genuine insecurities/goals (Jones, 1964).
- **Self-promotion** works when backed by evidence.
- **Gordon (1996)** meta-analysis: ingratiation reliably increases liking.

**Effective when:** tactics are **subtle**; target is not motivated to be accurate; manager is skilled.

**Backfires when:** flattery is unrealistic; target sees ulterior motives; claims are refutable; tactics seem insincere.

**Online IM:** social media profiles are key arenas; strategic curation of photos and updates; audiences increasingly aware of the performance.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Five basic emotions** (Izard, 1991): anger, fear, happiness, sadness, disgust. Surprise is NOT a confirmed sixth universal (frequent trick question).
2. **Facial feedback hypothesis** (Laird, 1984): bidirectional. McCanne & Anderson (1987, muscle suppression); Davis et al. (2010, Botox → weaker reactions to positive and negative clips).
3. **Kelley's covariation, three variables**: Consensus, Consistency, Distinctiveness. **Low C + High Cons + Low D = INTERNAL**; High + High + High = EXTERNAL.
4. **Correspondent inference** (Jones & Davis, 1965): freely chosen, noncommon effects, low social desirability. "Noncommon" ≠ "uncommon."
5. **Microexpressions** last a **few tenths of a second**; key deception cue.
6. **Asch (1946)**: "warm–cold" = **central trait**; "polite–blunt" = **peripheral trait**.
7. **FAE**: overattributing to disposition. Jones & Harris (1967) Castro essay. Two-step model (Gilbert): automatic inference, effortful situational correction.
8. **Actor–observer effect** (Jones & Nisbett, 1972): own → situational; others' → dispositional.
9. **Self-serving bias**: successes internal, failures external. Contrast with **depressive realism**.
10. **Miller & Maner (2010)**: testosterone higher after smelling ovulating women's shirts; scent undetectable consciously.
11. **Willis & Todorov (2006)**: impressions form in **100 ms**.
12. **Cultural emotion inference** (Uchida et al., 2009): individualistic (US) = inside the person; collectivist (Japan) = in relationships; Japanese used more emotion words with teammates.
13. **Olympic athletes** (Matsumoto & Willingham, 2006): gold/bronze = Duchenne smiles; silver = social smiles (counterfactual thinking).
14. **Ingratiation**: aimed at being liked; must be **subtle**; Gordon (1996) meta-analysis confirmed effectiveness.
15. **Self-handicapping**: obstacles created before performance so failure is attributed externally; protects self-esteem.
