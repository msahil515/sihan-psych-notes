# 2 Social Cognition: How We Think About the Social World

Social cognition is how we interpret, analyze, remember, and use information about the social world. This chapter covers mental shortcuts (heuristics), schemas, automatic vs. controlled processing, and the mutual influence of affect and cognition, explaining both the efficiency and the systematic errors in everyday social thinking.

---

## Heuristics: How We Reduce Our Effort in Social Cognition

- Our cognitive system has finite processing capacity. When input exceeds it, we enter **information overload**.
- Stress or competing tasks deplete capacity further (Chajut & Algom, 2003).
- Under **conditions of uncertainty**, people use strategies to "stretch" resources, mainly **heuristics**: simple rules for making complex decisions rapidly and seemingly effortlessly.
- **Social cognition**: how we interpret, analyze, remember, and use social information (Fiske & Taylor, 2008).
- **Affect**: our current feelings and moods, which interact with social cognition throughout.

> Much social thought is "automatic" (quick, effortless), but unexpected events can trigger careful **controlled processing**.

---

### Representativeness: Judging by Resemblance

- **Representativeness heuristic**: making judgments based on how much current stimuli/events resemble other stimuli or categories.
- Rule: the more an individual resembles a group's **prototype** (summary of common category attributes), the more likely they seem to belong to it.

#### Ignoring Base Rates
- Representativeness judgments tend to **ignore base rates**: the actual frequency of events/patterns in the population (Kahneman & Tversky, 1973; Kahneman & Frederick, 2002).
- Example: ~900 million peaceful Muslims vs. ~9,000 Al-Qaeda members = 1 per 100,000; blaming all Muslims ignores this base rate.

#### Cause-Effect Matching
- Representativeness is also used to judge whether causes produce effects **similar in magnitude** ("like goes with like"): big effects expected from big causes.

#### Cultural Differences

> **KEY STUDY: Spina et al. (2010)**
> - **Participants**: students in China and Canada.
> - **Method**: rated likelihood that a high- or low-magnitude effect (many/few deaths) came from a high- or low-magnitude virus (resistant vs. standard).
> - **Finding**: both showed "like goes with like," but Canadians significantly more than Chinese.
> - **Conclusion**: Asians consider more causal factors and make more complex attributions, so rely less on representativeness than Westerners.

---

### Availability: "If I Can Retrieve Instances, They Must Be Frequent"

- **Availability heuristic**: judgments based on how easily information comes to mind. The easier to recall, the more frequent/probable it seems.

#### Why availability leads to errors
- Dramatic rare events (plane crashes, murders) are vivid and media-covered, so easy to retrieve and overestimated.
- Common causes of death (heart disease, stroke) are harder to retrieve and underestimated.
- SUV safety illusion: vivid images of large vehicles crushing small ones create a false belief SUVs are safer, despite higher death rates per million vehicles.

#### Two factors (Schwarz et al., 1991)
1. **Ease of retrieval**: subjective fluency with which information comes to mind.
2. **Amount of information**: how much comes to mind.
- Emotional/feeling-based judgments rely on the **ease** rule; fact-based/difficult tasks rely on the **amount** rule (Rothman & Hardin, 1997; Ruder & Bless, 2003).

#### Self vs. Other
- Ease of retrieval affects self-relevant judgments more than judgments about others; less weight is given when we know little about others/unfamiliar objects.

> **KEY STUDY: Caruso (2008), Harvard campus safety**
> - Students recalled 2 or 6 instances of feeling unsafe (self or another student).
> - Recalling 2 (easy) vs. 6 (difficult) personal instances: easy recall led to rating the town as more unsafe.
> - The ease effect did NOT apply to another person's experiences. Same pattern with creativity ratings.

---

### Anchoring and Adjustment: Where You Begin Makes a Difference

- **Anchoring and adjustment heuristic**: using a number/value as a starting point and adjusting toward an answer; adjustments are typically **insufficient**.
- Common in negotiation (high asking prices) and "sale pricing" (original price as anchor makes the discount feel like a bargain).

#### Why adjustments are insufficient
- People stop adjusting once a plausible value is reached (Epley & Gilovich, 2006), to save effort. Worse when thought is limited (alcohol, distraction).

> **KEY STUDY: Englich, Mussweiler, & Strack (2006), Legal sentencing**
> - **Participants**: experienced German legal professionals.
> - **Method**: read a court case, then got sentencing recommendations from a journalist (irrelevant), a dice throw (random), or an experienced prosecutor (relevant); recommendations were lenient (1 month probation) or harsh (3 years prison).
> - **Finding**: experts' sentences were significantly influenced by anchors regardless of source, even random dice rolls.
> - **Conclusion**: anchoring is powerful with real-world consequences, even among experts.

---

### Status Quo Heuristic: "What Is, Is Good"

- **Status quo heuristic**: judging familiar or longer-existing options as better than newer alternatives.
- Rationale: bad products get removed over time, so age may signal quality, but this can be wrong (products persist through inertia/habit).

> **KEY STUDY: Eidelman, Pattershall, & Crandall (2010)**
> - **Study 1**: same chocolate rated more delicious when said to be on the market since 1937 (70 years) vs. 2003 (3 years).
> - **Study 2**: a degree requirement favored when framed as existing vs. as a new change.
> - **Study 3**: acupuncture rated more effective the older it was said to be (250, 500, 1,000, 2,000 years).
> - **Study 4**: a painting rated more pleasing when dated 1905 vs. 2005.
> - **Insight**: participants were unaware longevity influenced them.

## Schemas: Mental Frameworks for Organizing Social Information

- **Schemas** (Wyer & Srull, 1994): mental frameworks centering on a theme that help organize social information and guide processing in specific contexts.
- Formed through experience; shared within a culture (e.g., "visiting a doctor," "going to a restaurant").
- They influence what we **notice** (attention), **store** (encoding), and **recall** (retrieval).

---

### The Impact of Schemas: Attention, Encoding, Retrieval

#### 1. Attention
- Schemas act as a **filter**: schema-consistent information is more likely to be noticed.
- Relied on more heavily under **cognitive load** for efficiency (Kunda, 1999).

#### 2. Encoding
- Schema-consistent information is generally encoded into long-term memory.
- Schema-**inconsistent** information may be encoded separately and seize attention (e.g., a professor doing magic tricks instead of lecturing is memorable) (Stangor & McMillan, 1992).

#### 3. Retrieval
- People **report** remembering schema-consistent information more.
- But when corrected for response tendencies, or with actual recall, schema-**inconsistent** information is also strongly represented. People selectively report schema-consistent info, but incongruent info is robustly encoded too (Stangor & McMillan, 1992).

---

### Priming: Which Schemas Guide Our Thought?

- **Priming**: when stimuli/events increase the availability of specific information, activating related schemas.
- Two determinants of which schemas are active:
  1. **Schema strength**: stronger, better-developed schemas influence more.
  2. **Recent activation via priming**: temporary activation by recent experience.
- Example: after a violent movie, a driver taking your parking spot may seem more aggressive (aggression schema primed).

#### Unpriming
- **Unpriming**: a primed schema's effects dissipate once it finds expression in thought/behavior.
- If NOT expressed, effects may persist for years (Budson & Price, 2005).

> **KEY STUDY: Sparrow & Wegner (2006), Unpriming**
> - Participants answered easy yes/no questions (e.g., "Does a triangle have three sides?").
>   - **Group 1**: answer randomly (correct-answer schema primed but not expressed).
>   - **Group 2**: first answer correctly, then randomly (schema expressed, then unprimed).
> - **Finding**: Group 1 answered correctly 58% when trying to be random (interference); Group 2 only 49% (near chance), showing successful unpriming.
> - **Conclusion**: expressing a primed schema lets it dissipate; unexpressed schemas persist.

---

### Schema Persistence

- Schemas show a **perseverance effect**: beliefs/schemas remain unchanged despite contradictory information (Kunda & Oleson, 1995).
- Schemas can be **self-fulfilling**: they shape behavior to confirm the expectancy.

> **KEY STUDY: Rosenthal & Jacobson (1968), "Pygmalion in the Classroom"**
> - **Setting**: elementary school; all students given an IQ test.
> - **Manipulation**: teachers told that randomly selected students would "bloom" academically; others were controls.
> - **Finding (8 months later)**: "bloomers" showed significantly larger IQ gains.
> - **Conclusion**: teacher expectations (schemas) operated **self-fulfillingly**, changing treatment and actually improving performance.

- Schemas are a "two-edged sword": efficient, but can lock us into creating the world we expect.

---

### Reasoning by Metaphor

- **Metaphor**: a linguistic device comparing one abstract concept with another dissimilar one.
- Metaphors activate social knowledge and shape how we interpret events (Landau, Meier, & Keefer, 2010). Examples: "it bombed" (warfare), "warm reception" (temperature), "heading in the right direction" (journey).

#### Key Metaphor Priming Effects (Table 2.1)

| Metaphor | Researcher(s) | Effect on Social Judgment |
|---|---|---|
| Nations are bodies | Landau, Sullivan & Greenberg (2009) | Framing U.S. as a body led to harsher immigration attitudes in those motivated to protect body from contamination |
| Good is up; Bad is down | Crawford, Margolies, Drake & Murphy (2006) | Positive items higher on screen, negative lower, recalled best |
| God is up | Chasteen, Burdzy & Pratt (2009) | Photos higher on screen judged as stronger belief in God |
| Social exclusion is physical cold | Zhong & Leonardelli (2008) | Recalling exclusion (vs. acceptance) led to perceiving the room as 5 degrees colder |
| Past is backward; Future is forward | Miles, Nind & Macrae (2010) | Backward postural sway thinking of past; forward sway thinking of future |

> **KEY STUDY: Landau, Sullivan & Greenberg (2009), Body/Contamination Metaphor**
> - Phase 1: read about airborne bacteria, harmful or not.
> - Phase 2: statements about the U.S. used a body metaphor ("growth spurt") or no metaphor.
> - Phase 3: immigration attitudes measured.
> - **Finding**: those concerned about contamination (harmful-bacteria condition) expressed more negative immigration attitudes when the U.S.-as-body metaphor was salient.
> - **Conclusion**: the metaphors we prime can directly affect social attitudes.

## Automatic and Controlled Processing

- **Controlled processing**: systematic, logical, highly effortful thought.
- **Automatic processing**: fast, effortless, intuitive thought, developed after extensive experience until it becomes effortless and nonconscious.
- The two can occur together, especially under uncertainty (Sherman et al., 2008).

#### Neural Bases
- **Automatic evaluations** (rapid good-bad judgments): primarily the **amygdala** (Phelps et al., 2001).
- **Controlled, effortful evaluations**: primarily the **prefrontal cortex** (medial and ventrolateral) (Cunningham et al., 2003; Duncan & Owen, 2000).
- **Emotion-related automatic reactions**: primarily the **limbic system**.
- **Reasoning/logic (controlled)**: primarily the **prefrontal cortex** (Cohen, 2005).

---

### Automatic Processing and Automatic Social Behavior

- Once activated, a schema can influence behavior **without awareness or intention**.

> **KEY STUDY: Bargh, Chen & Burrows (1996), Rudeness/Politeness Priming**
> - **Method**: participants unscrambled sentences containing rudeness words (rude, impolitely, bluntly), politeness words (cordially, polite, courteous), or neutral words; then reported to an experimenter engaged in conversation.
> - **DV**: did participants interrupt?
> - **Finding**: rudeness-primed interrupted more, politeness-primed less; conscious ratings of the experimenter did not differ.
> - **Conclusion**: priming influenced behavior nonconsciously.

> **KEY STUDY: Bargh, Chen & Burrows (1996), Elderly Stereotype Priming**
> - **Method**: primed or did not prime the elderly stereotype.
> - **DV**: time to walk down a hallway afterward.
> - **Finding**: elderly-primed participants walked significantly slower.
> - **Conclusion**: stereotypes can produce automatic behavioral effects without awareness.

> **KEY STUDY: Cesario, Plaks & Higgins (2006), Schema Activation and Interaction Preparation**
> - **Method**: subliminal photos of men labeled "GAY" or "STRAIGHT" (11 msec, below awareness). The computer "locked up"; participants got the experimenter, who acted hostilely.
> - **DV**: participant hostility.
> - **Hypothesis**: does activation trigger schema-consistent behavior (gay stereotype = passivity) or motivated preparation to interact (hostility toward a disliked group)?
> - **Finding**: GAY-primed participants showed significantly more hostility, supporting the **motivated preparation** hypothesis.
> - **Conclusion**: schema activation may trigger preparation for interaction, not just schema-consistent behavior.

---

### The Benefits of Automatic Processing

- A familiar example: memory searches succeeding while attention is elsewhere.
- Automatic/unconscious thought can sometimes beat conscious thought for complex decisions (Galdi, Arcuri & Gawronski, 2008; Dijksterhuis & Nordgren, 2007).

> **KEY STUDY: Dijksterhuis & van Olden (2006), Poster Decision**
> - **Participants**: students viewed posters.
> - **Conditions**: **immediate** (choose at once); **conscious thought** (90 sec each, write evaluations, then choose); **unconscious thought** (solve anagrams to block conscious thought, then choose).
> - **Follow-up**: 3-5 weeks later, satisfaction and resale price.
> - **Finding**: the **unconscious thought** group was most satisfied and assigned highest prices (~9.56 satisfaction vs. ~6.68 conscious, ~6.39 immediate).
> - **Conclusion**: automatic processing sometimes yields better decisions.

#### Why unconscious thought may be better:
1. Conscious thought has strict capacity limits.
2. Conscious deliberation can misweight dimensions.
3. Unconscious processing may better reflect true preferences.

---

### SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Dealing with Information Overload

- The internet amplifies information overload. Strategies:
  - **Satisficing** ("good enough") vs. maximizing.
  - Taking breaks to let automatic processing work.
  - Limiting option sets.
  - Recognizing not all decisions need conscious analysis.

## Potential Sources of Error in Social Cognition

- Despite the advantages of heuristics, social thought is prone to **cognitive biases**: often adaptive in origin but leading to predictable mistakes.

---

### A Basic "Tilt": Overoptimism

- **Optimistic bias** (unrealistic optimism): expecting more positive outcomes for oneself than probability warrants.
- Manifestations:
  - **Overconfidence barrier**: excessive confidence in one's judgments.
  - **Planning fallacy** (Buehler, Griffin & Ross, 1994): believing one's own projects will finish on time/budget; others' projects judged more realistically (inside vs. outside view).
  - Believing oneself less likely than others to face negative events, more likely for positive ones.
  - Optimism can motivate and aid health, but causes poor planning.

#### When optimism is reduced:
- When considering obstacles (implementation intentions); when thinking about others (third-person).
- **Depressive realism**: mildly depressed people may have more accurate self-assessments (Taylor & Brown, 1988, debated).

---

### Counterfactual and Magical Thinking

#### Counterfactual Thinking
- **Counterfactual thinking**: mentally simulating "what might have been."
  - **Upward counterfactuals**: imagining better outcomes ("If only I'd studied more"). Produce regret/negative affect.
  - **Downward counterfactuals**: imagining worse outcomes ("At least it wasn't worse"). Provide comfort.

#### The Near-Miss Effect
- Those who nearly achieved an outcome do more upward counterfactual thinking. Bronze medalists appear happier than silver (Medvec, Madey & Gilovich, 1995): bronze use downward counterfactuals ("at least I got a medal"); silver use upward ("I almost won gold").

#### Factors increasing counterfactual thinking:
- Exceptional/unusual events; events with easily imagined alternatives; negative events (more upward counterfactuals).

#### Magical Thinking
- **Magical thinking**: beliefs violating the laws of science and physical causality. Two principles (Rozin & Nemeroff, 1990; Frazer, 1890):
  1. **Law of similarity**: things that resemble each other share properties (e.g., voodoo doll).
  2. **Law of contagion**: things once in contact keep sharing properties after separation (e.g., reluctance to wear an evil person's clothing).
- Together these produce **sympathetic magic**. Example: reluctance to drink juice a sterilized cockroach touched. Even scientifically literate people do this.

## Affect and Cognition: How Feelings and Thought Shape Each Other

- **Affect** and **cognition** are deeply intertwined and mutually influencing.
- Affect = moods (diffuse, low-intensity) and emotions (intense, object-focused).

---

### The Influence of Affect on Cognition

#### Mood-Congruence Effects
- **Mood congruence effect**: we notice and encode information consistent with current mood (positive aspects when happy, negative when sad).

#### Mood-Dependent Memory
- **Mood-dependent memory**: information learned in one mood is more easily recalled in that same mood.

#### Affect Infusion Model (Forgas, 1995a, 2000)
- **Affect infusion model (AIM)**: moods "infuse" social judgments, especially for complex, unfamiliar, or ambiguous situations.
- Mood as information: people ask "How do I feel about this?" Infusion is stronger under heuristic/substantive processing for novel, complex, ambiguous situations.

#### Affect and Creativity
- Positive moods increase creativity, broaden attention, promote **inclusive thinking** (Isen, 1987).
- Negative moods narrow attention, promote detail-focused processing (Schwarz, 1990): the **"mood as information" model**.

#### Affect and Social Judgments
- Positive moods produce more favorable judgments and attributions; negative affect raises hostile attribution bias.

---

### The Influence of Cognition on Affect

#### Two-Factor Theory of Emotion (Schachter & Singer, 1962)
- **Two-factor theory of emotion**: emotions = (1) physiological arousal + (2) a cognitive label. The same arousal yields different emotions depending on the label; the context provides the label.

#### Misattribution of Arousal
- **Misattribution of arousal**: attributing arousal to an incorrect source, changing the emotional experience.

> **KEY STUDY: Dutton & Aron (1974), Capilano Bridge**
> - Men approached on a high, swaying bridge (high fear-arousal) or a low, stable bridge (low arousal). An attractive female confederate gave a questionnaire and her number.
> - High-bridge men were more likely to call her and showed more sexual imagery.
> - **Interpretation**: arousal from fear misattributed as sexual attraction.

#### Excitation Transfer Theory (Zillmann, 1983)
- **Excitation transfer theory**: residual arousal from one source transfers to and intensifies a later emotional reaction (e.g., exercise arousal intensifies subsequent anger).

#### Affective Forecasting
- **Affective forecasting**: predicting how one will feel about future events (generally poor).
- **Impact bias** (Gilbert et al.): overestimating the intensity and duration of future emotional reactions.
- **Focalism**: focusing too narrowly on the target event, ignoring other life factors.
- **Immune neglect**: failing to account for the **psychological immune system** (processes that rationalize, minimize, or reframe negative events).

---

### EMOTIONS AND SOCIAL COGNITION: Predicting Responses to Tragedy

- After tragedy, people report more resilience than predicted, because the **psychological immune system** activates: rationalization, social comparison, positive reframing.
- People are largely unaware of it, so predictions about suffering are too extreme.
- After positive events, **hedonic adaptation** reduces positive feelings more than predicted.
- **Hedonic adaptation**: the emotional impact of a constant stimulus diminishes over time.
- Implication: predictions about major life events consistently overestimate emotional impact.

---

### Affect and Cognition: Two Separate Neural Systems

- Affect and cognition involve partially separate neural systems (Cohen, 2005; LeDoux, 1996).
- Two pathways for emotional stimuli:
  1. **Fast, automatic route**: sensory → thalamus → **amygdala** (rapid, rough threat/reward assessment before awareness).
  2. **Slow, controlled route**: sensory → thalamus → **cortex** → amygdala (deliberate, detailed).
- **Amygdala**: automatic emotional processing, fear, social threat detection; reacts to emotional faces outside awareness.
- **Prefrontal cortex**: emotion regulation, controlled processing, reasoning; can inhibit the amygdala.
- **Somatic Marker Hypothesis** (Damasio, 1994): body states (somatic markers) from past emotional experiences guide decisions before conscious reasoning. Patients with ventromedial prefrontal cortex damage reason logically but decide poorly because they lack somatic markers.

#### Dual-Process Implications
- Two systems explain why we react emotionally before understanding it, why rational control can be "hijacked" by automatic affect, and why we can hold conflicting evaluations simultaneously.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Representativeness heuristic and base rate neglect**: the key error is ignoring base rates (Kahneman & Tversky, 1973). Asians rely less than Westerners (Spina et al., 2010).
2. **Availability heuristic, two components**: ease of retrieval (emotional/self-relevant) and amount of information (factual/difficult) (Schwarz et al., 1991).
3. **Anchoring and adjustment**: even experienced judges influenced by random anchors (dice) (Englich et al., 2006); adjustments insufficient because people stop at the first plausible value.
4. **Status quo heuristic (Eidelman et al., 2010)**: older options rated better even when identical; participants unaware.
5. **Self-fulfilling schemas, Rosenthal & Jacobson (1968)**: "Pygmalion effect," randomly assigned "bloomer" beliefs caused IQ gains 8 months later.
6. **Priming and unpriming (Sparrow & Wegner, 2006)**: schemas persist until expressed; once expressed, effects dissipate.
7. **Automatic social behavior (Bargh et al., 1996)**: rudeness/politeness priming affected interruption; elderly priming slowed walking, without awareness.
8. **Two neural systems**: automatic/evaluative → amygdala; controlled/effortful → prefrontal cortex; emotional automatic → limbic system; reasoning → prefrontal cortex.
9. **Unconscious thought advantage (Dijksterhuis & van Olden, 2006)**: unconscious-thought group most satisfied weeks later.
10. **Counterfactual thinking**: upward → regret; bronze medalists happier than silver (Medvec et al., 1995) via downward vs. upward counterfactuals.
11. **Misattribution of arousal (Dutton & Aron, 1974, Capilano bridge)**: fear arousal misattributed as attraction; tests two-factor theory.
12. **Impact bias and immune neglect**: people overestimate intensity/duration of future emotion, ignoring the psychological immune system.
13. **Affect infusion model (Forgas, 1995a, 2000)**: moods infuse judgments most for complex, ambiguous situations processed heuristically.
14. **Metaphors and attitudes (Zhong & Leonardelli, 2008)**: exclusion priming made the room feel 5 degrees colder.
15. **Somatic marker hypothesis (Damasio, 1994)**: ventromedial prefrontal damage patients reason logically but decide poorly (no somatic markers).
