# 2 Social Cognition: How We Think About the Social World

Social cognition refers to how we interpret, analyze, remember, and use information about the social world. This chapter examines how people often take mental shortcuts (heuristics) rather than reasoning carefully, how schemas organize and sometimes distort social thought, how automatic vs. controlled processing differ, and how affect and cognition mutually influence each other. Together, these processes explain both the efficiency and the systematic errors in everyday social thinking.

---

## Heuristics: How We Reduce Our Effort in Social Cognition

- At any given time, our cognitive system has a finite processing capacity.
- When incoming information exceeds this capacity, we enter **information overload**: demands on the cognitive system exceed its capacity.
- High stress or competing tasks further deplete processing capacity (Chajut & Algom, 2003).
- Under **conditions of uncertainty** (where the correct answer is hard to determine), people adopt strategies to "stretch" cognitive resources.
- The primary strategy: use of **heuristics** — simple rules for making complex decisions or drawing inferences in a rapid and seemingly effortless manner.
- **Social cognition**: the manner in which we interpret, analyze, remember, and use information about the social world (Fiske & Taylor, 2008; Higgins & Kruglanski, 1996).
- **Affect**: our current feelings and moods; these interact with social cognition throughout.

> Much of social thought occurs on "automatic": quick, effortless, and without careful reasoning. Sometimes, however, unexpected events trigger more careful **controlled processing**.

---

### Representativeness: Judging by Resemblance

- **Representativeness heuristic**: a strategy for making judgments based on the extent to which current stimuli or events resemble other stimuli or categories.
- Core rule: the more an individual resembles a group's **prototype** (summary of common attributes of category members), the more likely they are perceived as belonging to that group.
- Example: judging a neighbor's occupation based on her resemblance to the typical librarian vs. manager.

#### Why representativeness can be wrong: Ignoring Base Rates
- Judgments based on representativeness tend to **ignore base rates** — the actual frequency with which given events or patterns occur in the total population (Kahneman & Tversky, 1973; Kahneman & Frederick, 2002).
- Example: even though someone resembles the librarian prototype more, base rates (far more managers than librarians) should weight the judgment toward "manager."
- Opening example: ~900 million peaceful Muslims vs. ~9,000 Al-Qaeda members = 1 Al-Qaeda per 100,000 peaceful Muslims; blaming all Muslims ignores this base rate.

#### Cause-Effect Matching via Representativeness
- Representativeness is also used when judging whether causes are likely to produce effects that are **similar in magnitude** ("like goes with like").
- High-magnitude effects are expected to come from high-magnitude causes, and vice versa.

#### Cultural Differences in Representativeness Use

> **KEY STUDY: Spina et al. (2010)**
> - **Participants**: Students in China and Canada.
> - **Method**: Rated likelihood that a high- or low-magnitude effect (many or few deaths) was caused by a high- or low-magnitude virus strain (treatment-resistant vs. standard).
> - **Finding**: Both groups showed some "like goes with like" reasoning, but Canadian participants showed this effect significantly more strongly than Chinese participants.
> - **Conclusion**: Asians consider more potential causal factors and arrive at more complex attributions; thus they show less reliance on the representativeness heuristic than Westerners.
> - **Implication**: Cross-cultural differences in reasoning may create difficulty when tackling global problems (e.g., climate change).

---

### Availability: "If I Can Retrieve Instances, They Must Be Frequent"

- **Availability heuristic**: a strategy for making judgments on the basis of how easily specific kinds of information can be brought to mind.
- Core rule: the easier it is to bring information to mind, the more frequent or probable it is judged to be.

#### Why availability leads to errors
- Dramatic but rare events (plane crashes, murders) are easy to retrieve from memory (vivid, emotionally salient, heavily media-covered), so their frequency is overestimated.
- Mundane but common causes of death (heart disease, stroke) are underestimated because they are harder to retrieve.
- SUV safety illusion: people recall vivid images of huge vehicles crushing smaller ones, leading to the false belief that SUVs are safer — while actual data show higher death rates per million vehicles for SUVs.
- Physicians reach different diagnoses partly because different types of diseases are more available in their memories based on prior experience.

#### Two factors in availability (Schwarz et al., 1991)
1. **Ease of retrieval**: subjective fluency with which relevant information comes to mind.
2. **Amount of information**: how much information comes to mind.
- Which matters more depends on the judgment type:
  - Emotional/feeling-based judgments: rely on the **ease** rule.
  - Fact-based or inherently difficult tasks: rely more on the **amount** rule (Rothman & Hardin, 1997; Ruder & Bless, 2003).

#### Self vs. Other in availability
- Ease of bringing instances to mind affects self-relevant judgments more than judgments about others.
- When we know less about others or unfamiliar objects, ease of retrieval is given less weight.
- Familiar tasks/objects: ease of retrieval is given more weight.

> **KEY STUDY: Caruso (2008) — Harvard campus safety**
> - Students recalled either 2 or 6 instances of feeling unsafe on campus (self-relevant) or another student's unsafe feelings.
> - Recalling 2 instances (easy) vs. 6 instances (difficult) of personal unsafety: easy recall led to rating the town as more unsafe.
> - This ease-of-retrieval effect did NOT apply when examples concerned another person's experiences.
> - Also demonstrated with creativity ratings: easy retrieval of 2 creative instances for self increased self-rated creativity; ease of retrieval for an acquaintance did not affect ratings of the acquaintance's creativity.

### Anchoring and Adjustment: Where You Begin Makes a Difference

- **Anchoring and adjustment heuristic**: the tendency to use a number or value as a starting point to which we then make adjustments.
- We start from a known value (the anchor) and adjust toward the final answer — but adjustments are typically insufficient.
- Common in negotiations: sellers set high asking prices; buyers bid low; the anchor shapes the final agreement.
- "Sale pricing" exploits this: original (higher) price is the anchor, making discounted price feel like a bargain.

#### Why adjustments are insufficient
- People stop adjusting as soon as a plausible value is reached (Epley & Gilovich, 2006).
- This reflects the general "save mental effort" principle.
- Insufficient adjustment is greater when effortful thought is limited: e.g., after consuming alcohol or when busy with other tasks.

> **KEY STUDY: Englich, Mussweiler, & Strack (2006) — Legal sentencing**
> - **Participants**: Highly experienced German legal professionals.
> - **Method**: Read a realistic court case and then received sentencing recommendations from:
>   - A journalist (no legal training — irrelevant source)
>   - A dice throw (purely random)
>   - An experienced prosecutor (relevant source)
>   - Recommendations were either lenient (1 month probation) or harsh (3 years prison).
> - **Finding**: Experts' own sentencing recommendations were significantly influenced by anchors regardless of source — harsher when exposed to harsh anchors, more lenient with lenient anchors. Source credibility did not matter; even random dice rolls influenced sentences.
> - **Conclusion**: Anchoring is a very powerful effect with real-world consequences, even among trained experts.

### Status Quo Heuristic: "What Is, Is Good"

- The **status quo heuristic**: the tendency to judge objects and options that are familiar or have existed longer as better or more preferable than newer alternatives.
- Objects/options more easily retrieved from memory are judged as "good."
- Rationale: bad products tend to be removed from the market over time — so older products may signal quality.
- But this can be wrong: old products remain through inertia; people continue buying out of habit.

> **KEY STUDY: Eidelman, Pattershall, & Crandall (2010)**
> - **Study 1**: Participants tasted the same chocolate but were told it was first sold in 1937 (70 years on market) or 2003 (3 years on market). Those told it was older rated it more delicious, even though it was identical chocolate.
> - **Study 2**: Students favored a degree requirement proposal described as already in existence over the same proposal framed as a new change.
> - **Study 3**: A practice (acupuncture) said to be 250, 500, 1,000, or 2,000 years old was rated increasingly effective with age.
> - **Study 4**: A painting was rated more aesthetically pleasing when said to be painted in 1905 vs. 2005.
> - **Key insight**: Participants were unaware that longevity influenced them; they attributed their evaluations to "taste" or other factors.

## Schemas: Mental Frameworks for Organizing Social Information

- **Schemas**: mental frameworks centering on a specific theme that help us to organize social information, guide actions, and process information relevant to specific contexts.
- Formed through personal experience; shared within a culture (e.g., "visiting a doctor," "going to a restaurant," "boarding an airplane").
- Once formed, schemas influence: what we **notice** (attention), what we **store** (encoding), and what we **recall** (retrieval).

---

### The Impact of Schemas on Social Cognition: Attention, Encoding, Retrieval

#### 1. Attention
- Schemas act as a **filter**: schema-consistent information is more likely to be noticed and enter consciousness.
- Schemas are relied on more heavily under **cognitive load** — when processing many things at once — because they enable efficient information processing (Kunda, 1999).

#### 2. Encoding
- Information consistent with schemas is generally encoded into long-term memory.
- Schema-**inconsistent** information (that violates expectations) may be encoded separately, tagged uniquely, and may literally seize attention.
- Example: A professor who does magic tricks instead of lecturing is memorable precisely because it violates the "professor" schema.
- (Stangor & McMillan, 1992)

#### 3. Retrieval
- People **report** remembering schema-consistent information more often.
- However, when memory measures are corrected for response tendencies, or when actual recall (not recognition) is measured, schema-**inconsistent** information is also strongly represented in memory.
- Conclusion: people selectively report schema-consistent information, but schema-incongruent information is actually robustly encoded too.
- (Stangor & McMillan, 1992; Tice, Bratslavsky, & Baumeister, 2000)

---

### Priming: Which Schemas Guide Our Thought?

- **Priming**: a situation that occurs when stimuli or events increase the availability in memory or consciousness of specific types of information, thereby activating related schemas.
- Two determinants of which schemas are active:
  1. **Schema strength**: stronger, better-developed schemas are more likely to influence thinking and memory.
  2. **Recent activation via priming**: temporary activation by recent experiences.
- Example: After watching a violent movie, a driver who takes your parking spot may be perceived as more aggressive (because the aggression schema has been primed).

#### Unpriming
- **Unpriming**: the process by which the effects of a primed schema dissipate once the schema finds expression in thought or behavior; effects then decrease or disappear.
- If primed schemas are NOT expressed, their effects may persist for very long periods — even years (Budson & Price, 2005; Mitchell, 2006).

> **KEY STUDY: Sparrow & Wegner (2006) — Unpriming**
> - Participants answered easy yes/no questions (e.g., "Does a triangle have three sides?") under different instructions:
>   - **Group 1**: Answer randomly (schema for "answer correctly" was primed but not expressed).
>   - **Group 2**: First answer correctly, then answer randomly (schema was first expressed, then unprimed).
> - **Finding**: Group 1 answered correctly 58% of the time even when trying to be random (schema interference). Group 2 answered correctly only 49% of the time when responding randomly (near true chance), demonstrating successful unpriming.
> - **Conclusion**: Expressing a primed schema allows it to dissipate; unexpressed schemas persist and continue to influence thought/behavior.

---

### Schema Persistence: Why Even Discredited Schemas Can Sometimes Influence Our Thought and Behavior

- Schemas show a strong **perseverance effect**: the tendency for beliefs and schemas to remain unchanged even in the face of contradictory information (Kunda & Oleson, 1995).
- Schemas can be **self-fulfilling**: they influence our behavior in ways that create confirmation of the expectancy.

> **KEY STUDY: Rosenthal & Jacobson (1968) — "Pygmalion in the Classroom"**
> - **Setting**: Elementary school; all students given an IQ test.
> - **Manipulation**: Teachers were randomly told that some students (randomly selected) had scored very high and were about to "bloom" academically. Other students served as the control group.
> - **Finding (8 months later)**: "Bloomers" showed significantly larger IQ gains than control students.
> - **Conclusion**: Teachers' expectations (schemas) operated in a **self-fulfilling** manner — they changed how teachers treated these students, which actually caused the students to perform better.
> - This is the classic demonstration that schemas not only reflect but actively shape the social world.

- Schemas are a "two-edged sword": they help us process information efficiently AND can lock us into creating the world we expect.

### Reasoning by Metaphor: How Social Attitudes and Behavior Are Affected by Figures of Speech

- **Metaphor**: a linguistic device that relates or draws a comparison between one abstract concept and another dissimilar concept.
- Metaphors can activate different kinds of social knowledge and thereby influence how we interpret events (Landau, Meier, & Keefer, 2010).
- Even novel metaphors are readily understood because they map abstract onto concrete.
- Examples: warfare metaphors for a talk ("it bombed"), weight/temperature metaphors for reception ("warm reception"), journey metaphors for relationships ("heading in the right direction").

#### Key Metaphor Priming Effects (Table 2.1)

| Metaphor | Researcher(s) | Effect on Social Judgment |
|---|---|---|
| Nations are bodies | Landau, Sullivan & Greenberg (2009) | Framing U.S. as a body led to harsher attitudes toward immigration in those motivated to protect body from contamination |
| Good is up; Bad is down | Crawford, Margolies, Drake & Murphy (2006) | Positive items presented higher on screen, negative items lower, recalled best |
| God is up | Chasteen, Burdzy & Pratt (2009) | Photos presented higher on screen judged as having stronger belief in God |
| Social exclusion is physical cold | Zhong & Leonardelli (2008) | Recalling social exclusion (vs. acceptance) led to perceiving the room as 5 degrees colder |
| Past is backward; Future is forward | Miles, Nind & Macrae (2010) | Backward postural sway when thinking of the past; forward sway when thinking of the future |

> **KEY STUDY: Landau, Sullivan & Greenberg (2009) — Body/Contamination Metaphor**
> - Phase 1: Participants read about airborne bacteria described as either harmful or not harmful to humans.
> - Phase 2: Statements about the United States used either a body metaphor ("experienced an unprecedented growth spurt") or no metaphor.
> - Phase 3: Attitudes toward immigration were measured.
> - **Finding**: Those with concern about "body contamination" (harmful bacteria condition) expressed more negative attitudes toward immigration when the U.S.-as-body metaphor had been made salient.
> - **Conclusion**: The precise language we use (the metaphors we prime) can directly affect social attitudes and behavior.

## Automatic and Controlled Processing: Two Basic Modes of Social Thought

- **Controlled processing**: systematic, logical, and highly effortful social thought.
- **Automatic processing**: fast, relatively effortless, and intuitive social thought; occurs after extensive experience with a task or type of information, reaching the stage where we process information in a seemingly effortless, automatic, and nonconscious manner.
- These two modes are not entirely independent; they can occur together, especially in situations involving uncertainty (Sherman et al., 2008).
- Supported by hundreds of studies AND social neuroscience research.

#### Neural Bases (Social Neuroscience Evidence)
- Two distinct neural systems for processing social information:
  - **Automatic evaluations** (simple good-bad judgments, rapid): primarily in the **amygdala** (Phelps et al., 2001).
  - **Controlled, effortful evaluations**: primarily in the **prefrontal cortex** — specifically medial prefrontal cortex and ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (Cunningham et al., 2003; Duncan & Owen, 2000).
  - **Emotion-related, automatic reactions**: primarily in the **limbic system** (structures deep inside the brain).
  - **Reasoning and logic (controlled processing)**: primarily in the **prefrontal cortex** (Cohen, 2005).

---

### Automatic Processing and Automatic Social Behavior

- Once a concept/schema is activated, it can influence social behavior **without awareness or intention**.

> **KEY STUDY: Bargh, Chen & Burrows (1996) — Rudeness/Politeness Priming**
> - **Method**: Participants unscrambled sentences containing words related to:
>   - Rudeness (bold, rude, impolitely, bluntly)
>   - Politeness (cordially, patiently, polite, courteous)
>   - Neutral control words
> - Then asked to report to experimenter who was engaged in conversation (an accomplice).
> - **DV**: Did participants interrupt?
> - **Finding**: Rudeness-primed participants interrupted more; politeness-primed participants interrupted less. Importantly, participants did not differ in their conscious ratings of the experimenter's politeness.
> - **Conclusion**: Priming schemas influenced behavior in a nonconscious, automatic manner.

> **KEY STUDY: Bargh, Chen & Burrows (1996) — Elderly Stereotype Priming**
> - **Method**: Primed or did not prime the elderly stereotype using related words.
> - **DV**: Time (seconds) to walk down a hallway after the study.
> - **Finding**: Participants primed with elderly-related words walked significantly slower.
> - **Conclusion**: Activating stereotypes can produce automatic behavioral effects without conscious awareness.

> **KEY STUDY: Cesario, Plaks & Higgins (2006) — Schema Activation and Interaction Preparation**
> - **Method**: Participants were subliminally shown photos of men labeled "GAY" or "STRAIGHT" (presented for only 11 msec — below conscious awareness threshold).
> - Computer then "locked up"; participants were instructed to get experimenter. Experimenter acted hostilely.
> - **DV**: Hostility of participants toward the experimenter.
> - **Hypothesis tested**: Does schema activation trigger (1) schema-consistent behavior, or (2) motivated preparation to interact with the schema-relevant group?
>   - The gay stereotype suggests passivity/nonaggression — schema-consistent behavior would predict passivity.
>   - But preparation for interaction with a disliked group would predict hostility.
> - **Finding**: Participants primed with GAY faces showed significantly more hostility than those primed with STRAIGHT faces — consistent with the "motivated preparation" hypothesis.
> - **Conclusion**: Activating schemas may trigger preparation for interaction with the target group, not just schema-consistent behavior.

### The Benefits of Automatic Processing: Beyond Mere Efficiency

- A familiar form of automatic processing: memory searches for forgotten information (a name, a fact) that succeed while attention is directed elsewhere.
- Evidence suggests automatic/unconscious thought can sometimes be superior to careful conscious thought for complex decisions (Galdi, Arcuri & Gawronski, 2008; Dijksterhuis & Nordgren, 2007).

> **KEY STUDY: Dijksterhuis & van Olden (2006) — Poster Decision Study**
> - **Participants**: Students viewed various posters.
> - **Three conditions**:
>   - **Immediate decision**: posters shown simultaneously; students chose immediately.
>   - **Conscious thought**: posters shown 90 sec each; then students wrote thoughts and evaluations before choosing.
>   - **Unconscious thought**: after viewing, students solved anagrams (preventing conscious thought about posters); then chose.
> - **Follow-up**: 3-5 weeks later, participants were phoned and asked how satisfied they were with the poster they took home and how much they would sell it for (in Euros).
> - **Finding**: Participants in the **unconscious thought condition** were most satisfied with their choice and assigned the highest selling prices (mean ~9.56 satisfaction vs. ~6.68 conscious, ~6.39 immediate).
> - **Conclusion**: Automatic (unconscious) processing sometimes leads to better decisions than conscious deliberation.

#### Why unconscious thought may be better:
1. Conscious thought has strict capacity limits; it cannot handle all available information.
2. Conscious deliberation may lead to incorrect weighting of dimensions.
3. Unconscious processing may more accurately reflect true preferences.

---

### SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Dealing with Information Overload and Improving Choices

- The internet and digital environments greatly amplify the problem of information overload.
- Strategies recommended for improving decision-making under overload:
  - **Satisficing** (choosing options that are "good enough") vs. maximizing every decision.
  - Taking breaks to allow unconscious/automatic processing to work.
  - Limiting option sets to reduce cognitive burden.
  - Recognizing that not all decisions require deliberate conscious analysis.

## Potential Sources of Error in Social Cognition: Why Total Rationality Is Rarer Than You Think

- Even with the advantages of heuristics and automatic processing, social thought is systematically prone to errors or **cognitive biases**.
- These biases often have adaptive origins but lead to predictable mistakes.

---

### A Basic "Tilt" in Social Thought: Our Powerful Tendency to Be Overly Optimistic

- Humans show a pervasive **optimistic bias**: the tendency to expect more positive outcomes for themselves than objective probability warrants.
- Also called **unrealistic optimism**.
- Manifestations:
  - **Overconfidence barrier**: excessive confidence in one's own judgments, even when objectively inaccurate.
  - **Planning fallacy** (Buehler, Griffin & Ross, 1994): tendency to believe one's own projects will be completed on time and on budget, underestimating delays and costs. Others' projects are judged more realistically (outside view vs. inside view).
  - People believe they are less likely than others to experience negative events (illness, accident, divorce) and more likely to experience positive events.
  - This optimism can be motivating and associated with better health outcomes, but also leads to poor planning and ignoring risks.

#### When optimism is reduced:
- When people are asked to consider obstacles carefully (implementation intentions).
- When thinking about others rather than self (third-person perspective).
- Depressive realism: mildly depressed people may actually have more accurate self-assessments than non-depressed people (Taylor & Brown, 1988 — though this finding has been debated).

---

### Situation-Specific Sources of Error in Social Cognition: Counterfactual Thinking and Magical Thinking

#### Counterfactual Thinking
- **Counterfactual thinking**: mental simulation of "what might have been" — the tendency to imagine alternative outcomes for events.
- Two directions:
  - **Upward counterfactuals**: imagining better outcomes ("If only I had studied more, I would have passed").
  - **Downward counterfactuals**: imagining worse outcomes ("At least it wasn't worse").
- Upward counterfactuals produce regret and negative affect; downward counterfactuals provide comfort.

#### The Near-Miss Effect
- People who nearly achieved an outcome (e.g., Olympic silver medalists) engage in more upward counterfactual thinking than those who finished farther away.
- Classic finding: bronze medalists appear happier than silver medalists (Medvec, Madey & Gilovich, 1995) because bronze medalists engage in downward counterfactuals ("At least I got a medal"), while silver medalists engage in upward counterfactuals ("I almost won gold").

#### Factors determining counterfactual thinking:
- Events that are exceptional (unusual, unexpected) generate more counterfactual thinking than normal events.
- Events with easily imagined alternatives generate more counterfactual thinking.
- Negative events generate more upward counterfactuals than positive events.

#### Magical Thinking
- **Magical thinking**: thoughts or beliefs that violate the laws of science and physical causality.
- Two key principles (Rozin & Nemeroff, 1990; Frazer, 1890):
  1. **Law of similarity**: things that resemble each other share properties (e.g., a voodoo doll shaped like a person can affect that person).
  2. **Law of contagion**: things that have once been in contact continue to share properties even after separation (e.g., reluctance to wear clothing previously owned by an evil person).
- Together these produce **sympathetic magic**.
- Example: people are reluctant to drink juice that a sterilized cockroach has touched, even knowing it is clean.
- Even highly educated, scientifically literate people engage in magical thinking.

## Affect and Cognition: How Feelings Shape Thought and Thought Shapes Feelings

- **Affect** and **cognition** are deeply intertwined; they mutually influence each other.
- Affect includes both moods (diffuse, low-intensity, often without a clear object) and emotions (more intense, focused on specific objects or events).

---

### The Influence of Affect on Cognition

#### Mood-Congruence Effects
- **Mood congruence effect**: people tend to notice and encode information that is consistent with their current mood.
  - In a positive mood: more likely to notice positive aspects of the environment.
  - In a negative mood: more likely to notice negative aspects.

#### Mood-Dependent Memory
- **Mood-dependent memory**: information learned in one mood state is more easily recalled when in the same mood state again.

#### Affect Infusion Model (Forgas, 1995a, 2000)
- **Affect infusion model (AIM)**: moods "infuse" into social judgments, especially for complex, unfamiliar, or ambiguous situations.
- Mood states serve as information: people ask themselves "How do I feel about this?" and use their mood as a data point.
- When processing is heuristic or substantive (not highly motivated), affect infuses more strongly into judgments.
- Key conditions for affect infusion:
  - Novel, complex, or ambiguous situations.
  - Heuristic processing (not careful deliberation).

#### Affect and Creativity
- Positive moods tend to increase creativity and broaden the scope of attention (Isen, 1987).
- Positive moods promote **inclusive thinking** and the ability to see connections.
- Negative moods tend to narrow attention and promote detail-focused, analytical processing (Schwarz, 1990).
- This is sometimes called the **"mood as information" model**.

#### Affect and Social Judgments
- People in positive moods tend to make more favorable judgments of others, make more positive attributions, and evaluate ambiguous information more positively.
- Negative affect increases the risk of hostile attribution bias.

---

### The Influence of Cognition on Affect

#### Two-Factor Theory of Emotion (Schachter & Singer, 1962)
- **Two-factor theory of emotion**: emotions result from two components: (1) physiological arousal and (2) a cognitive label applied to that arousal.
- The same state of physiological arousal can produce different emotions depending on the label applied.
- People search for explanations for unexplained arousal; the situational context provides the label.

#### Misattribution of Arousal
- **Misattribution of arousal**: the tendency to attribute physiological arousal to an incorrect source, thereby changing the emotional experience.

> **KEY STUDY: Dutton & Aron (1974) — Capilano Bridge Study**
> - Men approached on either a high, swaying suspension bridge (high arousal from fear) or a low, stable bridge (low arousal).
> - An attractive female confederate gave them a questionnaire and her phone number.
> - Men on the high bridge were more likely to call her later and showed more sexual imagery in their responses.
> - **Interpretation**: Men misattributed arousal from fear to sexual attraction — classic misattribution of arousal.

#### Excitation Transfer Theory (Zillmann, 1983)
- **Excitation transfer theory**: residual arousal from one source can transfer to and intensify a later emotional reaction in a different context.
- Example: arousal from exercise can intensify anger if provoked shortly afterward.

#### Affective Forecasting
- **Affective forecasting**: predicting how one will feel about future events.
- People are generally poor affective forecasters.
- **Impact bias** (Gilbert et al.): the tendency to overestimate the intensity and duration of one's emotional reactions to future events, both positive and negative.
- **Focalism**: focusing too narrowly on the target event while ignoring other factors that will fill one's life.
- **Immune neglect**: failure to take into account the operation of the **psychological immune system** — the cognitive and psychological processes that help us rationalize, minimize, or reframe negative events so they hurt less than expected.

---

### EMOTIONS AND SOCIAL COGNITION: Why We Can't Always Predict Our Responses to Tragedy

- When people experience tragedy (death of a loved one, serious illness, disability), they often report more resilience than they predicted.
- This is because the **psychological immune system** activates:
  - Rationalization ("everything happens for a reason")
  - Social comparison ("others have it worse")
  - Positive reframing ("this made me a stronger person")
- People are largely unaware of the psychological immune system's operation, hence their predictions about suffering are systematically too extreme.
- Similarly, after positive life events (winning a lottery, achieving a long-sought goal), the hedonic adaptation process reduces the intensity and duration of positive feelings more than people predicted.
- **Hedonic adaptation**: the tendency for the emotional impact of a constant stimulus to diminish over time.
- Research implication: predictions about major life events (marrying, winning an award, being rejected) consistently overestimate the emotional impact compared to what people actually experience.

---

### Affect and Cognition: Social Neuroscience Evidence for Two Separate Systems

- Social neuroscience evidence confirms that affect and cognition involve partially separate neural systems (Cohen, 2005; LeDoux, 1996).
- Two pathways for processing emotional stimuli:
  1. **Fast, automatic route**: sensory information → thalamus → **amygdala** (rapid, automatic, rough assessment of threat or reward; occurs before conscious awareness).
  2. **Slow, controlled route**: sensory information → thalamus → **cortex** → amygdala (more deliberate, detailed processing).
- **Amygdala**: key structure for automatic emotional processing, fear responses, and social threat detection; rapid responses to emotional faces even outside conscious awareness.
- **Prefrontal cortex**: involved in regulation of emotion, controlled processing, logical reasoning; can inhibit amygdala responses.
- **Somatic Marker Hypothesis** (Damasio, 1994): body states (somatic markers) associated with past emotional experiences guide decision-making by flagging options as beneficial or harmful before explicit conscious reasoning occurs.
  - Evidence from patients with ventromedial prefrontal cortex damage: they can reason logically but make poor real-life decisions because they lack somatic markers.

#### Dual-Process Implications
- The existence of two neural systems helps explain why:
  - We can have an emotional reaction before we cognitively understand it.
  - Efforts at rational control can still be "hijacked" by automatic affective processing.
  - People can hold conflicting evaluations simultaneously (e.g., intellectually approving of something while emotionally repulsed by it).

| **Automatic processing** | Processing that occurs in a seemingly effortless, automatic, and nonconscious manner after extensive experience |
| **Controlled processing** | Systematic, logical, and highly effortful social thought |
| **Optimistic bias** | The tendency to expect more positive outcomes for oneself than objective probability warrants |
| **Planning fallacy** | The tendency to believe own projects will be completed on time/budget while underestimating delays and costs |
| **Counterfactual thinking** | The tendency to imagine alternative outcomes to events that have already occurred ("what might have been") |
| **Upward counterfactuals** | Imagining how things could have turned out better |
| **Downward counterfactuals** | Imagining how things could have turned out worse |
| **Magical thinking** | Thoughts or beliefs that violate the laws of science and physical causality |
| **Law of similarity** | The principle that things resembling each other share essential properties |
| **Law of contagion** | The principle that things that have once been in contact continue to share properties after separation |
| **Mood congruence effect** | Tendency to notice and encode information consistent with one's current mood |
| **Mood-dependent memory** | Information learned in one mood state is more easily recalled when in the same mood state |
| **Affect infusion model (AIM)** | Forgas's model proposing that moods infuse into social judgments, especially in complex or unfamiliar situations |
| **Two-factor theory of emotion** | Schachter & Singer's theory that emotions result from physiological arousal plus a cognitive label |
| **Misattribution of arousal** | Attributing physiological arousal to an incorrect source, changing the emotional experience |
| **Excitation transfer theory** | Zillmann's theory that residual arousal from one source can transfer to and intensify a later emotional reaction |
| **Affective forecasting** | Predicting how one will feel about future events |
| **Impact bias** | Overestimating the intensity and duration of one's emotional reactions to future events |
| **Immune neglect** | Failure to account for the psychological immune system when predicting future emotional responses |
| **Psychological immune system** | Cognitive and psychological processes that help rationalize, minimize, or reframe negative events |
| **Hedonic adaptation** | The tendency for the emotional impact of a constant stimulus to diminish over time |
| **Somatic marker hypothesis** | Damasio's theory that body states associated with past emotions guide decision-making |
| **Self-fulfilling prophecy** | The tendency for schemas/expectations to influence behavior in ways that confirm those expectations |
| **Sympathetic magic** | The combination of the laws of similarity and contagion in magical thinking |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Representativeness heuristic and base rate neglect**: The most testable error associated with representativeness is ignoring base rates. Kahneman & Tversky (1973) are the key names. Cultural difference: Asians (Spina et al., 2010) show less reliance on representativeness than Westerners.

2. **Availability heuristic — two components**: ease of retrieval AND amount of information both matter; ease rule applies more to emotional/self-relevant judgments, amount rule to factual/difficult tasks (Schwarz et al., 1991; Rothman & Hardin, 1997; Ruder & Bless, 2003).

3. **Anchoring and adjustment — key finding**: Even experienced judges are influenced by random anchors (dice throws), not just expert recommendations (Englich, Mussweiler & Strack, 2006). Adjustments are insufficient because people stop as soon as a plausible value is reached.

4. **Status quo heuristic (Eidelman et al., 2010)**: Older/longer-existing products are rated as better even when objectively identical; participants are unaware of this bias.

5. **Self-fulfilling schemas — Rosenthal & Jacobson (1968)**: "Pygmalion effect": teachers' randomly assigned beliefs about "bloomers" actually caused IQ gains 8 months later. Classic schema perseverance and self-fulfilling prophecy demonstration.

6. **Priming and Unpriming (Sparrow & Wegner, 2006)**: Primed schemas persist until expressed; once expressed (unpriming), their effects dissipate. Unexpressed schemas can persist for years.

7. **Automatic social behavior (Bargh et al., 1996)**: Priming rudeness vs. politeness affected interruption behavior without awareness. Priming elderly schema slowed participants' walking speed. These are landmark studies for nonconscious/automatic behavior.

8. **Two neural systems**: Automatic/evaluative → amygdala; controlled/effortful → prefrontal cortex (medial and ventrolateral). Emotional automatic → limbic system; reasoning → prefrontal cortex.

9. **Unconscious thought advantage (Dijksterhuis & van Olden, 2006)**: Participants in the unconscious thought condition were most satisfied with their poster choice weeks later. Unconscious processing has larger capacity and better preserves true preferences than conscious deliberation.

10. **Counterfactual thinking**: Upward counterfactuals (imagining better outcomes) produce regret. The silver-bronze medalist finding (Medvec et al., 1995): bronze medalists appear happier because they engage in downward counterfactuals; silver medalists engage in upward counterfactuals.

11. **Misattribution of arousal (Dutton & Aron, 1974 — Capilano bridge)**: Men on high arousal (fear-inducing) bridge misattributed arousal as attraction to female confederate. Classic test of Schachter & Singer's two-factor theory.

12. **Impact bias and immune neglect**: People systematically overestimate how intensely and how long they will feel both positive and negative emotions about future events; they ignore the psychological immune system.

13. **Affect infusion model (Forgas, 1995a, 2000)**: Moods infuse into social judgments most strongly for complex, ambiguous, unfamiliar situations processed heuristically.

14. **Metaphors and social attitudes (Landau et al., 2010; Zhong & Leonardelli, 2008)**: Social exclusion priming led participants to perceive the room as 5 degrees colder — showing that abstract social states can alter physical perception through metaphorical mapping.

15. **Somatic marker hypothesis (Damasio, 1994)**: Patients with ventromedial prefrontal cortex damage reason logically but make poor real-life decisions because somatic markers linking body states to emotional outcomes are absent.
