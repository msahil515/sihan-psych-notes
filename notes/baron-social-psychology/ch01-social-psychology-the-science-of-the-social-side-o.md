# 1 Social Psychology: The Science of the Social Side of Life

Chapter 1 introduces social psychology as a scientific discipline that investigates the causes and nature of individual behavior, thought, and feeling in social situations. It establishes the scientific foundation of the field, surveys its major contemporary trends (social neuroscience, implicit processes, diversity, relationships, emotion-cognition integration), and explains the core research methods used by social psychologists.

---

## Social Psychology: An Overview

### Definition

**Social psychology** is defined as *the scientific field that seeks to understand the nature and causes of individual behavior, feelings, and thought in social situations.*

- Social situations include contexts involving the **actual presence** of other people **or** their **symbolic presence** (i.e., we imagine how others would react).
- Another way to state the definition: social psychology investigates the ways in which our thoughts, feelings, and actions are **influenced by the social environments** in which we live — by other people or our thoughts about them.
- The field covers: attraction, love, helping, prejudice, exclusion, violence, group influence, social thought, and everything in between.
- The **individual** is the primary unit of focus, even though group and cultural forces are acknowledged.

### Key Questions Addressed by the Field

- Why do people fall in — and out — of love?
- How can we influence others in the ways we desire?
- How do we know ourselves — our strengths, weaknesses, desires, needs?
- Why do we sometimes sacrifice our own welfare to help others, and sometimes withhold help?
- Why do we lose our tempers and act in ways we later regret? Why are anger, aggression, and violence so common?

## Social Psychology Is Scientific in Nature

### Science: A Clarification

- Science does **not** refer only to fields like chemistry, physics, or biology.
- **Science** refers to: (1) a **set of values** and (2) **a set of methods** that can be applied to a wide range of topics.
- A field is scientific if it adopts these values and methods — regardless of subject matter.
- Fields like **astrology** and **aromatherapy** rely on intuition, faith, and unobservable forces — the opposite of science.

### Four Core Scientific Values (All Fields Must Hold)

| Value | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | Gathering and evaluating information in as careful, precise, and error-free a manner as possible |
| **Objectivity** | Obtaining and evaluating information in a manner as free from bias as humanly possible |
| **Skepticism** | Accepting findings as accurate only to the extent they have been verified repeatedly |
| **Open-mindedness** | Willingness to change views — even strongly held ones — if evidence suggests they are inaccurate |

### Why Not Rely on Common Sense?

Common sense provides an **inconsistent and unreliable** guide to social behavior for two major reasons:

1. **Contradictory folk wisdom:** Common sense proverbs often directly contradict each other:
   - "Absence makes the heart grow fonder" vs. "Out of sight, out of mind"
   - "Two heads are better than one" vs. "Too many cooks spoil the broth"
   - "Familiarity breeds content" vs. "Familiarity breeds contempt"
   - Research (Norton, Frost, & Ariely, 2006) shows that sometimes the more we know about others, the **less** we like them — because awareness of dissimilarity increases with familiarity.

2. **Cognitive biases:** Unlike the fictional Mr. Spock (Star Trek), humans are not perfect information processors. Our thinking is subject to systematic biases.

> **KEY STUDY: Planning Fallacy**
> **Researchers:** Buehler, Griffin, & Ross (1994)
> **Finding:** People consistently believe projects will take **less time** than they actually do (the **planning fallacy**). This occurs because when estimating time, people think about the **future** rather than recalling how long similar past tasks took, leading them to underestimate the time needed. The bias persists even with repeated contrary experience.
> **Implication:** This is one example of systematic errors in informal social thinking — a key reason social psychologists rely on the scientific method rather than common sense.

## Social Psychology Focuses on the Behavior of Individuals

- Societies differ in views on courtship, marriage, violence — but it is still **individuals** who fall in love or commit aggression.
- Social behavior occurs in group settings, but the field's major interest is in factors shaping the **actions and thoughts of individuals** in social settings.
- Cultural and group forces are acknowledged but are studied through their influence on individuals.

---

## Social Psychology Seeks to Understand the Causes of Social Behavior and Thought

Social psychologists focus on four major categories of causes:

### 1. The Actions and Characteristics of Other People

- The **behavior** of others strongly impacts our own actions (e.g., seeing a police car slows speeding; an attractive person looking at you leads to approach behavior).
- The **appearance** of others affects us even when we try to consciously ignore it.
  - Research (Hassin & Trope, 2000): We **cannot ignore** others' appearance even when we consciously try to do so.
  - Nauman, Vazire, Rentfrow, & Gosling (2009): Relying on others' appearance as a guide to their characteristics is not always wrong; impressions can be relatively **accurate**, especially when observing spontaneous (not posed) behavior.
  - Appearance plays an important role in dating and romantic relationships (Burriss et al., 2011).

### 2. Cognitive Processes

- **Social cognition** — thinking about other people (what they are like, why they do what they do, how they might react) — plays a crucial role in social behavior and thought.
- We are always trying to make sense out of the social world.
- Example: Reactions to a late friend depend on memories of that friend's past behavior and inferences about the truth of their explanation.
- Social cognition is one of the **most important research areas** in the field (Fiske, 2009; Killeya & Johnson, 1998; Swann & Gill, 1997).

### 3. Environmental Variables: Impact of the Physical World

- **Full moon:** No reliable link to impulsive behavior (Rotton & Kelley, 1985).
- **Hot weather:** People are more irritable and aggressive in hot conditions than cool ones (Bell, Greene, Fisher, & Baum, 2001; Rotton & Cohn, 2000).
- **Pleasant smells:** Exposure to pleasant scents increases helping behavior (Baron, 1997); heat effects on aggression found even on baseball fields (Larrick, Timmerman, Carton, & Abrevaya, 2011).
- The **physical environment** does influence feelings, thoughts, and behavior.

### 4. Biological Factors

- Previously, most social psychologists said **no** to genetic influences on social behavior; now many believe preferences, behaviors, emotions, and attitudes are affected to some extent by **biological inheritance**.
- Social experiences interact with genetic factors (Gillath, Shaver, Baek, & Chun, 2008).
- This perspective comes from **evolutionary psychology**.

---

## Evolutionary Psychology: Detail

**Evolutionary psychology** is defined as *a branch of psychology that seeks to investigate the potential role of genetic factors in various aspects of human behavior.* (Buss, 2004; Buss & Shackelford, 1997)

### Three Components of Evolution

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Variation** | Organisms belonging to a given species vary in many ways (foundational to evolution) |
| **Inheritance** | Some variations can be passed from one generation to the next through hereditary mechanisms |
| **Selection** | Some variations give individuals an "edge" in reproduction — they are more likely to survive, find mates, and pass on these variations; over time, more members of the species possess these variations |

### Applications to Social Behavior

**Mate preference / Physical attractiveness:**
- Symmetrical facial features, well-toned bodies, clear skin, lustrous hair signal **"good genes"** — health and vigor, strong immune systems (Schmitt & Buss, 2001; Burriss et al., 2011; Li & Kenrick, 2006).
- These characteristics increased chances of successful reproduction for ancestors, shaping our current preferences.

**Humor and attraction:**
- Both genders, especially women, rate sense of humor highly in potential partners (Buss, 2008).
- Two evolutionary explanations:
  1. Humor signals **high intelligence** ("good genes")
  2. Humor signals **interest in forming new relationships** (availability)

> **KEY STUDY: Humor in Speed Dating**
> **Researcher:** Li et al. (2009)
> **Finding:** People are more likely to use humor and laugh at humor by others when they find those others attractive; humor during speed dating was perceived as signaling **romantic interest**.

**Critical clarification (exam-relevant):**
- Evolutionary psychology does **NOT** suggest that we inherit specific patterns of social behavior.
- It contends we inherit **tendencies or predispositions** that may manifest depending on the environment.
- These tendencies **can be overridden** by cognitive factors and learning (Pettijohn & Jungeberg, 2004).
- What is "attractive" varies across cultures and over time (e.g., overweight women are particularly desirable in Nigeria but less so in contemporary North America).

---

## The Search for Basic Principles in a Changing Social World

- Social psychology seeks **basic principles** that are accurate regardless of when or where they are applied.
- Challenge: Cultures differ greatly (e.g., views on dating supervision, marriage age, gifts to officials, retirement age).
- The social world is **rapidly changing** due to social networks, cell phones, online dating — new ways of meeting potential partners.
- Despite changes, the **same basic principles** apply: physical attractiveness remains central to romance; the basic principles of persuasion remain consistent (Goel, Mason, & Watts, 2010).
- Goal: Uncover basic, accurate facts about the social side of life that apply across a wide range of contexts.

## Social Psychology: Summing Up

Social psychology:
- Focuses on understanding **causes** of social behavior and social thought
- Uses **scientific methods**
- Acknowledges that social behavior and thought are influenced by **social, cognitive, environmental, cultural, and biological** factors
- Takes note of cultural differences while seeking universal basic principles

## Cognition and Behavior: Two Sides of the Same Social Coin

- **Historical division:** Researchers were either interested in social **behavior** (how people act) OR social **cognition** (how people make sense of the social world). This division has **totally disappeared** in modern social psychology.
- **Current consensus:** Behavior and cognition are **intimately and continuously linked**.
- Understanding how people behave requires considering their thoughts, memory, intentions, emotions, attitudes, and beliefs.
- There is a **continuous and complex interplay**: what we think about others influences actions toward them; consequences of actions affect social thought. The loop is continuous.
- Modern social psychology **integrates both** throughout all research areas.

## The Role of Emotion in the Social Side of Life

- Emotions and moods are a central aspect of social life and have always interested social psychologists.
- **Positive affect** (positive mood) increases the tendency to offer help to others (Isen & Levin, 1972).
- Current moods influence **first impressions** — our impressions of new people are strongly colored by our current emotional state.
- Social psychologists use the term **affect** to refer to feelings (moods and emotions).
- Recent expansion: Investigating emotions across a wider range of social behaviors and social thought (Forgas, Baumeister, & Tice, 2009).
- The book features special sections: "Emotion and Attitudes," "Emotion and Helping," "Emotion and Social Cognition."

## Relationships: How They Develop, Change, and Strengthen — or End

**Relationships** are defined as *our social ties with other persons, ranging from casual acquaintance or passing friendships, to intense, long-term relationships such as marriage or lifetime friendships.*

- Successful relationships add to happiness; failed relationships disrupt life and undermine psychological health, well-being, and **self-concept** (Slotter, Gardner, & Finkel, 2010).
- A growing body of research investigates: how relationships begin, change over time, why some strengthen and deepen while others weaken and die.

### Key Research on Relationships

> **KEY STUDY: Positivity Bias, Accuracy, and Marital Satisfaction**
> **Researcher:** Luo & Snider (2009)
> **Participants:** Several hundred newlywed couples
> **Method:** Measured (a) accuracy (comparing spouse's ratings of partner with partner's self-ratings), (b) positivity bias, (c) similarity bias; related these to marital satisfaction.
> **Findings:** All three dimensions predicted marital satisfaction. Positivity bias and similarity bias contributed to happiness, but **accuracy** did too. Conclusion: It is good to hold favorable perceptions of romantic partners, but these must be **moderated by a degree of accuracy**.
> **Folk saying tested:** "Love is blind" — partially true but must be restrained by reality.

> **KEY STUDY: Effects of Relationship Breakup**
> **Researcher:** Slotter, Gardner, & Finkel (2010)
> **Finding:** Breakup of romantic relationships **negatively affects self-concept** — people feel more vulnerable and less certain about who they are (reduced self-concept clarity).

> **KEY STUDY: Rebound Relationships**
> **Researcher:** Spielmann, MacDonald, & Wilson (2009)
> **Finding:** Experiencing a breakup **increases desire for another relationship** and encourages formation of new ones ("on the rebound"). Rebound relationships help people let go and "move on." These benefits are especially strong for people high in **anxious attachment**.

**Anxious attachment:** Anxiety over the possibility of losing a partner and/or an inability to get as close to partners as one would prefer.

## Social Neuroscience: Where Social Psychology and Brain Research Meet

### Tools of Social Neuroscience

- **Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI):** Measures brain activity as it functions in real time; allows scientists to observe what areas are active during various tasks.
- **Positron emission tomography (PET) scans:** Another brain imaging technique.
- Social psychologists use these tools to find which portions of the brain and which systems are involved in: prejudice, aggression, "choking under pressure," empathy, helping.

### N400 and Value-Inconsistent Information

> **KEY STUDY: Brain Responses to Value-Inconsistent Statements**
> **Researcher:** Van Berkum, Hollmean, Nieuwaland, Otten, & Murre (2009)
> **Participants:** Two groups: (1) strict Christians (against euthanasia, gender equality, abortion, drug use); (2) nonreligious individuals (opposite views).
> **Method:** Both groups viewed attitude-relevant statements on a computer screen; electrical brain activity was recorded.
> **Key Measure:** **N400** — an event-related potential (a kind of brain activity) that occurs approximately **250 milliseconds** after a value-inconsistent word is seen; indicates **intensified processing**.
> **Findings:** Each group showed stronger N400 reactions to words **inconsistent with their own values** (e.g., Christians reacted more to "acceptable" in "euthanasia is acceptable"). This occurs very quickly — **before** the entire statement is considered.
> **Conclusion:** We process value-inconsistent information **very quickly, automatically, and nonconsciously** — long before we can verbalize our reactions. Attitudes and values exert powerful and far-reaching effects on brain activity.

**N400:** An event-related potential (ERP) in brain activity occurring approximately 250 ms after encountering a value-inconsistent word; signals intensified processing.

### Mirror Neurons and Empathy

- **Mirror neurons:** Neurons in the brain that are activated both during the **observation** and **execution** of actions. Proposed to play a key role in **empathy** — the capacity to vicariously experience the emotions and feelings of others (Gazzola, Aziz-Zadeh, & Keysers, 2006).
- Mirror neurons are located in the **frontal operculum** (a portion of the brain).

> **KEY STUDY: Empathy and Mirror Neuron Activity**
> **Researcher:** Montgomery, Seeherman, & Haxby (2009)
> **Method:** Participants who scored high or low on a measure of empathy (capacity to take the perspective of others) viewed videos of: (a) social facial expressions (smiling, frowning) or (b) nonsocial facial movements. Brain activity was recorded via fMRI.
> **Findings:** People **high or moderate** in empathy showed **greater activity in the frontal operculum** (mirror neuron location) than low-empathy individuals — but only when watching **social** (emotional) facial expressions, not nonsocial movements.
> **Conclusion:** Mirror neurons in the frontal operculum are more active in high-empathy individuals when processing socially relevant emotional expressions.

### Limits of Social Neuroscience

- Social neuroscience **cannot answer every question** about social thought or behavior (Cacioppo et al., 2003).
- Many aspects of social thought (attitudes, attributions, group identities, reciprocity) cannot easily be related to specific brain areas (Willingham & Dunn, 2003).
- Analogy: All chemistry can theoretically be explained by physics, but chemistry remains useful as a distinct discipline. Similarly, social psychology does not need to reduce everything to neural activity.
- Modern social psychology uses a **wide range of methods** — from brain scans to direct observation.

## The Role of Implicit (Nonconscious) Processes

### Core Idea

- In many cases, we **do not know why** we think or behave as we do in social contexts.
- Our thoughts and actions are shaped by factors and processes of which we are **only dimly aware**, often operating in an **automatic manner** without conscious thought or intention.
- This is a key reason social psychologists distrust "common sense": we cannot accurately report on factors that unconsciously influence us (Pelham, Mirenberg, & Jones, 2002).

### First Impressions and Implicit Processes

- We form first impressions **incredibly quickly — often within seconds** of meeting people (Gray, 2008).
- Sometimes these impressions are **accurate**: we can form valid impressions of personality even from very brief exposures (Carney, Colvin, & Hall, 2007).
- But sometimes they are very **wrong**.

> **KEY STUDY: Accuracy of Snap Impressions**
> **Researcher:** Ames, Kammrath, Suppes, & Bolger (2010)
> **Finding:** We **cannot intuit** when our first impressions are likely to be accurate and when they are not. "Snap impression accuracy is sometimes above chance… but we can't tell when that is the case." (p. 273). Nonconscious processes influence judgments — but perhaps they should not.

### Other Topics Studied Under Implicit Processes

- Impact of moods on **memory** about other people or complex issues (Ruder & Bless, 2003).
- **Implicit negative attitudes** toward social outgroups that individuals deny having can still influence reactions toward those groups (Fazio & Hilden, 2001).
- **Automatic evaluation** of people based on perceived group membership (Castelli, Zobmaister, & Smith, 2004).

### Why This Matters

- People obtain tattoos thinking they will make them happy, only to find years later that they do not (Gilbert, 2006) — we are poor predictors of our own future happiness partly because of nonconscious biases.
- Nonconscious factors and processing play an important role in many aspects of social thought and behavior.

### Multicultural Perspective

**Multicultural perspective** is defined as *a focus on understanding the cultural and ethnic factors that influence social behavior.*

- Recognizes the importance of: **gender, age, ethnicity, sexual orientation, disability, socioeconomic status, religious orientation**, and many other dimensions.
- Contrast with past: Earlier perspective held that cultural, ethnic, and gender differences are **relatively unimportant**.
- Current view: Such differences are **very important** and must be carefully accounted for.

> **KEY STUDY: Cultural Differences in Preferred Body Types**
> **Researcher:** Swami et al. (2010)
> **Design:** Conducted in **10 different countries**; participants shown body weight drawings and asked to select the most attractive.
> **Findings:**
> - Raters in **Oceania, South and West Asia, Southeast Asia** preferred **heavier body types** than those in North America and East Asia.
> - Larger differences occurred **within cultures** based on **socioeconomic status (SES)**: Higher-SES individuals preferred **slimmer** body builds than lower-SES individuals.
> **Conclusion:** Differences within cultures (by SES) can be even greater than differences between cultures.

## How Social Psychologists Answer the Questions They Ask: Research as the Route to Increased Knowledge

Social psychologists use **systematic research** to answer questions. Three major research approaches:

1. Systematic Observation
2. Correlation
3. The Experimental Method

---

## Systematic Observation: Describing the World Around Us

**Systematic observation** is defined as carefully observing behavior as it occurs — NOT informal people-watching, but observation accompanied by careful, **accurate measurement** of specific behaviors across people.

### Naturalistic Observation

**Naturalistic observation:** Observation of people's behavior in **natural settings**, with no attempt to change behavior (Linden, 1992).

- Example: A social psychologist studying how frequently people touch each other in different settings would go to shopping malls, restaurants, bars, college campuses, and observe who touches whom, how, and how frequently.
- The researcher makes **no attempt to influence** people being observed.
- Researchers try to remain as **inconspicuous as possible** — hiding behind telephone poles, walls, or bushes to avoid detection.
- Key rule: The researcher simply **records** what is happening.

### Survey Method

**Survey method:** Researchers ask **large numbers of people** to respond to questions about their attitudes or behavior.

**Uses of surveys:**
- Measure attitudes toward issues (smoking, political candidates, social groups)
- Assess student reactions to professors
- Measure attitudes toward social policy (national health care, affirmative action)
- Assess life satisfaction globally
- Measure consumer reactions to new products

**Advantages of surveys:**
- Can gather information from **thousands or hundreds of thousands** of people with relative ease.
- Can be conducted **online** through the Internet (e.g., www.authentichappiness.com for happiness research).

## Correlation: The Search for Relationships

*(Section from chapter outline — content builds on methods introduced above)*

**Correlation** refers to the search for relationships between variables — whether two or more variables are related in a systematic way.

- **Positive correlation:** As one variable increases, the other increases.
- **Negative correlation:** As one variable increases, the other decreases.
- **Correlation coefficient:** A statistical measure ranging from -1.00 to +1.00 indicating the strength and direction of a relationship.

**Critical limitation:** Correlation does **NOT** establish causation.
- A correlation between two variables (A and B) may occur because: A causes B, B causes A, or a third variable (C) causes both A and B.

---

## The Experimental Method: Knowledge Through Systematic Intervention

**Experimental method:** The researcher **systematically varies** one or more variables (independent variables) and observes the effects on other variables (dependent variables), while holding all other factors constant.

### Key Components

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Independent variable (IV)** | The variable that is systematically varied or manipulated by the researcher |
| **Dependent variable (DV)** | The variable that is measured to assess the effect of the IV |
| **Random assignment** | Participants are assigned to experimental conditions by chance, so that pre-existing differences between participants are equalized across conditions |
| **Experimental group** | The group that receives the treatment/manipulation |
| **Control group** | The group that does not receive the treatment (serves as a baseline for comparison) |

**Advantage of experimental method:** It can establish **cause-and-effect** relationships (unlike correlation).

**Why random assignment matters:** It ensures that any differences observed between groups after the manipulation are due to the IV, not pre-existing differences between participants.

### Internal vs. External Validity

- **Internal validity:** The degree to which an experiment successfully establishes cause-and-effect (achieved through control).
- **External validity:** The degree to which results can be **generalized** beyond the specific experimental setting (often challenged by laboratory conditions that may not mirror real life).

---

## Further Thoughts on Causality: The Role of Mediating Variables

**Mediating variable (mediator):** A variable that **explains the mechanism** by which an independent variable produces its effects on a dependent variable — it lies on the causal pathway between IV and DV.

- Example: Heat (IV) may increase aggression (DV) because it increases feelings of discomfort (mediator) — discomfort is the mechanism by which heat affects aggression.
- Identifying mediating variables helps researchers understand **how** and **why** a cause produces its effect, not just **that** it does.

## The Role of Theory in Social Psychology

**Theory** in social psychology: A set of interrelated **propositions** or statements about why certain events or processes occur — an organized framework for explaining observed phenomena.

### Functions of Theory

1. **Organization:** Theories organize existing knowledge.
2. **Prediction:** Theories generate specific, testable **hypotheses** (predictions about what will happen under specific conditions).
3. **Explanation:** Theories tell us **why** observed relationships exist.

### How Theory and Research Interact

- Researchers begin with a theory, derive a **hypothesis** from it, design a study to test the hypothesis, collect data, and either support or disconfirm the theory.
- If the results do not support the hypothesis, the theory is revised or discarded.
- A **hypothesis** is a specific testable prediction derived from a theory.

**Good theories:**
- Are **falsifiable** (capable of being proven wrong)
- Generate clear, testable hypotheses
- Parsimonious (explain a lot with as little as possible)

## The Quest for Knowledge and the Rights of Individuals: In Search of an Appropriate Balance

### Ethical Issues in Social Psychological Research

- The scientific pursuit of knowledge must be **balanced** against the rights and well-being of research participants.
- Key ethical concerns:
  1. **Deception:** Social psychologists sometimes deceive participants about the true nature of a study (e.g., telling participants they are in a study on memory when it is actually about conformity). This raises ethical issues.
  2. **Informed consent:** Participants should be told enough about a study to decide whether they want to participate.
  3. **Debriefing:** After a study, participants must be fully informed about its true purpose, especially if deception was used.
  4. **Harm to participants:** Research must not cause lasting psychological or physical harm.

### The American Psychological Association (APA) Guidelines

- Social psychologists follow **APA ethical guidelines** governing the use of human participants.
- Research proposals are reviewed by **Institutional Review Boards (IRBs)** before studies begin.
- **Deception** is permitted only if:
  - There is no alternative way to conduct the study
  - The study has significant scientific merit
  - Participants will be fully **debriefed** afterward

### Balancing Deception and Knowledge

- Some classic studies (e.g., Milgram's obedience studies) used deception extensively — and generated important findings, but also raised significant ethical concerns.
- Modern social psychology uses deception more carefully and selectively.
- **Confederates:** Research assistants who pose as participants or other actors in a study, often to create experimental conditions.

## SUMMARY AND REVIEW

1. **Social psychology** is the scientific field that seeks to understand the nature and causes of individual behavior, feelings, and thought in social situations.
2. It is **scientific** because it adopts the values (accuracy, objectivity, skepticism, open-mindedness) and methods of science.
3. **Common sense** provides an unreliable guide — it is contradictory, and human thinking is subject to systematic biases (e.g., planning fallacy).
4. Social psychology focuses on **individuals** and seeks to understand causes of social behavior: actions/appearance of others, cognitive processes, environmental factors, biological/genetic factors.
5. **Evolutionary psychology** emphasizes that we have inherited tendencies (not fixed behaviors) shaped by variation, inheritance, and selection.
6. Major current trends: cognition-behavior integration, emotion, relationships, social neuroscience, implicit processes, multicultural perspective.
7. Research methods: systematic observation (naturalistic observation, surveys), correlational research, experimental method.
8. Mediating variables explain **how** IVs cause DVs.
9. Theories generate testable hypotheses and are revised based on evidence.
10. Ethical guidelines (APA; IRBs; informed consent, debriefing) balance knowledge-seeking with participant rights.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Definition of social psychology** (Baron & Branscombe): "the scientific field that seeks to understand the nature and causes of individual behavior, feelings, and thought in social situations" — note: includes BOTH actual AND symbolic presence of others.

2. **Four core scientific values**: Accuracy, Objectivity, Skepticism, Open-mindedness — all four are MCQ targets; know which is which.

3. **Planning fallacy** (Buehler, Griffin, & Ross, 1994): People underestimate task duration because they think about the future rather than recalling how long similar past tasks took.

4. **Three components of evolution**: Variation, Inheritance, Selection — listed in this order; all three are required for evolution to occur.

5. **Evolutionary psychology does NOT mean** we inherit fixed behaviors — we inherit **tendencies or predispositions** that can be overridden by cognitive factors and experience.

6. **Mirror neurons** are located in the **frontal operculum**; they are activated during both observation and execution of actions; linked to **empathy** (Gazzola et al., 2006; Montgomery et al., 2009).

7. **N400** occurs at approximately **250 milliseconds** after seeing a value-inconsistent word; it is an **event-related potential** indicating intensified processing (Van Berkum et al., 2009).

8. **Naturalistic observation**: Researcher observes behavior in natural settings WITHOUT attempting to influence or change behavior — researcher must remain inconspicuous.

9. **Correlation does NOT equal causation**: Three possible explanations for any correlation: A causes B, B causes A, or a third variable C causes both.

10. **Mediating variable**: Lies on the causal pathway between IV and DV; explains the **mechanism** of the effect (HOW the IV causes the DV), not just that it does.

11. **Multicultural perspective**: Recognizes importance of gender, age, ethnicity, sexual orientation, disability, SES, religious orientation in social behavior; emerged in contrast to earlier view that cultural/ethnic differences are relatively unimportant.

12. **Anxious attachment**: Anxiety about losing a partner or inability to get as close to partners as desired; people high in this trait benefit most from rebound relationships after a breakup (Spielmann et al., 2009).

13. **Luo & Snider (2009)**: In newlywed couples, both positivity bias AND accuracy predict marital satisfaction — neither alone is sufficient; too much idealization without accuracy is not optimal.

14. **Swami et al. (2010) — cross-cultural body preference study**: Conducted in 10 countries; within-culture SES differences in preferred body weight were LARGER than between-culture differences — higher SES = preference for slimmer builds.

15. **Snap impressions (Ames et al., 2010)**: We form first impressions within seconds; they are sometimes accurate, but we **cannot tell** when they are accurate vs. inaccurate — we lack metacognitive access to their reliability.
