# 1 Social Psychology: The Science of the Social Side of Life

Chapter 1 introduces social psychology as a scientific field studying the causes and nature of individual behavior, thought, and feeling in social situations. It covers the field's scientific foundation, major contemporary trends, and core research methods.

---

## Social Psychology: An Overview

### Definition

**Social psychology** is *the scientific field that seeks to understand the nature and causes of individual behavior, feelings, and thought in social situations.*

- Social situations involve the **actual presence** of others **or** their **symbolic presence** (imagining how others react).
- It investigates how our thoughts, feelings, and actions are **influenced by the social environment**: by others or our thoughts about them.
- Covers: attraction, love, helping, prejudice, exclusion, violence, group influence, social thought.
- The **individual** is the primary unit of focus, even though group and cultural forces are acknowledged.

### Key Questions Addressed

- Why do people fall in and out of love?
- How can we influence others?
- How do we know ourselves: our strengths, weaknesses, needs?
- Why do we sometimes help others and sometimes withhold help?
- Why are anger, aggression, and violence so common?

---

## Social Psychology Is Scientific in Nature

### Science: A Clarification

- **Science** = (1) a **set of values** and (2) **a set of methods** applicable to a wide range of topics, regardless of subject matter.
- It is not restricted to chemistry, physics, biology.
- **Astrology** and **aromatherapy** rely on intuition, faith, and unobservable forces: the opposite of science.

### Four Core Scientific Values

| Value | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | Gathering and evaluating information in as careful, precise, and error-free a manner as possible |
| **Objectivity** | Obtaining and evaluating information as free from bias as humanly possible |
| **Skepticism** | Accepting findings as accurate only to the extent they have been verified repeatedly |
| **Open-mindedness** | Willingness to change views, even strongly held ones, if evidence suggests they are inaccurate |

### Why Not Rely on Common Sense?

Common sense is **inconsistent and unreliable** for two reasons:

1. **Contradictory folk wisdom:** proverbs contradict each other:
   - "Absence makes the heart grow fonder" vs. "Out of sight, out of mind"
   - "Two heads are better than one" vs. "Too many cooks spoil the broth"
   - "Familiarity breeds content" vs. "Familiarity breeds contempt"
   - Norton, Frost, & Ariely (2006): sometimes the more we know about others, the **less** we like them (awareness of dissimilarity increases with familiarity).
2. **Cognitive biases:** humans are not perfect information processors; thinking is subject to systematic biases.

> **KEY STUDY: Planning Fallacy**
> **Researchers:** Buehler, Griffin, & Ross (1994)
> **Finding:** People believe projects will take **less time** than they actually do (the **planning fallacy**), because they think about the **future** rather than recalling how long past tasks took. Persists despite contrary experience.
> **Implication:** An example of systematic error in informal thinking, a reason social psychologists use the scientific method over common sense.

## Social Psychology Focuses on the Behavior of Individuals

- Societies differ on courtship, marriage, violence, but it is **individuals** who fall in love or act aggressively.
- Social behavior occurs in groups, but the field's interest is in factors shaping the **actions and thoughts of individuals**.
- Cultural and group forces are studied through their influence on individuals.

---

## Social Psychology Seeks to Understand the Causes of Social Behavior and Thought

Four major categories of causes:

### 1. The Actions and Characteristics of Other People

- The **behavior** of others impacts our actions (e.g., a police car slows speeding).
- The **appearance** of others affects us even when we try to ignore it:
  - Hassin & Trope (2000): We **cannot ignore** others' appearance even when consciously trying.
  - Nauman, Vazire, Rentfrow, & Gosling (2009): appearance-based impressions can be relatively **accurate**, especially from spontaneous (not posed) behavior.
  - Appearance matters in dating and romance (Burriss et al., 2011).

### 2. Cognitive Processes

- **Social cognition** (thinking about other people: what they are like, why they act, how they react) is crucial to social behavior.
- We always try to make sense of the social world. E.g., reactions to a late friend depend on memories and inferences about their explanation.
- One of the **most important research areas** in the field (Fiske, 2009).

### 3. Environmental Variables: The Physical World

- **Full moon:** no reliable link to impulsive behavior (Rotton & Kelley, 1985).
- **Hot weather:** more irritability and aggression (Bell, Greene, Fisher, & Baum, 2001; Rotton & Cohn, 2000); heat-aggression effect even on baseball fields (Larrick, Timmerman, Carton, & Abrevaya, 2011).
- **Pleasant smells:** increase helping behavior (Baron, 1997).

### 4. Biological Factors

- Many now believe preferences, behaviors, emotions, and attitudes are affected to some extent by **biological inheritance**.
- Social experiences interact with genetic factors (Gillath, Shaver, Baek, & Chun, 2008).
- This perspective comes from **evolutionary psychology**.

---

## Evolutionary Psychology: Detail

**Evolutionary psychology** is *a branch of psychology investigating the potential role of genetic factors in human behavior* (Buss, 2004; Buss & Shackelford, 1997).

### Three Components of Evolution

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Variation** | Organisms in a species vary in many ways |
| **Inheritance** | Some variations are passed across generations through heredity |
| **Selection** | Variations giving a reproductive "edge" make individuals more likely to survive, mate, and pass them on; over time more members possess them |

### Applications to Social Behavior

**Mate preference / Physical attractiveness:**
- Symmetrical features, well-toned bodies, clear skin, lustrous hair signal **"good genes"**: health, vigor, strong immune systems (Schmitt & Buss, 2001; Li & Kenrick, 2006). These traits increased ancestral reproductive success, shaping current preferences.

**Humor and attraction:**
- Both genders, especially women, rate sense of humor highly (Buss, 2008). Two evolutionary explanations:
  1. Humor signals **high intelligence** ("good genes").
  2. Humor signals **interest in forming relationships** (availability).

> **KEY STUDY: Humor in Speed Dating**
> **Researcher:** Li et al. (2009)
> **Finding:** People use and laugh at humor more when they find others attractive; humor during speed dating signaled **romantic interest**.

**Critical clarification (exam-relevant):**
- Evolutionary psychology does **NOT** mean we inherit specific behaviors; we inherit **tendencies/predispositions** that manifest depending on environment.
- These tendencies **can be overridden** by cognitive factors and learning (Pettijohn & Jungeberg, 2004).
- What is "attractive" varies across cultures and time (e.g., overweight women particularly desirable in Nigeria, less so in North America).

---

## The Search for Basic Principles in a Changing Social World

- The field seeks **basic principles** accurate regardless of when or where applied.
- Challenge: cultures differ greatly (dating supervision, marriage age, retirement age).
- The social world is **rapidly changing** (social networks, cell phones, online dating).
- Despite changes, the **same basic principles** apply: physical attractiveness remains central; persuasion principles stay consistent (Goel, Mason, & Watts, 2010).

---

## Social Psychology: Summing Up

Social psychology:
- Focuses on **causes** of social behavior and thought.
- Uses **scientific methods**.
- Recognizes influences from **social, cognitive, environmental, cultural, and biological** factors.
- Notes cultural differences while seeking universal basic principles.

## Cognition and Behavior: Two Sides of the Same Social Coin

- The historical division between researchers of social **behavior** vs. social **cognition** has **disappeared**.
- Current consensus: behavior and cognition are **intimately and continuously linked**.
- Understanding behavior requires considering thoughts, memory, intentions, emotions, attitudes, beliefs.
- A **continuous interplay**: thoughts about others shape actions; consequences of actions affect thought.
- Modern social psychology **integrates both** throughout.

---

## The Role of Emotion in the Social Side of Life

- Emotions and moods are central to social life.
- **Positive affect** increases helping (Isen & Levin, 1972).
- Current moods influence **first impressions**.
- **Affect** = feelings (moods and emotions).
- Recent expansion: emotion studied across a wider range of social behaviors (Forgas, Baumeister, & Tice, 2009).

## Relationships: How They Develop, Change, and Strengthen or End

**Relationships** = *our social ties with others, from casual acquaintance or passing friendship to intense long-term bonds such as marriage or lifetime friendship.*

- Successful relationships add to happiness; failed ones undermine well-being and **self-concept** (Slotter, Gardner, & Finkel, 2010).
- Research investigates how relationships begin, change, strengthen, or die.

### Key Research on Relationships

> **KEY STUDY: Positivity Bias, Accuracy, and Marital Satisfaction**
> **Researcher:** Luo & Snider (2009)
> **Participants:** Several hundred newlywed couples.
> **Method:** Measured (a) accuracy, (b) positivity bias, (c) similarity bias; related to marital satisfaction.
> **Findings:** All three predicted marital satisfaction. Positivity and similarity bias contributed, but so did **accuracy**. Favorable perceptions of partners are good but must be **moderated by accuracy**.
> **Folk saying tested:** "Love is blind" (partly true but restrained by reality).

> **KEY STUDY: Effects of Relationship Breakup**
> **Researcher:** Slotter, Gardner, & Finkel (2010)
> **Finding:** Breakup **negatively affects self-concept**: people feel more vulnerable and less certain who they are (reduced self-concept clarity).

> **KEY STUDY: Rebound Relationships**
> **Researcher:** Spielmann, MacDonald, & Wilson (2009)
> **Finding:** Breakup **increases desire for another relationship** and encourages new ones ("on the rebound"), helping people move on. Benefits especially strong for those high in **anxious attachment**.

**Anxious attachment:** anxiety over losing a partner and/or inability to get as close as desired.

## Social Neuroscience: Where Social Psychology and Brain Research Meet

### Tools

- **fMRI (functional magnetic resonance imaging):** measures real-time brain activity, showing which areas are active during tasks.
- **PET (positron emission tomography) scans:** another brain imaging technique.
- Used to find which brain systems are involved in prejudice, aggression, choking under pressure, empathy, helping.

### N400 and Value-Inconsistent Information

> **KEY STUDY: Brain Responses to Value-Inconsistent Statements**
> **Researcher:** Van Berkum, Hollmean, Nieuwaland, Otten, & Murre (2009)
> **Participants:** (1) strict Christians; (2) nonreligious individuals (opposite views).
> **Method:** Both viewed attitude-relevant statements; electrical brain activity recorded.
> **Key Measure:** **N400**, an event-related potential occurring ~**250 ms** after a value-inconsistent word; indicates **intensified processing**.
> **Findings:** Each group showed stronger N400 to words **inconsistent with their values** (e.g., Christians to "acceptable" in "euthanasia is acceptable"), occurring **before** the whole statement is processed.
> **Conclusion:** We process value-inconsistent information **very quickly, automatically, nonconsciously**. Attitudes/values powerfully affect brain activity.

**N400:** an event-related potential (ERP) ~250 ms after a value-inconsistent word; signals intensified processing.

### Mirror Neurons and Empathy

- **Mirror neurons:** activated during both **observation** and **execution** of actions; linked to **empathy** (vicariously experiencing others' emotions) (Gazzola, Aziz-Zadeh, & Keysers, 2006).
- Located in the **frontal operculum**.

> **KEY STUDY: Empathy and Mirror Neuron Activity**
> **Researcher:** Montgomery, Seeherman, & Haxby (2009)
> **Method:** High- vs. low-empathy participants viewed videos of (a) social facial expressions or (b) nonsocial facial movements; fMRI recorded.
> **Findings:** High/moderate-empathy people showed **greater frontal operculum activity** than low-empathy people, but only for **social** (emotional) expressions.
> **Conclusion:** Mirror neurons in the frontal operculum are more active in high-empathy people processing emotional expressions.

### Limits of Social Neuroscience

- It **cannot answer every question** about social thought/behavior (Cacioppo et al., 2003).
- Many aspects (attitudes, attributions, group identities, reciprocity) cannot easily map to specific brain areas (Willingham & Dunn, 2003).
- Analogy: chemistry could theoretically reduce to physics but remains useful; social psychology need not reduce everything to neural activity.
- The field uses a **wide range of methods**, from brain scans to direct observation.

## The Role of Implicit (Nonconscious) Processes

### Core Idea

- We often **do not know why** we think or act as we do socially.
- Thoughts and actions are shaped by factors we are **only dimly aware** of, often operating **automatically** without conscious intention.
- A key reason to distrust common sense: we cannot accurately report unconscious influences (Pelham, Mirenberg, & Jones, 2002).

### First Impressions and Implicit Processes

- We form first impressions **within seconds** (Gray, 2008).
- Sometimes **accurate** (valid impressions from brief exposures; Carney, Colvin, & Hall, 2007), sometimes very **wrong**.

> **KEY STUDY: Accuracy of Snap Impressions**
> **Researcher:** Ames, Kammrath, Suppes, & Bolger (2010)
> **Finding:** We **cannot intuit** when first impressions are accurate. Snap-impression accuracy is sometimes above chance, but we cannot tell when. Nonconscious processes influence judgments.

### Other Topics Under Implicit Processes

- Impact of moods on **memory** about others/issues (Ruder & Bless, 2003).
- **Implicit negative attitudes** toward outgroups that people deny can still influence reactions (Fazio & Hilden, 2001).
- **Automatic evaluation** based on perceived group membership (Castelli, Zobmaister, & Smith, 2004).

### Why This Matters

- People get tattoos expecting happiness, only to regret them later (Gilbert, 2006): we predict our own future happiness poorly, partly due to nonconscious biases.

### Multicultural Perspective

**Multicultural perspective** = *a focus on understanding the cultural and ethnic factors that influence social behavior.*

- Recognizes importance of **gender, age, ethnicity, sexual orientation, disability, socioeconomic status, religious orientation**.
- Contrast with the earlier view that cultural, ethnic, and gender differences are unimportant; current view holds they are **very important**.

> **KEY STUDY: Cultural Differences in Preferred Body Types**
> **Researcher:** Swami et al. (2010)
> **Design:** **10 countries**; participants chose the most attractive body weight drawing.
> **Findings:**
> - **Oceania, South/West Asia, Southeast Asia** preferred **heavier** body types than North America and East Asia.
> - Larger differences occurred **within cultures** by **socioeconomic status (SES)**: higher-SES preferred **slimmer** builds.
> **Conclusion:** Within-culture (SES) differences can exceed between-culture differences.

## How Social Psychologists Answer Questions: Research as the Route to Knowledge

Three major research approaches:

1. Systematic Observation
2. Correlation
3. The Experimental Method

---

## Systematic Observation: Describing the World Around Us

**Systematic observation** = carefully observing behavior as it occurs with careful, **accurate measurement** of specific behaviors (not informal people-watching).

### Naturalistic Observation

**Naturalistic observation:** observing behavior in **natural settings**, with no attempt to change it (Linden, 1992).

- Example: to study how often people touch, a researcher observes malls, restaurants, bars, campuses, recording who touches whom and how often.
- Makes **no attempt to influence** those observed; remains as **inconspicuous** as possible.
- Key rule: simply **record** what happens.

### Survey Method

**Survey method:** asking **large numbers of people** about their attitudes or behavior.

**Uses:** attitudes toward issues (smoking, candidates, groups), professor ratings, social policy (health care, affirmative action), life satisfaction, consumer reactions.

**Advantages:**
- Gathers data from **thousands or more** people easily.
- Can be conducted **online**.

---

## Correlation: The Search for Relationships

**Correlation** = the search for whether two or more variables are related in a systematic way.

- **Positive correlation:** as one variable increases, the other increases.
- **Negative correlation:** as one increases, the other decreases.
- **Correlation coefficient:** a statistical measure from -1.00 to +1.00 indicating strength and direction.

**Critical limitation:** correlation does **NOT** establish causation. A correlation between A and B may occur because A causes B, B causes A, or a third variable C causes both.

---

## The Experimental Method: Knowledge Through Systematic Intervention

**Experimental method:** systematically varying one or more variables (IVs) and observing effects on others (DVs), holding all else constant.

### Key Components

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Independent variable (IV)** | The variable systematically varied/manipulated by the researcher |
| **Dependent variable (DV)** | The variable measured to assess the effect of the IV |
| **Random assignment** | Assigning participants to conditions by chance, equalizing pre-existing differences |
| **Experimental group** | Group receiving the treatment/manipulation |
| **Control group** | Group receiving no treatment; serves as baseline |

**Advantage:** establishes **cause-and-effect** (unlike correlation).

**Why random assignment matters:** ensures group differences after manipulation are due to the IV, not pre-existing differences.

### Internal vs. External Validity

- **Internal validity:** degree to which an experiment establishes cause-and-effect (via control).
- **External validity:** degree to which results **generalize** beyond the setting (challenged by artificial lab conditions).

---

## Further Thoughts on Causality: Mediating Variables

**Mediating variable (mediator):** a variable that **explains the mechanism** by which an IV affects a DV; lies on the causal pathway.

- Example: heat (IV) may increase aggression (DV) by increasing discomfort (mediator).
- Identifying mediators shows **how/why** a cause produces its effect.

---

## The Role of Theory in Social Psychology

**Theory:** a set of interrelated **propositions** about why events or processes occur; a framework for explaining phenomena.

### Functions

1. **Organization:** organize existing knowledge.
2. **Prediction:** generate testable **hypotheses**.
3. **Explanation:** tell us **why** relationships exist.

### How Theory and Research Interact

- Begin with a theory, derive a **hypothesis**, design a study, collect data, support or disconfirm the theory; revise/discard if unsupported.
- A **hypothesis** is a specific testable prediction derived from a theory.

**Good theories** are **falsifiable**, generate testable hypotheses, and are parsimonious.

---

## The Quest for Knowledge and the Rights of Individuals

### Ethical Issues

Knowledge-seeking must be **balanced** against participants' rights. Key concerns:

1. **Deception:** misleading participants about a study's true nature (raises ethical issues).
2. **Informed consent:** participants told enough to decide whether to participate.
3. **Debriefing:** after a study, participants fully informed of its true purpose, especially if deceived.
4. **Harm to participants:** research must not cause lasting harm.

### APA Guidelines

- Social psychologists follow **APA ethical guidelines**.
- Proposals reviewed by **Institutional Review Boards (IRBs)**.
- **Deception** permitted only if: no alternative exists, the study has significant merit, and participants are fully **debriefed**.

### Balancing Deception and Knowledge

- Classic studies (e.g., Milgram's obedience studies) used deception, generating important findings but raising ethical concerns.
- Modern social psychology uses deception more carefully.
- **Confederates:** assistants posing as participants/bystanders to create experimental conditions.

## SUMMARY AND REVIEW

1. **Social psychology** seeks to understand the nature and causes of individual behavior, feelings, and thought in social situations.
2. It is **scientific**: adopts the values (accuracy, objectivity, skepticism, open-mindedness) and methods of science.
3. **Common sense** is unreliable: contradictory, and human thinking has systematic biases (e.g., planning fallacy).
4. Focuses on **individuals**; causes of social behavior: others' actions/appearance, cognition, environment, biology/genetics.
5. **Evolutionary psychology**: we inherit tendencies (not fixed behaviors) shaped by variation, inheritance, selection.
6. Major trends: cognition-behavior integration, emotion, relationships, social neuroscience, implicit processes, multicultural perspective.
7. Research methods: systematic observation (naturalistic, surveys), correlation, experiment.
8. Mediating variables explain **how** IVs cause DVs.
9. Theories generate testable hypotheses and are revised on evidence.
10. Ethical guidelines (APA, IRBs, informed consent, debriefing) balance knowledge with participant rights.

## Likely Exam Points

3. **Planning fallacy** (Buehler, Griffin, & Ross, 1994): underestimate task duration by thinking about the future, not past task length.
4. **Three components of evolution**: Variation, Inheritance, Selection (in this order).
5. **Evolutionary psychology does NOT mean** fixed inherited behaviors; we inherit **tendencies/predispositions** overridable by cognition and experience.

7. **N400** occurs ~**250 ms** after a value-inconsistent word; an **event-related potential** indicating intensified processing (Van Berkum et al., 2009).

9. **Correlation does NOT equal causation**: A→B, B→A, or third variable C causes both.

11. **Multicultural perspective**: recognizes gender, age, ethnicity, sexual orientation, disability, SES, religious orientation; emerged against the earlier "differences are unimportant" view.
12. **Anxious attachment**: anxiety about losing a partner or inability to get close; high scorers benefit most from rebound relationships (Spielmann et al., 2009).
13. **Luo & Snider (2009)**: in newlyweds, both positivity bias AND accuracy predict marital satisfaction.
