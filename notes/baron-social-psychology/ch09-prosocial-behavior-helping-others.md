# 9 Prosocial Behavior: Helping Others

**Chapter Overview:** This chapter examines why and when people help others, covering the major theoretical motives for prosocial behavior (empathy-altruism, negative-state relief, empathic joy, competitive altruism, kin selection, defensive helping), the conditions under which bystanders intervene or fail to intervene in emergencies (bystander effect, diffusion of responsibility, five-step decision model), situational and personal factors that increase or decrease helping, and the effects of being helped on recipients.

---

## Why People Help: Motives for Prosocial Behavior

- **Prosocial behavior**: Actions by individuals that help others, often with no immediate benefit to the helper.
- Multiple motives underlie prosocial behavior; it is not a simple, single-cause phenomenon.
- Both situational (external) and dispositional (internal/personal) factors determine who helps, when, and how much.

---

### Empathy-Altruism: It Feels Good to Help Others

#### Core Concept
- **Empathy**: Emotional reactions focused on or oriented toward other people; includes feelings of compassion, sympathy, and concern; also involves the capacity to share others' emotional states and take their perspective (Eisenberg, 2000; Hodges et al., 2010).
- **Empathy-Altruism Hypothesis** (Batson, Duncan, Ackerman, Buckley, & Birch, 1981): Proposes that at least some prosocial acts are motivated solely by the desire to help someone in need.
  - Such motivation can be strong enough that the helper engages in unpleasant, dangerous, or even life-threatening activities (Batson & Batson et al., 1995).
  - Compassion for others outweighs all other considerations (Batson, Klein, Highberger, & Shaw, 1995; Goetz, Keltner, & Simon-Thomas, 2010).
  - Helping is unselfish in motive but can also be self-serving in effect (reduces the helper's own negative feelings vicariously).

#### Three Distinct Components of Empathy
Research identifies three distinct, separable components:

| Component | Definition | Role in Prosocial Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Emotional Empathy** | Sharing the feelings and emotions of others (vicarious emotional experience) | Motivates direct helping to end others' distress |
| **Empathic Accuracy** | Cognitive component; accurately perceiving others' thoughts and feelings ("everyday mind-reading") | Linked to social adjustment and effective responding |
| **Empathic Concern** | Feelings of concern for another's well-being | Closely linked to helping; influenced by similarity |

- The three components are related to *different* aspects of prosocial behavior and have different long-term effects.

> **KEY STUDY: Gleason, Jensen-Campbell, & Ickes (2009) — Empathic Accuracy and Social Adjustment**
> - **Participants:** Adolescents
> - **Hypothesis:** Higher empathic accuracy → better social adjustment (more friends, greater peer acceptance, higher friendship quality, less bullying/social exclusion)
> - **Method:** Participants watched a videotape of a student interacting with a teacher; tape paused at specific points; participants wrote down what they thought people were thinking/feeling; accuracy compared against what people in the tape actually reported.
> - **Finding:** Higher empathic accuracy correlated with better social adjustment on all measured dimensions.
> - **Caveat:** Causality is unclear — good social relationships may also produce greater empathy.

#### Is Empathy Declining?
- Evidence from Konrath, O'Brien, & Hsing (2011): U.S. college students today report *lower* empathy than students in previous decades.
- Declines are small but significant for:
  1. **Empathic concern** (concern for feelings/well-being of others)
  2. **Empathic perspective taking** (ability to take others' perspective)
- Proposed causes (unproven):
  - Increased media violence exposure
  - Increased emphasis on individual self-esteem in schools
  - Reality television ("winners take all," "put yourself first")
  - Social media reducing face-to-face contact, making it easier to ignore others' needs

### Negative-State Relief: Helping Sometimes Reduces Unpleasant Feelings

- **Negative-State Relief Model** (Cialdini, Baumann, & Kenrick, 1981): Proposes that prosocial behavior is motivated by the bystander's desire to reduce his or her own uncomfortable negative emotions.
  - We help not because we care about others' welfare (empathic concern), but to stop feeling bad ourselves.
  - The source of the bystander's negative emotions is irrelevant: can be unrelated to the emergency (e.g., a bad grade) or caused by the emergency itself (e.g., witnessing injury).
  - Empathy is **not** a necessary component in this model.
  - Unhappiness leads to prosocial behavior as long as helping will relieve the negative mood (Dietrich & Berkowitz, 1997; Fultz, Shaller, & Cialdini, 1988).
- Mirror image of the empathy-altruism hypothesis: rather than focusing on the victim's feelings, focus is on the helper's own emotional discomfort.

---

### Empathic Joy: Helping as an Accomplishment

- **Empathic Joy Hypothesis** (Smith, Keating, & Stotland, 1989): Helpers enjoy the positive reactions shown by others whom they help; helping is rewarding in and of itself because of the positive impact on the recipient.
- Key implication: Helpers need **feedback** about the effectiveness of their help for empathic joy to occur. If helping were based purely on empathic concern, feedback would be irrelevant.

> **KEY STUDY: Smith, Keating, & Stotland (1989) — Empathic Joy**
> - **Method:** Participants watched a videotape of a female student who said she might drop out of college because she felt isolated.
>   - Manipulation 1: Student was either similar (high empathy) or dissimilar (low empathy) to participant.
>   - Manipulation 2: Participants were told either they would or would not receive feedback about the effectiveness of their advice.
> - **Finding:** Empathy alone was insufficient to produce prosocial response. Helping only occurred when there was **both high empathy AND feedback** about the impact on the victim.
> - **Conclusion:** The prospect of experiencing empathic joy (witnessing positive reactions in the helped person) is a necessary motivator.

### Why Nice People Sometimes Finish First: Competitive Altruism

- **Competitive Altruism** (also called competitive altruism approach): The view that one important reason people help others is that doing so boosts their own status and reputation, ultimately bringing them large benefits that more than offset the costs of prosocial actions.
- Logic: Helping others is costly → signals desirable personal qualities to others → confers high status → brings substantial advantages.
- Status gains motivate prosocial behavior, especially when such behavior brings **public recognition**.
- Research support: Flynn, Reagans, Amanatullah, & Ames (2006) — motive to experience status boost underlies many acts of prosocial behavior.
- Examples: Universities naming buildings after major donors; public recognition of philanthropists.
- Note: Competitive altruism does not negate genuine kindness as a motive — multiple motives can coexist.

> **Summary of Three Affective-State Models**

| Model | Core Mechanism | Key Theorists |
|---|---|---|
| Empathy-Altruism | We help because we empathically share others' distress and want to relieve it | Batson et al. (1981) |
| Negative-State Relief | We help to relieve our own negative emotions | Cialdini et al. (1981) |
| Empathic Joy | We help to experience the positive feelings from seeing recipients' positive reactions | Smith et al. (1989) |

### Kin Selection Theory: Helping Ourselves by Helping People Who Share Our Genes

- **Kin Selection Theory** (Cialdini, Brown, Lewis, Luce, & Neuberg, 1997; Pinker, 1998): From an evolutionary perspective, a key goal for all organisms is getting their genes into the next generation; thus, individuals are more likely to help others to whom they are closely related (genetic similarity increases helping).
- We help relatives because doing so increases the likelihood our shared genes are passed on.
- Support: Burnstein, Crandall, & Kitayama (1994) — participants were more likely to say they would help a close relative than a distant relative or nonrelative in an emergency. They were also more likely to help young relatives (more reproductive years ahead) over older relatives (e.g., pre-menopausal over post-menopausal female relatives).
- General finding (Neyer & Lang, 2003): We are more likely to help others to whom we are closely related.

#### Limitation and Extension
- Problem: We do help people unrelated to us. Kin selection theory alone cannot explain this.
- **Reciprocal Altruism Theory**: We may help people unrelated to us because helping is usually reciprocated. If we help them, they help us; our chances of survival are indirectly increased (Korsgaard, Meglino, Lester, & Jeong, 2010).

### Defensive Helping: Helping Outgroups to Reduce Their Threat to One's Ingroup

- **Defensive Helping**: Help given to members of outgroups primarily to reduce the threat they pose to the status or distinctiveness of one's own ingroup (not primarily to benefit the recipients).
- Mechanism: When outgroups achieve successes that threaten ingroup superiority, helping them (especially in ways that make them seem dependent/incompetent) can remove the status threat.
- This type of helping stems from a selfish motive: protecting ingroup distinctiveness and status, not from empathy or genuine concern for recipients.

> **KEY STUDY: Nadler, Harpaz-Gorodeisky, & Ben-David (2009) — Defensive Helping**
> - **Method:** Students at one school were told that students at another school scored either substantially higher (high threat to ingroup superiority) or about the same (low threat) on a test of cognitive abilities.
> - **Finding:** Participants offered more help to the high-threat school than the low-threat school.
> - **Interpretation:** Help was offered to reduce the status threat from the rival institution, not out of genuine concern.

## Responding to an Emergency: Will Bystanders Help?

### Helping in Emergencies: Apathy or Action?

- Emergency responses range from heroism to complete apathy.
- The historical case that catalyzed research: The murder of **Kitty Genovese** in New York City.
  - She was assaulted over many minutes; the attacker left and returned.
  - Many witnesses could see/hear from apartment windows.
  - Not a single person reported the crime to police.
  - Media interpreted this as widespread selfishness/urban indifference.
  - **Darley and Latané** proposed a different, testable explanation.

---

### Is There Safety in Numbers? Sometimes, But Not Always

- Common sense: More bystanders = more likely at least one will help.
- **Research finding: This is WRONG.** More bystanders = LESS likely any one will help.
- **Diffusion of Responsibility** (Darley & Latané): The greater the number of witnesses to an emergency, the less likely victims are to receive help, because each bystander assumes that someone else will do it; personal responsibility is spread (diffused) across all witnesses.

> **KEY STUDY: Darley & Latané (1968) — Diffusion of Responsibility / Bystander Effect**
> - **Participants:** Male college students
> - **Method:** Participants believed they were in a group interacting via intercom. A fellow student apparently had a seizure, began choking, and was clearly in need of help. Participants believed they were either:
>   - The only person aware of the emergency (alone condition)
>   - One of 2 bystanders
>   - One of 5 bystanders
> - **Measures:** (1) Percentage who attempted to help; (2) Time elapsed before help was offered
> - **Results:**
>   - 1 bystander: 85% helped
>   - 2 bystanders: 62% helped
>   - 5 bystanders: 31% helped
>   - More bystanders = longer delay before help AND lower proportion helping
> - **Conclusion:** Diffusion of responsibility clearly demonstrated; more witnesses = each individual feels less personally responsible.

- **Bystander Effect**: The tendency for individuals to be less likely to offer help in an emergency when other bystanders are present, compared to when they are alone.
- The bystander effect is most pronounced for helping between **strangers**.
- If the person in need appears to be a member of one's own **ingroup**, they are more likely to get help (Levine, Prosser, Evans, & Reicher, 2005).
- Race of victim and helper: Kuntsman & Plant (2009) — black victims less likely to receive help from white bystanders, especially if those bystanders are high in **aversive racism** (negative emotional reactions to black people).

> **KEY STUDY: Latané & Darley (1968) — Smoke-Filled Room (Pluralistic Ignorance)**
> - **Method:** Students placed in a room alone or with two other students; asked to fill out questionnaires. Experimenters secretly pumped smoke into the room through a vent.
> - **Results:**
>   - Alone condition: 75% left the room to report the smoke.
>   - Three-person condition: Only 38% responded; even when smoke became so thick it was difficult to see, 62% continued working on questionnaires.
> - **Conclusion:** Presence of others (especially strangers who remain calm) inhibits response to potential emergencies via pluralistic ignorance.

### Understanding the Bystander Effect: Five Crucial Steps in Deciding to Help or Not

(Latané & Darley, 1970 — the five-step decision model)

At each step, a failure produces non-helping. All five must yield a positive decision for helping to occur.

#### Step 1: Noticing That Something Unusual Is Happening
- If distracted, asleep, or deeply focused on something else, a bystander may simply fail to notice an emergency.
- Noise, sensory overload (common in urban environments), or absorption in other tasks all reduce noticing.
- The passengers on Flight 93 noticed because they saw weapons and heard from the captain.

#### Step 2: Correctly Interpreting the Event as an Emergency
- Even after noticing, bystanders often have incomplete information about what is happening.
- Most unusual events are NOT emergencies, so bystanders tend to assume no emergency is occurring.
- Responding to a non-emergency would be embarrassing; people hold back waiting for clearer information.
- **Pluralistic Ignorance**: Because none of the bystanders initially responds, each uses the others' inaction as a cue that nothing serious is happening; each depends on others to interpret the situation. No one knows for sure what is happening.
- The Kitty Genovese neighbors could not clearly see what was happening; they may have assumed it was a loud argument.
- Groups of friends (vs. strangers) are less susceptible because friends communicate about the situation (Rutkowski, Gruder, & Romer, 1983).
- Small town vs. big city: People in small towns (who know each other) are more likely to interpret and respond to emergencies than strangers in big cities (Levine, Martinez, Brase, & Sorenson, 1994).
- Alcohol reduces anxiety about others' reactions and doing the wrong thing, thereby increasing helping (Steele, Critchlow, & Liu, 1988).

#### Step 3: Deciding That It Is Your Responsibility to Provide Help
- When responsibility is clear (firefighters, police, medical personnel), the relevant person takes action.
- When responsibility is unclear, people assume anyone in a leadership role must take it (adults with children, professors with students).
- With only one bystander, he/she usually takes charge because there is no alternative.
- With multiple bystanders: diffusion of responsibility reduces any individual's felt obligation.

#### Step 4: Deciding That You Have the Knowledge and/or Skills to Act
- Some emergencies are simple (e.g., helping someone who slipped on ice) — most people have the skills.
- Other emergencies require specialized skills:
  - Drowning: only competent swimmers can help directly.
  - Medical emergencies: a registered nurse is more helpful than a history professor (Cramer, McMaster, Bartell, & Pragma, 1988).
  - Car trouble: only those with mechanical knowledge can provide direct help.
- Without the requisite skills, the most a bystander can do is call for assistance.

#### Step 5: Making the Final Decision to Provide Help
- Even with all previous steps resolved positively, helping may be inhibited by fears about potential negative consequences ("cognitive algebra" — weighing costs vs. benefits of helping).
- Potential costs (Fritzsche, Finkelstein, & Penner, 2000):
  - Physical danger to self (e.g., being stabbed while intervening)
  - Risk of injury (slipping on ice while helping)
  - Risk of being tricked/victimized (fake distress leading to robbery)
  - Legal liability
- Potential rewards are primarily emotional (feeling good, sense of efficacy).
- If the perceived costs outweigh the benefits, the bystander does not help.

> **Summary Table: Five Steps to Helping (Latané & Darley, 1970)**

| Step | Decision Required | Key Barrier to Helping |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Notice something unusual is happening | Distraction, sensory overload, inattention |
| 2 | Interpret it as an emergency | Ambiguity, pluralistic ignorance, embarrassment |
| 3 | Accept responsibility to help | Diffusion of responsibility (more bystanders) |
| 4 | Judge self as having skills/knowledge | Lack of relevant skills or training |
| 5 | Decide to actually help | Perceived costs outweigh benefits; fear of negative consequences |

## Factors That Increase or Decrease the Tendency to Help

### Situational (External) Factors Influence Helping: Similarity and Responsibility

#### Helping People We Like (Similarity Effect)
- We are more likely to help people who are **similar** to ourselves (in age, nationality, group membership, etc.) than those who are dissimilar (Hayden, Jackson, & Guydish, 1984; Shaw, Borough, & Pink, 1994).
- Mechanism (Hodges et al., 2010): Similarity to others increases **empathic concern** (emotional component of empathy), but does NOT significantly increase empathic accuracy (cognitive component).

> **KEY STUDY: Hodges et al. (2010) — Similarity, Empathy, and Helping**
> - **Groups:** New mothers, pregnant women, women who had never been pregnant — all watching a videotape of a new mother describing her experiences.
> - **Prediction:** New mothers (most similar) > Pregnant women (less similar) > Never-pregnant women (least similar) in empathic response.
> - **Findings:** Similarity significantly influenced **empathic concern** (emotional empathy) but did NOT significantly influence **empathic accuracy** (cognitive empathy).
> - **Conclusion:** Similarity primarily boosts the emotional, not the cognitive, component of empathy.

#### Helping Those Who Are Not Responsible for Their Problem (Attribution Effect)
- Attributions about the cause of a victim's need strongly influence helping.
- Helping is more likely if the victim is perceived as NOT responsible for their situation (e.g., a man in an expensive suit who was apparently mugged).
- Helping is less likely if the victim is perceived as responsible (e.g., a man lying unconscious with an empty wine bottle, suggesting alcoholism).
- General principle (Higgins & Shaw, 1999; Weiner, 1980): We are less likely to act if we believe the victim is to blame for their current state.

---

### Exposure to Live Prosocial Models

- Presence of a bystander who **does** help provides a strong prosocial social model and increases subsequent helping.

> **KEY STUDY: Bryan & Test (1967) — Live Prosocial Models**
> - **Method:** Field experiment. A young woman (research assistant) with a flat tire parked just off the road.
> - **Condition:** Motorists either had or had not previously driven past a staged scene of another woman with car trouble receiving assistance.
> - **Finding:** Motorists who had seen the prosocial model were much more likely to stop and help.

- Even **symbolic** prosocial models increase helping (e.g., money already in donation boxes in museums signals that others have donated, increasing further donations).
- Museums strategically place bills in donation cases to exploit this modeling effect.

### Playing Prosocial Video Games

- Not all video games are aggressive; some involve prosocial actions (characters help and support each other).
- Theoretical basis (Bushman & Anderson, 2002; Gentile & Gentile, 2008): Playing prosocial video games primes prosocial thoughts and schemas, generates favorable attitudes toward helping, and produces lasting changes in how individuals think about helping.

> **KEY STUDY: Greitmeyer & Osswald (2010) — Prosocial Video Games and Helping**
> - **Method:** Participants played either:
>   - Prosocial games (e.g., Lemmings)
>   - Aggressive games (e.g., Lamers)
>   - Neutral game (Tetris)
> - **Opportunity to help (Study 1):** Experimenter spilled a cup of pencils on the floor.
>   - Results: Prosocial game — 57% helped; Neutral — 33% helped; Aggressive — 28% helped.
> - **Opportunity to help (Study 2):** Intervening when a male assistant harassed a female experimenter.
>   - Results: Prosocial game — 56% intervened; Neutral — 22% intervened.
> - **Mechanism study:** Those who played prosocial games reported more prosocial thoughts while playing.
> - **Conclusion:** Prosocial video games increase helping by priming prosocial thoughts and cognitive schemas.

> **KEY STUDY: Gentile et al. (2009) — Longitudinal Study on Prosocial Games**
> - Found that more time spent playing prosocial video games predicted greater helping behavior (e.g., "helping a person who was in trouble") several months later.
> - Demonstrates lasting, not merely short-term, effects.

- Summary: Video game content determines effect:
  - Prosocial content → increases prosocial actions
  - Aggressive content → decreases prosocial actions, increases aggressive ones
  - The nature of the games, not games per se, determines social outcomes.

### Gratitude: How It Increases Further Helping

- **Gratitude** (thanks expressed by the recipient of help) increases subsequent helping.
- When helpers are thanked, they are more willing to help again or to help other people (McCullough, Kilpatrick, Emmons, & Larson, 2001).

#### Why Does Gratitude Increase Helping? (Grant & Gino, 2010)
Two candidate mechanisms:
1. **Self-efficacy**: Being thanked makes helpers feel capable and competent.
2. **Self-worth**: Being thanked makes helpers feel valued by others.

- Research finding (Grant & Gino, 2010): Gratitude increases helping primarily by increasing helpers' **feelings of self-worth** (not self-efficacy).
  - Both self-efficacy and self-worth were enhanced by expressions of gratitude.
  - Only boosts in **self-worth** were significantly related to subsequent helping.

> **KEY STUDY: Grant & Gino (2010) — Gratitude and Self-Worth**
> - **Study 1:** Participants helped a student with a cover letter.
>   - Thanked condition: "Thank you so much! I am really grateful."
>   - Not thanked condition: "I just wanted to let you know that I received your feedback."
>   - The student then asked for help with a second cover letter.
>   - Results: 55% of thanked participants helped again vs. 25% of non-thanked.
>   - Mediation: Effect was mediated by self-worth, not self-efficacy.
> - **Study 2:** Volunteer fundraisers for a university — thanked vs. not thanked by manager.
>   - Thanked fundraisers made **50% more calls** than non-thanked fundraisers.
>   - Effect again mediated by self-worth, not self-efficacy.

- Practical implication: Saying "thank you" increases the probability of receiving future help.

## EMOTIONS AND PROSOCIAL BEHAVIOR: Mood, Feelings of Elevation, and Helping

### Positive Emotions and Prosocial Behavior
- General finding: People are more willing to help a stranger when in a good mood.
- Mood elevators that increase helping include:
  - Listening to a comedian (Wilson, 1981)
  - Finding money in the coin return slot of a public telephone (Isen & Levin, 1972)
  - Spending time outdoors on a pleasant day (Cunningham, 1979)
  - Receiving a small unexpected gift (Isen, 1970)
  - A pleasant fragrance in the air (Baron, 1990; Baron & Thomley, 1994)
- Exception: Under certain circumstances, a positive mood can **decrease** prosocial behavior:
  - Good mood may lead to interpreting emergencies as not serious.
  - If helping requires difficult actions or would disrupt a good mood, people in good moods may help less (Rosenhan, Salovey, & Hargis, 1981; Isen, 1984).

### Negative Emotions and Prosocial Behavior
- General pattern: Negative moods reduce helping (Amato, 1986).
- BUT: Negative moods can **increase** helping if:
  - The act of helping generates positive feelings (consistent with negative-state relief model)
  - People in a bad mood want to make themselves feel better, and helping achieves that (Cialdini, Kenrick, & Baumann, 1982)
- Conditions under which negative mood is most likely to increase helping:
  1. Negative feelings are not too intense.
  2. The emergency is clear-cut, not ambiguous.
  3. The act of helping is interesting and satisfying, not dull and unrewarding (Cunningham et al., 1990).

### Feelings of Elevation and Helping Others
- **Feelings of Elevation**: When witnessing a kind or helpful act by another person, observers can feel inspired, uplifted, and optimistic about human nature.
- Elevation goes beyond mere positive affect.

> **KEY STUDY: Schnall, Roper, & Fessler (2010) — Elevation and Helping**
> - **Conditions:** Participants exposed to either:
>   - Elevating video (showing prosocial actions by others)
>   - Neutral video (ocean clip)
>   - Mirth condition (funny comedian) — controlled for positive affect
> - **Measure:** How many minutes participants volunteered to help the experimenter with a boring questionnaire.
> - **Finding:** Participants who watched the elevating video volunteered about **twice as much time** as those in the other two conditions.
> - The mirth condition did NOT significantly increase helping, showing elevation effects go beyond mere positive affect.
> - **Conclusion:** Kindness is "contagious" — witnessing prosocial actions inspires prosocial behavior in observers.

## Empathy: An Important Foundation for Helping

- Among personal/individual-difference factors, empathy is the most important determinant of prosocial behavior.
- **Empathy** (full definition): Emotional reactions focused on or oriented toward other people; includes feelings of compassion, sympathy, and concern; the capacity to share others' emotional states and take their perspective.
- Empathy as an individual difference: Some people are chronically higher in empathy than others, and this trait-level difference predicts helping across many contexts.
- Three components (reviewed earlier): emotional empathy, empathic accuracy, empathic concern — each has distinct relationships to different aspects of prosocial behavior.
- Empathic accuracy specifically predicts quality of social relationships and adjustment (Gleason et al., 2009).
- Note: Empathy declining in U.S. college students (Konrath et al., 2011) — concerning trend for prosocial behavior.

## Factors That Reduce Helping: Social Exclusion, Darkness, and Putting an Economic Value on Our Time and Effort

- Several factors systematically reduce helping:

### Social Exclusion
- People who have been **socially excluded** (rejected by others, told they have a future with few social relationships) show significantly reduced prosocial behavior.
- Social exclusion reduces empathy and concern for others.
- Being made to feel excluded from a group undermines the motivation to help outsiders.

### Darkness
- Environmental darkness (reduced lighting) reduces helping.
- Darkness reduces feelings of accountability — people feel less observed and evaluated.
- When anonymity is higher, prosocial behavior tends to decrease (consistent with research on deindividuation).

### Putting an Economic Value on Time and Effort
- When people are primed to think about money or the economic value of their time, they become less likely to help others.
- "Time is money" mindset makes unpaid helping seem irrational or costly.
- Simply thinking about money activates marketplace norms over communal norms — reducing spontaneous, free helping.
- This is linked to broader findings that activating economic/market frameworks reduces prosocial motivations.

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Helping Others via the Internet — the Case of Kiva

- The internet has created new forms of prosocial behavior beyond face-to-face helping.
- **Kiva** is an example: An online microlending organization where individuals can lend money (typically small amounts, e.g., $25) to entrepreneurs in developing countries.
- Key features:
  - Loans are typically repaid, often at near-100% repayment rates.
  - Lenders can view photos and stories of borrowers — this humanizes the recipients and increases empathy.
  - The internet removes geographic barriers to prosocial action.
  - Online prosocial acts can aggregate into large-scale social impact.
- Illustrates that the mechanisms of empathy and prosocial motivation can operate even in technologically mediated (non-face-to-face) contexts, provided the recipient is made sufficiently "real" and identifiable to the helper.
- Contrasts with the concern that social media reduces face-to-face empathy: targeted online platforms for prosocial action can leverage empathy effectively.

## The Effects of Being Helped: Why Perceived Motives Really Matter

- Receiving help is not uniformly positive for recipients; reactions depend on how the recipient perceives the helper's motives.

### Positive Reactions to Being Helped
- If help is perceived as:
  - Genuinely altruistic
  - Appropriate to the need
  - Respectful of the recipient's autonomy
  - Not implying incompetence or dependency
- Then the recipient feels grateful, positive, and likely to seek future help when needed.

### Negative Reactions to Being Helped
- **Threat to Self-Esteem**: Receiving help can threaten recipients' self-esteem if it implies they are incompetent or incapable.
- **Reactance**: If help is perceived as controlling, recipients may resist or feel resentful.
- **Indebtedness**: Recipients may feel obligated to reciprocate, which can be uncomfortable.
- **Attributions about helper motives matter most**: Help perceived as:
  - Motivated by pity → negative reactions, reduced self-esteem
  - Motivated by genuine concern → positive reactions
  - Motivated by self-interest or status-seeking (competitive altruism) → mixed reactions

### Defensive Helping and Recipient Reactions
- Help that implies the recipient is dependent or incompetent (as in defensive helping) produces resentment and negative self-evaluations in the recipient.
- The manner and form of help communicate messages about the helper's view of the recipient.

### Practical Implications
- How help is offered matters as much as whether help is offered.
- Effective helpers:
  - Convey genuine concern rather than pity
  - Preserve the recipient's sense of competence and autonomy
  - Allow the recipient to reciprocate in some way when possible

## Final Thoughts: Are Prosocial Behavior and Aggression Opposites?

- It might seem intuitive that prosocial behavior and aggression are simply opposite ends of a single dimension: people who are high in one should be low in the other.
- **Research finding**: The relationship is more complex; they are not simply opposites.
- Prosocial behavior and aggression are influenced by some of the same factors (e.g., empathy tends to decrease aggression AND increase prosocial behavior), but also by many different factors.
- It is possible for the same person to be high in both prosocial behavior AND aggression in different contexts.
- The underlying motives for each can be quite independent.
- Some forms of helping (e.g., defensive helping) actually involve elements of both prosocial action and subtle aggression (harming the recipient's self-concept).
- The two behaviors should be treated as distinct dimensions, not as mirror images.
- Social psychology studies both as independent but related aspects of human social behavior.

| **Negative-State Relief Model** | The proposal (Cialdini et al., 1981) that prosocial behavior is motivated by the bystander's desire to reduce his or her own uncomfortable negative emotions |
| **Empathic Joy Hypothesis** | The view (Smith et al., 1989) that helpers are motivated by the desire to experience positive feelings upon seeing recipients' positive reactions to being helped |
| **Competitive Altruism** | The view that people help others partly to boost their own status and reputation, generating benefits that offset the costs of helping |
| **Kin Selection Theory** | Theory (Cialdini et al., 1997; Pinker, 1998) proposing that organisms are more likely to help genetically related others because this increases the transmission of their own genes |
| **Reciprocal Altruism Theory** | The view that we may help unrelated individuals because helping is typically reciprocated, indirectly increasing our own survival chances |
| **Defensive Helping** | Help given to outgroup members primarily to reduce the threat they pose to the ingroup's status or distinctiveness, rather than out of genuine concern |
| **Bystander Effect** | The tendency for individuals to be less likely to offer help in an emergency when other bystanders are present than when alone |
| **Diffusion of Responsibility** | The principle that the greater the number of witnesses to an emergency, the less likely each will feel personally responsible to help, because each assumes someone else will act |
| **Pluralistic Ignorance** | A state in which no bystanders respond to an emergency, so each individual uses the others' inaction as evidence that nothing serious is happening; collective misinterpretation of a situation |
| **Feelings of Elevation** | The experience of feeling inspired, uplifted, and optimistic about human nature when witnessing kind or helpful acts by others |
| **Aversive Racism** | Negative emotional reactions to outgroup members (e.g., black people) that can reduce helping toward those individuals |
| **Empathic Perspective Taking** | The ability to understand and adopt the viewpoint of another person; a component of empathy that has been found to be declining in U.S. college students |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Bystander Effect (Darley & Latané, 1968):** In the classic seizure experiment, 85% helped when alone, 62% with 2 bystanders, 31% with 5 bystanders — the more bystanders, the less each individual helps and the longer the delay.

2. **Diffusion of Responsibility vs. Pluralistic Ignorance:** Diffusion of responsibility = each person feels less personally obligated (Step 3). Pluralistic ignorance = each person misinterprets the situation as not serious because others are also not responding (Step 2). These are two distinct mechanisms, both reducing helping in group settings.

3. **Five Steps of the Latané & Darley (1970) Decision Model:** Notice event → Interpret as emergency → Accept responsibility → Assess skills/knowledge → Decide to act. Failure at ANY step results in non-helping.

4. **Empathy-Altruism Hypothesis (Batson et al., 1981):** Some prosocial acts are motivated solely by genuine desire to help (altruistic, not self-serving), driven by empathic concern for the victim.

5. **Three Components of Empathy:** Emotional empathy (feeling sharing), Empathic accuracy (cognitive, "mind-reading"), Empathic concern (concern for well-being). Each predicts different aspects of prosocial behavior. Similarity primarily increases empathic concern, NOT empathic accuracy (Hodges et al., 2010).

6. **Negative-State Relief Model (Cialdini et al., 1981):** People help to reduce their own negative mood; source of negative mood (related or unrelated to emergency) is irrelevant; empathy is NOT necessary.

7. **Empathic Joy Hypothesis (Smith et al., 1989):** Helping is motivated by desire to see the positive reactions of the helped person; BOTH high empathy AND feedback about impact are necessary for this mechanism to produce helping.

8. **Competitive Altruism:** People help partly to boost status and reputation. Named buildings, public recognition, and social status gains are motivators. (Flynn et al., 2006 confirmed this empirically.)

9. **Kin Selection Theory:** We preferentially help closer genetic relatives AND younger relatives (more reproductive years). Reciprocal altruism explains helping of non-relatives.

10. **Defensive Helping (Nadler et al., 2009):** Outgroups that pose a high status threat to the ingroup receive MORE help (not less) — but the help is given to reduce their threat by fostering dependency, not out of genuine concern.

11. **Gratitude increases helping via self-worth, NOT self-efficacy (Grant & Gino, 2010):** Thanked volunteer fundraisers made 50% more calls. Effect mediated by self-worth, not self-efficacy.

12. **Prosocial Video Games (Greitmeyer & Osswald, 2010; Gentile et al., 2009):** Playing prosocial games (e.g., Lemmings) increases prosocial behavior (57% helped vs. 28% for aggressive games). Mechanism: priming of prosocial thoughts and schemas. Effects are lasting, not merely short-term.

13. **Feelings of Elevation (Schnall et al., 2010):** Watching others perform prosocial acts inspires helpers to volunteer twice as much time as those who watched funny videos. Elevation is distinct from general positive mood.

14. **Kitty Genovese Case:** The real-world event that sparked Darley & Latané's research. Key point for MCQs: The case illustrated diffusion of responsibility, NOT simple indifference or urban apathy.

15. **Empathy Declining (Konrath et al., 2011):** U.S. college students show declining empathic concern and empathic perspective-taking. Proposed causes: media violence, self-esteem culture, reality TV, and reduced face-to-face contact due to social media.
