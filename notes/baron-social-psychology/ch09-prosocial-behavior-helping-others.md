# 9 Prosocial Behavior: Helping Others

This chapter covers why and when people help: theoretical motives (empathy-altruism, negative-state relief, empathic joy, competitive altruism, kin selection, defensive helping), bystander intervention (bystander effect, diffusion of responsibility, five-step model), factors increasing/decreasing helping, and effects of being helped.

---

## Why People Help: Motives for Prosocial Behavior

- **Prosocial behavior**: actions that help others, often with no immediate benefit to the helper.
- Multiple motives; both situational and dispositional factors determine who helps.

---

### Empathy-Altruism

- **Empathy**: emotional reactions oriented toward others (compassion, sympathy, concern); sharing others' emotional states and taking their perspective (Eisenberg, 2000; Hodges et al., 2010).
- **Empathy-Altruism Hypothesis** (Batson et al., 1981): some prosocial acts are motivated solely by the desire to help, even at danger to the helper. Compassion outweighs other considerations (Batson, Klein, Highberger, & Shaw, 1995).

#### Three Components of Empathy

| Component | Definition | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Emotional Empathy** | Sharing others' feelings (vicarious experience) | Motivates helping to end distress |
| **Empathic Accuracy** | Cognitive; accurately perceiving others' thoughts/feelings | Linked to social adjustment |
| **Empathic Concern** | Concern for another's well-being | Closely linked to helping; influenced by similarity |

> **KEY STUDY: Gleason, Jensen-Campbell, & Ickes (2009), Empathic Accuracy**
> - Adolescents watched a student-teacher video, paused at points, and wrote what people were thinking/feeling; accuracy compared to actual reports.
> - Higher empathic accuracy correlated with better social adjustment (more friends, less bullying). Causality unclear.

#### Is Empathy Declining?
- Konrath, O'Brien, & Hsing (2011): U.S. college students report lower **empathic concern** and **empathic perspective taking** than past decades. Proposed (unproven) causes: media violence, self-esteem culture, reality TV, reduced face-to-face contact.

---

### Negative-State Relief

- **Negative-State Relief Model** (Cialdini, Baumann, & Kenrick, 1981): prosocial behavior is motivated by the desire to reduce one's own negative emotions. The source of the negative mood (related or unrelated to the emergency) is irrelevant; empathy is NOT necessary (Dietrich & Berkowitz, 1997). Mirror image of empathy-altruism (focus on the helper's discomfort).

---

### Empathic Joy

- **Empathic Joy Hypothesis** (Smith, Keating, & Stotland, 1989): helpers enjoy the positive reactions of those they help; helping is rewarding in itself. Helpers need **feedback** about effectiveness.

> **KEY STUDY: Smith, Keating, & Stotland (1989)**
> - Participants watched a student considering dropping out, varying (1) similarity (high/low empathy) and (2) whether they would receive feedback on their advice.
> - Helping occurred only with **both high empathy AND feedback**.
> - The prospect of empathic joy is a necessary motivator.

---

### Competitive Altruism

- **Competitive Altruism**: people help partly because it boosts status and reputation, bringing benefits that offset the costs. Status gains especially motivate helping with **public recognition** (Flynn, Reagans, Amanatullah, & Ames, 2006). E.g., naming buildings after donors. Genuine kindness can coexist.

> **Three Affective-State Models**

| Model | Core Mechanism | Theorists |
|---|---|---|
| Empathy-Altruism | Help to relieve others' shared distress | Batson et al. (1981) |
| Negative-State Relief | Help to relieve our own negative emotions | Cialdini et al. (1981) |
| Empathic Joy | Help to feel positive from recipients' reactions | Smith et al. (1989) |

---

### Kin Selection Theory

- **Kin Selection Theory** (Cialdini et al., 1997; Pinker, 1998): organisms help genetically related others to pass on shared genes.
- Burnstein, Crandall, & Kitayama (1994): people more likely to help close (vs. distant) relatives and young (more reproductive years) over older relatives. Neyer & Lang (2003): we help closer relatives.
- **Limitation/Extension:** we also help non-relatives. **Reciprocal Altruism Theory**: helping is usually reciprocated, indirectly increasing survival (Korsgaard et al., 2010).

---

### Defensive Helping

- **Defensive Helping**: help given to outgroups primarily to reduce the threat they pose to the ingroup's status/distinctiveness, not to benefit recipients (often by fostering dependency). A selfish motive.

> **KEY STUDY: Nadler, Harpaz-Gorodeisky, & Ben-David (2009)**
> - Students told a rival school scored higher (high threat) or the same (low threat).
> - More help offered to the high-threat school, to reduce the status threat.

## Responding to an Emergency: Will Bystanders Help?

- The **Kitty Genovese** murder catalyzed research: many witnesses, none reported the crime. Media saw indifference; **Darley and Latané** proposed a testable alternative.

### Is There Safety in Numbers?
- Common sense (more bystanders = more help) is **WRONG**: more bystanders = LESS likely any one helps.
- **Diffusion of Responsibility** (Darley & Latané): more witnesses → each assumes someone else will help; responsibility is spread.

> **KEY STUDY: Darley & Latané (1968), Diffusion of Responsibility**
> - Male students believed they were on an intercom when a fellow student apparently had a seizure. They believed they were the only witness, 1 of 2, or 1 of 5.
> - **Results:** 1 bystander = 85% helped; 2 = 62%; 5 = 31%. More bystanders = longer delay and lower helping.

- **Bystander Effect**: less likely to help when others are present than when alone; most pronounced between **strangers**.
- Ingroup victims are more likely helped (Levine, Prosser, Evans, & Reicher, 2005). Black victims less likely helped by white bystanders high in **aversive racism** (Kuntsman & Plant, 2009).

> **KEY STUDY: Latané & Darley (1968), Smoke-Filled Room**
> - Students alone or with two others filled out questionnaires while smoke was pumped in.
> - Alone: 75% reported the smoke. Three-person: only 38% responded; 62% kept working even when smoke was thick.
> - Pluralistic ignorance: calm strangers inhibit emergency response.

---

### Five Steps in Deciding to Help (Latané & Darley, 1970)

Failure at any step = non-helping.

**Step 1: Noticing something unusual.** Distraction, sensory overload, or absorption reduce noticing.

**Step 2: Interpreting it as an emergency.** Most unusual events aren't emergencies, so bystanders assume none. **Pluralistic Ignorance**: each uses others' inaction as a cue that nothing serious is happening. Friends (vs. strangers) communicate and are less susceptible (Rutkowski, Gruder, & Romer, 1983); small-town people respond more than big-city strangers (Levine et al., 1994); alcohol increases helping by reducing anxiety about others' reactions (Steele, Critchlow, & Liu, 1988).

**Step 3: Deciding it's your responsibility.** Clear when responsibility is assigned (firefighters); with one bystander, they take charge; with many, diffusion of responsibility reduces obligation.

**Step 4: Deciding you have the skills.** Some emergencies are simple; others need specialized skills (drowning → swimmers; medical → a nurse vs. a history professor; Cramer et al., 1988). Without skills, the most one can do is call for help.

**Step 5: Deciding to actually help.** "Cognitive algebra" weighs costs vs. benefits. Potential costs (Fritzsche, Finkelstein, & Penner, 2000): physical danger, injury, being tricked, legal liability. If costs outweigh benefits, no help.

> **Five Steps (Latané & Darley, 1970)**

| Step | Decision | Barrier |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Notice something unusual | Distraction, overload |
| 2 | Interpret as emergency | Ambiguity, pluralistic ignorance |
| 3 | Accept responsibility | Diffusion of responsibility |
| 4 | Judge self as having skills | Lack of skills/training |
| 5 | Decide to help | Perceived costs outweigh benefits |

## Factors That Increase or Decrease Helping

### Situational Factors

#### Helping People We Like (Similarity)
- We help **similar** others more (Hayden, Jackson, & Guydish, 1984). Similarity increases **empathic concern** but not empathic accuracy (Hodges et al., 2010).

> **KEY STUDY: Hodges et al. (2010), Similarity and Empathy**
> - New mothers, pregnant women, and never-pregnant women watched a new mother describe her experiences.
> - Similarity significantly increased **empathic concern** (emotional) but NOT **empathic accuracy** (cognitive).

#### Helping Those Not Responsible (Attribution)
- More helping if the victim is NOT responsible (mugged man in a suit); less if responsible (unconscious man with an empty wine bottle) (Weiner, 1980; Higgins & Shaw, 1999).

### Exposure to Live Prosocial Models

> **KEY STUDY: Bryan & Test (1967)**
> - Motorists who had previously seen a woman receiving help for car trouble were much more likely to stop for a woman with a flat tire.

- Even **symbolic** models increase helping (e.g., money already in a donation box).

### Prosocial Video Games
- Playing prosocial games primes prosocial thoughts/schemas (Bushman & Anderson, 2002; Gentile & Gentile, 2008).

> **KEY STUDY: Greitmeyer & Osswald (2010)**
> - Participants played prosocial (Lemmings), aggressive (Lamers), or neutral (Tetris) games.
> - Study 1 (spilled pencils): prosocial 57% helped, neutral 33%, aggressive 28%. Study 2 (intervening in harassment): prosocial 56%, neutral 22%. Prosocial play increased prosocial thoughts.

> **KEY STUDY: Gentile et al. (2009), Longitudinal**
> - More prosocial-game time predicted greater helping months later (lasting effects).

- Content determines effect: prosocial → more helping; aggressive → more aggression.

### Gratitude
- **Gratitude** increases subsequent helping (McCullough et al., 2001).

> **KEY STUDY: Grant & Gino (2010), Gratitude and Self-Worth**
> - Study 1: thanked participants helped again 55% vs. 25% non-thanked.
> - Study 2: thanked fundraisers made **50% more calls**.
> - Both studies: effect mediated by **self-worth**, not self-efficacy.

## EMOTIONS AND PROSOCIAL BEHAVIOR: Mood, Elevation, and Helping

### Positive Emotions
- Good moods increase helping: comedy (Wilson, 1981), finding money (Isen & Levin, 1972), pleasant weather (Cunningham, 1979), small gifts (Isen, 1970), pleasant scents (Baron, 1990).
- Exception: good moods can decrease helping if helping is difficult or would disrupt the mood (Rosenhan, Salovey, & Hargis, 1981).

### Negative Emotions
- Negative moods generally reduce helping (Amato, 1986), but can increase it when helping relieves the bad mood (negative-state relief; Cialdini, Kenrick, & Baumann, 1982). Most likely when feelings aren't too intense, the emergency is clear, and helping is satisfying (Cunningham et al., 1990).

### Feelings of Elevation
- **Feelings of Elevation**: feeling inspired/uplifted when witnessing kind acts; beyond mere positive affect.

> **KEY STUDY: Schnall, Roper, & Fessler (2010), Elevation**
> - Participants watched an elevating video (prosocial acts), a neutral video (ocean), or a mirth condition (comedian).
> - The elevating group volunteered about **twice as much time**; the mirth condition did not increase helping.
> - Kindness is "contagious"; elevation goes beyond positive affect.

---

## Empathy: Foundation for Helping

- Empathy is the most important personal determinant of prosocial behavior; some people are chronically higher in it.
- Three components (above) each predict different aspects of helping; empathic accuracy predicts social adjustment (Gleason et al., 2009). Empathy is declining in U.S. students (Konrath et al., 2011).

---

## Factors That Reduce Helping

- **Social exclusion**: rejected people show reduced prosocial behavior, empathy, and concern.
- **Darkness**: reduces accountability and felt observation, decreasing helping (like deindividuation).
- **Economic value on time/effort**: priming money/"time is money" activates marketplace over communal norms, reducing helping.

---

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Helping via the Internet (Kiva)

- The internet creates new prosocial forms. **Kiva**: online microlending; individuals lend small amounts (e.g., $25) to entrepreneurs in developing countries.
- Near-100% repayment; borrower photos/stories humanize recipients and increase empathy; removes geographic barriers.
- Empathy and prosocial motivation can operate in mediated contexts when the recipient is made "real" and identifiable.

---

## The Effects of Being Helped

- Reactions depend on perceived helper motives.

### Positive Reactions
- When help is altruistic, appropriate, respectful of autonomy, and not implying incompetence, recipients feel grateful and positive.

### Negative Reactions
- **Threat to self-esteem** (implies incompetence), **reactance** (controlling help), **indebtedness** (obligation to reciprocate).
- Attributions matter most: pity → negative reactions; genuine concern → positive; self-interest → mixed.
- Defensive helping (implying dependency) produces resentment.

### Practical Implications
- Effective helpers convey concern (not pity), preserve competence/autonomy, and allow reciprocation.

---

## Final Thoughts: Are Prosocial Behavior and Aggression Opposites?

- Not simply opposites. They share some factors (empathy decreases aggression and increases helping) but differ on many others. The same person can be high in both in different contexts; motives can be independent; some helping (defensive) involves subtle aggression. Treat them as distinct dimensions.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Bystander Effect (Darley & Latané, 1968)**: 85% (alone), 62% (2), 31% (5); more bystanders = less help, longer delay.
2. **Diffusion of responsibility vs. pluralistic ignorance**: feeling less obligated (Step 3) vs. misinterpreting the situation (Step 2).
3. **Five steps (Latané & Darley, 1970)**: notice → interpret → accept responsibility → assess skills → decide to act; failure at any step = no help.
4. **Empathy-Altruism (Batson et al., 1981)**: genuinely altruistic helping from empathic concern.
5. **Three components of empathy**: emotional, accuracy, concern. Similarity increases concern, NOT accuracy (Hodges et al., 2010).
6. **Negative-State Relief (Cialdini et al., 1981)**: help to relieve own mood; source irrelevant; empathy not necessary.
7. **Empathic Joy (Smith et al., 1989)**: needs BOTH high empathy AND feedback.
8. **Competitive Altruism**: helping for status/reputation (Flynn et al., 2006).
9. **Kin Selection**: help closer and younger relatives; reciprocal altruism for non-relatives.
10. **Defensive Helping (Nadler et al., 2009)**: high-threat outgroups receive MORE help, to reduce threat.
11. **Gratitude (Grant & Gino, 2010)**: increases helping via **self-worth**, not self-efficacy; 50% more calls.
12. **Prosocial Video Games (Greitmeyer & Osswald, 2010; Gentile et al., 2009)**: prosocial play increases helping (57% vs. 28%); lasting effects; thought priming.
13. **Elevation (Schnall et al., 2010)**: prosocial videos doubled volunteering; distinct from positive mood.
14. **Kitty Genovese**: sparked the research; illustrates diffusion of responsibility, not indifference.
15. **Empathy declining (Konrath et al., 2011)**: lower empathic concern and perspective-taking; causes include media violence and reduced face-to-face contact.
