# 6 The Causes, Effects, and Cures of Stereotyping, Prejudice, and Discrimination

Chapter 6 examines the cognitive, affective, and behavioral components of intergroup bias (stereotyping, prejudice, discrimination), their psychological origins, and evidence-based reduction strategies. These are treated as products of normal cognitive and motivational processes, not pathology.

---

## How Different Groups Perceive Inequality

- **Prejudice** = negative emotional responses based on group membership (racial, gender, religious, age, occupational, sexual orientation, body weight, language).
- **Discrimination** = differential (usually negative) behaviors toward members of social groups.
- High-status groups perceive less inequality than low-status groups in the same situation (Exline & Lobel, 1999).
- U.S. surveys: whites perceive "a lot of progress" toward racial equality; blacks perceive "not much progress" (the **racial divide**).

### Prospect Theory Applied to Racial Perceptions

- **Prospect theory** (Kahneman & Tversky, 1984; Nobel 2002): people are **risk averse**; possible losses are weighed more heavily than equivalent gains.
- Applied: whites frame greater equality as a **loss** of privilege; blacks frame it as a **gain**. Because losses evoke stronger emotion, equality is more negative for whites than positive for blacks.
- Highly race-identified whites respond to challenged privileges with **increased racism** and support for **tokenism** (Branscombe, Schmitt, & Schiffhauer, 2007; Richard & Wright, 2010).

> **KEY STUDY: Eibach & Keegan (2006), Gains vs. Losses Framing**
> - White and non-white participants graphed racial composition change (1960-present) in one of three conditions: "minority gains AND white losses," "white losses only," "minority gains only."
> - In both "white losses" conditions, whites perceived greater progress than non-whites (mirrors surveys). In "minority gains only," whites perceived LESS progress, no different from non-whites.
> - The racial divide partly stems from whites framing change as losses to their group.

### Obama Election Effect (Kaiser et al., 2009)
- Post-election, whites believed there was LESS need for further racial progress and less supported equality policies.
- Visible "token" minorities lead majority members to believe substantial change has occurred. Even ~2% of low-status members advancing deters collective resistance (Lalonde & Silverman, 1994; Wright, Taylor, & Moghaddam, 1990).

### Affirmative Action and Perceived Threat
- Whites who expect affirmative action to harm their group oppose it regardless of minority impact (Lowery et al., 2006).
- White South Africans oppose it when it threatens their jobs/housing (Durrheim et al., 2009).
- In 21 European nations, perceived immigrant economic threat increases opposition to naturalization (Pereira, Vala, & Costa-Lopes, 2009).
- Hate crimes increase as minorities are perceived gaining political power (Dancygier & Green, 2010).

## The Nature and Origins of Stereotyping

The three concepts map onto the tripartite attitude structure:

| Component | Concept | Definition |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Cognitive** | Stereotypes | Beliefs/expectations about what group members are like |
| **Affective** | Prejudice | Feelings toward a group |
| **Behavioral** | Discrimination | Differential actions toward group members |

- Classic model: negative stereotypes → hostility → intentional discrimination.
- **Modern critique** (Adams et al., 2008): stereotypes aren't always negative; prejudice isn't always hostile; discrimination can occur without conscious intent; "benevolent prejudice" exists.

## Stereotyping: Beliefs About Social Groups

- **Stereotypes** = beliefs about what group members are like (traits, appearance, abilities, behaviors) (Biernat & Thompson, 2002). Can be positive or negative, accurate or inaccurate.

### Gender Stereotypes

- **Gender stereotypes** = beliefs about characteristics of women and men; typically the **converse** of one another.
- **Women:** high warmth, low competence (kind, nurturant; also dependent, weak, emotional) (Fiske, Cuddy, Glick, & Xu, 2002).
- **Men:** high competence, low warmth (decisive, accomplished; also aggressive, arrogant).
- **"Women are wonderful" effect** (Eagly & Mladinic, 1994): the strong warmth emphasis makes people feel more positively about women overall, yet women's traits are seen as less fit for leadership.

**Female stereotypic traits (Table 6.1):** warm, emotional, kind/polite, sensitive, follower, weak, friendly, fashionable, gentle.
**Male stereotypic traits (Table 6.1):** competent, stable, tough/coarse, self-confident, leader, strong, accomplished, nonconformist, aggressive.

### Stereotypes and the Glass Ceiling

- **Glass ceiling** = barriers from attitudinal/organizational bias preventing qualified women from reaching top positions.
- Only 16% of U.S. corporate officers and ~1% of Fortune 500 CEOs are women (Catalyst, 2010).
- **"Think manager, think male" bias** (Schein, 2001; Bruckmüller & Branscombe, 2010): "typical manager" attributes overlap "typical man," not "typical woman," creating perceived **"lack of fit"** for women (Eagly & Sczesny, 2009; Heilman, 2001).
- Women's labor force participation: 20% (1900) → 59% (2005).
- Women succeeding in male-dominated environments are MOST likely to report discrimination (Redersdorff, Martinot, & Branscombe, 2004).
- Women leading with a task-focused/authoritarian style (violating warmth expectations) get especially negative evaluations (Eagly & Karau, 2002).
- **Rudman & Fairchild (2004):** people told they did well on an other-gender-typical test were more likely to lie about it (social costs of defying gender stereotypes).
- **Duehr & Bono (2006):** the women-leadership stereotype inconsistency has decreased over 10 years, especially among women.

### Glass Cliff Effect

- **Glass cliff effect** (Ryan & Haslam, 2005, 2007) = choosing women for risky/precarious leadership positions likely to fail.
- **Archival (London Stock Exchange):** companies with poor recent stock performance were more likely to appoint a woman.
- **Experimental:** with equally qualified candidates, the woman was chosen more often when the position was risky; the man when not (Ryan et al., 2009).
- **Interpretation (Bruckmüller & Branscombe, 2010):** women's communal attributes are sought ONLY when men's leadership attributes appear to be failing.
- Examples: Lynn Laverty Elsenhans (Sunoco); Johanna Siguroardottir (Iceland PM after collapse).

### Token Women in High Places

- **Tokenism** = numerically infrequent presence of previously excluded members, or trivial positive actions used to justify refusing meaningful ones.
- Visible tokens may lead observers to attribute underrepresentation to women's qualities rather than structural barriers.

> **KEY STUDY: Danaher & Branscombe (2010), Token Representation**
> - Women told the Board of Regents was "open" (5/10 female), "token" (1/10), or "closed" (0/10).
> - Women in BOTH open AND token conditions reported greater belief in meritocracy and organizational identification than in the closed condition.
> - Token practices, like equal representation, sustain faith in advancement and allegiance, maintaining the status quo.

> **KEY STUDY: Brown et al. (2000), Gender Quota and Performance**
> - Women told they were selected as leader ONLY by quota showed reduced leadership performance vs. other conditions.

**Negative consequences of tokenism:**
1. Token hires perceived negatively by coworkers (Fuegen & Biernat, 2002).
2. Affirmative action hires perceived as less competent (Heilman, Block, & Lucas, 1992).
3. Damages the token's self-esteem and confidence.
4. Lets prejudiced people "off the hook."
5. Maintains the perception that the system is legitimate, even among the disadvantaged.

### Responses to Those Who Speak Out

> **KEY STUDY: Kaiser & Miller (2001), Complaining About Discrimination**
> - White participants read about a Black student attributing a bad grade to discrimination ("complaint") or to himself ("I'm responsible").
> - The student was evaluated MORE negatively in the complaint condition, regardless of whether perceivers thought discrimination occurred.
> - Even accurate discrimination claims draw negative reactions to complainers.

- The ingroup may disapprove of claimers if it suggests unjustified griping (Garcia et al., 2005), supporting complaints only when discrimination is serious and complaining could improve the group's situation (Garcia et al., 2010).

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Gender in Video Games

- Market: $10 billion/year in the U.S. (Behm-Morawitz & Mastro, 2009); 40% of U.S. players are female; 80% of girls grades 4-12 play. Average devotee: male, ~34.
- **Dill & Thill (2007):** video games show the most blatant sex-role stereotyping of any mass media. 83% of male characters are violent/hypermasculine; females are "damsel in distress" or sex object.
- **Objectification of females** = regarding women as mere bodies existing for others' pleasure.

> **KEY STUDY: Fox & Bailenson (2009), Sexualized Avatars**
> - Participants viewed female avatars varying in dress (suggestive/conservative) and gaze (high/low). Measured hostile/benevolent sexism and Burt's (1980) rape myth acceptance.
> - Suggestive dress + high gaze → highest rape myth acceptance and hostile sexism (both genders). Conservative dress + low gaze → more benevolent sexism.

> **KEY STUDY: Dill, Brown, & Collins (2008), Objectified Images**
> - Objectified female game character vs. female politician image.
> - Males exposed to objectified images → increased tolerance for sexual harassment; females → DECREASED tolerance (energized to advocate). Real attitude/behavior change.

## Is Stereotyping Absent If Ratings Are the Same?

- **NO.** Equal ratings can still involve stereotyping.
- **Shifting standards** (Biernat, 2005) = using one group as the standard but shifting standards across groups (like "large cat" vs. "small car," same word, different referent).
- **Objective scales** = units tied to external reality (dollars, feet/inches, % free throws); meaning is constant.
- **Subjective scales** = interpretable scales lacking external referent ("good-bad," "weak-strong"); meaning varies by group.
- Subjective scales **CONCEAL** stereotyping; objective scales **EXPOSE** it.
- **Biernat et al. (2009):** more race-based shifting standards predicts more behavioral discrimination against black candidates.

## Can We Be Victims and Not Recognize It?: Single People

- **Singlism** (DePaulo, 2006) = negative stereotyping and discrimination toward single people, who are >40% of American adults (U.S. Census, 2007).

> **KEY STUDY: DePaulo & Morris (2006), Single vs. Married Stereotypes**
> - 1,000+ undergraduates described "singles" vs. "married" (Table 6.3).
> - Singles: immature, insecure, self-centered, unhappy, lonely. Married: mature, stable, kind, happy, loving. Married described as kind/giving 50% of the time vs. 2% for singles. Gap larger for over-40 targets.
> - Discrimination: undergraduates preferred renting to a married couple (70%) over a single man (12%) or woman (18%).
- Married status confers legal privileges (health benefits, insurance discounts, tax/Social Security benefits).

### Why Singlism Is Not Challenged
- Only **4%** of singles spontaneously mention "single" as a discrimination target; only **30%** say singles could be stigmatized (vs. nearly all members of other stigmatized groups).
- **Morris, Sinclair, & DePaulo (2007):** refusing to rent was seen as discrimination for all groups EXCEPT singles; discrimination against singles is seen as MORE legitimate by both single and married people.
- **Explanation:** negative singles stereotyping protects and glorifies marriage as an institution.

## Why Do People Form and Use Stereotypes?

- Stereotypes function as **schemas** (Fiske & Taylor, 2008).
- Primary reason: **cognitive efficiency**; humans are "cognitive misers" (Bodenhausen, 1993; Macrae, Milne, & Bodenhausen, 1994).
- Also **motivational**: feeling positive about one's own group.

### Which Stereotype Is Activated?
- Context determines salience (Yzerbyt & Demoulin, 2010); e.g., "waitress" role may dominate over race/gender.

### How Stereotypes Operate
1. Once activated, stereotypic traits come automatically to mind (Bodenhausen & Hugenberg, 2009).
2. Stereotype-relevant information is processed faster and remembered better (Dovidio, Evans, & Tyler, 1986).
3. They direct attention to stereotype-consistent information.
4. They act as **theories** guiding processing (Yzerbyt, Rocher, & Schadron, 1997).

### Subtyping (When Disconfirmed)
- **Subtyping** = placing disconfirming individuals into a special subcategory seen as atypical, protecting the stereotype (Queller & Smith, 2002). E.g., an intelligent low-status member is "exceptional."

### Do Stereotypes Change?
- Yes, when intergroup relations change (Eagly, 1987; Pettigrew, 1981).

> **KEY STUDY: Dasgupta & Asgari (2004), Women's vs. Coeducational College**
> - Women's gender stereotypes assessed Year 1 and Year 2 at a women's college (more women faculty in nontraditional roles) or coeducational college.
> - Women's college students showed decreased stereotyping by Year 2. Counter-stereotypic role models weaken stereotypes.

## Prejudice: Feelings Toward Social Groups

- **Prejudice** = a negative attitude toward group members based solely on their membership. Cognitive (stereotypes), affective (feelings), behavioral (discriminatory intent). Can be legitimate or illegitimate, blatant or subtle.
- **Crandall, Eshleman, & O'Brien (2002):** some prejudice is perceived as justified.

### Benevolent Prejudice and Ambivalent Sexism

- **Ambivalent sexism theory** (Glick & Fiske, 1996):
  - **Hostile sexism** = negative, resentful attitudes toward women who challenge traditional roles.
  - **Benevolent sexism** = positive but patronizing attitudes; women on a pedestal but restricted.
- Together they maintain gender hierarchy.

### Affect: Distinct Emotions for Different Groups

- Prejudice isn't a single emotion (Cottrell & Neuberg, 2005).
- **Stereotype Content Model** (Fiske, Cuddy, Glick, & Xu, 2002), warmth × competence:
  - High warmth + high competence (ingroup): **admiration**
  - Low warmth + high competence (rich, Asians): **envy**
  - High warmth + low competence (elderly, disabled): **pity**
  - Low warmth + low competence (welfare recipients, homeless): **contempt/disgust**

## The Origins of Prejudice: Contrasting Perspectives

### 1. Realistic Conflict Theory
- **Realistic conflict theory** (Sherif, 1966) = prejudice from competition for scarce resources (jobs, land, power).

> **KEY STUDY: Robbers Cave Experiment (Sherif et al., 1961)**
> - Summer camp, Oklahoma; 11-12 year old boys.
> - **Phase 1:** two groups formed (Eagles, Rattlers), developed identities.
> - **Phase 2 (Competition):** competing for prizes → rapid hostility, name-calling, raids, flag-burning.
> - **Phase 3 (Superordinate goals):** problems requiring cooperation (fixing the water supply) reduced hostility.
> - Competition creates prejudice; cooperation toward superordinate goals reduces it.
> - **Limitation:** prejudice exists even without real conflict (minimal group paradigm).

### 2. Social Identity Theory
- **Social identity theory** (Tajfel & Turner, 1979, 1986): group membership provides **social identity**; people seek **positive social identity** by favoring the ingroup = **ingroup favoritism**.
- **Social categorization** = classifying people into groups by shared attributes.
- **Self-categorization theory** (Turner et al., 1987): identity shifts between individual and group levels.
- **Minimal group paradigm** (Tajfel, 1971): even arbitrary group assignments (Klee vs. Kandinsky preference) produce ingroup favoritism.
- **Positive distinctiveness** = wanting one's group seen as better on valued dimensions.
- Applied to same-sex marriage (Schmitt, Lehmiller, & Walsh, 2007): the "marriage" label threatens heterosexual positive distinctiveness; "civil union" does not.

### 3. Social Learning Perspective
- Children acquire prejudice through socialization (parents, peers, media), **operant conditioning** (reinforced expression), and **observational learning/modeling**.

### 4. Cognitive Perspective: Social Categorization
- Categorizing people is fundamental (Allport, 1954; Tajfel, 1981).
- **Outgroup homogeneity effect** = perceiving outgroup members as more similar to one another than ingroup members (Park & Rothbart, 1982): "they all look alike."

### 5. Right-Wing Authoritarianism (RWA)
- **RWA** (Altemeyer, 1981, 1988): (1) **authoritarian submission**, (2) **authoritarian aggression** (toward authority-sanctioned targets), (3) **conventionalism**. High RWA → more prejudice across outgroups.

### 6. Social Dominance Orientation (SDO)
- **SDO** (Sidanius & Pratto, 1999) = desire for one's group to dominate; support for group-based hierarchy. SDO and RWA are independent and additively predict prejudice.

## EMOTIONS AND PREJUDICE: Dying and Killing for One's Group

- **Intergroup emotions theory** (Smith, 1993; Mackie & Smith, 2002): we feel emotions as group members based on our group's fortunes.
- **Sacred values** (Tetlock, 2003): some commitments resist trade-offs; "taboo trade-offs" cause moral outrage. Threatened sacred values motivate fighting and dying (Atran).
- **Schadenfreude** = pleasure in rivals' misfortune; stronger among highly identified members (Leach, Spears, Branscombe, & Doosje, 2003).

---

## Discrimination: Prejudice in Action

- Discrimination can be **blatant** or **subtle**; can occur without conscious intent.
- **Ambivalent racism** (Katz, 1981): simultaneously holding egalitarian and stereotypic beliefs → inconsistent behavior.
- **Aversive racism** (Dovidio & Gaertner, 1986, 2004): aversive racists endorse egalitarian values and don't see themselves as prejudiced, but harbor unconscious negative feelings and discriminate subtly, especially when there is a justification or unclear norms.

> **KEY STUDY: Dovidio & Gaertner (2000), Aversive Racism in Hiring**
> - White participants reviewed white/black applications with clearly strong, ambiguous, or clearly weak qualifications.
> - STRONG/WEAK: no racial discrimination. AMBIGUOUS: white candidates favored over equally qualified black candidates.
> - Aversive racists discriminate when there is justification (ambiguity).

---

## Modern Racism

- **Modern racism** (McConahay, 1986) = subtle prejudice; people sincerely believe they aren't prejudiced and that discrimination is over, while holding negative attitudes manifesting non-obviously.
- Scale items: "Discrimination against black people is no longer a problem"; "Blacks are getting too demanding."

### Measuring Implicit Prejudice
- **IAT** (Greenwald, McGhee, & Schwartz, 1998): automatic associations via reaction time (e.g., faster "Black + Unpleasant" indicates implicit negative association). Most white Americans show implicit negative associations, even low scorers on explicit measures.
- **Dovidio, Kawakami, & Gaertner (2002):** explicit prejudice predicts deliberate behaviors; implicit predicts automatic ones.
- **Fazio et al. (1995):** whites with stronger negative automatic associations treated a black experimenter more negatively.

### Stereotype Threat
- **Stereotype threat** (Steele & Aronson, 1995) = fear of confirming a negative group stereotype, which impairs performance.
- Black students reminded of race before a test perform worse. Mediated by working memory depletion (self-monitoring) and physiological arousal.
- Affects women in math (Spencer, Steele, & Quinn, 1999), elderly, low-SES students.

## Why Prejudice Is Not Inevitable

### On Learning Not to Hate
- Socialization shapes prejudice early but can be countered: egalitarian parental modeling, counter-stereotypic media, multicultural education all reduce children's prejudice.

### The Benefits of Contact
- **Contact hypothesis** (Allport, 1954): intergroup contact under optimal conditions reduces prejudice (superficial/competitive contact can increase it).

**Allport's four conditions:**
1. **Equal status** in the contact situation.
2. **Cooperative interdependence** (common goals, not competition).
3. **Acquaintance potential** (close enough to disconfirm stereotypes).
4. **Support from authority/social norms**.

> **KEY STUDY: Pettigrew & Tropp (2006), Meta-Analysis**
> - 515 studies, 250,000+ participants. Contact reliably reduces prejudice; effect larger with Allport's conditions, but contact reduces prejudice even without all four.
> - Main mechanism: reduces **intergroup anxiety** and increases **empathy** (Pettigrew & Tropp, 2008).
> - Reduction generalizes to the outgroup as a whole (Pettigrew, 1997).

- **Extended contact effect** (Wright et al., 1997): merely KNOWING an ingroup member has a close outgroup relationship reduces prejudice and anxiety, without direct contact.

### Recategorization
- **Recategorization** = redefining boundaries so distinct groups merge into a superordinate group ("us" replaces "us vs. them").
- **Common ingroup identity model** (Gaertner & Dovidio, 2000): recategorizing into one superordinate group reduces bias; ingroup favoritism extends to former outgroup members.
- **Decategorization** = breaking down category distinctions, emphasizing individual identity.
- **Cross-cutting social categories** = when memberships cross-cut, ingroup/outgroup lines blur.

**Limitations:**
- **Subgroup identity threat:** strong subgroup identifiers may resist superordinate categorization.
- **Dual identity model** (Dovidio & Gaertner): maintaining BOTH subgroup AND superordinate identity ("African American AND American") is often more effective.

### Benefits of Guilt
- **Collective guilt** = emotion when one's group is responsible for harm to another; motivates amends (affirmative action, reparations).
- **Branscombe et al. (2004):** whites induced to feel collective guilt for white privilege showed more support for affirmative action.
- **Boundary conditions:** works only when people (1) identify with the harming group AND (2) recognize the harm as illegitimate.
- **Doosje, Branscombe, Spears, & Manstead (1998):** Dutch low identifiers felt more collective guilt about colonial history; high identifiers used defensive strategies.

### Controlling Stereotyping
- Automatic activation does NOT require stereotyped responses; people CAN control stereotypic thinking.
- **Monteith (1993):** low-prejudice individuals self-regulate; responding in a prejudiced way produces **discrepancy-associated affect** (guilt) that cues careful thinking.
- **Stereotype suppression** can cause a **rebound effect** (Wegner, 1994): suppressed thoughts become hyperaccessible. Not inevitable: motivated people with resources can suppress without rebound.
- **Implementation intentions** (Gollwitzer, 1999) = if-then plans ("If I'm about to judge by race, I'll stop and think") reduce automatic stereotyping.

### Attributional Biases
- **Ultimate attribution error** (Pettigrew, 1979): intergroup extension of the FAE. Outgroup negatives → dispositional; outgroup positives → situational ("exception"); reverse for ingroup.
- **Czopp & Monteith (2003):** confronting biased attributions causes guilt and reduces future bias.

### Social Influence
- Social norms regulate prejudice expression; **normative influence** makes prejudice costly.
- **Ingroup confrontation** (Czopp, Monteith, & Mark, 2006): confrontation by an INGROUP member causes more guilt and less subsequent prejudice than an outgroup member (harder to dismiss as self-interested).
- **Consensus information** (Stangor, Sechrist, & Jost, 2001): telling people most have changed a stereotype shifts individuals' stereotypes.
- **Pluralistic ignorance** = privately rejecting a norm while assuming others accept it; learning others also oppose it removes its influence.
- **Galinsky & Moskowitz (2000):** perspective-taking (imagining an outgroup member's life) reduces prejudice, partly by reducing intergroup anxiety.

| Strategy | Mechanism | Key Evidence |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Intergroup contact (optimal conditions) | Reduces anxiety, increases empathy | Pettigrew & Tropp (2006) |
| Superordinate goals / cooperation | Replaces competition with interdependence | Sherif, Robbers Cave (1961) |
| Recategorization / common identity | Extends ingroup favoritism to former outgroup | Gaertner & Dovidio (2000) |

| Counter-stereotypic role models | Weakens stereotypic expectancies | Dasgupta & Asgari (2004) |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Tripartite model:** stereotypes = cognitive; prejudice = affective; discrimination = behavioral. Stereotypes aren't always negative ("women are wonderful").
2. **Prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1984):** losses weigh heavier than gains; whites frame equality as loss → perceive more progress (racial divide). Nobel 2002.
3. **Glass ceiling vs. glass cliff:** ceiling = barrier to the top; cliff (Ryan & Haslam, 2005) = women selected for risky/failing positions.
4. **Shifting standards (Biernat, 2005):** objective scales expose stereotyping; subjective scales conceal it.
5. **Singlism (DePaulo, 2006):** widespread but unrecognized; only 4% of singles identify as targets; discrimination seen as more legitimate than for race/gender/age/orientation/weight.
6. **Tokenism:** deters resistance more than total exclusion; token = equal conditions for meritocracy belief (Danaher & Branscombe, 2010); quota-only selection undermines performance (Brown et al., 2000).
7. **Stereotype Content Model (Fiske et al., 2002):** High W/Low C = pity (elderly); Low W/High C = envy (rich, Asians); Low W/Low C = contempt (homeless); High W/High C = admiration (ingroup).
8. **Robbers Cave (Sherif et al., 1961):** competition creates prejudice; superordinate goals reduce it. Classic realistic conflict theory.
9. **Aversive racism (Dovidio & Gaertner):** discrimination only under ambiguity; aversive racists believe they're unprejudiced.
10. **Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson, 1995):** stereotype salience impairs performance; Black students with race salience, women in math (Spencer et al., 1999); working memory depletion + arousal.
11. **Contact hypothesis (Allport, 1954):** four conditions (equal status, cooperative interdependence, acquaintance potential, authority support). Pettigrew & Tropp (2006): reduces prejudice via lower anxiety, higher empathy.
12. **Common ingroup identity model (Gaertner & Dovidio, 2000):** recategorization extends favoritism; dual identity often preferred.
13. **Collective guilt:** requires identification with the harming group AND seeing harm as illegitimate; low identifiers feel more (Doosje et al., 1998); leads to reparative support (Branscombe et al., 2004).
14. **Ultimate attribution error (Pettigrew, 1979):** intergroup FAE; outgroup negatives dispositional, positives situational.
15. **Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979):** membership → social identity → positive distinctiveness → ingroup favoritism (even minimal groups). RWA and SDO are independent personality predictors.
