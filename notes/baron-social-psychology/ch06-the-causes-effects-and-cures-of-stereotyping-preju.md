# 6 The Causes, Effects, and Cures of Stereotyping, Prejudice, and Discrimination

Chapter 6 examines the cognitive, affective, and behavioral components of intergroup bias —
stereotyping, prejudice, and discrimination — exploring how group membership shapes
perceptions of inequality, the psychological origins of bias, and evidence-based strategies
for reducing prejudice. The chapter treats these phenomena as products of normal cognitive
and motivational processes rather than pathological aberrations, making them universal
concerns for clinical and social psychology.

---

## How Members of Different Groups Perceive Inequality

- **Prejudice** = negative emotional responses based on group membership (can be toward
  any group: racial, gender, religious, age, marital status, occupational, sexual orientation,
  body weight, language spoken).
- **Discrimination** = differential (usually negative) behaviors directed toward members of
  different social groups.
- High-status groups consistently perceive less inequality than low-status groups in the same
  situation (Exline & Lobel, 1999).
- National surveys in the U.S.: white respondents perceive "a lot of progress" toward racial
  equality; black respondents perceive "not much progress" — the **racial divide** in perception.

### Prospect Theory Applied to Racial Perceptions

- **Prospect theory** (Kahneman & Tversky, 1984; Nobel Prize in Economics 2002): people are
  **risk averse** — they weigh possible losses more heavily than equivalent potential gains.
  A possible loss of $1 is subjectively more negative than a possible gain of $1 is positive.
- Applied to racial equality: whites frame greater equality as a **loss** from historically
  privileged position; blacks frame it as a **gain**. Because losses evoke stronger emotion
  than gains, increased equality is more negative for whites than it is positive for blacks.
- Whites who are highly identified with their racial group respond to challenged race-based
  privileges with **increased racism** and greater support for **tokenism** (limiting the number
  of minorities employed) (Branscombe, Schmitt, & Schiffhauer, 2007; Richard & Wright, 2010).

### KEY STUDY: Eibach & Keegan (2006) — Gains vs. Losses Framing

- **Method:** White and non-white participants drew graphs depicting changes in racial
  composition of U.S. university students from 1960 to present in one of three conditions:
  - "Minority gains AND white losses"
  - "White losses only"
  - "Minority gains only"
- **Findings:** In both conditions involving "white losses," white participants perceived greater
  progress toward equality than non-white participants (mirrors national surveys). In the
  "minority gains only" condition, whites perceived LESS progress — not different from
  non-white participants.
- **Conclusion:** The racial divide in perception partly stems from whites framing social change
  as losses in status/outcomes for their own group.

### Obama Election Effect (Kaiser et al., 2009)

- Pre- to post-Obama election: white Americans came to believe there was LESS need for
  further racial progress and expressed LESS support for social policies aimed at increasing
  racial equality.
- Presence of "token" minorities in highly visible positions leads majority group members to
  believe substantial change has occurred and less further change is needed.
- Tokenism: even a small percentage (e.g., 2%) of low-status group members advancing deters
  collective resistance (Lalonde & Silverman, 1994; Wright, Taylor, & Moghaddam, 1990).

### Affirmative Action and Perceived Threat

- When whites expect affirmative action will harm their group (framed as loss), they oppose
  it regardless of impact on minorities (Lowery, Unzueta, Goff, & Knowles, 2006).
- White South Africans oppose affirmative action when they perceive it as a threat to their
  high-status jobs and housing (Durrheim et al., 2009).
- In 21 European nations: perceived immigrant economic threat increases opposition to
  naturalization (Pereira, Vala, & Costa-Lopes, 2009).
- Hate crimes increase as minorities are perceived as gaining political power
  (Dancygier & Green, 2010).

## The Nature and Origins of Stereotyping

### Attitude-Based Framework: The Three Components

The three concepts map onto the classical tripartite attitude structure:

| Component | Concept | Definition |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Cognitive** | Stereotypes | Beliefs/expectations about what members of a group are like |
| **Affective** | Prejudice | Feelings we have toward a group |
| **Behavioral** | Discrimination | Differential actions toward members of specific groups |

- Classic model assumes: negative stereotypes → hostility → intentional discrimination.
- **Modern critique** (Adams, Biernat, Branscombe, Crandall, & Wrightsman, 2008): Are
  stereotypes always negative? Is prejudice always exclusion/hostility? Can discrimination
  occur without conscious intent? Can there be "benevolent prejudice"?

## Stereotyping: Beliefs About Social Groups

- **Stereotypes** = beliefs and expectations about what members of social groups are like.
  Includes traits, physical appearance, abilities, and behaviors (Biernat & Thompson, 2002;
  Deaux & LaFrance, 1998).
- Stereotypic traits can be positive or negative, accurate or inaccurate, and may or may not
  be accepted by members of the stereotyped group.

### Gender Stereotypes

- **Gender stereotypes** = beliefs concerning characteristics of women and men; stereotypes
  for each gender are typically the **converse** of one another.
- **Women:** High on warmth, low on competence — kind, nurturant, considerate (positive);
  dependent, weak, overly emotional (negative) (Fiske, Cuddy, Glick, & Xu, 2002).
- **Men:** High on competence, low on warmth — decisive, assertive, accomplished (positive);
  aggressive, insensitive, arrogant (negative).
- **"Women are wonderful" effect** (Eagly & Mladinic, 1994): Because of the strong warmth
  emphasis in women's stereotype, people feel more positively about women overall.
- Despite greater likeability, women's traits are seen as less appropriate for high-status/
  leadership positions.

#### Female Stereotypic Traits (Table 6.1)
Warm, Emotional, Kind/polite, Sensitive, Follower, Weak, Friendly, Fashionable, Gentle

#### Male Stereotypic Traits (Table 6.1)
Competent, Stable, Tough/coarse, Self-confident, Leader, Strong, Accomplished, Nonconformist, Aggressive

### Stereotypes and the Glass Ceiling

- **Glass ceiling** = barriers based on attitudinal or organizational bias that prevent qualified
  females from advancing to top-level positions.
- Only 16% of corporate officers in the U.S. are women; ~1% of Fortune 500 CEO positions
  are women (Catalyst, 2010).
- **"Think manager — think male" bias** (Schein, 2001; Bruckmüller & Branscombe, 2010):
  Stereotypic attributes of "typical manager" overlap considerably with "typical man" but
  share few attributes with "typical woman."
- This creates perceived **"lack of fit"** of women for organizational leadership
  (Eagly & Sczesny, 2009; Heilman, 2001).
- Women's labor force participation: 20% in 1900 → 59% in 2005.
- Women who succeed in competitive male-dominated environments are MOST likely to report
  gender discrimination (Redersdorff, Martinot, & Branscombe, 2004).
- When women lead with task-focused/authoritarian style (violating warmth expectations),
  they receive especially negative evaluations (Eagly & Karau, 2002).
- **Rudman & Fairchild (2004):** Participants told they performed well on a test typical of
  the other gender were more likely to lie about it — evidence of the social costs of defying
  gender stereotypes.
- **Duehr & Bono (2006):** Inconsistency between women's stereotype and the leadership
  stereotype has decreased over the past 10 years, particularly among women.

### Stereotypes and the Glass Cliff Effect

- **Glass cliff effect** (Ryan & Haslam, 2005, 2007) = choosing women for leadership positions
  that are risky, precarious, or when outcome is more likely to result in failure.
- **Archival studies (London Stock Exchange):** Companies with consistently poor stock
  performance in preceding months were more likely to appoint a woman to their boards.
- **Experimental confirmation:** When presented with equally qualified male and female
  candidates, female was selected significantly more often when the position was risky;
  male selected more often when not risky (Ryan et al., 2009).
- **Interpretation (Bruckmüller & Branscombe, 2010):** When men's stereotypic leadership
  attributes appear not to be working (organization on downward trend), women's presumed
  communal attributes are seen as suitable — ONLY then.
- Historical examples: Lynn Laverty Elsenhans (Sunoco, after 52% share drop); Johanna
  Siguroardottir (Iceland PM, after economic collapse).

### Consequences of Token Women in High Places

- **Tokenism** = numerically infrequent presence of previously excluded group members, or
  trivial positive actions for outgroup members used as justification for refusing more
  meaningful actions.
- Highly visible token women may lead observers to attribute women's general underrepresentation
  to their lacking qualities rather than structural barriers.

#### KEY STUDY: Danaher & Branscombe (2010) — Token Representation and Meritocracy

- **Method:** University women told composition of Board of Regents:
  - "Open" condition: 5/10 board members female
  - "Token" condition: 1/10 board members female
  - "Closed" condition: 0/10 board members female
- **Findings:** Women in BOTH open AND token conditions reported greater belief in
  meritocracy and greater organizational identification than in the closed condition.
- **Conclusion:** Token conditions — same as open/equal representation — encourage women
  to maintain faith they can advance and engender allegiance to organizations where they
  are substantially underrepresented; token practices serve to maintain the status quo.

#### KEY STUDY: Brown et al. (2000) — Gender Quota and Leadership Performance

- **Method:** Women told they were selected as group leader due to a gender quota vs.
  qualifications + gender vs. no information given.
- **Finding:** Women told selected ONLY by quota showed reduced leadership performance
  compared to other conditions.

### Negative Consequences of Tokenism (Full List)
1. Token hires perceived negatively by coworkers (Fuegen & Biernat, 2002; Yoder & Berendsen, 2001).
2. Affirmative action hires perceived as less competent (Heilman, Block, & Lucas, 1992).
3. Damages self-esteem and confidence of the token.
4. Lets prejudiced people "off the hook" — token is public proof of non-bigotry.
5. Token's presence maintains perception that the existing system is legitimate and fair, even
   among disadvantaged group members.

### Responses to Those Who Speak Out About Discrimination

#### KEY STUDY: Kaiser & Miller (2001) — Complaining About Discrimination

- **Method:** White participants read about an African American student who attributed his
  bad grade to racial discrimination ("complaint" condition) or accepted personal responsibility
  ("I'm responsible" condition).
- **Finding:** White perceivers evaluated the student MORE negatively in the complaint
  condition, regardless of whether they personally thought the grade was due to discrimination.
- **Conclusion:** Even when attribution to discrimination is accurate, observers respond
  negatively to complainers.
- Own ingroup may disapprove of discrimination claimers when they believe it could suggest
  the ingroup is given to unjustified griping (Garcia et al., 2005).
- Ingroup supports complaints only when: (1) discrimination is serious, and (2) complaining
  is likely to improve group's situation (Garcia, Schmitt, Branscombe, & Ellemers, 2010).

## SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Representations of Female and Male Figures in Video Games

- Video game market: $10 billion/year in the U.S. (Behm-Morawitz & Mastro, 2009).
- 40% of all game players in the U.S. are female; 80% of girls grades 4-12 play video games.
- Average devotee: male, ~34 years of age.
- 80% of role-playing games (e.g., Second Life) now have some female characters.
- **Dill & Thill (2007):** Video games offer the most blatant sex-role stereotyping of ANY
  type of mass media. 83% of male video game characters exhibit violent and hypermasculine
  attributes. Female characters: portrayed as "damsel in distress" or alluring sex object.
- **Objectification of females** = regarding women as mere bodies that exist for the pleasure
  of others.

### KEY STUDY: Fox & Bailenson (2009) — Sexualized Avatars and Rape Myth Acceptance

- **Method:** Male and female undergraduates exposed to female avatars varying in dress
  (suggestive vs. conservative) and gaze behavior (high-responsive vs. low-responsive).
- **Measures:** Hostile sexism, benevolent sexism, Burt's (1980) rape myth acceptance.
- **Findings:**
  - Suggestive dress + high gaze: highest rape myth acceptance AND more hostile sexism
    (both male and female participants).
  - Conservative dress + low gaze: more benevolent sexism (submissive/virgin stereotype).
- **Conclusion:** Sexualized avatars increase rape myth acceptance in both male and female
  observers.

### KEY STUDY: Dill, Brown, & Collins (2008) — Objectified Images and Tolerance for Sexual Harassment

- **Method:** Participants exposed to objectified female video game character vs. female
  politician image.
- **Findings:**
  - Males exposed to objectified images: increased tolerance for sexual harassment in a
    real-life case (female student/male professor).
  - Females exposed to objectified images: DECREASED tolerance for sexual harassment
    (energized to advocate for just treatment of women).
- **Conclusion:** Exposure to stereotypically drawn characters produces real change in
  attitudes that translate into real-life behavioral changes.

## Is Stereotyping Absent If Members of Different Groups Are Rated the Same?

- **Answer: NO.** Even when two people receive the same rating, stereotyping may have
  occurred.
- **Shifting standards** (Biernat, 2005) = using one group as the standard but shifting to
  use another group as the comparison when judging members of a different group.
- Example: "Large cat" and "small car" use the same word but entirely different referents.

### Objective vs. Subjective Scales

- **Objective scales** = measurement units tied to external reality, meaning the same
  regardless of category membership (e.g., dollars earned, feet and inches, percentage of
  free throws made, chosen or rejected).
- **Subjective scales** = response scales open to interpretation, lacking an externally
  grounded referent (e.g., "good to bad," "weak to strong"). Meaning can differ depending
  on the group membership of the person being evaluated.
- Subjective scales **CONCEAL** stereotypical judgments; objective scales **EXPOSE** them.
- Example: Female and male candidates both rated "good" on a subjective scale — but when
  rated in objective terms (dollars sold per year), the male may be rated higher.
- **Biernat, Collins, Katzarska-Miller, & Thompson (2009):** The more people show
  race-based shifting standards, the more they behaviorally discriminate against black job
  candidates and organizations.

## Can We Be Victims of Stereotyping and Not Even Recognize It?: The Case of Single People

- **Singlism** (DePaulo, 2006) = negative stereotyping and discrimination directed toward
  people who are single.
- Single people represent more than 40% of American adults (U.S. Census Bureau, 2007).

### KEY STUDY: DePaulo & Morris (2006) — Stereotypes of Single vs. Married People

- **Method:** Over 1,000 undergraduates described attributes of "singles" vs. "married" people.
- **Findings (Table 6.3):**
  - Single people described as: immature, insecure, self-centered, unhappy, ugly, lonely,
    independent.
  - Married people described as: mature, stable, kind, happy, honest, loving, giving.
  - 50% of time, married described as kind/giving/caring; only 2% for singles.
  - The difference is even greater when targets are described as over 40 vs. 25 years old.
- **Discrimination evidence:** Undergraduates preferred to rent to a married couple (70%)
  over a single man (12%) or single woman (18%).
- Legal privileges of married status: employer-subsidized health benefits, auto insurance
  discounts, club memberships, tax and Social Security benefits.

### Why Singlism Is Not Challenged

- Only **4%** of single people spontaneously mention "single" as a discrimination target.
- Only **30%** of singles say single people could be stigmatized — contrast with nearly ALL
  members of other stigmatized groups (race, weight, sexual orientation).
- **Morris, Sinclair, & DePaulo (2007):** When asked about a landlord refusing to rent to
  various groups, participants agreed it was discrimination for all EXCEPT when the person
  refused was single. Discrimination against single people is seen as MORE LEGITIMATE
  than any of these other forms by both single and married people.
- **Explanation:** Negative stereotyping of singles serves to protect and glorify marriage
  as a social institution; singles challenge the cultural belief that finding and marrying
  one's soulmate is crucial to a meaningful life.

## Why Do People Form and Use Stereotypes?

- Stereotypes function as **schemas** — cognitive frameworks for organizing, interpreting,
  and recalling information (Fiske & Taylor, 2008).
- Primary reason: **cognitive efficiency** — humans act as "cognitive misers" and invest the
  least cognitive effort possible (Bodenhausen, 1993; Macrae, Milne, & Bodenhausen, 1994).
- Stereotypes also serve **motivational purposes**: help us feel positive about our own group
  identity in comparison to others.

### Which Stereotype Is Activated?

- Context determines which stereotype is most salient (Yzerbyt & Demoulin, 2010) — e.g.,
  in a restaurant context, the occupational role "waitress" may be most salient even when
  race and gender are also visible.

### How Stereotypes Operate

1. Once activated, stereotypic traits come automatically to mind (Bodenhausen & Hugenberg, 2009).
2. Information relevant to an activated stereotype is processed more quickly and remembered
   better (Dovidio, Evans, & Tyler, 1986; Macrae, Bodenhausen, Milne, & Ford, 1997).
3. Stereotypes direct attention to stereotype-consistent information.
4. Stereotypes act as **theories** guiding what we attend to and how we process social
   information (Yzerbyt, Rocher, & Schadron, 1997).

### When Stereotypes Are Disconfirmed: Subtyping

- **Subtyping** = placing disconfirming individuals into a special subcategory seen as not
  typical of the group, protecting the overall stereotype from revision (Queller & Smith, 2002;
  Richards & Hewstone, 2001; Park, Wolsko, & Judd, 2001).
- Example: A highly intelligent member of a low-status group is "exceptional" and does not
  require updating the stereotype.

### Do Stereotypes Ever Change?

- Stereotype change occurs when the relations between groups change (Eagly, 1987;
  Oakes et al., 1994; Pettigrew, 1981; Tajfel, 1981).

#### KEY STUDY: Dasgupta & Asgari (2004) — Women's College vs. Coeducational College

- **Method:** Women's gender stereotypes assessed in Year 1 and Year 2 at either a women's
  college (more exposure to women faculty in nontraditional roles) or a coeducational
  college (less such exposure).
- **Finding:** Women at women's colleges showed decreased gender stereotyping by Year 2
  compared to women at coeducational colleges.
- **Conclusion:** Exposure to counter-stereotypic role models weakens stereotypes over time.

## Prejudice: Feelings Toward Social Groups

- **Prejudice** = a negative attitude toward members of some group based solely on their
  membership in that group.
- Components: cognitive (stereotypes), affective (feelings), behavioral (discriminatory intent).
- Can be directed toward any group membership; can be perceived as legitimate or illegitimate;
  blatant or subtle (Barreto & Ellemers, 2005; Dovidio, Gaertner, & Kawakami, 2010).
- **Crandall, Eshleman, & O'Brien (2002):** Some prejudice is perceived (by perpetrators
  AND victims) as justified and legitimate.

### Benevolent Prejudice and Ambivalent Sexism

- Not all prejudice involves hostility.
- **Ambivalent sexism theory** (Glick & Fiske, 1996): Two forms of sexism coexist:
  - **Hostile sexism** = negative, resentful attitudes toward women who challenge traditional
    gender roles.
  - **Benevolent sexism** = positive but patronizing attitudes; women placed on a pedestal
    but still restricted to traditional roles.
- Together, hostile and benevolent sexism serve to maintain gender hierarchy.

### Affect in Prejudice: Distinct Emotions for Different Groups

- Prejudice is not a single emotion; different groups evoke different emotional responses
  (Cottrell & Neuberg, 2005).
- **Stereotype Content Model** (Fiske, Cuddy, Glick, & Xu, 2002): Groups vary on two
  dimensions — warmth and competence:
  - High warmth + high competence (ingroup): **admiration**
  - Low warmth + high competence (e.g., rich people, Asians): **envy**
  - High warmth + low competence (e.g., elderly, disabled): **pity**
  - Low warmth + low competence (e.g., welfare recipients, homeless): **contempt/disgust**

## The Origins of Prejudice: Contrasting Perspectives

### 1. Realistic Conflict Theory

- **Realistic conflict theory** (Sherif, 1966) = prejudice stems from competition between
  groups for scarce, valued resources (jobs, land, power, housing).
- Competition creates hostility and negative attitudes toward the competitor.
- Prejudice is "realistic" because it IS based on genuine conflict of interests.

#### KEY STUDY: Robbers Cave Experiment (Sherif et al., 1961)

- **Setting:** Summer camp, Robbers Cave State Park, Oklahoma; 11-12 year old boys.
- **Phase 1:** Two groups formed (Eagles and Rattlers); developed separate identities.
- **Phase 2 (Competition):** Groups competed for prizes. Result: rapid hostility escalation,
  name-calling, raids on cabins, burning of the other group's flag.
- **Phase 3 (Superordinate goals):** Introduced problems requiring cooperation (e.g., fixing
  water supply). Shared goals requiring interdependence reduced intergroup hostility.
- **Key finding:** Competition creates prejudice; cooperation toward superordinate goals
  reduces it.
- **Limitation:** Competition does not fully account for all prejudice — prejudice exists even
  without real conflict (see minimal group paradigm).

### 2. Social Identity Theory

- **Social identity theory** (Tajfel & Turner, 1979, 1986) = people categorize themselves
  and others into social groups; group membership provides **social identity** (part of
  self-concept).
- People are motivated to maintain **positive social identity** → evaluate their own group
  (ingroup) more favorably than other groups (outgroup) = **ingroup favoritism**.
- **Social categorization** = classifying people into groups based on shared attributes.
- **Self-categorization theory** (Turner et al., 1987): At different times, we see ourselves
  more in terms of individual identity or group identity.
- **Minimal group paradigm** (Tajfel, 1971): Even arbitrary/trivial group assignments
  (preference for Klee vs. Kandinsky paintings) produce ingroup favoritism and outgroup
  discrimination.
- **Positive distinctiveness** = the desire to have one's own group perceived as better than
  outgroups on valued dimensions.
- Applied to same-sex marriage (Schmitt, Lehmiller, & Walsh, 2007): "marriage" label for
  same-sex relationships threatens heterosexual positive distinctiveness; "civil union" does
  not.

### 3. Social Learning Perspective

- Children acquire prejudice through **socialization**: parents, peers, teachers, and media.
- **Operant conditioning:** Prejudiced attitudes reinforced when expressed in approving
  social contexts.
- **Observational learning/modeling:** Children observe parents and peers expressing
  prejudiced attitudes.

### 4. Cognitive Perspective: Social Categorization

- Tendency to categorize people is a fundamental cognitive process (Allport, 1954; Tajfel, 1981).
- **Outgroup homogeneity effect** = perceiving members of outgroups as more similar to one
  another than ingroup members (Park & Rothbart, 1982). "They all look alike."
- Ingroup-outgroup distinction automatically triggers preferential treatment of ingroup.

### 5. Right-Wing Authoritarianism (RWA)

- **Right-wing authoritarianism (RWA)** (Altemeyer, 1981, 1988) = personality dimension
  characterized by:
  1. **Authoritarian submission** — submission to established authority
  2. **Authoritarian aggression** — aggression toward targets sanctioned by authority
  3. **Conventionalism** — adherence to conventional norms/values
- High-RWA individuals show more prejudice across a broader range of outgroups.

### 6. Social Dominance Orientation (SDO)

- **Social dominance orientation (SDO)** (Sidanius & Pratto, 1999) = degree to which
  individuals desire their own social group to be dominant over other social groups; support
  for group-based hierarchy.
- High-SDO individuals are less egalitarian and more likely to endorse group inequality.
- SDO and RWA are independent predictors of prejudice; they often correlate and additively
  predict prejudice toward many outgroups.

## EMOTIONS AND PREJUDICE: When Are People Willing to Die and Kill for Their Group?

- **Intergroup emotions theory** (Smith, 1993; Mackie & Smith, 2002): We experience
  emotions as group members based on the fortunes and actions of our group.
- **Sacred values** (Tetlock, 2003): Some group-related commitments (religious, national,
  moral) resist trade-offs with material goods; people react with moral outrage to "taboo
  trade-offs."
- When a group's sacred values are threatened, members are willing to fight and die in
  defense of those values (Atran and colleagues).
- Ingroup identification increases willingness to engage in extreme behavior on behalf of
  the group when group identity or values are threatened.
- **Schadenfreude** = pleasure in the misfortune of rival groups; more intense among highly
  identified group members (Leach, Spears, Branscombe, & Doosje, 2003).

## Discrimination: Prejudice in Action

- Discrimination can be **blatant** (overt, intentional) or **subtle** (covert, unintentional).
- Differential treatment can occur even without conscious intent.
- **Ambivalent racism** (Katz, 1981): White Americans simultaneously hold positive
  (egalitarian) attitudes and negative (stereotypic) beliefs about black Americans, resulting
  in ambivalent and inconsistent behavior.
- **Aversive racism** (Dovidio & Gaertner, 1986, 2004):
  - Aversive racists sincerely endorse egalitarian values; do NOT consider themselves
    prejudiced.
  - They harbor unconscious negative feelings and beliefs about racial minorities.
  - They avoid blatant discrimination but discriminate in subtle ways, especially when there
    is a justification or when norms about appropriate behavior are unclear.

### KEY STUDY: Dovidio & Gaertner (2000) — Aversive Racism in Hiring Decisions

- **Method:** White participants reviewed applications from white or black candidates
  varying in qualification strength: clearly strong, ambiguous, or clearly weak.
- **Findings:**
  - STRONG or WEAK qualifications: no racial discrimination (blatant discrimination would
    be obvious).
  - AMBIGUOUS qualifications: white candidates favored over equally qualified black
    candidates.
- **Conclusion:** Aversive racists discriminate when there is justification (ambiguity) to
  avoid appearing racially biased.

## Modern Racism: More Subtle, but Just as Deadly

- **Modern racism** (McConahay, 1986) = a subtle form of racial prejudice in which people
  sincerely believe they are not prejudiced and that discrimination is largely over, while
  simultaneously holding negative attitudes that manifest in non-obvious ways.
- Modern racism scale items:
  - "Discrimination against black people is no longer a problem."
  - "Blacks are getting too demanding in their push for equal rights."
- High scorers show subtle discrimination in hiring and other decisions.

### Measuring Implicit (Unconscious) Prejudice

- **Implicit Association Test (IAT)** (Greenwald, McGhee, & Schwartz, 1998): Measures
  strength of automatic associations between concepts (e.g., Black/white faces and
  pleasant/unpleasant words) using reaction time.
  - Faster responding to "Black + Unpleasant" than "Black + Pleasant" indicates implicit
    negative association.
  - Most white Americans show implicit negative associations with black Americans on the
    IAT, even those who score low on explicit prejudice measures.
- **Implicit vs. explicit prejudice dissociation (Dovidio, Kawakami, & Gaertner, 2002):**
  - Explicit prejudice predicts deliberate, controlled behaviors.
  - Implicit prejudice predicts automatic, less controlled behaviors.
- **Fazio et al. (1995):** White participants with stronger negative automatic associations
  with Black faces treated a black experimenter more negatively in a subsequent interaction.

### Stereotype Threat

- **Stereotype threat** (Steele & Aronson, 1995) = the fear of confirming a negative
  stereotype about one's group, which itself impairs performance.
- Black students reminded of their race before a difficult test perform worse than in a
  control condition where race is not made salient.
- Mediated by cognitive processes (working memory depletion due to self-monitoring) and
  physiological arousal.
- Affects multiple groups: women in math (Spencer, Steele, & Quinn, 1999), elderly in
  memory tasks, low-SES students.
- **Implication:** Minimizing stereotype threat in test environments improves actual
  performance for stigmatized group members.

## Why Prejudice Is Not Inevitable: Techniques for Countering Its Effects

Prejudice is not an inevitable result of cognitive and motivational processes — it CAN be
reduced through evidence-based strategies.

---

## On Learning Not to Hate

- **Socialization** shapes prejudice early — it can be countered by teaching children more
  inclusive norms.
- Parental expression of egalitarian norms and modeling of inclusive behavior → children
  show less prejudice.
- **Media representation:** Positive, counter-stereotypic portrayals of outgroups reduce
  prejudice.
- **Multicultural education** emphasizing positive aspects of diverse groups reduces
  prejudice in children.

---

## The Potential Benefits of Contact

- **Contact hypothesis** (Allport, 1954) = increased contact between members of different
  social groups can reduce prejudice between them.
- Not all contact reduces prejudice — superficial or competitive contact can increase it.

### Conditions for Effective Intergroup Contact (Allport's Four Conditions)

1. **Equal status** within the contact situation (not hierarchical).
2. **Cooperative interdependence** (working toward common goals, not competing).
3. **Acquaintance potential** (contact close enough to disconfirm negative stereotypes and
   allow personal interaction).
4. **Support from authority/social norms** (institutional support from laws, customs, or
   authorities).

### KEY STUDY: Pettigrew & Tropp (2006) — Meta-Analysis of Contact

- **515 studies, 250,000+ participants.**
- **Finding:** Intergroup contact reliably reduces prejudice; effect is larger when Allport's
  conditions are met, but even contact without all four conditions tends to reduce prejudice.
- **Primary mechanism:** Contact reduces prejudice mainly by reducing **intergroup anxiety**
  and increasing **empathy** for outgroup members (Pettigrew & Tropp, 2008).
- **Generalization:** Prejudice reduction from contact with specific outgroup members can
  generalize to the outgroup as a whole (Pettigrew, 1997).

### Extended Contact Effect

- **Extended contact effect** (Wright et al., 1997) = merely KNOWING that an ingroup
  member has a close relationship with an outgroup member can reduce intergroup prejudice
  and anxiety, even without direct contact oneself.

---

## Recategorization: Changing the Boundaries

- **Recategorization** = redefining group boundaries so that previously distinct groups are
  combined into a single superordinate group ("us" replaces "us vs. them").
- **Common ingroup identity model** (Gaertner & Dovidio, 2000) = inducing members of two
  groups to recategorize themselves as members of one superordinate group reduces bias and
  discrimination.
- When people think of themselves as members of one group (e.g., "Americans" rather than
  "African Americans" vs. "white Americans"), they extend ingroup favoritism to former
  outgroup members.
- **Decategorization** = breaking down category distinctions entirely, emphasizing individual
  rather than group identities; can also reduce prejudice.
- **Cross-cutting social categories** = when group memberships cross-cut rather than
  coincide, ingroup/outgroup lines blur — reduces the strength of any single categorization.

### Limitations of Recategorization

- **Subgroup identity threat:** Members who strongly identify with their original subgroup
  may resist superordinate categorization as threatening their group's distinctiveness.
- **Dual identity model** (Dovidio & Gaertner): Allowing people to maintain BOTH subgroup
  identity AND superordinate identity (e.g., "I am both African American AND American") is
  often more effective than requiring abandonment of subgroup identity.

---

## The Benefits of Guilt for Prejudice Reduction

- **Collective guilt** = an emotion experienced by group members when their group is
  responsible for harm to another group.
- When white Americans are made aware of historical injustices perpetrated by white
  Americans against Black Americans, they can experience collective guilt.
- Collective guilt motivates willingness to make amends and support restorative actions
  (e.g., affirmative action, reparations).
- **Branscombe et al. (2004):** White Americans induced to feel collective guilt for white
  privilege showed increased support for affirmative action policies.
- **Boundary conditions:** Collective guilt only works when:
  (1) People identify with the group that committed the harm, AND
  (2) They recognize the harm as illegitimate.
- **Doosje, Branscombe, Spears, & Manstead (1998):** Dutch participants low in national
  identification were more likely to experience collective guilt about Dutch colonial history
  than highly identified participants (who used defensive strategies: minimizing harm or
  denying group responsibility).

---

## Can We Learn to "Just Say No" to Stereotyping and Biased Attributions?

- Automatic stereotype activation does NOT necessarily lead to stereotyped responses —
  people CAN control stereotypic thinking.
- **Monteith (1993):** Individuals internally motivated to avoid prejudice (low-prejudice-
  oriented individuals) engage in **self-regulation** to inhibit stereotypic responding.
  - When low-prejudice individuals respond in a prejudiced way and are made aware of it,
    they experience **discrepancy-associated affect** (guilt, discomfort) which motivates
    change.
  - This guilt becomes a cue for slowing down and thinking carefully in situations where
    stereotypic responses are likely.

### Stereotype Suppression and Rebound Effect

- Initial attempts to suppress stereotypic thoughts can produce a **rebound effect**
  (Wegner, 1994): suppressed thoughts become hyperaccessible and return more strongly
  after suppression is stopped.
- However: the rebound is NOT inevitable — people who are motivated AND have cognitive
  resources can suppress stereotypes without subsequent rebound.
- **Implementation intentions** (Gollwitzer, 1999) = specific if-then plans for stereotyping-
  prone situations (e.g., "If I am about to judge this person based on their race, I will stop
  and think carefully") — reduce automatic stereotypic responding.

### Attributional Biases Related to Prejudice

- **Ultimate attribution error** (Pettigrew, 1979) = attributional extension of the
  fundamental attribution error to intergroup situations:
  - Outgroup members' **negative** behaviors attributed to internal, stable dispositions.
  - Outgroup members' **positive** behaviors attributed to external, unstable causes
    ("They got lucky" / "This is an exception").
  - Ingroup members' **negative** behaviors attributed to situational factors.
  - Ingroup members' **positive** behaviors attributed to internal dispositions.
- **Czopp & Monteith (2003):** Confronting people about racially biased attributions causes
  guilt and subsequently reduces biased attributions.

---

## Social Influence as a Means of Reducing Prejudice

- Social norms powerfully regulate the expression of prejudice — people generally want to
  be seen as non-prejudiced by others.
- **Normative influence** = when social norms make prejudice costly (social disapproval),
  expressions of prejudice decrease.

### Ingroup Confrontation Is More Effective

- **Czopp, Monteith, & Mark (2006):** Being confronted about a prejudiced remark by an
  INGROUP member causes more guilt and less subsequent prejudiced responding than when
  confronted by an outgroup member.
- **Why:** Ingroup confrontation is harder to dismiss as self-interested, so it is more
  persuasive.

### Intergroup Norms and Consensus Information

- **Stangor, Sechrist, & Jost (2001):** Telling participants that most people have changed
  their stereotype about a group shifts individual stereotypes in that direction.
- **Pluralistic ignorance** = situation where people privately reject a norm but assume others
  accept it, leading them to conform; when people learn others also privately oppose the norm,
  it loses influence.

### Empathy-Based Perspective-Taking

- **Galinsky & Moskowitz (2000):** Imagining life from the perspective of an outgroup member
  reduces prejudice toward that outgroup.
- Reducing intergroup anxiety is one of the key mechanisms through which contact, empathy,
  and social influence reduce prejudice (Pettigrew & Tropp, 2008).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Tripartite model:** Stereotypes = cognitive; Prejudice = affective; Discrimination =
   behavioral. This is the foundational framework for the entire chapter. Stereotypes are
   NOT necessarily negative (women are wonderful effect).

2. **Prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1984):** Risk averse = losses weigh heavier than
   equivalent gains. Applied to race: whites frame equality as loss → perceive more progress
   has been made than blacks do → racial divide in perception. Nobel Prize 2002.

3. **Glass ceiling vs. glass cliff:** Glass ceiling = invisible barrier preventing women from
   reaching top positions. Glass cliff (Ryan & Haslam, 2005) = women selected specifically
   for risky/precarious/failing positions. Both are high MCQ targets.

4. **Shifting standards (Biernat, 2005):** Same subjective rating does NOT mean equal
   expectation across groups. Objective scales (dollars/year, % free throws) expose
   stereotyping; subjective scales (good/bad) conceal it.

5. **Singlism (DePaulo, 2006):** Discrimination against single people is widespread but
   largely unrecognized. Only 4% of singles spontaneously identify as a discrimination
   target; discrimination against singles seen as MORE legitimate than discrimination
   based on race, gender, age, sexual orientation, or weight.

6. **Tokenism effects (Wright et al., 1990; Danaher & Branscombe, 2010):** (a) Deters
   collective resistance even more effectively than total exclusion; (b) Token conditions
   encourage meritocracy beliefs and organizational identification to the same degree as
   equal representation; (c) Performance is undermined when selection is attributed solely
   to group membership quota (Brown et al., 2000).

7. **Stereotype Content Model (Fiske et al., 2002):** Warmth × Competence matrix:
   High W/Low C = pity (elderly, disabled); Low W/High C = envy (rich, Asians); Low W/Low
   C = contempt (homeless, welfare); High W/High C = admiration (ingroup). Women and
   elderly stereotyped as high warmth/low competence.

8. **Robbers Cave Experiment (Sherif et al., 1961):** Phase 1 (ingroup formation) → Phase
   2 (competition creates hostility) → Phase 3 (superordinate goals reduce prejudice). THE
   classic demonstration of realistic conflict theory.

9. **Aversive racism (Dovidio & Gaertner):** Blatant discrimination avoided (obvious);
   subtle discrimination occurs in ambiguous situations when justification exists. Aversive
   racists sincerely believe they are not prejudiced. Key finding: discrimination in ambiguous
   (not clear-cut) qualification conditions only.

10. **Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson, 1995):** Mere salience of negative group
    stereotype impairs performance. Black students + race salience = worse test performance.
    Also: women in math (Spencer et al., 1999). Mechanism: working memory depletion +
    physiological arousal.

11. **Contact hypothesis conditions (Allport, 1954):** Four conditions: equal status +
    cooperative interdependence + acquaintance potential + institutional/authority support.
    Pettigrew & Tropp (2006) meta-analysis (515 studies, 250,000+ participants): contact
    reduces prejudice primarily by reducing intergroup anxiety and increasing empathy.

12. **Common ingroup identity model (Gaertner & Dovidio, 2000):** Recategorization into
    a superordinate group extends ingroup favoritism to former outgroup members. Dual
    identity approach (both subgroup AND superordinate identity maintained) often preferred.

13. **Collective guilt:** Triggered when one identifies with a group that harmed another AND
    recognizes the harm as illegitimate. Low national identifiers experience more collective
    guilt than high identifiers, who use defensive strategies (Doosje et al., 1998). Leads to
    support for reparative policies (Branscombe et al., 2004).

14. **Ultimate attribution error (Pettigrew, 1979):** Intergroup version of FAE: outgroup
    negatives → dispositional; outgroup positives → situational ("exception to the rule").
    Reverse for ingroup. Distinguish from FAE by the intergroup context specification.

15. **Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979):** Group membership → social identity
    → motivation for positive distinctiveness → ingroup favoritism even with minimal groups.
    Directly explains same-sex marriage opposition: "marriage" label threatens heterosexual
    positive distinctiveness (Schmitt et al., 2007). RWA and SDO are independent
    personality predictors of prejudice.
