# 4 The Self: Answering the Question "Who Am I?"

The chapter explores how people understand, present, and evaluate themselves in social contexts. It examines methods of self-knowledge (introspection, observer perspective), the personal-social identity continuum, self-esteem, social comparison, and the consequences of being a target of prejudice. Social media (Facebook) is used as a running illustration of modern self-presentation dynamics.

---

## Self-Presentation: Managing the Self in Different Social Contexts

- **Self-presentation** refers to managing the image we project to different audiences in different social contexts.
- Shakespeare's metaphor: "All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players" (from *As You Like It*) captures the social psychological view that we play different roles for different audiences.
- On social networking sites (SNS) like Facebook, self-presentation choices are especially visible: users control what information is shared, with whom, and in what form.
- Key tension: How much can we control what others learn about us, and how accurately do those impressions reflect who we really are?

### Self-Other Accuracy in Predicting Our Behavior

- Intuitive assumption: We know ourselves better than anyone else, because only we have access to our **internal mental states** (feelings, thoughts, aspirations, intentions) — Pronin & Kruger, 2007; Wilson & Dunn, 2004.
- Paradox (Pronin, Kruger, Savitsky & Ross, 2001): People believe they know themselves better than others do, AND claim they know some others better than those others know themselves.
- Problem with self-reports: Behavioral self-reports are not objective. Knowing one's own intentions can bias self-perception (e.g., "I mean to be on time" leads one to believe they usually are).

> **KEY STUDY: Vazire & Mehl (2008) — EAR Study (Electronically Activated Recorder)**
> - **Method:** Participants wore a digital audio recorder (EAR device) with a microphone that recorded ambient sounds every ~12.5 minutes for 4 days during waking hours. Research assistants coded the sounds into behavioral categories.
> - **Measures:** Participants rated their own behavioral frequencies; three informants (friends, parents, romantic partners) rated the participant's behavioral frequencies; actual frequencies coded from recordings.
> - **Finding:** Results were mixed. Sometimes self-ratings correlated more strongly with actual behavior (e.g., talking to same sex, watching TV, listening to music). Other times, informants' aggregated ratings were more accurate (e.g., being with other people, attending class, socializing). Aggregated informant ratings often outperformed single informants.
> - **Conclusion:** Sometimes we can predict our own behavior better than others; sometimes others know us better. Self-other accuracy is domain-specific.

- **Self and other agreement on Web-based vs. real-world interaction (Marcus, Machilek, & Schütz, 2006):** Agreement between self-view and others' inferences was *higher* for Web-based social interactions than for real-world interactions, likely because people have total control over information conveyed on personal web pages.

### SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Does Facebook Use Change Our Offline Behavior?

#### Two Competing Hypotheses (Zywicka & Danowski, 2008)

1. **Social Compensation Hypothesis:** Introverts and socially anxious adolescents use Facebook to substitute online contacts for an undesirable offline social life. Those who lack self-presentational skills are more attracted to online interaction (Caplan, 2005).
2. **Social Enhancement Hypothesis:** Extroverted, outgoing adolescents are motivated to add online contacts to an already large network to reflect their existing positive self-view (Valkenburg, Schouten, & Peter, 2005).
- Both hypotheses received some support.

#### Social Capital and Facebook (Ellison, Steinfield, & Lampe, 2007)

- **Social capital** = the number of social ties each person has to others (connections people draw on for knowledge, assistance, or other social goods).
- Stronger support found for the **Social Compensation Hypothesis**: Those lower in life satisfaction and self-esteem developed *more* social capital via Facebook — relating to more diverse others and forming useful relationships.
- Socially skilled users maintained high self-esteem through Facebook use; initially less skilled users *increased* their self-esteem with higher Facebook usage.
- Result: Both high and low self-esteem users find Facebook desirable, but for different reasons.

#### Internet and the "True Self" (Bargh, McKenna & Fitzsimons, 2002)

- Shy, less socially skilled people are better able to express their **"true self"** over the Internet than in face-to-face interaction.
- Introverts find their "true self" *online*; extroverts find it in *face-to-face* interactions (Amichai-Hamburger, Wainapel, & Fox, 2002).

#### Possible Selves and Offline Transfer (Joinson, 2003; Sheeks & Birchmeier, 2007)

- Facebook acceptance may activate a **"possible self"** as a popular, socially skilled person.
- This can cause users to interpret offline experiences differently — fostering higher offline self-esteem and social success.
- **Sheeks & Birchmeier (2007):** Shy, socially anxious people gained social skills online that were *transferred* to real life — primarily among initially shy, unskilled individuals.
- Conclusion: **Cyber-optimists are right** — online experience reduces the skills gap between low- and high-social-skill individuals in offline settings.

## Self-Presentation Tactics

### Self-Promotion
- **Self-promotion:** Attempting to present ourselves to others as having positive attributes (e.g., emphasizing intelligence credentials, awards, degrees).
- Saying we are good at something often leads others to believe it, and may even convince ourselves.

### Self-Verification
- **Self-verification perspective** (Swann, 2005): Processes by which we lead others to agree with our own self-views. We negotiate with others to ensure they agree with our self-claims.
- Even if it means receiving *negative* information, we prefer others — especially close others — to see us as we see ourselves (Swann & Bosson, 2010).
- We prefer to be with people who verify our self-views over those who do not — even if those self-views are unflattering (Chen, Chen, & Shaw, 2004).
- **Limit:** People who fear they are low in physical attractiveness do NOT appreciate close others who verify this self-view (Swann & Bosson, 2010).

### Ingratiation and Self-Deprecation
- **Ingratiation:** Trying to make others like us by conveying that we like them; praising others to flatter them. Effective unless overdone (then perceived as insincere — Vonk, 1999).
- **Self-deprecating:** Putting ourselves down or implying we are not as good as someone else — used to communicate admiration or to lower the audience's expectations.

### Honesty in Self-Presentation
- College students report telling lies approximately **twice a day** (Kashy & DePaulo, 1996) — frequently to advance their own interests but sometimes to protect others.
- Those who tell more lies are *more popular*.
- Online self-presentation (Ellison, Heino, & Gibbs, 2006): People attempt to balance authentic self-presentation with "self-deceptive white lies." Online profiles typically reflect **ideal self** rather than **actual self**.

## Self-Knowledge: Determining Who We Are

Two primary methods examined:
1. Introspection (looking inward)
2. Taking the observer's perspective on the self

---

### Introspection: Looking Inward to Discover the Causes of Our Own Behavior

- **Introspection:** To privately contemplate "who we are"; a method for attempting to gain self-knowledge. Widely recommended in self-help literature.
- Central question: Is introspection actually the best method for gaining accurate self-knowledge?

#### Limits of Introspection

**1. Lack of conscious access to real reasons (Nisbett & Wilson, 1977):**
- People do not always know the actual causes of their actions.
- After the fact, they generate plausible but potentially inaccurate "theories" about their behavior.

**2. Attitude change from introspection (Wilson & Kraft, 1993):**
- Studies on introspection about romantic partners and jam preferences showed that after introspecting on reasons for feelings, people **changed their attitudes temporarily** to match their stated reasons.
- Problem: Original feelings (based on other factors) persist, leading to regrettable inferences and choices.

**3. Affective forecasting failures (Gilbert & Wilson, 2000):**
- When predicting future feelings about negative events, people **focus exclusively on the awfulness of the event** and neglect other factors contributing to happiness — leading to predictions of feeling *much worse* than they actually do.
- For positive events, focusing only on the great future event leads to predicting *much higher* happiness than the actual moderate feelings experienced.
- **Focalism:** Tendency to focus on one future event while neglecting other life factors.

**4. Prosocial spending vs. personal spending (Dunn, Aknin, & Norton, 2008):**

> **KEY STUDY: Dunn, Aknin & Norton (2008) — Prosocial Spending and Happiness**
> - **Survey study:** Nationally representative sample of Americans rated happiness and indicated how much monthly income they spent on themselves vs. others/charity. Personal spending was *unrelated* to happiness; spending on others *predicted* greater happiness — regardless of annual income level.
> - **Experiment:** Psychology students rated happiness in the morning, then received $5 or $20 to spend by 5:00 PM. Half spent on a personal bill/gift for themselves; half on a charitable donation/gift for someone else. Results: Those who spent on *others* reported significantly greater happiness, regardless of amount.
> - **Introspection failure:** New participants asked to estimate which condition would bring greater happiness overwhelmingly predicted spending on themselves would be happier — wrong. They also predicted $20 would bring more happiness than $5 — also wrong.
> - **Conclusion:** We often do not know how events will affect us, and introspecting about it will not help.

**5. Mental subtraction of positive events (Koo, Algoe, Wilson, & Gilbert, 2008):**
- Thinking about how positive outcomes might *not* have happened (mental subtraction) makes people feel *happier* than simply thinking about positive outcomes that did happen — counterintuitive and not predicted by introspection.

#### When Introspection IS Helpful
- When behavior is based on **conscious decision-making** (not unconscious emotional factors), introspection may lead to accurate self-judgments.
- Introspection fails when real causes are emotional or unconscious.

### The Self from the Other's Standpoint

#### Actor-Observer Differences in Self-Perception
- **Actors** direct attention outward → attribute more situational causes for behavior.
- **Observers** focus on the actor → attribute more dispositional/trait causes.
- If we take an **observer's perspective on ourselves**, we should characterize ourselves in more dispositional/trait terms.

> **KEY STUDY: Pronin & Ross (2006) — Observer Perspective on Past Self**
> - **Study 1:** Both college-aged and middle-aged participants described their past self (5 years ago) and their current self. Past self was described using *more trait terms* (consistent dispositions) than current self — regardless of age group.
> - Interpretation: Viewing the past self is like taking an observer's perspective — we see consistent behavioral tendencies.
> - **Study 2 (Acting techniques):** Participants divided into "method-acting" condition (goal: feel as if you ARE this person) vs. "standard-acting" (goal: appear to others as though you are this person). Then they enacted a family dinner at age 14 as either an "experiencing" actor or "outside observer."
> - Results: Those using observer perspective described their 14-year-old self with more consistent traits. Those using method-actor technique saw fewer consistent traits.
> - **Conclusion:** To gain self-insight, try to see ourselves as others do — we see ourselves in terms of consistent behavioral tendencies.

## Who Am I?: Personal versus Social Identity

### Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1986)

- **Social identity theory:** Addresses how we respond when our group identity is salient. We can perceive ourselves differently depending on where we are on the **personal-versus-social identity continuum**.
- **Personal-versus-social identity continuum:** At the personal end, the self is seen as a unique individual; at the social identity end, the self is seen as a member of a group.
- **Salience:** When someone or some object stands out from its background or is the focus of attention. The part of our identity that is salient influences how we perceive ourselves and respond to others.

#### Personal Identity
- At the **personal** end: emphasis on how we differ from other individuals.
- Self-descriptions involve **intragroup comparisons** — comparisons with other individuals who share our group membership.
- Example: You might call yourself "liberal" when comparing to parents, but "conservative" when comparing to other college students — same person, different content, based on comparison.

#### Social Identity
- At the **social identity** end: emphasis on what we share with other group members.
- Self-descriptions involve **intergroup comparisons** — contrasts between groups.
- Self-stereotyping: Attributing to oneself traits believed to characterize one's group (e.g., when gender identity is salient, women may describe themselves as warm and caring).

#### Key Principle
- There is no single "true" self. All self-definitions (personal or social) can be accurate and predict behavior, depending on context and comparison dimension (Oakes & Reynolds, 1997; Reynolds et al., 2010).
- Most people maintain a coherent self-image despite this variability by deeming inconsistent domains as relatively unimportant or by not making them salient simultaneously.

---

### Who I Think I Am Depends on the Social Context

#### Context-Dependent Self-Descriptions (Mendoza-Denton, Ayduk, Mischel, Shoda, & Testa, 2001)
- Open-ended prompt ("I am a(n)... person"): Responses were **trait-like and global** (e.g., "I am an ambitious person").
- Situationally-cued prompt ("I am a(n)... when..."): Responses were **contingent on the situation** (e.g., "I am an ambitious person when a professor provides me with a challenge").

#### Baby Naming Trends and Individualism (Twenge, Abebe, & Campbell, 2010)
- Analysis of 325 million American babies born 1880-2007: Parents increasingly gave children **less common names**, especially after 1980.
- Reflects an increasing emphasis on **individualism** across the 20th-21st centuries.
- Corroborated by increasing endorsement of individualistic traits (Twenge, Konrath, Foster, Campbell, & Bushman, 2008).

#### Language and Self-Construal (Trafimow, Silverman, Fan, & Law, 1997)
- Bilingual Asian students in Hong Kong:
  - Asked "Who am I?" in **English**: described themselves in terms of personal traits differentiating them from others → **individualistic self-construal**.
  - Asked "Who am I?" in **Chinese**: described themselves in terms of group memberships → **interdependent self-construal**.
- **Self-construal:** How we characterize ourselves; varies depending on which identity is salient.

#### Gender Salience and Caring Responses (Ryan, David, & Reynolds, 2004)
- When participants categorized a person in need as a fellow university student (shared category): men and women were **equally** likely to show care-oriented responses.
- When participants categorized themselves in terms of gender: Women showed significantly *more* care-oriented responses; men *reduced* their care-oriented responses compared to the shared identity condition.
- Gender differences in behavior depend on gender being a **salient** category.

#### Gender, Self-Perception, and Comparison Type (Guimond et al., 2007)
- Five-nation study (France, Belgium, Malaysia, Netherlands, USA), N=950.
- **Intergroup comparison** (women vs. men): Women said they were more insecure; men said they were less insecure — consistent with gender stereotypes.
- **Intragroup comparison** (women vs. women; men vs. men): **No reliable gender difference** in perceived insecurity.
- Conclusion: How we see ourselves depends on the comparison we use when assessing ourselves.

#### When and Why Are Some Aspects of the Self More Salient?

Four factors determining salience of a self-aspect:
1. **Contextual relevance** — the self-aspect is especially relevant to the current context.
2. **Distinctiveness** — features of the context make one aspect distinctive (e.g., the only woman in an office dominated by men is likely to experience gender as highly salient; Fuegen & Biernat, 2002; Yoder & Berendsen, 2001).
3. **Personal importance** — some people are more "ready" to categorize themselves in terms of a particular trait or social identity because of its centrality to them (e.g., highly nationally-identified Americans are more reactive to national identity threat — Branscombe & Wann, 1994).
4. **Linguistic cuing by others** — how others refer to us activates personal vs. social identity:
   - **Nouns** (e.g., "woman," "student") → activate **social identities** (suggest discrete categories with shared essence).
   - **Adjectives/verbs** (e.g., "athletic," "taller," "supportive") → activate **personal identity level** (reference individual differences within a category).
   - Source: Simon (2004); Lickel, Hamilton, & Sherman (2001); Turner & Onorato (1999).

#### Emotional Consequences When Choices Are Made by Different Selves

> **KEY STUDY: LeBoeuf, Shafir & Bayuk (2010) — Salient Self Aspects and Consumer Choice**
> - **Study 1:** Made either the "Scholar" or "Socialite" aspect of student identity salient (via surveys about world issues or campus socializing). Participants then chose magazines. Scholar condition → chose scholarly publications (The Economist, WSJ); Socialite condition → chose social publications (Cosmopolitan, Sports Illustrated).
> - **Cross-cultural replication:** Chinese Americans primed with either Chinese identity (favorite Chinese holiday) or American identity (favorite American holiday). American identity → chose more unique car colors; Chinese identity → chose more traditional car colors.
> - **Study 3 (Regret):** Participants made film choices under Scholar or Socialite identity condition. Before watching, original or other identity was made salient. When identity at choice and at experience **matched**: positive experience, liked film, no regret. When identities were **inconsistent**: negative experience, disliked film, regretted choice.
> - **Conclusion:** Salient identity at time of choice vs. time of experience determines satisfaction and regret.

### Who I Am Depends on Others' Treatment

- Others' treatment and expected future treatment have important implications for self-concept (Tajfel, 1978).
- When facing expected rejection for some aspect of the self, response options include:
  1. **Change** the rejectable attribute (if possible).
  2. **Hide** the attribute from disapproving others (e.g., "Don't Ask Don't Tell" military policy).
  3. **Rebel** — emphasize that feature even more as a form of self-definition, publicly rejecting the rejecting group's standards.

> **KEY STUDY: Jetten, Branscombe, Schmitt & Spears (2001) — Body Piercings and Identity**
> - Young people with visible body piercings (navel, tongue, eyebrow) — identity markers communicating difference from mainstream.
> - People with piercings who were led to **expect rejection** from the mainstream identified **more strongly** with other people with body piercings than those led to expect acceptance.
> - Analogy: Like 1960s hippies wearing blue jeans/long hair as rebel identity markers. If the practice becomes mainstream, those conveying difference must become more extreme to achieve the same identity end.

## The Self Across Time: Past and Future Selves

- **Autobiographical memory:** Memory of ourselves in the past, sometimes over the life course as a whole (Wilson & Ross, 2001).
- Comparing present self to past self → feel good about ourselves to the extent we perceive **improvement over time**.

> **KEY STUDY: Ross & Wilson (2003) — Temporal Self-Appraisal**
> - People were asked to describe a past self perceived as either far in the past or relatively recent.
> - Criticism of the **distant** past self was greater than criticism of the **nearer** past self.
> - Derogating the distant past self allows us to feel good ("I've really grown").
> - When self-failure is perceived as **close** in time, current self is seen less positively than when that same failure is seen as distant.
> - Conclusion: We use temporal distance to protect self-esteem.

## Self-Control: Why It Can Be Difficult to Do

- The chapter addresses why self-control is difficult, though the specific content of this section from the text was not fully provided. Key concepts known from Baron & Branscombe Ch. 4:

- **Self-regulatory resource model** (Baumeister and colleagues): Self-control draws on a limited resource (sometimes metaphorically described as "willpower" or energy). As this resource is depleted by prior acts of self-control (a state called **ego depletion**), subsequent self-control becomes harder.
- **Ego depletion:** The state of reduced capacity for self-control following prior exertion of willpower. Self-control failures are most likely after prior demands on this resource.
- Self-control is required any time we must override an immediate impulse or desire in favor of a long-term goal.
- People with high self-control tend to achieve better outcomes (academic, interpersonal, health).

## Self-Esteem: Attitudes Toward Ourselves

- **Self-esteem:** The overall attitude we have toward ourselves — ranging from positive to negative. It is not just a trait; it encompasses evaluations of specific domains.
- Self-esteem can be thought of as a **sociometer** (Leary et al.) — an internal gauge of social acceptance/rejection. High self-esteem signals we are accepted; low self-esteem signals social rejection threat.

### The Measurement of Self-Esteem

- Most common measure: **Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale** (RSE; Rosenberg, 1965).
  - 10-item scale.
  - Items include: "On the whole, I am satisfied with myself," "I feel that I have a number of good qualities."
  - Rated on a 4-point Likert scale (strongly agree to strongly disagree).
  - Considered the gold standard for self-esteem measurement.
- **Implicit self-esteem** (vs. explicit/reported self-esteem): Measured using reaction time tasks (e.g., Implicit Association Test, IAT). Reflects automatic, unconscious evaluations of self.

---

### EMOTIONS AND THE SELF: Does Talking Positively to Ourselves Really Work?

- Common advice: Use **positive self-talk** (affirmations like "I am a lovable person") to boost self-esteem.
- Research question: Does this actually work?

> **KEY STUDY: Wood, Perunovic & Lee (2009) — Positive Self-Statements**
> - Participants with **low** self-esteem who repeated positive self-statements ("I am a lovable person") actually felt **worse** than low self-esteem participants who did not repeat the statement.
> - Participants with **high** self-esteem benefited modestly from the same statements.
> - Explanation: Positive self-statements that are highly discrepant from one's actual self-view trigger a **contrast effect** — the gap between the statement and actual self-view is highlighted, making low-SE individuals feel worse.
> - Implication: Positive self-statements may backfire for those who need them most.

### Is High Self-Esteem Always Beneficial?

- Widely assumed that high self-esteem is associated with positive outcomes. But the relationship is complex.
- **Benefits of high self-esteem:** Greater happiness, persistence in tasks, resilience.
- **Potential downsides:**
  - **Threatened egotism** (Baumeister, Smart, & Boden, 1996): Aggression can result from threats to high self-esteem, especially among those with **unstable** or **narcissistic** high self-esteem.
  - High self-esteem can co-occur with **narcissism** — inflated, unrealistic positive self-views that may drive interpersonal difficulties.
  - People with high self-esteem are not necessarily better liked by others than those with moderate self-esteem.
- **Contingent self-esteem:** Self-esteem that rises and falls based on outcomes in specific domains (e.g., academic performance). Associated with fragility and vulnerability to failure.
- **True/secure self-esteem** (Kernis): Non-contingent, stable self-esteem; associated with better psychological outcomes than contingent high self-esteem.

---

### Do Women and Men Differ in Their Levels of Self-Esteem?

- Meta-analyses (e.g., Kling, Hyde, Showers, & Buswell, 1999; Major, Barr, Zubek, & Babey, 1999):
  - Men tend to score **slightly higher** on self-esteem than women, but the difference is **small** (d ≈ 0.21).
  - The gap is larger for **global** self-esteem than for domain-specific self-esteem.
  - The gap is more pronounced in **adolescence** and **early adulthood**, then narrows.
- Gender differences in self-esteem are moderated by culture and domain: Men and women may differ on specific dimensions (e.g., appearance-related self-esteem vs. academic self-esteem) even when overall self-esteem is similar.
- The social context in which self-esteem is assessed matters: When gender identity is salient, gender differences may be amplified.

## Social Comparison: How We Evaluate Ourselves

- **Social comparison theory** (Festinger, 1954): People evaluate their opinions and abilities by comparing themselves to others, especially when objective standards are unavailable.
- Two primary types of comparison:
  1. **Upward social comparison:** Comparing ourselves to someone better off. Can be demoralizing OR inspiring, depending on whether the comparison target is seen as similar and attainable.
  2. **Downward social comparison:** Comparing ourselves to someone worse off. Typically boosts self-esteem and well-being (Wills, 1981).
- **Lateral comparison:** Comparing to someone similar (most common).

### Who Do We Compare Ourselves To?

- People prefer to compare with **similar others** (Festinger, 1954) — provides the most diagnostic information.
- When threatened, people often engage in **downward social comparison** to feel better (Wills, 1981).
- The target of comparison shapes the content of the self-description (as demonstrated by Guimond et al., 2007 above).

## Self-Serving Biases and Unrealistic Optimism

- **Self-serving bias:** The tendency to attribute positive outcomes to the self (internal causes) and negative outcomes to external factors.
  - Example: Success on an exam → "I am smart"; Failure → "The test was unfair."
- **Unrealistic optimism** (Weinstein, 1980): People believe they are more likely than others to experience positive events and less likely to experience negative events.
  - Example: Most people believe they are better-than-average drivers, less likely to get cancer, etc.
  - Logically impossible — not everyone can be above average.
  - Also called the **optimistic bias** or **better-than-average effect**.
- **Better-than-average effect (BTAE):** Most people rate themselves as above average on a wide range of positive attributes.
- **False uniqueness effect:** People underestimate the extent to which others share their positive qualities.
- **False consensus effect:** People overestimate the extent to which others share their attitudes, beliefs, and behaviors.

### Functions of Self-Serving Biases

- Protect and enhance self-esteem.
- Maintain motivation.
- May contribute to persistence and positive health outcomes.
- But: Can impede learning from mistakes, create interpersonal friction, and contribute to overconfidence.

## The Self as Target of Prejudice

### Emotional Consequences: How Well-Being Can Suffer

- **Stigma:** A deeply discrediting attribute that reduces the bearer from a whole person to a tainted, discounted one (Goffman, 1963).
- People who are targets of prejudice face a **double bind**:
  1. Internalizing the stigma can damage self-esteem.
  2. Attributing negative outcomes to prejudice can be protective, but may feel discounting.

- **Rejection-identification model** (Branscombe, Schmitt, & Harvey, 1999): 
  - Perceiving discrimination → increases identification with the stigmatized group → which in turn buffers well-being.
  - Despite higher perceived discrimination, those who identify more strongly with their stigmatized group show better psychological well-being.
  - High group identification acts as a **buffer** against the negative effects of discrimination.

- **Attributional ambiguity** (Crocker & Major, 1989): Targets of prejudice are uncertain whether negative feedback reflects their own performance or the evaluator's prejudice. This ambiguity can:
  - **Protect** self-esteem when negative feedback is attributed to prejudice rather than ability.
  - **Undermine** self-esteem when positive feedback is also dismissed ("they were just being nice because they pity me").

- **Emotional consequences** of being a target of prejudice include:
  - Chronic stress (vigilance for potential discrimination).
  - Anxiety, depression.
  - Reduced psychological well-being.
  - Internalized stigma.

### Behavioral Consequences: Stereotype Threat Effects on Performance

- **Stereotype threat** (Steele & Aronson, 1995): The concern that one will confirm a negative stereotype about one's group; this concern itself impairs performance in stereotype-relevant domains.
  - Originally demonstrated with African American students on verbal ability tests.
  - Activated by simply reminding people of the relevant group membership before a test.

> **KEY STUDY: Steele & Aronson (1995) — Stereotype Threat and Academic Performance**
> - African American and White students took a verbal test described either as a test of "intellectual ability" (stereotype-threat condition for Black students) or as a "laboratory task" (no stereotype threat).
> - African American students performed significantly worse than White students in the stereotype-threat condition, but performed comparably in the non-diagnostic condition.
> - Merely making race salient (asking participants to indicate their race before the test) was sufficient to impair African American students' performance.
> - **Conclusion:** The fear of confirming a negative stereotype (rather than lack of ability) can cause performance decrements.

- **Generality of stereotype threat:** Documented across many groups and domains:
  - Women in mathematics (Spencer, Steele, & Quinn, 1999).
  - White men in athletics when race stereotype was activated.
  - Elderly people on memory tasks.
  - Low-income students on intellectual tasks.

- **Mechanisms by which stereotype threat impairs performance:**
  1. **Cognitive load / Distraction:** Worrying about confirming the stereotype takes up working memory.
  2. **Physiological arousal:** Anxiety disrupts optimal performance.
  3. **Reduced effort/disengagement:** People may disengage from the domain to protect self-esteem.
  4. **Reduced sense of belonging** in the domain.

- **Reducing stereotype threat:**
  - Affirming a valued alternative identity before testing.
  - Framing a test as non-diagnostic of group differences.
  - Providing role models from one's group.
  - Teaching about stereotype threat itself (Aronson et al., 2002).

- **Disidentification:** Over time, individuals may psychologically disengage from domains in which their group is stereotyped — protecting self-esteem at the cost of achievement motivation in that domain.

| **Salience** | When something stands out from its background or is the focus of attention |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Vazire & Mehl (2008) EAR study:** The key finding is that accuracy of self vs. others is **domain-specific** — for some behaviors others predict us better; the method used digital audio recording (EAR) every 12.5 minutes for 4 days.

2. **Social Compensation vs. Social Enhancement Hypothesis:** Social Compensation = introverts/low SE use Facebook to compensate for poor offline social life; Social Enhancement = extroverts add to already-large network. Ellison et al. (2007) found stronger support for Social Compensation.

3. **Self-verification theory (Swann):** Even when self-views are negative, people prefer others to confirm those views. Exception: low-attractiveness self-views are NOT appreciated when confirmed by close others.

4. **Introspection failures:** Nisbett & Wilson (1977) — people lack conscious access to actual reasons for behavior. Wilson & Kraft (1993) — introspecting on reasons changes attitudes temporarily. Dunn et al. (2008) — people incorrectly predict that spending on themselves will make them happier than spending on others.

5. **Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1986):** Personal identity = intragroup comparison (how I differ from ingroup members); Social identity = intergroup comparison (how my group differs from another group). Salience determines which operates.

6. **Salience determinants (4 factors):** (1) Contextual relevance, (2) Distinctiveness in context, (3) Personal importance/readiness, (4) Linguistic cuing — nouns activate social identities; adjectives/verbs activate personal identity.

7. **Pronin & Ross (2006):** Past self is described in more trait/dispositional terms than present self, regardless of age. Taking an observer's perspective on the self leads to more trait-consistent self-perception.

8. **Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson, 1995):** The concern of confirming a negative group stereotype impairs performance on stereotype-relevant tasks. Even marking race on a form before a verbal test impaired Black students' performance.

9. **Rejection-identification model (Branscombe, Schmitt & Harvey, 1999):** Perceived discrimination → higher group identification → buffered psychological well-being. High identifiers feel better despite more perceived discrimination.

10. **Attributional ambiguity (Crocker & Major, 1989):** Stigmatized individuals are uncertain whether negative feedback is due to prejudice or own performance. This ambiguity can protect self-esteem but also undermines the meaning of positive feedback.

11. **Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale:** 10-item, Likert scale, gold standard measure of global self-esteem. Know the name and researcher (Rosenberg, 1965).

12. **Positive self-statements (Wood, Perunovic & Lee, 2009):** Repeating "I am a lovable person" makes low SE individuals feel *worse* (contrast effect), while high SE individuals benefit modestly.

13. **Downward vs. upward social comparison:** Downward → typically boosts self-esteem (Wills, 1981); Upward → can inspire or demoralize depending on perceived attainability of comparison target.

14. **Language and self-construal (Trafimow et al., 1997):** Bilingual Hong Kong students describe themselves more individualistically in English and more interdependently in Chinese — same person, different salient identity.

15. **Dunn, Aknin & Norton (2008) prosocial spending:** Spending on others produces significantly more happiness than spending on oneself, regardless of income level. Counter-intuitive and not predicted by introspection.
