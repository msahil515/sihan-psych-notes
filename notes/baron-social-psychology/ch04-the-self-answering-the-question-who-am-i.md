# 4 The Self: Answering the Question "Who Am I?"

This chapter covers how people understand, present, and evaluate themselves: methods of self-knowledge (introspection, observer perspective), the personal-social identity continuum, self-esteem, social comparison, and being a target of prejudice. Facebook serves as a running illustration of modern self-presentation.

---

## Self-Presentation: Managing the Self

- **Self-presentation**: managing the image we project to different audiences in different contexts.
- Shakespeare: "All the world's a stage" captures playing different roles for different audiences.
- On social networking sites (SNS) like Facebook, self-presentation is highly visible: users control what is shared, with whom, and how.
- Key tension: how much can we control what others learn, and how accurately do impressions reflect us?

### Self-Other Accuracy in Predicting Behavior

- Intuition: we know ourselves best because only we access our **internal mental states** (Pronin & Kruger, 2007; Wilson & Dunn, 2004).
- Paradox (Pronin, Kruger, Savitsky & Ross, 2001): we believe we know ourselves better than others do, AND that we know some others better than they know themselves.
- Problem: self-reports are biased; knowing our intentions biases self-perception ("I mean to be on time" → "I usually am").

> **KEY STUDY: Vazire & Mehl (2008), EAR (Electronically Activated Recorder)**
> - **Method:** Participants wore a recorder capturing ambient sound every ~12.5 minutes for 4 days; assistants coded behaviors.
> - **Measures:** self-ratings, three informants' ratings (friends, parents, partners), and actual coded frequencies.
> - **Finding:** Mixed. Self-ratings were sometimes more accurate (talking to same sex, watching TV, music); informants sometimes more accurate (being with others, attending class). Aggregated informant ratings often beat single informants.
> - **Conclusion:** Self-other accuracy is **domain-specific**.

- **Marcus, Machilek, & Schütz (2006):** self-other agreement was *higher* for Web-based than real-world interactions, likely because people control web-page information completely.

### SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: Does Facebook Change Offline Behavior?

#### Two Hypotheses (Zywicka & Danowski, 2008)
1. **Social Compensation Hypothesis:** introverts/socially anxious people use Facebook to substitute for an undesirable offline life (Caplan, 2005).
2. **Social Enhancement Hypothesis:** extroverts add online contacts to an already large network, reflecting their positive self-view (Valkenburg, Schouten, & Peter, 2005).
- Both received some support.

#### Social Capital (Ellison, Steinfield, & Lampe, 2007)
- **Social capital** = the number of social ties a person has (connections drawn on for knowledge, assistance, social goods).
- Stronger support for **Social Compensation**: those lower in life satisfaction and self-esteem developed *more* social capital via Facebook.
- Initially less-skilled users *increased* self-esteem with more Facebook use; both high and low self-esteem users find Facebook desirable, for different reasons.

#### Internet and the "True Self" (Bargh, McKenna & Fitzsimons, 2002)
- Shy, less skilled people express their **"true self"** better online than face-to-face. Introverts find it online; extroverts find it face-to-face (Amichai-Hamburger et al., 2002).

#### Possible Selves and Offline Transfer (Joinson, 2003; Sheeks & Birchmeier, 2007)
- Facebook acceptance can activate a **"possible self"** as popular and socially skilled, fostering higher offline self-esteem.
- **Sheeks & Birchmeier (2007):** shy, anxious people gained social skills online that *transferred* to real life. Cyber-optimists are right: online experience reduces the skills gap offline.

## Self-Presentation Tactics

- **Self-promotion:** presenting ourselves as having positive attributes (intelligence, awards, degrees). Claiming a skill often convinces others and ourselves.
- **Self-verification perspective** (Swann, 2005): leading others to agree with our self-views. We prefer others, especially close others, to see us as we see ourselves, even if *negative* (Swann & Bosson, 2010; Chen, Chen, & Shaw, 2004). **Limit:** people fearing low attractiveness do NOT want close others to verify it.
- **Ingratiation:** making others like us by conveying that we like them; effective unless overdone (seems insincere, Vonk, 1999).
- **Self-deprecating:** putting ourselves down, to communicate admiration or lower expectations.

### Honesty in Self-Presentation
- College students report lying about **twice a day** (Kashy & DePaulo, 1996), often to advance interests, sometimes to protect others; those who lie more are *more popular*.
- Online (Ellison, Heino, & Gibbs, 2006): people balance authenticity with "self-deceptive white lies"; profiles reflect the **ideal self** rather than the actual self.

## Self-Knowledge: Determining Who We Are

Two methods: (1) introspection, (2) the observer's perspective on the self.

---

### Introspection

- **Introspection:** privately contemplating "who we are." Widely recommended, but is it the best route to accurate self-knowledge?

#### Limits of Introspection
1. **Lack of conscious access (Nisbett & Wilson, 1977):** people don't always know the actual causes of their actions; they generate plausible but inaccurate "theories" afterward.
2. **Attitude change (Wilson & Kraft, 1993):** introspecting on reasons for feelings (romantic partners, jams) **temporarily changed attitudes** to match stated reasons, while original feelings persisted.
3. **Affective forecasting failures (Gilbert & Wilson, 2000):** focusing only on a negative event predicts feeling *much worse* than reality; for positive events, predicting *much higher* happiness. **Focalism:** focusing on one event, neglecting other life factors.

> **KEY STUDY: Dunn, Aknin & Norton (2008), Prosocial Spending**
> - **Survey:** personal spending was unrelated to happiness; spending on others *predicted* greater happiness, regardless of income.
> - **Experiment:** students given $5 or $20 spent it on themselves or on others by 5 PM. Those who spent on *others* were significantly happier, regardless of amount.
> - **Introspection failure:** new participants wrongly predicted spending on themselves and $20 (vs. $5) would bring more happiness.
> - **Conclusion:** we often don't know how events will affect us, and introspection won't help.

4. **Mental subtraction (Koo, Algoe, Wilson, & Gilbert, 2008):** imagining how a positive outcome might *not* have happened makes people *happier* than recalling the positive outcome, counterintuitively.

#### When Introspection Helps
- When behavior is based on **conscious decision-making**, introspection can yield accurate judgments. It fails when causes are emotional or unconscious.

---

### The Self from the Other's Standpoint

- **Actors** attend outward → more situational attributions; **observers** focus on the actor → more dispositional. Taking an observer's view of ourselves → more trait-based self-description.

> **KEY STUDY: Pronin & Ross (2006), Observer Perspective**
> - **Study 1:** participants described their past self (5 years ago) using *more trait terms* than their current self, regardless of age.
> - **Study 2:** "method-acting" (feel as if you ARE the person) vs. "standard-acting" (appear to others), enacting a family dinner at age 14. Observer perspective → more consistent traits; method-acting → fewer.
> - **Conclusion:** to gain self-insight, see ourselves as others do.

## Personal versus Social Identity

### Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1986)

- **Social identity theory:** how we respond when group identity is salient; we perceive ourselves along the **personal-versus-social identity continuum**.
- **Continuum:** personal end = unique individual; social end = group member.
- **Salience:** when something stands out or is the focus of attention; the salient identity shapes self-perception.

#### Personal Identity
- Emphasis on how we differ from individuals; self-descriptions involve **intragroup comparisons**.
- Example: "liberal" compared to parents, "conservative" compared to other students.

#### Social Identity
- Emphasis on what we share with group members; self-descriptions involve **intergroup comparisons**.
- Self-stereotyping: attributing group-typical traits to oneself (e.g., women describing themselves as warm and caring when gender is salient).

#### Key Principle
- No single "true" self; all self-definitions can be accurate depending on context and comparison (Oakes & Reynolds, 1997). Coherence is maintained by deeming inconsistent domains unimportant or not co-salient.

---

### Self Depends on Social Context

- **Context-dependent descriptions (Mendoza-Denton et al., 2001):** "I am a(n)... person" → **trait-like, global**; "I am a(n)... when..." → **situation-contingent**.
- **Baby naming (Twenge, Abebe, & Campbell, 2010):** analysis of 325 million babies (1880-2007) showed less common names, especially after 1980, reflecting increasing **individualism**.
- **Language and self-construal (Trafimow et al., 1997):** bilingual Hong Kong students asked "Who am I?" in **English** described personal traits (**individualistic self-construal**); in **Chinese** described group memberships (**interdependent self-construal**). **Self-construal:** how we characterize ourselves, varying with salient identity.
- **Gender salience (Ryan, David, & Reynolds, 2004):** when categorized as fellow students, men and women showed equal care-oriented responses; when gender was salient, women showed *more* and men *less*. Gender differences depend on gender being **salient**.
- **Comparison type (Guimond et al., 2007):** five-nation study (N=950). **Intergroup** (women vs. men): women more insecure, men less (stereotype-consistent). **Intragroup** (women vs. women): **no reliable gender difference**. Self-perception depends on the comparison used.

#### Four Factors Determining Salience
1. **Contextual relevance.**
2. **Distinctiveness** (e.g., the only woman in a male office; Fuegen & Biernat, 2002).
3. **Personal importance** (e.g., highly nationally-identified Americans react more to national threat; Branscombe & Wann, 1994).
4. **Linguistic cuing:** **nouns** ("woman," "student") activate **social identities**; **adjectives/verbs** ("athletic," "taller") activate **personal identity** (Simon, 2004; Turner & Onorato, 1999).

> **KEY STUDY: LeBoeuf, Shafir & Bayuk (2010), Salient Self and Choice**
> - **Study 1:** priming "Scholar" vs. "Socialite" identity → scholar chose The Economist/WSJ; socialite chose Cosmopolitan/Sports Illustrated.
> - **Replication:** Chinese Americans primed Chinese vs. American identity → American identity chose unique car colors; Chinese identity chose traditional colors.
> - **Study 3 (regret):** when identity at choice matched identity at experience → liked the film, no regret; when **inconsistent** → disliked it, regret.
> - **Conclusion:** the salient identity at choice vs. experience determines satisfaction and regret.

---

### Self Depends on Others' Treatment

- Others' treatment shapes self-concept (Tajfel, 1978). Facing expected rejection, options are:
  1. **Change** the attribute, 2. **Hide** it (e.g., "Don't Ask Don't Tell"), 3. **Rebel** (emphasize it more, rejecting the group's standards).

> **KEY STUDY: Jetten, Branscombe, Schmitt & Spears (2001), Body Piercings**
> - People with visible piercings led to **expect rejection** identified **more strongly** with other pierced people than those expecting acceptance.
> - Analogy: 1960s hippies. If a difference marker becomes mainstream, those conveying difference must become more extreme.

## The Self Across Time

- **Autobiographical memory:** memory of ourselves in the past (Wilson & Ross, 2001). We feel good to the extent we perceive **improvement over time**.

> **KEY STUDY: Ross & Wilson (2003), Temporal Self-Appraisal**
> - Criticism of the **distant** past self exceeded criticism of the **nearer** past self. Derogating the distant past self lets us feel "I've grown." A failure seen as **close** lowers current self-view more than the same failure seen as distant.
> - **Conclusion:** we use temporal distance to protect self-esteem.

## Self-Control

- **Self-regulatory resource model** (Baumeister): self-control draws on a limited resource ("willpower"). Depletion of it (**ego depletion**) makes later self-control harder.
- **Ego depletion:** reduced capacity for self-control following prior willpower exertion.
- Self-control is needed to override impulses for long-term goals; high self-control predicts better academic, interpersonal, and health outcomes.

---

## Self-Esteem

- **Self-esteem:** the overall attitude toward ourselves (positive to negative), including domain-specific evaluations.
- **Sociometer** (Leary): self-esteem as an internal gauge of social acceptance/rejection.

### Measurement
- **Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale** (RSE; Rosenberg, 1965): 10 items (e.g., "On the whole, I am satisfied with myself"), 4-point Likert; gold standard.
- **Implicit self-esteem** (vs. explicit): reaction-time tasks (e.g., IAT); automatic, unconscious self-evaluations.

---

### EMOTIONS AND THE SELF: Does Positive Self-Talk Work?

> **KEY STUDY: Wood, Perunovic & Lee (2009), Positive Self-Statements**
> - **Low** self-esteem participants repeating "I am a lovable person" felt **worse** than those who didn't; **high** self-esteem participants benefited modestly.
> - Explanation: statements discrepant from one's self-view trigger a **contrast effect**.
> - Implication: positive self-statements may backfire for those who need them most.

---

### Is High Self-Esteem Always Beneficial?

- **Benefits:** greater happiness, persistence, resilience.
- **Downsides:**
  - **Threatened egotism** (Baumeister, Smart, & Boden, 1996): aggression from threats to high self-esteem, especially **unstable** or **narcissistic** self-esteem.
  - High self-esteem can co-occur with **narcissism**; high-SE people are not necessarily better liked.
- **Contingent self-esteem:** rises/falls with outcomes in specific domains; fragile, vulnerable to failure.
- **True/secure self-esteem** (Kernis): non-contingent, stable; better outcomes than contingent high self-esteem.

---

### Gender Differences in Self-Esteem

- Meta-analyses (Kling, Hyde, Showers, & Buswell, 1999; Major et al., 1999):
  - Men score **slightly higher**, but the difference is **small** (d ≈ 0.21).
  - Larger for **global** than domain-specific self-esteem.
  - More pronounced in **adolescence/early adulthood**, then narrows.
- Moderated by culture and domain; salient gender identity can amplify differences.

## Social Comparison

- **Social comparison theory** (Festinger, 1954): we evaluate opinions and abilities by comparing to others, especially when objective standards are absent.
- **Upward social comparison:** comparing to someone better off; demoralizing or inspiring depending on perceived similarity/attainability.
- **Downward social comparison:** comparing to someone worse off; typically boosts self-esteem (Wills, 1981).
- **Lateral comparison:** comparing to a similar other (most common; most diagnostic, Festinger, 1954).
- When threatened, people engage in downward comparison (Wills, 1981).

---

## Self-Serving Biases and Unrealistic Optimism

- **Self-serving bias:** attributing positive outcomes internally, negative outcomes externally ("I'm smart" vs. "the test was unfair").
- **Unrealistic optimism / optimistic bias** (Weinstein, 1980): believing oneself more likely than others to experience positive events, less likely negative ones (logically impossible for all). Also the **better-than-average effect**.
- **Better-than-average effect (BTAE):** most people rate themselves above average on positive attributes.
- **False uniqueness effect:** underestimating how much others share one's positive qualities.
- **False consensus effect:** overestimating how much others share one's attitudes/behaviors.

### Functions
- Protect/enhance self-esteem, maintain motivation, aid persistence and health. But: impede learning from mistakes, create friction, foster overconfidence.

## The Self as Target of Prejudice

### Emotional Consequences

- **Stigma:** a deeply discrediting attribute reducing a person to a tainted identity (Goffman, 1963).
- Targets face a **double bind**: internalizing stigma damages self-esteem; attributing outcomes to prejudice is protective but can feel discounting.
- **Rejection-identification model** (Branscombe, Schmitt, & Harvey, 1999): perceiving discrimination → increased group identification → buffered well-being. High identifiers feel better despite more perceived discrimination.
- **Attributional ambiguity** (Crocker & Major, 1989): uncertainty whether negative feedback reflects performance or prejudice. Can **protect** self-esteem (blame prejudice) but **undermine** it (dismiss positive feedback as pity).
- Emotional consequences: chronic stress/vigilance, anxiety, depression, reduced well-being, internalized stigma.

---

### Behavioral Consequences: Stereotype Threat

- **Stereotype threat** (Steele & Aronson, 1995): the concern of confirming a negative group stereotype, which itself impairs performance in relevant domains. Activated by reminding people of group membership.

> **KEY STUDY: Steele & Aronson (1995), Stereotype Threat**
> - African American and White students took a verbal test framed as "intellectual ability" (threat) or "laboratory task" (no threat).
> - Black students performed worse in the threat condition but comparably otherwise. Merely indicating race before the test was enough to impair their performance.
> - **Conclusion:** fear of confirming a stereotype (not lack of ability) causes performance decrements.

- **Generality:** women in math (Spencer, Steele, & Quinn, 1999), White men in athletics, elderly on memory, low-income students.
- **Mechanisms:** (1) cognitive load/distraction (uses working memory), (2) physiological arousal, (3) reduced effort/disengagement, (4) reduced belonging.
- **Reducing it:** affirming an alternative identity, framing tests as non-diagnostic, providing role models, teaching about stereotype threat (Aronson et al., 2002).
- **Disidentification:** psychological disengagement from stereotyped domains, protecting self-esteem at the cost of achievement motivation.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Vazire & Mehl (2008) EAR study:** self-other accuracy is **domain-specific**; EAR recorded every 12.5 minutes for 4 days.
2. **Social Compensation vs. Social Enhancement:** Compensation = introverts/low-SE compensate; Enhancement = extroverts add to a large network. Ellison et al. (2007) supported Compensation.
3. **Self-verification (Swann):** people prefer others to confirm self-views even when negative; exception is low-attractiveness self-views.
4. **Introspection failures:** Nisbett & Wilson (1977, no conscious access); Wilson & Kraft (1993, attitude change); Dunn et al. (2008, mispredict prosocial spending).
5. **Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1986):** personal = intragroup comparison; social = intergroup comparison; salience determines which.
6. **Four salience determinants:** contextual relevance, distinctiveness, personal importance, linguistic cuing (nouns → social; adjectives/verbs → personal).
7. **Pronin & Ross (2006):** past self described in more trait terms; observer perspective → trait-consistent self-perception.
8. **Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson, 1995):** concern of confirming a stereotype impairs performance; marking race before a test impaired Black students.
9. **Rejection-identification model (Branscombe, Schmitt & Harvey, 1999):** discrimination → group identification → buffered well-being.
10. **Attributional ambiguity (Crocker & Major, 1989):** uncertainty about prejudice vs. performance; protects self-esteem but undermines positive feedback.
11. **Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale:** 10-item Likert, gold standard (Rosenberg, 1965).
12. **Positive self-statements (Wood, Perunovic & Lee, 2009):** make low-SE people feel *worse* (contrast effect); high-SE benefit modestly.
13. **Downward vs. upward comparison:** downward boosts self-esteem (Wills, 1981); upward inspires or demoralizes by attainability.
14. **Language and self-construal (Trafimow et al., 1997):** English → individualistic; Chinese → interdependent.
15. **Dunn, Aknin & Norton (2008):** spending on others brings more happiness regardless of income; not predicted by introspection.
