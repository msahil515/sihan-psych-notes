# 11 Groups and Individuals: The Consequences of Belonging

Groups shape identity, performance, decision making, and well-being. This chapter covers why people join and leave groups, how the presence of others alters performance, cooperation and conflict, fairness judgments, group decision making (including groupthink and polarization), and effective leadership.

---

## Groups: When We Join and When We Leave

### Defining a Group
- A **group** is a collection of people who perceive themselves as part of a coherent unit different from other groups (Dasgupta, Banaji, & Abelson, 1999). Key criterion: *perceived coherence*, not proximity (an elevator vs. a sports team).

### Types of Groups

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Common-bond groups** | Face-to-face; members bonded to *each other* | Sports teams, friendship groups |
| **Common-identity groups** | Linked via the *category as a whole*; face-to-face often absent | National, linguistic, gender groups |

### Entitativity
- **Entitativity** (Campbell, 1958): the extent to which a group is perceived as a coherent whole. Correlates strongly with importance to members (r = .75; Lickel et al., 2000).
- **High-entitativity groups:** members interact frequently, the group is important, members share goals, members are similar.
- High-entitativity groups are more **stereotyped** (Yzerbyt et al., 2001) and described with *abstract* language (Spencer-Rodgers, Hamilton, & Sherman, 2007). Behavioral features (resource sharing, norm adherence) matter more than size (Lickel et al., 2006).

#### Entitativity Ratings (Lickel et al., 2000)

### Evolutionary Basis
- Brewer & Caporael (2006): interdependence is the *primary survival strategy*. Schachter (1959): emotion drives social comparison. Hogg (2007): under threat/uncertainty, people identify more with groups. Social connectedness is among the best predictors of well-being (Diener & Oishi, 2005).

## Key Components of Groups: Status, Roles, Norms, Cohesiveness

### Status
- **Status**: rank/position within a group, conferring respect, deference, material benefits, and (evolutionarily) reproductive advantages (Buss, 1999).
- **Determinants**: physical attributes (taller people earn more, seen as leaders; Judge & Cable, 2004), **prototypicality** (Haslam & Platow, 2001), longevity/seniority.
- High-status members behave more idiosyncratically and report conforming *less* than low-status members (Guinote et al., 2002; Jetten, Hornsey, & Adarves-Yorno, 2006). Low-status newcomers conform more.

### Roles
- **Roles**: behaviors expected of those in specific group positions; formal or informal.
- Bettencourt et al. (2006): when traits matched the assigned role, behavior felt more **authentic**, with better mood and enjoyment.

> **KEY STUDY: Simulated Prison Study (Reicher & Haslam, 2006)**
> - Guards *failed to identify* with their role (worried about being liked), didn't impose authority, and were overcome by prisoners.
> - Prisoners showed *increasing identification*, developed rebellion norms, and showed less depression. Guards showed burnout and cortisol reactivity.
> - Role effects are powerful mainly when roles are *internalized*.

### Norms
- **Norms**: implicit/explicit rules about how members should behave.
- **Feeling rules** (Hochschild, 1983): expectations about appropriate emotions (e.g., flight attendants must smile).
- Wilkins (2008): new Evangelical converts learned to model emotions; being happy = moral goodness.
- **Collectivism** (maintain harmony) vs. **individualism** (stand out).

> **KEY STUDY: Hornsey, Jetten, McAuliffe, & Hogg (2006), Dissent and Norms**
> - **High identifiers** liked a dissenter under an individualist norm, disliked one under a collectivist norm. Low identifiers were unaffected.
> - Violating norms is most costly when others highly value the group.

### Cohesiveness
- **Cohesiveness**: all forces that cause members to remain in the group (Ellemers, de Gilder, & Haslam, 2004); brings solidarity, support, high morale, and better performance.
- Outgroup/competitive threat increases cohesion (Putnam, 2000); wartime increases support for in-group leaders (Landau et al., 2004). Perceived threat to group distinctiveness/future → collective anxiety → protective actions (Wohl, Branscombe et al., 2010, 2011).

## The Benefits and Costs of Joining

### Benefits
1. **Self-knowledge** (Tajfel & Turner, 1986). Ostracism, even online, lowers control and self-esteem (Zadro, Boland, & Richardson, 2006), worse for socially anxious people (Williams, 2001).
2. **Goal attainment**; self-enhancers value the group's status more (Roccas, 2003).
3. **Power match**: people prefer groups matching their goal orientation (Sassenberg et al., 2007).
4. **Social change via politicized collective identity**: recognizing shared grievances and engaging in a power struggle for one's devalued group (Branscombe, Schmitt, & Harvey, 1999; Simon & Klandermans, 2001).

### Costs of Acceptance: Initiation

> **KEY STUDY: Aronson & Mills (1959), Initiation Severity**
> - Students read very embarrassing, mildly embarrassing, or no material before "joining" a group.
> - Those with the most severe initiation rated the (deliberately boring) group more attractive.
> - **Cognitive dissonance/effort justification**: liking increases with initiation severity.

### Costs of Membership
1. Loss of personal freedom (norm compliance).
2. Demands on time, energy, resources (e.g., tithing).
3. Mandatory silence or exit on disagreed positions.

### Why Groups Splinter: Schism
- **Ideology**: a group's philosophical/political values.
- **Schism**: splintering into factions after an ideological rift (Sani, 2005, 2009).

> **KEY STUDY: Church of England Women Priests (Sani, 2005)**
> - After women's ordination (1994), hundreds left. Leavers felt the change subverted doctrine; distress resembled bereavement.
> - Model: ideological change → perceived subversion of identity → distress + reduced entitativity/identification → schism.

## Effects of the Presence of Others

## Social Facilitation

- **Social facilitation**: effects of others' presence (audience/co-actors) on performance. Triplett (1898): cyclists rode faster with companions (earliest social psychology experiment).

### Drive Theory (Zajonc, 1965)
- Presence of others → increased **arousal** → strengthened **dominant responses**. Correct dominant responses (well-learned task) → **enhanced** performance; incorrect (novel/complex) → **impaired**.

> **KEY STUDY: Cockroach Maze (Zajonc, Heingartner, & Herman, 1969)**
> - Cockroaches ran faster with a "roach audience." Since roaches cannot worry about being judged, mere presence alone is sufficient.

### Evaluation Apprehension (Cottrell, Wack, Sekerak, & Rittle, 1968)
- **Evaluation apprehension**: concern over being judged increases arousal. Facilitation did NOT occur when the audience was blindfolded/uninterested.

### Distraction Conflict Theory (Baron, 1986)
- Social facilitation from **conflict** between attending to the task and to others → **narrowed attentional focus**.

> **KEY STUDY: Distraction and Eating (Hetherington et al., 2006)**
> - Eating with friends or while watching TV (distracting) increased intake; eating with strangers (less distracting) did not.

| Theory | Mechanism | Researcher |
|---|---|---|
| Drive Theory | Presence → arousal → dominant responses | Zajonc (1965) |
| Evaluation Apprehension | Fear of judgment → arousal | Cottrell et al. (1968) |
| Distraction Conflict | Competing attention → narrowed focus | Baron (1986) |

## Social Loafing

- **Social loafing**: reduced effort when working collectively vs. individually (Karau & Williams, 1993), on **additive tasks**.

> **KEY STUDY: Latané, Williams, & Harkins (1979), Clapping/Shouting**
> - Students clapped/cheered in groups of 1, 2, 4, or 6. Total noise rose with size, but *individual output fell*. First systematic loafing demonstration.

**Why it occurs:** dispensability, perceived unfairness, dissimilarity (Price, Harrison, & Gavin, 2006).

**Reducing it:** make output **identifiable** (Williams, Harkins, & Latané, 1981), increase commitment, increase task importance, provide performance standards, use peer feedback (Aggarwal & O'Brien, 2008). Englehart (2006): larger classes → less participation.

## Effects of Being in a Crowd

### Deindividuation
- **Deindividuation**: reduced personal identity and concern with evaluation in a crowd, lowering restraint on normally inhibited behavior (hooliganism, mob violence).
- **Produced by:** anonymity, diffusion of responsibility, high arousal, outward focus.

### SIDE Model (Reicher, Spears, & Postmes, 1995)
- Deindividuation does not erase norms; people shift from personal to **social identity**, and behavior follows the **salient group's norms**. Explains why crowd behavior is *patterned*, not random.

### Emergent Norm Theory (Turner & Killian, 1972)
- In ambiguous crowds, new norms emerge and then exert conformity pressure, giving crowds a consistent character.

## Coordination: Cooperation or Conflict?

### SOCIAL LIFE IN A CONNECTED WORLD: CMC
- **Computer-mediated communication (CMC)**: digital interaction (email, video, social media).
- Effects: increases participation from inhibited/lower-status members; reduces status cues; reduces social presence (can increase loafing); anonymity can increase flaming (online deindividuation). Electronic brainstorming often outperforms face-to-face (less production blocking).

### Cooperation
- **Cooperation**: working with others toward shared goals.
- **Prisoner's dilemma**: each individual's most rewarding choice is to defect, but mutual defection is worse than mutual cooperation; people often defect even when cooperation benefits both.
- **Factors promoting cooperation**: communication, reciprocity (tit-for-tat; Axelrod, 1984), group identity, trust, perceived fairness, interdependence.
- **Commons dilemma** ("tragedy of the commons"): self-interest collectively depletes a shared resource (overfishing, pollution); solved by regulation, privatization, or norms.

### Resolving Conflicts
- **Conflict**: perceiving goals/interests as incompatible; escalates via threats and reciprocal retaliation.
- **Techniques:** **superordinate goals** (Sherif, 1954/1961; Robbers Cave); **GRIT** (Graduated and Reciprocated Initiatives in Tension Reduction; Osgood, 1962); negotiation/mediation; **contact hypothesis** (Allport, 1954); perspective taking; attribution retraining.

## Perceived Fairness in Groups

People care about *what* they receive and *how* decisions are made.

### Distributive Justice
- **Distributive justice**: fairness of *outcomes*. Western default = **equity** (outcomes proportional to inputs); other rules: **equality** (same for all), **need**. Violations → anger/resentment.

### Procedural Justice
- **Procedural justice**: fairness of the *processes*. People accept unfavorable outcomes if the process was fair.
- Components: **voice** (express views), **consistency**, **accuracy**, **ethicality**. Thibaut & Walker (1975, legal); Leventhal (1980, organizational).

### Transactional/Interactional Justice
- **Transactional/interactional justice**: fairness of interpersonal treatment during implementation. Two components: **interpersonal justice** (dignity/respect) and **informational justice** (adequate explanations) (Bies & Moag, 1986).
- Perceived injustice → anger, reduced cooperation, retaliation. Even monkeys protest unequal treatment (de Waal & Brosnan, 2003).

## EMOTIONS AND GROUPS: Perceiving Intergroup Rejection

- **Rejection sensitivity**: heightened vigilance to rejection from important groups.
- Intergroup rejection → in-group cohesion, hostility toward the rejecting outgroup, motivation for collective action.
- **Rejection-identification model** (Branscombe et al.): more perceived discrimination → more in-group identification → buffered well-being.
- **Collective emotions**: felt as a group member (collective guilt, anger, pride); motivate collective action.

## Decision Making by Groups

### Social Decision Schemes
- **Social decision schemes**: rules for combining individual preferences.

| Scheme | Description |
|---|---|
| **Majority-wins** | Group adopts the majority position |
| **Truth-wins** | A correct answer carries the group once stated |
| **First-shift rule** | Group moves toward the first opinion shift |
| **Unanimity rule** | Discussion continues until all agree |

- Most tasks follow **majority-wins**; tasks with a correct answer follow **truth-wins**.

### Information Sharing
- **Shared information bias** (Stasser & Titus, 1985, "hidden profile"): groups over-discuss already-**shared** information and miss critical **unshared** information → suboptimal decisions.

## The Downside of Group Decision Making

### Group Polarization
- **Group polarization**: discussion shifts the average opinion toward a more extreme version of the initially favored direction (**risky shift** or **cautious shift**).
- Mechanisms: **persuasive arguments theory** (novel pro-attitudinal arguments) and **social comparison theory** (wanting to hold the "right," more extreme position).

### Groupthink
- **Groupthink** (Janis, 1972): desire for harmony/conformity in a cohesive group overrides realistic appraisal → poor decisions.
- **Conditions:** high cohesiveness, group insulation, directive leadership, high stress, lack of methodical procedures.

**Eight symptoms (Janis, 1972):**
1. Illusion of invulnerability.
2. Collective rationalization.
3. Belief in inherent morality.
4. Stereotyping outgroups.
5. Pressure on dissenters.
6. Self-censorship.
7. Illusion of unanimity.
8. Self-appointed mindguards.

- **Examples:** Bay of Pigs (1961), Challenger (1986), Pearl Harbor (1941), Vietnam escalation.
- **Prevention:** leader withholds preferences, use a devil's advocate, invite outside experts, hold a "second chance" meeting, subdivide the group, encourage doubts.

### Do Groups Make Riskier Decisions?
- Not inherently. Groups **polarize** in whichever direction members initially lean; not always riskier.

## Leadership

- **Leadership**: the process by which one individual influences, motivates, and directs others.

### Theories
**Great Person Theory (trait approach):** leaders possess special innate traits (intelligence, dominance, self-confidence, energy, task knowledge); modest but consistent correlations; traits alone insufficient.

**Fiedler's Contingency Model (1978):** stable style, **task-oriented** (low LPC) or **relationship-oriented** (high LPC), measured by the **Least Preferred Co-Worker (LPC) scale**. Effectiveness depends on *situational control*:
- High control → task-oriented (low LPC) best.
- Moderate control → relationship-oriented (high LPC) best.
- Low control → task-oriented (low LPC) best.

**Path-Goal Theory (House, 1971):** leaders clarify paths to goals and remove obstacles; four styles (directive, supportive, participative, achievement-oriented), matched to followers and task.

**Transformational vs. Transactional:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Transactional leadership** | Exchange-based: rewards/punishments; maintains status quo |
| **Transformational leadership** | Inspires followers beyond self-interest; vision, intellectual stimulation, individual consideration; produces change |

- **Transformational** (Burns, 1978; Bass, 1985): components are charisma/idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, individualized consideration; more effective for sustained change.
- **Charismatic leaders**: inspire devotion; positive (Gandhi, MLK) or destructive (cult leaders).
- **Social identity theory of leadership** (Hogg, 2001): in cohesive groups, the most **prototypical** member is most likely chosen as leader ("one of us").

### Gender and Leadership
- **"Think manager, think male"** stereotype (Schein, 1973). **Role incongruity** (Eagly & Karau, 2002): women evaluated less favorably for agentic styles. Transformational leadership (communal qualities) tends to favor women.

### Leadership and Power
- **Idiosyncrasy credit** (Hollander, 1958): conformity credits let leaders later deviate without losing influence. Power can corrupt: focus on own goals, take resources, objectify others (Keltner et al., 2003).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Entitativity** (Campbell, 1958): perceived group coherence; correlates with importance (r = .75; Lickel et al., 2000).
2. **Drive theory (Zajonc, 1965)**: presence → arousal → dominant responses; well-learned tasks improve, novel tasks worsen.
3. **Evaluation apprehension (Cottrell et al., 1968)** vs. Zajonc's cockroach study (1969): facilitation can occur WITHOUT evaluation apprehension.
4. **Social loafing (Karau & Williams, 1993)**: on additive tasks; first shown by Latané, Williams, & Harkins (1979); reduced by identifiability, importance, standards.
5. **Groupthink (Janis, 1972)**: 8 symptoms; fostered by high cohesiveness + directive leadership + insulation.
6. **Group polarization**: shifts toward a more extreme initial tendency (risky OR cautious); persuasive arguments + social comparison.
7. **Aronson & Mills (1959)**: severe initiation → greater liking (effort justification/dissonance).
8. **Sani (2005)**: ideological change → perceived subversion → distress → schism (bereavement-like).
9. **Reicher & Haslam (2006)**: role effects depend on internalization; non-identifying guards lost control.
10. **Three justices**: distributive (outcomes), procedural (process: voice, consistency, accuracy, ethicality), transactional/interactional (treatment, explanations).
11. **Transformational leadership** (Burns, 1978; Bass, 1985): vision, stimulation, individual consideration; more effective than transactional.
12. **SIDE Model (Reicher, Spears, & Postmes, 1995)**: deindividuation shifts to social identity; behavior follows group norms.
13. **Shared information bias (Stasser & Titus, 1985)**: groups under-use unshared information (hidden profile).
14. **Fiedler's contingency model**: task-oriented (low LPC) best at high AND low control; relationship-oriented (high LPC) best at moderate control.
15. **Common-bond vs. common-identity groups**: face-to-face/bonded (sports team) vs. category-linked (national/gender).
