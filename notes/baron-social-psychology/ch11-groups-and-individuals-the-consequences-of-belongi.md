# 11 Groups and Individuals: The Consequences of Belonging

Groups are an inescapable feature of human life, shaping identity, performance, decision making, and well-being. This chapter examines why people join and leave groups, how the mere presence of others alters performance, how groups cooperate and conflict, how fairness is judged, how groups make decisions (including pitfalls like groupthink and group polarization), and what makes an effective leader.

---

## Groups: When We Join . . . and When We Leave

### Defining a Group

- A **group** is a collection of people who perceive themselves to be part of a coherent unit that is perceived as different from other groups (Dasgupta, Banaji, & Abelson, 1999; Haslam, 2004).
- Key criterion: *perceived coherence*, not mere physical proximity.
- Two people standing in an elevator are not a group; a sports team is.

### Types of Groups

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Common-bond groups** | Face-to-face interaction; members are bonded to *each other* | Sports teams, friendship groups, work teams |
| **Common-identity groups** | Members linked via the *category as a whole*; face-to-face often absent | National, linguistic, university, gender groups |

### Entitativity

- **Entitativity** (coined by Campbell, 1958): the extent to which a group is perceived as a coherent, unified whole ("a real group").
- Ranges from a mere collection of individuals (low) to intimate families sharing a name, history, and identity (high).
- Research: Lickel et al. (2000) found considerable agreement on which group types are high vs. low in entitativity.
- Perceived importance to members correlates strongly with entitativity (r = .75; Lickel et al., 2000).

#### Characteristics of High-Entitativity Groups
1. Members interact frequently (including online).
2. The group is important to its members.
3. Members share common goals.
4. Members are similar to one another in important ways.

#### Entitativity and Stereotyping
- High-entitativity groups are more likely to be **stereotyped** (Yzerbyt et al., 2001).
- *Abstract language* is used to describe high-entitativity groups (implying endurance and distinctiveness); concrete language for low-entitativity groups (Spencer-Rodgers, Hamilton, & Sherman, 2007).
- Group *size* per se does not determine entitativity; **behavioral features** (resource sharing, reciprocating favors, recognizing group authorities, adhering to norms) matter more than structural features (Lickel, Rutchick, Hamilton, & Sherman, 2006).

#### Entitativity Ratings (Lickel et al., 2000)

|---|---|---|
| Families | 8.57 | 8.78 |
| Friends/romantic partners | 8.27 | 8.06 |
| Religious groups | 8.20 | 7.34 |
| Music groups | 7.33 | 5.48 |

### Evolutionary Basis for Group Membership

- Brewer & Caporael (2006): Interdependence among group members is the *primary survival strategy* for humans; the group buffers individuals from the physical environment.
- Schachter (1959): Strong emotion creates a need to compare reactions with others — a driver of group affiliation.
- Hogg (2007): Under threatening/uncertain conditions, people increasingly identify with social groups for psychological security.
- Diener & Oishi (2005); Lyubomirsky, King, & Diener (2005): Degree of social connectedness is among the *best predictors* of psychological well-being.

## Groups: Their Key Components

Every group has four key structural features: **status, roles, norms, and cohesiveness**.

---

### Status: Hierarchies in Groups

- **Status**: an individual's rank or position within a group.
- High status confers advantages: respect, deference, and material benefits (salary, etc.).
- Evolutionary psychologists: high status confers important reproductive and survival advantages (Buss, 1999).

#### Determinants of Status
- **Physical attributes**: Taller individuals earn more, are perceived as more skilled, and are more likely to be nominated as leaders (meta-analysis: Judge & Cable, 2004).
  - Average U.S. Presidential height exceeds the population average.
  - People even judge election *winners* as taller after they win, and losers as shorter (Higham & Carment, 1992).
- **Prototypicality**: Being seen as embodying the group's central attributes increases status and likelihood of being selected as leader (Haslam & Platow, 2001).
- **Longevity/seniority**: Seen as reflecting wisdom and knowledge of in-group ways (Haslam, 2004).

#### Status and Conformity
- High-status members behave more idiosyncratically and variably than low-status members (Guinote, Judd, & Brauer, 2002).
- Jetten, Hornsey, & Adarves-Yorno (2006): In professional and student groups, those with *high status* report conforming *less* than those with low status.
- Low-status newcomers conform more to be accepted; failure to yield to higher-status members can lead to punishment (Levine, Moreland, & Hausmann, 2005).

---

### Roles: Differentiation of Functions

- **Roles**: the set of behaviors that individuals occupying specific positions within a group are expected to perform.
- Roles can be formally assigned (leader, treasurer, secretary) or informally acquired ("good listener," "task specialist").

#### Role Internalization and Authenticity
- Bettencourt, Molix, Talley, & Sheldon (2006): When personal traits matched their assigned role ("idea generator" vs. "devil's advocate"), participants felt their behavior was *more authentic*, showed more positive mood, and enjoyed the task more.

#### KEY STUDY: Simulated Prison Study (Reicher & Haslam, 2006)
- Participants randomly assigned as "prisoners" or "guards."
- Guards *failed to identify* with their role (concerned about being liked and public perception); did not impose collective authority and were eventually overcome by prisoners.
- Prisoners showed *increasing identification* with their role, developed norms of rebellion, showed reductions in depression.
- Guards showed increased burnout and cortisol reactivity (physiological stress indicator; Haslam & Reicher, 2006).
- Conclusion: Role effects on behavior are powerful primarily when roles are *internalized*.

---

### Norms: The Rules of the Game

- **Norms**: implicit (or explicit) rules within a group about how members should (or should not) behave.

#### Feeling Rules
- **Feeling rules** (Hochschild, 1983): explicit expectations about the emotions that are appropriate to express.
- Example: Service workers (flight attendants, cashiers) are required to smile; funeral directors are expected to display only a serious, empathic expression.

#### Emotion Norms and Group Membership Acquisition
- Wilkins (2008) study of Evangelical Christians: New converts initially did not enjoy meetings, but over time learned to model emotions on others, acquired a new emotional vocabulary, and came to claim happiness as necessary to being a "good group member."
- Being happy = moral goodness in this community.

#### Collectivism vs. Individualism as Group Norms
- **Collectivism**: Group norm to maintain harmony among members, avoid conflict; personal costs acceptable.
- **Individualism**: Group norm to stand out, be different; disagreement is tolerated or even admired.

#### KEY STUDY: Dissent and Group Norms (Hornsey, Jetten, McAuliffe, & Hogg, 2006)
- Design: Participants high or low in university identification; group norm described as collectivist or individualist; rated a dissenting student.
- Finding: *High identifiers* liked the dissenter when the norm was individualist; *disliked* the dissenter when the norm was collectivist.
- *Low identifiers*: norm had no effect on evaluations.
- Implication: Violating group norms is most costly when others highly value the group.

---

### Cohesiveness: The Force That Binds

- **Cohesiveness**: all forces (factors) that cause group members to remain in the group (Ellemers, de Gilder, & Haslam, 2004).
- Cohesive groups: sense of solidarity, homogeneity, mutual support, cooperation, group-level goal focus, high morale, and better performance (Hogg, 2007; Mullen & Cooper, 1994).

#### Threats and Cohesiveness
- Presence of an outgroup or competitive threat increases cohesion (Putnam, 2000).
- Wartime: support for in-group leaders dramatically increases (Landau et al., 2004).
- Wohl, Giguère, Branscombe, & McVicar (2011): French Canadians who worry about losing cultural distinctiveness from English Canadians favor Quebec separation.
- English Canadians threatened by a fictitious "North American Union" favor limiting American media and favor politicians who protect Canadian distinctiveness.
- Wohl, Branscombe, & Reysen (2010): Perceiving in-group's future as in jeopardy → collective anxiety → actions to strengthen in-group (e.g., in-group marriage, segregated education, protective voting).

## The Benefits — and Costs — of Joining

### Benefits of Group Membership

1. **Self-knowledge**: Group membership clarifies who we are; becomes central to self-concept (Tajfel & Turner, 1986).
   - Being ostracized — even from an online group — lowers feelings of control and self-esteem immediately and after a 45-minute delay (Zadro, Boland, & Richardson, 2006); worse for those high in social anxiety (Williams, 2001).
2. **Goal attainment**: Groups help achieve goals (prestige, career, social change).
   - The more an individual seeks self-enhancement, the more important is the group's status, and the more strongly they identify with it (Roccas, 2003).
3. **Power match**: People prefer to be in groups that match their current goal orientation (high-power vs. low-power group preference; Sassenberg, Jonas, Shah, & Brazy, 2007).
4. **Social change via politicized collective identity**:
   - **Politicized collective identity**: Recognizing shared grievances and engaging in a power struggle on behalf of one's devalued group.
   - Branscombe, Schmitt, & Harvey (1999): Discriminated groups increasingly identify with their group and develop collective identity.
   - Simon & Klandermans (2001): Collective mobilization wins better treatment for the group.

### Costs of Getting Accepted: Initiation Effects

#### KEY STUDY: Initiation Severity and Group Attraction (Aronson & Mills, 1959)
- **Method**: Students read either very embarrassing, mildly embarrassing, or no material aloud before "joining" a group.
- **Finding**: Those who underwent the most severe (embarrassing) initiation rated the group more attractive — even though the group discussion they heard was deliberately boring.
- **Explanation**: **Cognitive dissonance theory** — when we have put in considerable effort to join a group, we change our attitudes toward the group in a *positive* direction to justify our effort.
- Rule: Liking for a group increases with severity of initiation.

### Costs of Membership

1. **Loss of personal freedom**: Members must comply with group norms or face sanctions/expulsion (e.g., military officers prohibited from public political statements).
2. **Demands on time, energy, and resources** (e.g., tithing 10% of income to churches).
3. **Mandatory silence or exit** when the group adopts positions members oppose.

### Why Groups Splinter: Schism

- **Ideology**: The philosophical and political values that govern a group.
- **Schism**: Splintering of a group into distinct factions following an ideological rift among members (Sani, 2005, 2009).

#### KEY STUDY: Church of England Women Priests (Sani, 2005)
- In 1994, women were first ordained as priests; hundreds of clergy left.
- Sani surveyed over 1,000 priests/deacons.
- Leavers: felt the policy change subverted fundamental doctrines; the church was no longer the same organization; felt unheard.
- Mechanism: Ideological change → perceived subversion of group identity → emotional distress + reduced entitativity + reduced identification → schism.
- The distress of leaving resembled *bereavement*.
- Applies beyond religious groups: political parties, social movements, any belief-based organization.

#### Model of Group Splintering (Sani, 2009)
```
Ideological change in core group aspect
        ↓
Perceived subversion of group identity
        ↓
Emotional distress (if no chance of reversal) + Reduced entitativity & identification
        ↓
Group schism
```

## Effects of the Presence of Others: From Task Performance to Behavior in Crowds

Even the *mere presence* of others — without any formal group membership — alters behavior significantly.

---

## Social Facilitation: Performing in the Presence of Others

- **Social facilitation**: the effects of the presence of others (as audience or co-actors) on an individual's performance.
- Historical note: Norman Triplett (1898) first observed cyclists rode faster with companions than alone — the earliest social psychology experiment.

### Drive Theory of Social Facilitation (Zajonc, 1965)

- **Mechanism**: The presence of others increases **physiological arousal**, which strengthens the tendency to perform **dominant responses** (the best-learned, most practiced behaviors).
- If dominant responses are **correct** (well-learned task) → performance is **enhanced**.
- If dominant responses are **incorrect** (novel/complex task) → performance is **impaired**.

#### KEY STUDY: Cockroach Maze Experiment (Zajonc, Heingartner, & Herman, 1969)
- **Method**: Cockroaches ran a maze either alone or observed by a "roach audience" in clear plastic boxes.
- **Finding**: Cockroaches ran faster with an audience.
- **Purpose**: Demonstrate that social facilitation does not require evaluation apprehension — cockroaches cannot worry about being judged.
- **Conclusion**: Mere presence alone is sufficient to produce facilitation effects.

### Evaluation Apprehension Theory (Cottrell et al., 1968)

- **Evaluation apprehension**: Concern over being evaluated by others, which increases arousal and contributes to social facilitation effects.
- **Evidence**: Social facilitation did *not* occur when the audience was blindfolded or showed no interest in watching — suggesting evaluation concerns play a role.
- Key researcher: Cottrell, Wack, Sekerak, & Rittle (1968).

### Distraction Conflict Theory (Baron, 1986)

- **Distraction conflict theory**: Social facilitation stems from the *conflict* produced when individuals simultaneously attend to other people *and* to the task.
- This cognitive overload leads to **narrowed attentional focus** — screening out nonessential cues, concentrating only on essential ones.
- Explains facilitation in both animals and humans (even cockroaches experience conflicting attention demands).

#### KEY STUDY: Distraction and Eating (Hetherington, Anderson, Norton, & Newson, 2006)
- Eating with *friends* (distracting) and while watching TV (distracting) both *increased* caloric intake.
- Eating with *strangers* (less distracting) → no increase in caloric intake.
- Conclusion: Distraction → narrowed focus on food → "improved" eating performance.

### Summary: Three Explanations for Social Facilitation

| Theory | Mechanism | Key Researcher |
|---|---|---|
| Drive Theory | Mere presence → arousal → enhanced dominant responses | Zajonc (1965) |
| Evaluation Apprehension | Fear of being judged → arousal → facilitation/impairment | Cottrell et al. (1968) |
| Distraction Conflict | Competing attentional demands → narrowed focus | Baron (1986) |

## Social Loafing: Letting Others Do the Work

- **Social loafing**: reductions in effort that individuals show when working collectively compared to when working individually (Karau & Williams, 1993).
- Occurs on **additive tasks**: tasks in which the contributions of each member are combined into a single group output.

### KEY STUDY: Clapping and Shouting (Latané, Williams, & Harkins, 1979)
- **Method**: Male students clapped/cheered in groups of 1, 2, 4, or 6; wore headphones masking others' noise.
- **Finding**: Total noise increased with group size, but *individual output decreased* as group size grew.
- This was one of the first systematic demonstrations of social loafing.
- Also: Restaurant tips are proportionally less as group size increases.

### Why Social Loafing Occurs
1. **Dispensability**: Feeling one's contribution doesn't matter (Price, Harrison, & Gavin, 2006).
2. **Perceived unfairness**: When fairness in the group is low, loafing increases (Price et al., 2006).
3. **Dissimilarity**: Being dissimilar from group members increases perceived dispensability.

### Reducing Social Loafing
1. **Make output identifiable**: When individual contributions are identifiable, people cannot hide (Williams, Harkins, & Latané, 1981).
2. **Increase group commitment**: Create pressures toward high performance (Brickner, Harkins, & Ostrom, 1986).
3. **Increase task importance/value**: People loaf less on tasks they consider important (Karau & Williams, 1993).
4. **Provide performance standards**: Comparison to others' performance or own past performance reduces loafing (Williams et al., 1981).
5. **Peer feedback**: Group members providing each other feedback during joint projects reduces loafing (Aggarwal & O'Brien, 2008).

### Social Loafing in Educational Settings
- Englehart (2006): Larger class size → less student participation (social loafing principle).
- Students with more task-relevant knowledge feel less dispensable → less loafing.

## Effects of Being in a Crowd

### Deindividuation

- **Deindividuation**: a state in which individuals feel reduced personal identity and decreased concern with how others evaluate them when in a large crowd or group, leading to a lowered threshold for engaging in behavior normally restrained.
- Crowds and groups create conditions that reduce self-awareness and personal accountability.
- Examples: hooliganism at sports events, mob violence.

### What Produces Deindividuation?
1. **Anonymity**: Being unknown reduces accountability.
2. **Diffusion of responsibility**: In a crowd, no single individual feels fully responsible.
3. **High arousal**: Crowd situations are physiologically activating.
4. **Focus on external events**: Crowd attention directed outward rather than inward.

### Social Identity Model of Deindividuation Effects (SIDE Model)
- **SIDE Model** (Reicher, Spears, & Postmes, 1995): Deindividuation does not produce a loss of all norms; rather, people shift from personal identity to *social identity* (group membership).
- Behavior in crowds is governed by the *norms of the salient group*, not random impulsivity.
- This explains why crowd behavior is often *patterned* and *consistent* with group identity rather than completely random.
- Key insight: "Deindividuated" people are not normless — they follow a different set of norms.

### Emergent Norm Theory (Turner & Killian, 1972)
- **Emergent norm theory**: In ambiguous crowd situations, new norms emerge that guide behavior; once established, these norms exert conformity pressure on crowd members.
- Explains why crowds develop a consistent behavioral character over time.

## Coordination in Groups: Cooperation or Conflict?

### Social Life in a Connected World: Computer-Mediated Communication (CMC)

- **Computer-mediated communication (CMC)**: interactions via digital channels (email, video conferencing, social media, instant messaging).
- CMC changes group dynamics by altering anonymity, reducing nonverbal cues, and changing turn-taking.

#### Effects of CMC on Group Work
- Can increase participation from those who might be inhibited face-to-face (especially shy individuals or lower-status members).
- Reduces status cues: in CMC, low-status members may participate more equally.
- Reduces social presence cues, which can decrease social facilitation and increase social loafing in some contexts.
- Anonymity in CMC can increase flaming (hostile, aggressive communication) — related to online deindividuation.
- **Virtual teams** can cooperate effectively but require clear communication norms and accountability structures to avoid loafing.
- Research shows that groups performing **brainstorming tasks** often perform *better* via computer (electronic brainstorming) because production blocking (one person talking at a time) is reduced.

## Cooperation: Working with Others to Achieve Shared Goals

- **Cooperation**: working together with others to attain shared goals.
- Cooperation vs. competition: both are possible in group settings; the outcome depends on the incentive structure.

### The Prisoner's Dilemma

- **Prisoner's dilemma**: a situation in which the most rewarding choice for each individual is to defect, yet if both defect, both receive worse outcomes than if both cooperated.
- Models real-world cooperation-competition dilemmas (arms races, resource sharing, environmental protection).
- Key finding: people often defect even when cooperation would benefit both parties.

### Factors That Promote Cooperation
1. **Communication**: Ability to communicate increases cooperation dramatically.
2. **Reciprocity**: Tit-for-tat strategy (cooperate first, then mirror partner's last move) is highly effective (Axelrod, 1984).
3. **Group identity/cohesiveness**: People cooperate more with in-group members.
4. **Trust**: Higher trust → more cooperation.
5. **Perceived fairness**: Perceived equity in outcomes promotes cooperation.
6. **Interdependence**: When outcomes are functionally interdependent, cooperation increases.

### The Commons Dilemma

- **Commons dilemma** (also "tragedy of the commons"): a situation in which individuals acting in self-interest collectively deplete a shared resource.
- Example: overgrazing, overfishing, pollution.
- Solved through regulation, privatization, or strong group norms.

## Responding to and Resolving Conflicts: Some Useful Techniques

- **Conflict**: a state in which individuals or groups perceive their goals, interests, or actions to be incompatible.

### Conflict Escalation
- Conflicts often escalate via threats, negative attributions about the other side, and reciprocal retaliation.

### Techniques for Reducing Conflict

1. **Superordinate goals** (Sherif, 1954/1961): Goals that can only be achieved through the joint efforts of conflicting groups; reduces intergroup hostility.
   - Classic: Robbers Cave Experiment — introduced broken-down truck that both groups had to fix together; conflict was greatly reduced.
2. **GRIT (Graduated and Reciprocated Initiatives in Tension Reduction)** (Osgood, 1962):
   - One side makes a small unilateral concession, inviting the other to reciprocate; gradually de-escalates conflict.
3. **Negotiation and mediation**: Third-party mediators can reframe positions and find mutually acceptable solutions.
4. **Contact hypothesis**: Mere contact under favorable conditions (equal status, institutional support, cooperative goals, personal acquaintance) reduces intergroup conflict (Allport, 1954).
5. **Perspective taking**: Imagining the other party's viewpoint reduces hostility and increases cooperation.
6. **Attribution retraining**: Avoiding attribution of hostile intent to the other side; attributing negative behavior to situational factors.

---

## Basic Rules for Judging Fairness: Distributive, Procedural, and Transactional Justice

### Distributive Justice

- **Distributive justice**: perceived fairness of the *outcomes* or rewards distributed among group members.
- The dominant standard in Western cultures is **equity**: outcomes should be proportional to inputs (contributions).
- Other distribution rules: **equality** (everyone gets the same regardless of input); **need** (those with greatest need receive the most).
- When distributive justice is violated, people experience anger and resentment.

### Procedural Justice

- **Procedural justice**: perceived fairness of the *processes* used to determine how outcomes are distributed.
- Key finding: People are more accepting of unfavorable outcomes if they believe the *process* was fair.
- Components of procedural fairness:
  - **Voice**: Having the opportunity to express one's views before a decision is made.
  - **Consistency**: Procedures applied equally to all.
  - **Accuracy**: Decisions based on accurate information.
  - **Ethicality**: Decisions consistent with moral standards.
- Thibaut & Walker (1975): original procedural justice researchers; studied legal settings.
- Leventhal (1980): expanded the model to organizational and social contexts.

### Transactional Justice (Interactional Justice)

- **Transactional justice** (also called **interactional justice**): perceived fairness of the interpersonal treatment received during the implementation of decisions; the quality of the explanations and social sensitivity shown.
- Two components:
  - **Interpersonal justice**: Being treated with dignity and respect.
  - **Informational justice**: Receiving adequate explanations and justifications for decisions.
- Bies & Moag (1986): proposed interactional justice as a distinct dimension.

### Effects of Perceived Unfairness
- Perceived injustice triggers anger, resentment, reduced cooperation, retaliation, and withdrawal.
- Even animals (monkeys) respond to unequal treatment with protest behavior (de Waal, Brosnan, 2003) — suggesting evolved sensitivity to fairness.

## Emotions and Groups: When Members of One Group Perceive Members of Another Group as Rejecting Them

- **Rejection sensitivity**: heightened vigilance to interpersonal rejection, especially from important groups.
- When members of one group perceive that they are rejected by members of another group, strong negative emotions (anger, humiliation) result.
- **Intergroup rejection** can trigger:
  - Increased in-group cohesion and identification.
  - Hostility toward the rejecting out-group.
  - Motivation for social change or collective action.
- Branscombe & colleagues: Perceived discrimination against one's group leads to greater in-group identification (the "rejection-identification model").
  - The more minority group members perceive discrimination, the more they identify with their group, which buffers psychological well-being.
- **Collective emotions**: emotions felt *as a group member* rather than as an individual; can motivate collective action.
  - Collective guilt (felt by advantaged group), collective anger, collective pride.

## Decision Making by Groups: How It Occurs and the Pitfalls It Faces

### The Decision-Making Process: How Groups Attain Consensus

- Groups must move from individual judgments to a shared collective decision.
- **Social decision schemes**: rules or strategies implicitly adopted by groups to combine individual preferences.

| Social Decision Scheme | Description |
|---|---|
| **Majority-wins** | Group adopts the position held by most members |
| **Truth-wins** | Correct answer recognized once stated; one member's correct solution carries the group |
| **First-shift rule** | Group moves in direction of first shift in opinion |
| **Unanimity rule** | Discussion continues until all members agree |

- For most tasks, the **majority-wins** rule accurately predicts final group decisions.
- For tasks with a demonstrably correct answer, the **truth-wins** rule is more applicable.

### Information Sharing in Groups
- Groups often fail to share **unshared information** (information known only to one or a few members) and instead discuss **shared information** (already known by most members).
- Stasser & Titus (1985): "hidden profile" paradigm — groups tend to discuss pre-existing shared information and miss critical unshared information that could lead to a better decision.
- This "shared information bias" leads to suboptimal group decisions.

## The Downside of Group Decision Making

### Group Polarization

- **Group polarization**: the tendency for group discussion to shift members' average opinion toward a more extreme position in the direction they already favored before discussion.
- If individuals initially lean toward a risky position → group shifts even more toward risk (**risky shift**).
- If individuals initially lean toward a cautious position → group shifts even more toward caution (**cautious shift**).
- Two mechanisms:
  1. **Persuasive arguments theory**: Discussion generates more arguments favoring the initial dominant position; members hear novel pro-attitudinal arguments.
  2. **Social comparison theory**: Members want to be seen as holding the "right" (and perhaps more extreme) position; comparison with others who share and slightly exceed one's position drives movement toward the extreme.

### Groupthink

- **Groupthink** (Janis, 1972): a mode of thinking in which the desire for harmony and conformity in a cohesive group overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives; leads to poor collective decisions.

#### Conditions Fostering Groupthink
1. High group cohesiveness.
2. Group insulation from outside information.
3. Directive leadership (leader advocates a preferred solution early).
4. High stress with low hope for better solution than leader's.
5. Lack of methodical decision-making procedures.

#### Symptoms of Groupthink (Janis, 1972 — 8 symptoms)
1. **Illusion of invulnerability**: Excessive optimism; group believes it cannot fail.
2. **Collective rationalization**: Members rationalize away warning signs.
3. **Belief in inherent morality**: Members believe their cause is righteous; ignore ethical implications.
4. **Stereotyping out-groups**: Adversaries seen as weak, evil, or stupid.
5. **Pressure on dissenters**: Members who question the group are pressured to conform.
6. **Self-censorship**: Members with doubts keep quiet.
7. **Illusion of unanimity**: Silence interpreted as agreement; false sense of consensus.
8. **Self-appointed mindguards**: Some members take it upon themselves to shield the group from contradictory information.

#### Historical Examples of Groupthink (Janis)
- Bay of Pigs invasion (1961).
- Challenger space shuttle disaster (1986).
- Pearl Harbor attack failure (1941).
- Escalation of the Vietnam War.

#### Preventing Groupthink
1. Leader refrains from stating preferences at outset.
2. Assign a "devil's advocate" role to challenge consensus.
3. Invite outside experts to critique group's plans.
4. Hold a "second chance" meeting after a preliminary decision.
5. Subdivide the group to work separately, then compare.
6. Encourage open expression of doubts and concerns.

### Do Groups Make Riskier Decisions than Individuals?
- Earlier belief: the "risky shift" suggested groups are always riskier.
- Corrected view: Groups show **polarization** in whatever direction members initially lean — groups are not inherently riskier OR more cautious than individuals.
- Both individuals and groups bear responsibility for disasters; group processes (especially groupthink) can amplify poor individual judgments.

## The Role of Leadership in Group Settings

### What Is Leadership?

- **Leadership**: the process by which one individual exerts influence over others and inspires, motivates, and directs their activities; involves social influence.

### Theories of Leadership

#### Great Person Theory (Trait Approach)
- **Great person theory**: the idea that leaders possess special traits that set them apart from followers; leaders are born, not made.
- Key traits associated with leadership: intelligence, dominance, self-confidence, energy, task-relevant knowledge.
- Research support: modest but consistent correlations between certain traits and leadership emergence.
- Limitation: traits alone do not fully explain who becomes a leader or who is effective.

#### Contingency Theories
- Leadership effectiveness depends on the *fit* between leader style and situational characteristics.

##### Fiedler's Contingency Model (Fiedler, 1978)
- Leaders have a stable style: **task-oriented** or **relationship-oriented** (measured by the Least Preferred Co-Worker (LPC) scale).
- **LPC scale**: High LPC = relationship-oriented; Low LPC = task-oriented.
- Effectiveness depends on *situational control* (degree of certainty and predictability the leader has).
  - **High situational control** (clear task, good leader-member relations, high position power): Task-oriented (low LPC) leaders most effective.
  - **Moderate situational control**: Relationship-oriented (high LPC) leaders most effective.
  - **Low situational control** (unclear task, poor relations, low position power): Task-oriented (low LPC) leaders again most effective.

##### Path-Goal Theory (House, 1971)
- Leaders are most effective when they help followers clarify *paths* to their goals and remove obstacles.
- Four leadership styles: directive, supportive, participative, achievement-oriented.
- Style effectiveness depends on followers' characteristics and task structure.

#### Transformational vs. Transactional Leadership

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Transactional leadership** | Exchange-based: rewards for performance, punishment for failure; maintains status quo |
| **Transformational leadership** | Inspires followers beyond self-interest; provides vision, intellectual stimulation, individual consideration; produces change |

- **Transformational leadership** (Burns, 1978; Bass, 1985): leaders who inspire followers to transcend self-interest for a higher collective vision.
- Components of transformational leadership (Bass): charisma/idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, individualized consideration.
- Transformational leaders are more effective at producing sustained change and high follower commitment.

#### Charismatic Leadership
- **Charismatic leaders**: inspire intense devotion, unconditional loyalty, and willingness to sacrifice; seen as extraordinary.
- May be positive (Gandhi, MLK) or destructive (cult leaders, authoritarian figures).

#### Prototypicality and Leadership
- **Social identity theory of leadership** (Hogg, 2001): In highly cohesive/identified groups, the most *prototypical* member (most representative of the group's defining attributes) is most likely to be chosen as leader.
- Prototypical leaders are seen as "one of us" and thus trusted to act in the group's interest.

### Gender and Leadership
- Research has found a **"think manager — think male"** stereotype (Schein, 1973): traits associated with effective managers overlap more with masculine than feminine stereotypes.
- Meta-analyses (Eagly & Karau, 2002): Women face a **role incongruity** — being female conflicts with the normative expectations for leaders; women are evaluated less favorably when they adopt agentic (stereotypically "male") leadership styles.
- However, transformational leadership, which involves communal qualities (empathy, inspirational motivation), tends to be associated with female advantage.

### Leadership and Power
- High-status group members granted more **idiosyncrasy credit** (Hollander, 1958): accumulated credits from past conformity allow leaders to deviate from norms without losing influence.
- Power corrupts: those given power focus on their own goals, take more resources, objectify others, and may become less sensitive to others' emotions (Keltner et al., 2003).

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Entitativity** was coined by Campbell (1958) and refers to the degree to which a group is perceived as a coherent entity; it correlates strongly (r = .75) with group importance to members (Lickel et al., 2000).

2. **Drive theory of social facilitation** (Zajonc, 1965): presence of others → increased arousal → enhanced *dominant* responses. If the task is well-learned, performance improves; if it is new or complex, performance deteriorates.

3. **Evaluation apprehension** (Cottrell et al., 1968) posits social facilitation requires concern about being judged; Zajonc's cockroach study (1969) proved social facilitation can occur WITHOUT evaluation apprehension.

4. **Social loafing** (Karau & Williams, 1993) occurs on *additive* tasks; first demonstrated by Latané, Williams, & Harkins (1979) using clapping/cheering; reduced by making contributions identifiable, increasing task importance, and providing performance standards.

5. **Groupthink** (Janis, 1972) has 8 symptoms (illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalization, belief in inherent morality, out-group stereotyping, pressure on dissenters, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, self-appointed mindguards); promoted by high cohesiveness + directive leadership + group insulation.

6. **Group polarization**: Discussion moves groups toward a *more extreme* version of members' initial tendency — this can be risky shift OR cautious shift. Two mechanisms: persuasive arguments and social comparison.

7. **Aronson & Mills (1959)** initiation study: severe initiation → greater liking for the group (cognitive dissonance reduction/effort justification).

8. **Sani (2005)** Church of England study: ideological change → perceived subversion of group identity → emotional distress + reduced entitativity → schism; leavers experienced something akin to bereavement.

9. **Reicher & Haslam (2006)** simulated prison study: guards who *failed to identify* with their role lost control; prisoners who *highly identified* developed rebellion norms; role effects depend on internalization.

10. **Distributive justice** = fairness of outcomes (equity/equality/need); **Procedural justice** = fairness of the process (voice, consistency, accuracy); **Transactional/Interactional justice** = quality of interpersonal treatment and explanations during implementation.

11. **Transformational leadership** (Burns, 1978; Bass, 1985) inspires followers beyond self-interest using vision, intellectual stimulation, and individualized consideration; more effective for sustained change than transactional (exchange-based) leadership.

12. **SIDE Model** (Reicher, Spears, & Postmes, 1995): Deindividuation in crowds does not eliminate norms — it shifts individuals from personal to *social* (group-based) identity, and behavior follows group norms; explains patterned crowd behavior.

13. **Shared information bias** (Stasser & Titus, 1985): Groups disproportionately discuss pre-existing shared information and fail to surface critical *unshared* information, leading to suboptimal decisions ("hidden profile" paradigm).

14. **Fiedler's contingency model**: Task-oriented (low LPC) leaders are most effective under both *high* and *low* situational control; relationship-oriented (high LPC) leaders are most effective under *moderate* situational control.

15. **Common-bond vs. common-identity groups**: Common-bond = face-to-face, bonded to each other (sports team); common-identity = linked via category, face-to-face often absent (national or gender groups). Both are important sources of social identity.
