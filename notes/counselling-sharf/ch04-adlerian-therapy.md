# Ch 4: Adlerian Therapy (Individual Psychology)

**Overview:** Adlerian therapy, called **Individual Psychology** by its founder **Alfred Adler** (1870-1937), was the second great revision of psychoanalysis after Jung. Adler was an early member of Freud's Vienna Psychoanalytic Society and even its president, but broke from Freud in **1911** over Freud's insistence on the sexual basis of behaviour. Where Freud was biological, deterministic, reductionistic, and past-driven, Adler built a theory that is **holistic, social, teleological (goal-directed), phenomenological, and growth-oriented**. The name "Individual Psychology" does NOT mean it focuses on the individual in isolation; the Latin root *individuum* means **"indivisible"** - Adler insisted the person is a unified, indivisible whole that cannot be split into parts (id/ego/superego, conscious/unconscious). The person is understood only as a single moving unity, embedded in a social field, striving toward a self-created future goal. Adler is the great **forerunner** of much that came later: humanistic therapy (Maslow, Rogers), cognitive therapy (the role of beliefs and "private logic" anticipates Beck and Ellis), family/parent education (Dreikurs), and the whole idea of the encouraging, egalitarian therapeutic relationship. **Rudolf Dreikurs** brought Adlerian work to the United States and built its child-guidance and family-education applications.

---

## 4.1 Adler vs Freud: The Three Pillars of the Split

Adler did not merely tinker with Freud; he reversed Freud's core assumptions. Memorise this contrast - it is the single most tested feature of the chapter.

| Dimension | Freud (Psychoanalysis) | Adler (Individual Psychology) |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **View of the person** | Reductionistic: person split into id, ego, superego; conscious vs unconscious | **Holistic / indivisible**: one unified, self-consistent whole |
| **Prime motive** | Sexual instinct (libido), pleasure principle | **Striving for superiority / completion**, social belonging |
| **Causation** | **Causal / deterministic** - the past pushes us (childhood, drives) | **Teleological / purposive** - the future goal pulls us (finalism) |
| **Conscious vs unconscious** | Behaviour mostly **unconscious**; iceberg | Mostly **conscious / accessible**; we are aware of our goals if we look |
| **Social vs biological** | Biological drives are primary | **Social** embeddedness is primary; humans are inherently social |
| **Free will** | Determined by drives and past | **Creative power of the self** - we author our own style of life |
| **Optimism** | Pessimistic (conflict-ridden) | **Optimistic** (growth, choice, social interest) |

Three pillars to lock in:

1. **Holism (organismic unity).** The person is indivisible. You cannot understand a symptom by isolating it; you read it as serving the whole personality's goal. All behaviour has a purpose for the unified self.
2. **Social embeddedness.** Humans are fundamentally social, not driven by internal biology. Mental health is measured by **social interest** (community feeling), not by drive-reduction. This is why Adler is grouped with the social/interpersonal theorists.
3. **Teleology / purposive striving.** Behaviour is **goal-directed**: we are pulled by future, often **fictional** goals, not pushed by the past. To ask "what is this behaviour FOR?" (its purpose) replaces Freud's "what CAUSED this?"

> **Key Point:** Freud = **causal, past, biological, unconscious, deterministic**. Adler = **teleological, future-goal, social, conscious, creative/free**. "What is the purpose of the symptom?" is the signature Adlerian question.

## 4.2 Inferiority Feelings and the Inferiority Complex

- Every human begins life **small, weak, and dependent**, surrounded by stronger, more capable adults. This produces a normal, universal **feeling of inferiority** (*Minderwertigkeitsgefühl*).
- **Inferiority feelings are NOT pathological.** Adler regarded them as the **wellspring of all human striving and improvement** - the motive force that drives a person to grow, master, and complete themselves. Normal development means **compensating** (overcoming the felt deficiency through effort and competence).
- **Organ inferiority** was Adler's early (pre-split) idea: a constitutionally weak organ (eyes, lungs, limbs) can spur **compensation** or **overcompensation** (e.g., a sickly child becomes an athlete; a stammerer becomes an orator like Demosthenes).
- **Inferiority complex**: a deep, **exaggerated, discouraged** sense of inferiority that **paralyses** rather than motivates. The person feels unable to cope and retreats, makes excuses, or avoids the life tasks. It is the *failure* to compensate constructively.
- **Superiority complex**: the **flip side / overcompensation** - a façade of arrogance, boasting, vanity, or domineering used to **cover** underlying feelings of inferiority. A superiority complex always conceals an inferiority complex beneath it.

> **Key Point:** Distinguish three terms. **Inferiority FEELINGS** = universal, normal, motivating. **Inferiority COMPLEX** = exaggerated, discouraging, immobilising. **Superiority COMPLEX** = overcompensating mask hiding the inferiority complex underneath.

---

## 4.3 Striving for Superiority (Striving for Completion / Perfection)

- Adler's view of the **one dynamic force** behind all behaviour evolved across his career, and the exam loves the sequence:
  1. Earliest: **aggression drive** / will to overcome.
  2. Middle: **striving for power** ("masculine protest" - striving to be strong, not weak/"feminine," a culturally shaped reaction; Adler is the source of the **"will to power"** label in psychology).
  3. Mature / final formulation: **striving for superiority**, better understood as **striving for completion, mastery, perfection, and self-realisation** - moving from a felt **minus situation to a perceived plus situation**.
- This is not striving to dominate others (that is the neurotic, self-centred form). In the healthy person, striving for superiority is **fused with social interest**, so the person strives to perfect themselves *and* contribute to the common good.
- It is **innate** (the striving itself) but its **direction and content** are individually created.

> **Key Point:** The contrast triad: **will to PLEASURE = Freud; will to POWER = Adler; will to MEANING = Frankl (logotherapy).** This three-way comparison is almost guaranteed.

## 4.4 Style of Life

- The **style of life** (lifestyle) is the person's **unique, characteristic pattern** of striving toward their goal - their distinctive way of perceiving self, others, and the world, and of moving through the life tasks. It is the unifying principle that gives the personality its consistency.
- It is **largely formed by about age 4-5** and is remarkably stable thereafter; later behaviour is an expression of this early-set pattern.
- It is shaped by, but not caused by, the **family atmosphere, family values, birth order/family constellation, and the child's creative interpretation** of these. Two children in the same family develop different lifestyles because each **creatively interprets** their situation differently.
- Dreikurs described **types** of mistaken lifestyle goals; Adler resisted rigid typologies but sketched four attitudinal types (ruling/dominant, getting/leaning, avoiding, and the socially useful type).
- A **mistaken style of life** (one low in social interest, organised around self-protection and private goals) is the Adlerian equivalent of "pathology."

> **Key Point:** The style of life is set by ~age 5, is **creatively constructed by the child** (not merely imposed), and is the consistent thread the therapist tries to read. Adler is **soft-deterministic**: early experience influences but does not dictate, because of the creative self.

## 4.5 Fictional Finalism and Fictional Goals

- Adler borrowed from Hans **Vaihinger's** philosophy of **"As If"** (*The Philosophy of 'As If'*). Vaihinger argued people live by **fictions** - ideas that are not literally true but are useful for navigating life (e.g., "all people are equal," "honesty is always best").
- **Fictional finalism / fictional goal**: the central, self-created, often only dimly conscious **future goal** toward which the whole personality strives (e.g., "I must be perfect," "I must be superior to be safe," "I will be loved only if I please everyone"). It is "fictional" because it is a subjective, idealised construction that may never be fully attainable, yet it **organises and pulls** all of present behaviour.
- This is the heart of Adlerian **teleology**: we are governed by **subjectively held future goals**, not by objective past causes. The fiction is more powerful than the reality. The later term "**guiding self-ideal**" / "**guiding fiction**" captures the same idea.

> **Key Point:** **Fictional finalism = a future, subjective, fictional goal that PULLS behaviour.** It is the clearest statement of Adler's teleology and the cleanest contrast to Freud's mechanical past-causation. Source concept = Vaihinger's "As If."

## 4.6 Social Interest (Gemeinschaftsgefühl)

- **Social interest** (German ***Gemeinschaftsgefühl***, also translated "community feeling" or "social feeling") is the **innate but underdeveloped potential** for **feeling kinship with all humanity** - a sense of belonging, cooperation, empathy, and concern for the welfare of the community and ultimately all of humankind and even the cosmos.
- It must be **deliberately fostered and developed**, first by the **mother** (the child's first social bond) and then by the wider family and school. It is a potential, not an automatic given.
- Social interest is **Adler's single criterion of mental health**. The well-adjusted person has high social interest and meets the life tasks **cooperatively**, contributing to others. Neurotics, criminals, and the maladjusted are characterised by **low/undeveloped social interest** and self-centred, "useless-side" striving.
- The "**useful side vs useless side of life**" is Adler's moral-functional axis: useful = contributing, cooperative, courageous; useless = self-protective, withdrawing, demanding.

> **Key Point:** *Gemeinschaftsgefühl* / **social interest is THE yardstick of mental health in Adlerian theory**, and the central goal of therapy is to **increase it**. Memorise the German term - it is a frequent direct-match item.

## 4.7 Birth Order and the Family Constellation

- The **family constellation** is the whole web of relationships, sibling positions, ages, gender, and family atmosphere into which a child is born and develops.
- **Birth order** refers to the **psychological position** the child occupies, NOT merely the ordinal/biological number. Two children can share the same ordinal position yet occupy different psychological positions, and the psychological position is what shapes the lifestyle.

### Birth Order Table (full)

| Position | Typical Situation | Likely Tendencies / Lifestyle |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Oldest / First-born** | Receives full attention, then is **"dethroned"** by the second child | Often responsible, conforming, conservative, achievement-oriented, leader/authority-respecting; can feel insecure or hostile after dethronement; may overvalue power and rules |
| **Second / Second-born** | Has a **pacemaker** (the older sibling) ahead from birth | Competitive, ambitious, always racing to catch up and overtake; often the opposite of the first-born; can become rebellious if it can't win |
| **Middle child** | "Squeezed" between siblings; neither the eldest privileges nor the youngest indulgence | May feel left out, overlooked, or treated unfairly; can become a peacemaker/diplomat, develop strong social skills, or feel life is unfair |
| **Youngest / Last-born** | The "baby"; pampered, never dethroned, has many pacemakers | Can be spoiled and dependent, or intensely ambitious to outdo all the older siblings; may be the most successful or the most pampered/helpless |
| **Only child** | Never dethroned; spends life among adults; centre of attention | Often precocious, mature, high-achieving; may be pampered and self-centred, want to be the centre, struggle to share or cooperate with peers |

> **Key Point:** Birth order in Adler = **PSYCHOLOGICAL position, not biological number.** The first-born's defining experience is **DETHRONEMENT** (loss of the parents' full attention to the new baby). The second-born is defined by having a **PACEMAKER** ahead.

## 4.8 Early Recollections (Early Memories)

- **Early recollections** (ERs) are the client's **earliest specific memories** - single, concrete remembered incidents from roughly before age 8-10 ("I remember the time when...").
- Adler treated ERs as a **projective key to the present style of life**: out of countless events, the person *selectively remembers* (or even unconsciously invents) precisely those that **confirm and express their current outlook** on self, others, and life. The memory's literal accuracy is irrelevant; its **meaning and theme** are what matter.
- A recollection must be a **single, specific event** the person can picture, not a general report ("we always went to the lake" is a report, not an ER).
- Therapists collect several ERs and read the **recurring themes** (e.g., danger and the need for control; being overlooked; being cared for) to infer the **basic mistakes** and the lifestyle goal.

> **Key Point:** Early recollections are a **projective assessment of the current style of life**, NOT an objective record of childhood (contrast Freud, for whom early memories are causal historical events). Their truth-value does not matter; their **selective theme** does.

## 4.9 The Life Tasks

- Adler held that life confronts everyone with three inescapable **life tasks** that all require **social interest and cooperation**. **Harold Mosak and Rudolf Dreikurs** later added two more.

### The Life Tasks Table

| # | Life Task | Domain | Added by |
|---|-----------|--------|----------|
| 1 | **Work / Occupation** | Contributing through productive labour; vocation | **Adler** |
| 2 | **Friendship / Social** | Relating to others, community, belonging | **Adler** |
| 3 | **Love / Intimacy** | Marriage, sexual/romantic partnership, family | **Adler** |
| 4 | **Self (Self-acceptance)** | Getting along with oneself; coping with the "I" | **Mosak (with Dreikurs)** |
| 5 | **Spiritual** | Relating to the universe, meaning, religion, the cosmos | **Mosak (with Dreikurs)** |

- The way a person meets (or evades) these tasks reveals the style of life and the level of social interest. **Avoiding** a task (e.g., "I won't risk love") is a hallmark of discouragement and low social interest.

> **Key Point:** **Adler's three = Work, Friendship, Love.** **Mosak added two = Self and Spiritual.** Do not credit all five to Adler - the "self" and "spiritual" tasks are Mosak's (with Dreikurs) addition.

## 4.10 Private Logic vs Common Sense

- **Private logic** (private intelligence) is the person's **subjective, idiosyncratic reasoning** that justifies their style of life and goal - the personal "rules" and convictions that make sense only from inside the individual's frame (e.g., "I must control everything or disaster will strike").
- **Common sense** (*Gemeinsinn*) is the **shared, socially validated reasoning** of the community - reality as agreed by people of good social interest.
- **Maladjustment = behaviour governed by private logic that departs from common sense.** The neurotic acts on private convictions they treat as facts. This idea directly **anticipates Beck's "automatic thoughts/schemas" and Ellis's "irrational beliefs"** - hence Adler as a cognitive-therapy forerunner.

> **Key Point:** **Private logic** = self-serving subjective reasoning behind the lifestyle; **common sense** = shared social reasoning. The gap between them defines maladjustment, and the concept prefigures cognitive therapy.

---

## 4.11 Basic Mistakes

- **Basic mistakes** are the **self-defeating, faulty convictions** embedded in the style of life - the mistaken conclusions about self and world that the private logic protects. Mosak grouped them into five classes:

| Class of Basic Mistake | Example Conviction |
|------------------------|--------------------|
| **Overgeneralisations** | "People are hostile." "Nothing is ever fair." |
| **False or impossible goals of security** | "I must please everyone to be safe." "One slip and I'm finished." |
| **Misperceptions of life and life's demands** | "Life never gives me a break." "Life is too hard." |
| **Denial / minimisation of one's worth** | "I'm stupid." "I'm worthless." "I'm only a housewife." |
| **Faulty values** | "Get to the top no matter who gets hurt." |

- Therapy works by surfacing these basic mistakes (largely from early recollections and lifestyle assessment) and **re-educating** the client toward more useful, socially interested convictions.

> **Key Point:** Basic mistakes = the **mistaken beliefs** in the lifestyle, organised by Mosak into the **five categories above**. They are the target of the insight/interpretation phase.

---

## 4.12 Goals of Adlerian Therapy

The overarching aim is **re-education and reorientation toward social interest**. Specifically:

1. **Foster / increase social interest** (*Gemeinschaftsgefühl*) - the central goal.
2. **Reduce discouragement** and feelings of inferiority; build **encouragement** and confidence.
3. **Identify and modify the mistaken style of life** and its **basic mistakes / faulty private logic**.
4. **Change faulty motivation / goals** - help the client see the purpose behind their behaviour and choose more useful goals.
5. **Help the client feel a sense of equality and belonging**, and move from the **useless to the useful side of life**.

> **Key Point:** Adlerian therapy is fundamentally an **educational, growth model**, not a medical/illness model. The therapist is a **teacher and encourager**, and **encouragement** is the single most important therapeutic act.

---

## 4.13 The Four Phases of Adlerian Therapy

Adlerian treatment proceeds through four overlapping phases (Dreikurs's formalisation):

| Phase | Name | What Happens |
|-------|------|--------------|
| **1** | **Relationship** | Establish a warm, **egalitarian, collaborative** alliance built on mutual respect, trust, and shared goals; engagement and **encouragement** begin here |
| **2** | **Assessment / Analysis (Investigation)** | Conduct the **lifestyle assessment**: family constellation, birth order, family atmosphere, **early recollections**, "**The Question**," dreams - to understand the style of life and its goals |
| **3** | **Insight / Interpretation** | Offer **tentative, respectful interpretations** ("Could it be that...?") that help the client gain **insight** into their lifestyle, fictional goals, private logic, and **basic mistakes** |
| **4** | **Reorientation / Reeducation** | Translate insight into action: challenge basic mistakes, set new goals, take risks, increase social interest, apply action techniques; **encouragement** drives change |

> **Key Point:** Order the four phases: **(1) Relationship → (2) Assessment/Analysis → (3) Insight/Interpretation → (4) Reorientation.** Insight is necessary but, unlike Freud, it is in service of **action and reorientation**, not an end in itself.

## 4.14 Adlerian Techniques

| Technique | What It Is |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Encouragement** | The **core, defining** Adlerian technique - building the client's courage and self-belief, conveying faith in their capacity to change. Counters discouragement (the root of pathology) |
| **Acting "as if"** | Client behaves **as if** they were already the competent/confident person they wish to be ("act as if you were not shy"), turning the fictional ideal into rehearsed reality. Forerunner of behavioural rehearsal |
| **Catching oneself** | Client learns to **catch themselves** in the act of an old self-defeating pattern (and the warning signs), then stop or redirect it. Self-monitoring |
| **The push-button technique** | Client deliberately alternates between recalling a **pleasant** memory and an **unpleasant** one, noticing the feeling each "push" produces - demonstrating that they **create their own feelings** and can choose them |
| **Paradoxical intention** | Client is told to **exaggerate / deliberately do** the symptom (e.g., try to be more anxious), removing the fight against it. (Also named by Frankl in logotherapy) |
| **Spitting in the client's soup** | Therapist names the **hidden payoff/purpose** of a behaviour, spoiling its appeal so the client can no longer enjoy it "innocently" (the behaviour stays possible but loses its savour) |
| **Task setting / homework** | Assigning small, concrete, achievable **tasks** between sessions to build courage and momentum |
| **"The Question"** | Diagnostic + therapeutic: **"How would your life be different if you were well?"** If the answer is purely external, the problem may be organic; if it is interpersonal/avoidant, it reveals the purpose the symptom serves |

> **Key Point:** **Encouragement** is the heart of Adlerian practice. **"Spitting in the soup"** = exposing the secret payoff to spoil it. **"Acting as if"** = rehearsing the desired self. **The push-button technique** teaches that *you* generate your feelings.

---

## 4.15 The Therapeutic Relationship

- **Egalitarian, democratic, collaborative.** Therapist and client are equals working as **partners** toward agreed goals; this contrasts sharply with the hierarchical, neutral analyst of Freud.
- The relationship is **warm, empathic, and built on mutual respect and trust**, often formalised by a **shared agreement/contract** on goals.
- The therapist is an **encourager, teacher, and role model**, not a blank screen. **Encouragement** permeates every phase.

---

## 4.16 Applications, Multicultural Considerations, and Critique

- **Applications.** Adlerian ideas are powerful in **parent education and child guidance** (Dreikurs's child-guidance clinics, family-education centres), **school counselling and classroom management** (logical and natural consequences vs punishment), **group and family counselling**, marriage counselling, and brief therapy. Dreikurs's **"goals of children's misbehaviour"** (attention, power, revenge, display of inadequacy) come from this tradition.
- **Multicultural strengths.** The emphases on **social embeddedness, family, community, belonging, and the social context** of behaviour fit **collectivist cultures** well; the **egalitarian relationship**, flexibility, and focus on the individual's subjective frame (phenomenology) suit diverse and feminist applications. Attention to **social/gender context** (Adler championed gender equality and coined "masculine protest" to critique it) is congenial to feminist therapy.
- **Multicultural cautions.** The push toward **insight and verbal exploration**, and the encouragement of individual goal-setting, may not fit every client.
- **Critiques.** Constructs are **loosely / vaguely defined** and hard to operationalise; the theory is more a **practical philosophy** than a tightly tested model; **birth-order research findings are weak and inconsistent**; the **empirical/research base is modest** compared with CBT. Strengths: highly **influential** (a forerunner of humanistic, cognitive, and family approaches), optimistic, practical, and integrative.

> **Key Point:** Adler's lasting importance is as the **great forerunner**: he seeded the **cognitive** (private logic, basic mistakes), **humanistic** (holism, social interest, growth), and **family/parent-education** traditions decades before they were named.

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **"Individual Psychology" does NOT mean focus on the individual alone.** *Individuum* = "indivisible" - it stresses the **holistic unity** of the person. This reversed meaning is a classic trap.

2. **Will to PLEASURE = Freud; will to POWER = Adler; will to MEANING = Frankl.** The most common three-way matching item in the whole counselling syllabus.

3. **Inferiority FEELINGS (normal, universal, motivating) vs inferiority COMPLEX (exaggerated, paralysing) vs superiority COMPLEX (overcompensating mask).** Inferiority feelings are healthy and the source of all striving - do not call them pathological.

4. **A superiority complex always HIDES an underlying inferiority complex.** They are not opposites at different people; they are two faces of the same discouragement.

5. **Teleology, not causality.** Adler is **future-goal-pulled** (fictional finalism), not past-pushed. Freud = causal/past; Adler = purposive/future. "What is the PURPOSE of the symptom?" is the Adlerian question.

6. ***Gemeinschaftsgefühl* (social interest / community feeling) is the single criterion of mental health.** Direct-match the German term. The chief goal of therapy is to **increase social interest**.

7. **Birth order = PSYCHOLOGICAL position, not biological number.** And the first-born's signature experience is **DETHRONEMENT**; the second-born's is having a **PACEMAKER** ahead.

8. **Style of life is set by about age 4-5** and is **creatively constructed by the child** (the creative power of the self), not merely imposed by environment - hence Adler is **soft-deterministic**, not deterministic.

9. **Three life tasks (Work, Friendship, Love) are ADLER's; the Self and Spiritual tasks were added by MOSAK** (with Dreikurs). Do not credit all five to Adler.

10. **Early recollections are PROJECTIVE indicators of the CURRENT lifestyle, not factual childhood causes.** Their literal accuracy is irrelevant; the selective theme matters. Contrast Freud, for whom early memories are causal events.

11. **Fictional finalism comes from Vaihinger's philosophy of "As If."** A subjective, fictional FUTURE goal that pulls behaviour.

12. **"Spitting in the client's soup"** = revealing the hidden payoff of a behaviour to spoil its appeal. Do not confuse with **paradoxical intention** (deliberately exaggerate the symptom) or with the **push-button technique** (alternate pleasant/unpleasant memories to show you create your feelings).

13. **The four phases in order: Relationship → Assessment/Analysis → Insight/Interpretation → Reorientation.** Insight serves **action/reorientation** (unlike Freud, where insight is the cure).

14. **Encouragement is the core technique** of Adlerian therapy, and **discouragement** is the root of maladjustment. The relationship is **egalitarian**, not hierarchical (vs Freud's neutral analyst).

15. **Rudolf Dreikurs**, not Adler, formalised the **four goals of children's misbehaviour** (attention, power, revenge, inadequacy) and brought Individual Psychology to the USA. Adler is the founder; **Dreikurs** is the chief populariser/applier.

16. **"Masculine protest"** is Adler's term (the striving to be powerful rather than "weak/feminine"), reflecting his early **will-to-power** phase and his support for gender equality - not a Freudian term.
