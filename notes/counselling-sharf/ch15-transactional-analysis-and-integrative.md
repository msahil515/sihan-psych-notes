# Ch 15: Transactional Analysis & Integrative Approaches

**Overview:** This chapter pairs two things Sharf treats as a closing unit. **Part A** is **Transactional Analysis (TA)**, developed by **Eric Berne** (1910-1970) and popularised in his bestseller *Games People Play* (1964). TA is a theory of personality and social interaction built on three **ego states** (Parent, Adult, Child), the **transactions** (units of social exchange) that pass between them, the **strokes** (units of recognition) people seek, the **life positions** and **life scripts** formed in childhood, and the repetitive **games** people play to confirm those scripts. Its goal is **autonomy**: awareness, spontaneity, and the capacity for intimacy. **Part B** is Sharf's note on **integrative and eclectic approaches**: the recognition that most working clinicians do not practise a single "pure" theory, and the four named routes to combining theories (technical eclecticism, theoretical integration, assimilative integration, common factors). It includes two landmark systems: **Prochaska & DiClemente's Transtheoretical Model** (stages of change) and **Lazarus's Multimodal Therapy** (the BASIC ID). Both are heavily tested at MET / NIMHANS because they invite "which theorist said this?" matching items.

# PART A - TRANSACTIONAL ANALYSIS (ERIC BERNE)

## 15.1 Origins and Nature

- **Eric Berne**, a Canadian-born psychiatrist trained in psychoanalysis, broke from orthodox Freudian practice to create a system that was **observable, contractual, and accessible** to clients in plain language.
- TA is at once a **theory of personality**, a **theory of communication**, and a **method of therapy** (usually conducted in **groups**).
- It is grounded in three philosophical assumptions:
  1. **People are OK** (worthy of respect, capable of change).
  2. **Everyone has the capacity to think** (and therefore to decide their own destiny).
  3. **Decisions can be changed** (early decisions are **redecidable**).
- Berne's roots are partly psychoanalytic: the **Parent-Adult-Child** ego states loosely echo the Freudian **superego-ego-id**, but ego states are **observable behaviour patterns**, not hidden structures.

> **Key Point:** Ego states are NOT the same as Freud's superego/ego/id. Freud's structures are inferred and unconscious; Berne's ego states are **observable, here-and-now states of mind expressed in behaviour, voice, and posture**. The resemblance is thematic, not identical.

---

## 15.2 Ego States (Structural Analysis)

An **ego state** is a consistent pattern of feeling and experience directly related to a corresponding consistent pattern of behaviour. Berne identified three.

| Ego State | Source | Function / Content | Healthy Expression | Unhealthy Expression |
|-----------|--------|--------------------|--------------------|----------------------|
| **Parent (P)** | Internalised messages from parents/authority ("taught" concept of life) | Values, rules, "shoulds," caregiving | **Nurturing Parent**: supports, comforts, permits | **Critical (Controlling) Parent**: criticises, commands, prejudges |
| **Adult (A)** | Here-and-now reality processing ("thought" concept) | Rational data processing, problem-solving, probability estimation | Objective, computes facts, mediates between P and C | (Contaminated by P or C; rarely "too much" Adult) |
| **Child (C)** | Recordings of internal events from early childhood ("felt" concept) | Emotions, creativity, intuition, spontaneity | **Free (Natural) Child**: spontaneous, playful, creative; **Little Professor (Adult in the Child)**: intuitive, manipulative cleverness | **Adapted Child**: compliant or rebellious to please/defy the Parent |

- **Parent** subdivides functionally into **Nurturing Parent** and **Critical/Controlling Parent**.
- **Child** subdivides functionally into the **Free (Natural) Child**, the **Adapted Child** (compliant or rebellious), and the **Little Professor** (the intuitive, creative "Adult in the Child").
- A psychologically healthy person can move flexibly among all three ego states as the situation requires; pathology = being **stuck** in one, or having the boundaries between them disturbed.

### Structural pathology

- **Contamination**: the **Adult** is intruded upon by Parent or Child material, mistaken for reality.
  - **Parent contamination** = **prejudice** ("All teenagers are lazy" experienced as fact).
  - **Child contamination** = **delusion / distortion** (fears or fantasies treated as fact).
- **Exclusion**: an ego state is rigidly shut out (a **constant Parent**, **constant Adult**, or **constant Child**), e.g. the "always-on-duty" workaholic Parent who never plays.

> **Key Point:** **Structural analysis** examines the *make-up* of one person's ego states (how P, A, C are organised inside the individual). **Functional analysis** examines *how* those ego states are used in behaviour (Nurturing vs Critical Parent; Free vs Adapted Child). Structure = anatomy; function = behaviour.

---

## 15.3 Transactions

A **transaction** is the basic unit of social intercourse: one person's **stimulus** plus another person's **response**, traced between ego states. Berne identified three types.

| Type | Definition | Diagram (vectors) | Outcome | Example |
|------|------------|-------------------|---------|---------|
| **Complementary** | Response comes from the ego state addressed, and is directed back to the ego state that sent the stimulus; vectors are **parallel** | A→A and A←A (or P→C and C←P, etc.) | Communication can continue **indefinitely**; smooth | "What time is it?" (A→A) / "It's three o'clock." (A→A) |
| **Crossed** | Response comes from an ego state different from the one addressed; vectors **cross** | A→A stimulus, but C→P response | Communication **breaks down**; source of conflict and hurt | "Where are my keys?" (A→A) / "Why do you always blame me?!" (C→P) |
| **Ulterior** | Two messages sent at once: an overt **social** message (usually A→A) and a covert **psychological** message (the real one); has a **double** level | Social vector + hidden vector | Behavioural outcome is decided at the **psychological (hidden)** level; the basis of **games** | "Would you like to see my etchings?" (social A→A; psychological C→C flirtation) |

- **Rule of communication 1**: as long as transactions are complementary, communication can proceed smoothly.
- **Rule of communication 2**: a crossed transaction interrupts communication; one or both parties must shift ego states to restart it.
- **Rule of communication 3**: the outcome of an **ulterior** transaction is determined at the **psychological** (hidden), not the social (surface), level. This rule is the engine of **games**.

> **Key Point:** **Crossed** transactions are where conflict starts; **ulterior** transactions are where games live. A complementary transaction can be perfectly healthy or perfectly dysfunctional, what matters is whether vectors are parallel, not whether the content is "good."

## 15.4 Strokes

- A **stroke** is a **unit of recognition** (a fundamental human need; Berne drew on the idea that infants deprived of physical touch fail to thrive, "If you are not stroked, your spinal cord will shrivel up").
- Strokes are classified along two axes:

| | **Conditional** (for *doing*) | **Unconditional** (for *being*) |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | "Good job on that report." | "I love you." / "I'm glad you exist." |
| **Negative** | "That report was sloppy." | "I hate you." / "You're worthless." |

- A **positive stroke** invites good feelings (a "warm fuzzy"); a **negative stroke** invites bad feelings (a "cold prickly").
- **Stroke economy**: Berne's follower Claude Steiner argued people are trained by restrictive parental rules ("don't give, don't ask for, don't accept, don't reject, don't self-stroke") to ration strokes artificially.
- **Key principle**: people would rather receive **negative** strokes than **no** strokes at all, stroke deprivation drives much game-playing. A child ignored will misbehave to be noticed.

---

## 15.5 Life Positions

Early in life (by around age 7) a child adopts a basic existential stance about self and others, the **life position**, captured in the famous **OK / not-OK grid** (popularised by Thomas Harris in *I'm OK, You're OK*).

| | **I'm OK** | **I'm Not OK** |
|---|---|---|
| **You're OK** | **I'm OK, You're OK** - the **healthy** position; "get-on-with" stance; trust, mutual respect; the goal of therapy | **I'm Not OK, You're OK** - depressive position; "get-away-from"; inferiority, self-blame, withdrawal |
| **You're Not OK** | **I'm OK, You're Not OK** - paranoid/blaming position; "get-rid-of"; projection, distrust, may become delusional/criminal | **I'm Not OK, You're Not OK** - futile/hopeless position; "get-nowhere-with"; despair, schizoid withdrawal, may be suicidal |

- Only **I'm OK, You're OK** is freely **chosen and Adult-based**; the other three are **emotionally based decisions** formed in early childhood.
- The life position underlies the **life script** and is reinforced by the **games** a person plays.

> **Key Point:** "I'm OK, You're OK" is the **healthy / goal** position and the one most likely to be a *conscious Adult choice*. The other three are early, feeling-based decisions. If asked "Which position is the therapeutic aim?" the answer is always **I'm OK, You're OK**.

---

## 15.6 Life Script and Script Analysis

- A **life script** is a **preconscious life plan**, decided in childhood, reinforced by parents, justified by subsequent events, and culminating in a chosen alternative (Berne's classic definition).
- It is laid down through **injunctions** (negative "don't" messages from the parent's Child, e.g. "Don't be," "Don't be close," "Don't grow up," "Don't succeed"), **counterinjunctions / drivers** (the parent's Parent "should" messages, e.g. **Be Perfect, Please Others, Try Hard, Be Strong, Hurry Up**, the **five drivers**), and **permissions** (freeing messages, e.g. "It's OK to think for yourself").
- Scripts can be **winning** ("winner"), **losing** ("loser"/hamartic, ending in tragedy), or **non-winning** ("banal"/at-rest, going nowhere).
- **Script analysis** identifies the life plan the client is following and the early decisions behind it, so that the client can make a **redecision** (see 15.9).

---

## 15.7 Games and the Drama Triangle

- A **game** is an **ongoing series of complementary ulterior transactions progressing to a well-defined, predictable outcome** (a **payoff** of bad/racket feelings). Games are played outside Adult awareness.
- Berne's formula: **Con + Gimmick = Response → Switch → Crossup → Payoff** (the famous **G formula**). A hidden hook (con) catches a vulnerability (gimmick); a series of transactions follows; then a sudden **switch** of roles produces confusion (crossup) and the **payoff** of familiar bad feelings.
- Classic games from *Games People Play*: **"Why Don't You / Yes But"** (YDYB), **"Now I've Got You, You SOB"** (NIGYSOB), **"Kick Me," "If It Weren't For You" (IWFY),** **"See What You Made Me Do," "Wooden Leg."**

### Karpman's Drama Triangle

Stephen Karpman mapped the role-switching of games onto a triangle of three roles. In a game, players occupy one role and then **switch**, which is what produces the payoff.

| Role | Stance | Underlying belief |
|------|--------|-------------------|
| **Persecutor** | "It's all your fault" - blames, criticises, controls | I'm OK, You're Not OK |
| **Rescuer** | "Let me help you" - helps from a one-up position, keeps others dependent | I'm OK, You're Not OK |
| **Victim** | "Poor me" - feels helpless, oppressed, seeks a Persecutor or Rescuer | I'm Not OK, You're OK |

- Both **Persecutor** and **Rescuer** keep the **Victim** "not OK." The therapeutic move is to step **off** the triangle into Adult responsibility.

> **Key Point:** The **drama triangle** (Persecutor, Rescuer, Victim) is **Karpman's**, not Berne's, though it formalises Berne's games. The defining event of a game is the **switch** of roles, which delivers the payoff of bad feelings.

---

## 15.8 Rackets, Racket Feelings, and Trading Stamps

- A **racket feeling** is a familiar, **learned emotion** experienced in many different stress situations, and which is **maladaptive** as a way of solving problems. It was permitted/encouraged in the family of origin (e.g. a family that allows anger but forbids sadness raises an "angry racket").
- A **racket** is the **behaviour pattern** by which a person manipulates the environment to justify experiencing the racket feeling, the racket feeling is the substitute emotion that *covers* an authentic, forbidden feeling.
- **Trading stamps**: people **collect** racket feelings like saving stamps, then "cash them in" for a guilt-free payoff (e.g. saving up anger to justify an outburst, or saving up "depression" to justify quitting).

---

## 15.9 Time Structuring

Berne held that people **structure time** to obtain strokes, ordered from **least** to **most** intimate (and least to most risky):

1. **Withdrawal** - no transaction with others; staying in one's own fantasy/Parent or Child; safest, fewest strokes.
2. **Rituals** - stereotyped, programmed exchanges (greetings: "Hi, how are you?" "Fine, you?"); safe, predictable, low-stroke.
3. **Pastimes** - semi-ritual talk about safe topics ("small talk": weather, sports, "PTA," cars); fills time and gives mild strokes.
4. **Activities** - work and goal-directed dealing with external reality ("getting things done"); productive strokes.
5. **Games** - repetitive ulterior transactions producing a bad-feeling payoff; higher-intensity (though negative) strokes.
6. **Intimacy** - direct, game-free, candid expression of feelings between OK people; the **most rewarding** and most **risky**; the goal.

> **Key Point:** **Intimacy** (game-free closeness) is the most rewarding way to structure time and the therapeutic aim; **withdrawal** is the safest but most stroke-poor. **Games** rank *above* activities in stroke intensity because they deliver strong (if negative) recognition.

## 15.10 Goals, Techniques, and Contracts

### Goal: Autonomy

The overarching goal of TA is **autonomy**, defined by Berne as the recovery of three capacities:

1. **Awareness** - the capacity to experience the here-and-now directly (Adult and Free Child), uncontaminated by Parent/Child distortions.
2. **Spontaneity** - the freedom to choose among the full range of ego states and feelings, rather than being script-bound.
3. **Intimacy** - the capacity for open, game-free, authentic relationship.

Therapy aims to free clients from their **scripts**, strengthen the **Adult**, decontaminate ego states, and reach the **I'm OK, You're OK** position.

### The four levels of TA technique

- **Structural analysis** - analysing the client's ego states (P, A, C) and their contaminations/exclusions.
- **Transactional analysis (proper)** - analysing the exchanges between people (complementary, crossed, ulterior).
- **Game analysis** - identifying the recurrent games and their payoffs.
- **Script analysis** - uncovering and rewriting the life script.

### Redecision

- The **Redecision school** (Robert & Mary Goulding), blending TA with Gestalt, holds that because the original script decisions were made by the **Child**, lasting change requires a **redecision** made from the Child ego state in an emotionally vivid (often Gestalt two-chair) moment, not merely an Adult insight.

### Contracts

- TA is explicitly **contractual**: client and therapist agree on a clear, specific, attainable, measurable **treatment contract** ("By when will you do what?"). The contract embodies the OK-OK, Adult-Adult, mutually responsible relationship and protects against game-playing inside therapy itself.

### Criticisms

- **Jargon-heavy** and **popularised** (the catchy game names invited pop-psychology dilution).
- **Uneven empirical evidence**; few controlled outcome studies.
- Concepts can be **applied glibly** as labels rather than as careful analysis.

# PART B - INTEGRATIVE & ECLECTIC APPROACHES

## 15.11 Why Integration Matters

- Surveys consistently show that the **single most common theoretical orientation** among practising clinicians is **"eclectic" or "integrative."** Most counsellors are not "pure" anything.
- Drivers of the integration movement: dissatisfaction with single-school rigidity; the **Dodo bird verdict** (Rosenzweig/Luborsky: "Everyone has won and all must have prizes", many therapies show roughly equal outcomes); the search for **common factors**; the demand to **fit the therapy to the client**, not the client to a theory.
- The field is organised by the **Society for the Exploration of Psychotherapy Integration (SEPI)**.

## 15.12 The Four Routes to Integration

| Route | Core idea | What is combined | Representative figure / example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Technical eclecticism** | Use whatever **techniques** work for this client/problem, **regardless of the theory** they come from; pragmatic, atheoretical at the level of mixing | Techniques only (theory stays in the background) | **Arnold Lazarus** - **Multimodal Therapy / BASIC ID** |
| **Theoretical integration** | **Blend two or more theories** into a new conceptual whole that is more than the sum of its parts | Theories themselves | **Paul Wachtel** - cyclical psychodynamics (psychoanalytic + behavioural); Dollard & Miller (earlier) |
| **Assimilative integration** | Work from **one "home" theory** as a base while **importing** selected techniques from others into it | One theory + borrowed techniques | Assimilative psychodynamic therapy (Stricker & Gold) |
| **Common-factors approach** | Identify and use the **factors common to all therapies** that actually drive change | The shared ingredients | **Jerome Frank**; the **therapeutic alliance** (Rogerian conditions, hope, a credible rationale, a healing ritual) |

> **Key Point:** Distinguish **technical eclecticism** from **theoretical integration**. Eclecticism mixes **techniques** while staying agnostic about theory (Lazarus); integration fuses the **theories** themselves into a new model (Wachtel). "Assimilative" = one home base + borrowed tools; "common factors" = use what all therapies share.

### Common factors (what all therapies share)

- A **therapeutic relationship / alliance** (warmth, empathy, trust).
- A **healing setting** and a **rationale** (a believable explanation of the problem).
- A **ritual or procedure** requiring active participation.
- The instillation of **hope** and **expectancy** of improvement (Frank's "demoralisation" model).

---

## 15.13 Prochaska & DiClemente - The Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change)

The **Transtheoretical Model (TTM)** is the leading **common-factors / integrative** framework. It asks not *which* therapy but *where the client is in the change process*, and matches intervention to stage. Developed by **James Prochaska** and **Carlo DiClemente**.

| Stage | Client's relationship to the problem | Clinical task |
|-------|--------------------------------------|---------------|
| **1. Precontemplation** | No intention to change in the foreseeable future; often unaware or in denial ("I don't have a problem") | Raise awareness; avoid pushing; consciousness-raising |
| **2. Contemplation** | Aware a problem exists and thinking about change "someday," but **not yet committed**; ambivalent | Tip the decisional balance; explore pros and cons |
| **3. Preparation** | Intends to act **soon** (within ~30 days); has a plan; may have taken small steps | Help build a concrete plan and commitment |
| **4. Action** | Actively modifying behaviour, experiences, or environment (the visibly "changing" stage) | Reinforce, support, teach coping and substitution |
| **5. Maintenance** | Sustaining the change and **preventing relapse** (roughly 6 months onward) | Relapse prevention; consolidate gains |
| **6. Termination** | Zero temptation and total self-efficacy; the problem no longer exerts pull (often described as the ideal endpoint; some behaviours stay in maintenance instead) | Exit; the change is complete |

- Change is **cyclical / spiral**, not linear: **relapse** is normal and returns the person to an earlier stage rather than to "square one."
- TTM also specifies **processes of change** (e.g. consciousness-raising, self-reevaluation, counterconditioning, stimulus control, reinforcement management) and the constructs of **decisional balance** and **self-efficacy**.

> **Key Point:** Memorise the order: **Pre**contemplation → **Contemplation** → **Preparation** → **Action** → **Maintenance** → **Termination**. The classic distractor is mixing up **precontemplation** ("no intention, often unaware") with **contemplation** ("aware, ambivalent, thinking about it"). Relapse is expected and is **not** a separate forward stage.

---

## 15.14 Lazarus - Multimodal Therapy (BASIC ID)

**Arnold Lazarus** developed **Multimodal Therapy**, the prototype of **technical eclecticism**. He held that a person is the product of **seven interacting modalities**, and that comprehensive assessment and treatment must address **all** of them, selecting techniques from any theory that fit each modality.

The seven modalities spell the acronym **BASIC ID**:

| Letter | Modality | What it covers |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| **B** | **Behaviour** | Observable acts, habits, responses |
| **A** | **Affect** | Emotions, moods, feelings |
| **S** | **Sensation** | Bodily sensations (tension, pain, pleasure) |
| **I** | **Imagery** | Mental images, self-image, fantasies, dreams |
| **C** | **Cognition** | Thoughts, beliefs, attitudes, values |
| **I** | **Interpersonal** | Relationships and interactions with others |
| **D** | **Drugs / Biology** | Physical health, medication, nutrition, exercise (the biological modality) |

- Lazarus used a **Modality Profile** (a chart listing problems and matched techniques across all seven modalities) and **tracking** (the typical *firing order* of a client's modalities, e.g. Sensation → Cognition → Affect) to tailor treatment.
- Multimodal Therapy is **technically eclectic**: it draws techniques from behavioural, cognitive, and other schools **without** importing their theories. Lazarus explicitly distinguished his position from theoretical integration, which he considered conceptually muddled.

> **Key Point:** **BASIC ID** = Lazarus = Multimodal Therapy = the model case of **technical eclecticism**. The final **"D"** is **Drugs/Biology** (the biological modality), not "Drives." A frequent trap is mis-expanding the acronym or attributing it to Beck or Ellis.

## 15.15 Critique of Integration

- **For**: matches the reality of practice; client-centred and pragmatic; supported by common-factors and Dodo-bird research; flexible across problems and cultures.
- **Against**: risk of **undisciplined "kitchen-sink" eclecticism** (mixing techniques with no rationale); theoretical incoherence when incompatible theories are forced together; harder to **train, manualise, and research** than a single school; the danger of being a **jack of all theories, master of none**.

> **Key Point:** Sharf's distinction: **systematic / technical eclecticism** (Lazarus, principled, assessment-driven) is respectable; **unsystematic ("syncretic") eclecticism** (random technique-grabbing without a guiding framework) is the thing critics rightly attack.

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **Ego states** are **Parent, Adult, Child** (Berne). Parent = "taught" (Nurturing vs Critical); Adult = "thought" (rational data-processor); Child = "felt" (Free/Natural, Adapted, and the **Little Professor**). They are **observable**, not Freud's hidden superego/ego/id.

2. **Structural vs functional analysis**: structural = the **internal make-up** of P-A-C; functional = **how** they behave (Nurturing/Critical Parent, Free/Adapted Child). Do not confuse with the four *levels* of technique.

3. **Transaction types**: **complementary** (parallel vectors, smooth, can continue), **crossed** (vectors cross, communication breaks down, source of conflict), **ulterior** (two-level: social + psychological hidden message, the basis of games). Outcome of an ulterior transaction is set at the **psychological** level.

4. **Life positions** - all four: **I'm OK, You're OK** (healthy, Adult-chosen, therapeutic goal), I'm Not OK/You're OK (depressive), I'm OK/You're Not OK (paranoid/blaming), I'm Not OK/You're Not OK (futile/hopeless). Only the first is freely chosen.

5. **Games** = repetitive **complementary ulterior** transactions ending in a predictable **payoff** of bad feelings; the defining moment is the **switch** (Berne's G formula). *Games People Play* (1964) is the source book.

6. **Drama triangle** = **Karpman**, not Berne: **Persecutor, Rescuer, Victim**. Both Persecutor and Rescuer keep the Victim "not OK." Players **switch** roles.

7. **Strokes** = units of recognition; classified **positive/negative** × **conditional/unconditional**. People prefer **negative strokes to no strokes**, stroke deprivation drives game-playing.

8. **Time structuring** ranked least-to-most intimate: **withdrawal, rituals, pastimes, activities, games, intimacy**. **Intimacy** is the goal (most rewarding, riskiest); **games** rank above activities in stroke intensity.

9. **Autonomy** (the TA goal) = **awareness + spontaneity + intimacy**. **Redecision** (the Gouldings, TA + Gestalt) is made from the **Child** ego state, not by Adult insight alone.

10. **Rackets / racket feelings**: a racket feeling is a **learned substitute emotion** that covers a forbidden authentic feeling; **trading stamps** = collecting racket feelings to cash in for a payoff.

11. **Four routes to integration**: **technical eclecticism** (mix techniques, ignore theory - Lazarus), **theoretical integration** (fuse theories - Wachtel), **assimilative integration** (one home theory + borrowed techniques), **common factors** (use the shared active ingredients - Frank, the alliance).

12. **Technical eclecticism vs theoretical integration** is the single most-tested distinction here: techniques-without-theory (Lazarus) vs theories-fused-into-a-new-whole (Wachtel). Do not swap them.

13. **Stages of change (Transtheoretical Model, Prochaska & DiClemente)** in order: **Precontemplation → Contemplation → Preparation → Action → Maintenance → Termination**. Classic trap: precontemplation (no intention/unaware) vs contemplation (aware but ambivalent). Change is **cyclical**; relapse is expected.

14. **BASIC ID (Lazarus, Multimodal Therapy)**: **B**ehaviour, **A**ffect, **S**ensation, **I**magery, **C**ognition, **I**nterpersonal, **D**rugs/Biology. The final **D = Drugs/Biology**, NOT "Drives." It is the example of **technical eclecticism**.

15. **Matching shortcuts**: Parent-Adult-Child / games / scripts / strokes / life positions / "I'm OK–You're OK" = **Berne (TA)**. Multimodal Therapy / BASIC ID / technical eclecticism = **Lazarus**. Stages of change = **Prochaska & DiClemente**. Drama triangle = **Karpman**. Common factors / demoralisation = **Frank**. Redecision = **the Gouldings**.

## Self-Test

1. Name and define Berne's three ego states, including the two subtypes of Parent and the three aspects of the Child. Which "concept of life" does each correspond to (taught, thought, felt)?
2. Distinguish **structural analysis** from **functional analysis**. Then distinguish **contamination** from **exclusion**, and say which contamination produces prejudice and which produces delusion.
3. Define complementary, crossed, and ulterior transactions, give one example of each, and state which one underlies games and why.
4. Draw the four-cell life-position grid. Label all four positions, give each a "get-..." slogan, and identify which one is the therapeutic goal and why.
5. What is a **game** in TA? Lay out Berne's G formula and explain the role of the **switch** and the **payoff**. Then place the game's roles on Karpman's drama triangle.
6. Classify strokes on both axes and give an example in each of the four cells. Why would a person seek negative strokes?
7. List the six ways of structuring time from least to most intimate. Why do games rank above activities, and what is the ultimate goal?
8. Define autonomy in TA. What is a **redecision**, from which ego state is it made, and which school is associated with it?
9. Name and describe the four routes to integration, with a representative figure or example for each. Which one is "techniques without theory" and which is "theories fused"?
10. List the six stages of change in order and define each in one line. Where do most distractor items try to trip you up, and how is relapse handled in the model?
11. Expand the acronym **BASIC ID** in full, name its originator and the therapy, and state which of the four integration routes it exemplifies. What does the final letter actually stand for?
12. State the case **for** and **against** integration. Distinguish systematic/technical eclecticism from unsystematic ("syncretic") eclecticism, and say which one Sharf and critics defend.
