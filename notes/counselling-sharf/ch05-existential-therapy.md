# Ch 5: Existential Therapy (May, Yalom, Frankl)

**Overview:** Existential therapy is less a single technique-driven system and more a *philosophical attitude* toward what it means to be human, brought into the consulting room. It has **no single founder**: it grew out of European existential philosophy (Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Heidegger, Sartre) and phenomenology (Husserl), was carried into psychiatry by the **Daseinsanalysts** (Ludwig Binswanger and Medard Boss), and was given its three main therapeutic voices by **Rollo May** (who introduced existentialism to American psychology and centred it on anxiety, the daimonic, and courage), **Viktor Frankl** (who built **logotherapy** around the *will to meaning*, forged in the Nazi concentration camps), and **Irvin Yalom** (who systematised the approach around **four ultimate concerns**: death, freedom, isolation, meaninglessness). Where most theories ask "what is wrong and how do we fix it," existential therapy asks "how is this person living, avoiding, and choosing in the face of the unalterable conditions of existence." Distress is not primarily a product of the past (psychoanalysis) or of faulty learning (behaviourism) but of how the person confronts, or flees, the **givens of existence**. The core wager is **existence precedes essence**: there is no fixed human nature handed to us in advance; we are radically free, and we *create ourselves* through our choices. Therapy is therefore aimed not at symptom removal but at a fuller, more **authentic** way of living. This chapter gives the deep, exam-grade treatment tested at NIMHANS and MET, kept inside Sharf's recurring frame: roots → view of human nature → key figures → goals → relationship → techniques → applications → critique.

## PART ONE: PHILOSOPHICAL ROOTS

## 5.1 Why a Philosophy Becomes a Therapy

Existential therapy is unusual among the schools in Sharf because it descends directly from a body of **philosophy** rather than from clinical observation or experiment. To understand the therapy you must first understand the thinkers it rests on. The throughline across all of them is a rejection of abstract, system-building philosophy in favour of the **concrete, lived experience of the individual existing person** ("existence" = *ex-sistere*, to "stand out," to be there).

> **Key Point:** The defining existential claim is **"existence precedes essence"** (Sartre). We are not born with a fixed essence/nature that determines us; we first exist, and then through free choice we *make* who we are. This is the philosophical opposite of both Freudian determinism (the past makes us) and behavioural determinism (conditioning makes us).

---

## 5.2 The Key Philosophers

### Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855), Danish; the "father of existentialism"
- First to make **anxiety (angst) and dread** central to the human condition. Distinguished ordinary fear (of a definite object) from **anxiety**, which has no object: it is the dizziness of **freedom**, the awareness that we *could* choose otherwise.
- Stressed **subjective truth** ("truth is subjectivity") and the **leap of faith**, meaning is achieved through committed personal choice, not detached reasoning.
- Source of the idea that anxiety is not merely pathological but the price and signal of being a free, choosing creature.

### Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900), German
- "**God is dead**": the collapse of inherited, given meaning, leaving humans to *create* their own values.
- The **will to power**, self-overcoming, and the **Übermensch** (the person who creates their own meaning and values). Stressed personal responsibility for one's life.

### Martin Heidegger (1889–1976), German; phenomenology → existential ontology
- Coined **Dasein** ("being-there"): the human being is not an object among objects but a being for whom its own *being is a question*.
- **Being-in-the-world** (*In-der-Welt-sein*): person and world are not separable; we are always already engaged in a world (the basis for the *Umwelt/Mitwelt/Eigenwelt* modes below).
- **Being-toward-death** (*Sein-zum-Tode*): authentic existence requires facing one's own finitude; awareness of death is what makes life urgent and authentic. The fall into the impersonal "**they**" (*das Man*) is the inauthentic flight from this.
- **Thrownness** (*Geworfenheit*): we are "thrown" into a particular time, body, and situation we did not choose, yet remain responsible for what we do with it.

### Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980), French
- "**Existence precedes essence**"; we are "**condemned to be free**", freedom is not optional, it is our inescapable condition, and with it comes total responsibility.
- **Bad faith** (*mauvaise foi*): the self-deception of denying one's freedom, pretending one "had no choice," treating oneself as a fixed thing. The direct opposite of authenticity.
- We are "**nothing**" until we act; the self is what we make through choices.

### Edmund Husserl (1859–1938), German; founder of **phenomenology**
- The method underlying existential therapy: study experience **as it presents itself to consciousness**, setting aside theories and presuppositions.
- **Bracketing / epoché**: suspend your assumptions and explanations to grasp the phenomenon directly. Consciousness is always **intentional** (consciousness *of* something).
- For the therapist this means meeting the client's world **on its own terms**, not slotting it into a diagnostic category.

### Martin Buber (1878–1965), Austrian-Jewish philosopher of dialogue
- The **I–Thou** vs **I–It** distinction. In **I–It** we relate to the other as an object to be used, analysed, categorised. In **I–Thou** we meet the other as a full, present subject in genuine, mutual encounter.
- Therapy at its best is an **I–Thou** meeting; the relationship itself, not technique, is the healing medium. (Buber's I–Thou is the philosophical backbone of the existential therapeutic relationship and is shared with Gestalt's dialogic stance.)

> **Key Point (founder map):** Kierkegaard = **anxiety/dread + leap of faith**; Nietzsche = **God is dead + will to power**; Heidegger = **Dasein, being-in-the-world, being-toward-death**; Sartre = **existence precedes essence, condemned to be free, bad faith**; Husserl = **phenomenology, bracketing**; Buber = **I–Thou**.

## 5.3 From Philosophy to the Clinic: Binswanger and Boss

- **Ludwig Binswanger** (1881–1966) and **Medard Boss** (1903–1990), Swiss psychiatrists, founded **Daseinsanalysis**, the first explicit application of Heidegger's philosophy to psychotherapy.
- They rejected reducing patients to drives and mechanisms; instead they sought to understand each patient's unique **world-design** (the structure of their being-in-the-world) phenomenologically.
- Binswanger elaborated the three simultaneous modes of being-in-the-world (below); Boss stayed closer to Heidegger. They are the bridge between European philosophy and the American existential therapists (May, Yalom).

## PART TWO: VIEW OF HUMAN NATURE AND THE MODES OF BEING

## 5.4 Being-in-the-World and Its Modes

For the existential therapist, a person cannot be understood apart from their world; subject and world are one structure (*being-in-the-world*, **Dasein**). Binswanger described the person as existing **simultaneously** in three (and in May's extension, four) interrelated modes. A person is never in just one mode at a time; psychological trouble often involves an **overdevelopment of one mode at the expense of another** (e.g., living almost entirely in the Umwelt of biological need, or having no Eigenwelt / no relationship to oneself).

| Mode | Meaning | The relationship it covers | Example of distress |
|------|---------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| **Umwelt** | "Around-world" | The **physical/biological** world, the natural environment, the body, drives, instincts, the laws of nature we are *thrown* into | Reducing life to bodily needs; ignoring the body; somatic anxiety |
| **Mitwelt** | "With-world" | The **social** world, relationships, community, the world of fellow humans (relation *with*, not mere use) | Loneliness, alienation, treating others as objects (I–It) |
| **Eigenwelt** | "Own-world" | The **self-relationship**, one's relationship to oneself, one's own inner experience and identity | No sense of self; not knowing what one feels or wants; self-estrangement |
| **Überwelt** (May's addition) | "Over-world" / "above-world" | The **spiritual / ideal** world, relationship to beliefs, values, the spiritual or transcendent, one's worldview | Spiritual emptiness; loss of guiding values or faith |

> **Key Point:** Memorise the three classic modes as a set: **Umwelt = physical/biological**, **Mitwelt = social/with-others**, **Eigenwelt = self/own-world**. The fourth, **Überwelt = spiritual/ideal**, is **Rollo May's addition** and is the most common distractor on exams. The person lives in all of them **at once**.

## PART THREE: THE KEY FIGURES

## 5.5 Rollo May (1909–1994): anxiety, the daimonic, courage

- The principal figure who **brought existential thought into American psychology and psychotherapy**, largely through his edited volume *Existence* (1958, with Angel and Ellenberger) and *The Meaning of Anxiety* (1950). Studied with the theologian Paul Tillich; his own near-fatal tuberculosis deepened his confrontation with death and finitude.
- **Anxiety as central and normal.** Following Kierkegaard, May argued anxiety is not just a symptom but a fundamental feature of being human, arising whenever a value the person holds essential to existence is threatened. He drew the crucial distinction (below) between **normal** and **neurotic** anxiety.
- **The daimonic.** Any natural function (sex, anger, the urge for power, creativity) that has the power to **take over the whole person**. The daimonic is **neither good nor evil in itself**, it is potential energy. Repressed and denied, it becomes destructive (it "possesses" the person); integrated and acknowledged, it becomes a source of creativity and vitality. Health = *integrating* the daimonic, not suppressing it.
- **Courage and will.** Authentic living requires the **courage to be** (a phrase from Tillich), the courage to affirm oneself and create meaning despite anxiety, freedom, and the certainty of death. May emphasised **intentionality** and the recovery of **will** in an age he saw as paralysed.
- **The human dilemma:** the human is simultaneously **subject** (the one who experiences and chooses) and **object** (a thing shaped by the world). Holding both poles is the existential task.

## 5.6 Viktor Frankl (1905–1997): logotherapy and the will to meaning

- Viennese psychiatrist and neurologist; survived **Auschwitz and other Nazi camps**, where he observed that those who kept a sense of **meaning** and a future to live for were most likely to survive. Recorded in *Man's Search for Meaning*. He called logotherapy the "**third Viennese school of psychotherapy**" (after Freud's psychoanalysis and Adler's individual psychology).
- **The will to meaning.** Frankl's central motivational claim: the primary human drive is not Freud's **will to pleasure** nor Adler's **will to power** but the **will to meaning**, the striving to find purpose. *Logos* = meaning. (Useful contrast triad: **Freud = will to pleasure; Adler = will to power; Frankl = will to meaning**.)
- **Existential vacuum / existential frustration.** When the will to meaning is blocked, the person experiences an **existential vacuum**, a pervasive sense of **emptiness, boredom, and meaninglessness**. Chronic frustration of meaning can produce a **noögenic neurosis** (a neurosis arising from a *spiritual/meaning* conflict, not a psychological/drive conflict).
- **Three sources of meaning (the value triad):** meaning can always be found through **(1) creative values**, what we *give* to the world (work, deeds, creation); **(2) experiential values**, what we *take* from the world (love, beauty, encounter); and **(3) attitudinal values**, the **stand we take toward unavoidable suffering**. The third is the most distinctive: even when a situation cannot be changed, we remain free to choose our *attitude* toward it. This is the "last of the human freedoms."
- **The tragic triad.** The three unavoidable negatives of existence: **pain (suffering), guilt, and death**. Logotherapy's "**tragic optimism**" = saying "yes to life" and finding meaning *in spite of* the tragic triad.
- **Self-transcendence.** The more a person forgets themselves (by devoting themselves to a cause or another person), the more human and self-actualised they become. Meaning is found in the *world*, not in self-absorption, the basis of dereflection.
- **Logotherapy techniques** (the one genuinely technique-rich corner of existential therapy):
  - **Paradoxical intention**, the client is encouraged to **deliberately wish for, or intend, the very thing they fear** (e.g., an insomniac tries to stay awake; a person with a fear of sweating tries to sweat buckets). This breaks the **anticipatory anxiety** cycle that maintains the symptom, often using **humour** and self-detachment. Used for phobias and obsessive-compulsive patterns.
  - **Dereflection**, the client is directed to **shift attention away from the self and the symptom** toward something meaningful outside themselves (counteracting "**hyper-reflection**," excessive self-observation; classically applied to sexual dysfunction and sleep problems).
  - **Socratic dialogue**, gentle, leading questioning that helps the client discover the meanings already latent in their own life and choices.

> **Key Point:** **Paradoxical intention vs dereflection** is a favourite trap. *Paradoxical intention* = intend the feared symptom (break anticipatory anxiety) → for **phobic/obsessive** anxiety. *Dereflection* = redirect attention *away* from the self/symptom (counter hyper-reflection) → classically for **sexual dysfunction and insomnia**.

## 5.7 Irvin Yalom (b. 1931): the four ultimate concerns

- American existential psychiatrist; his *Existential Psychotherapy* (1980) is the most systematic statement of the approach. Also the leading writer on **group psychotherapy** (the therapeutic factors) and a noted novelist of therapy (*Love's Executioner*, *When Nietzsche Wept*).
- Yalom argued that the deepest source of inner conflict is the individual's confrontation with **four ultimate concerns** (the **givens of existence**). Defence against, and avoidance of, these givens generates psychopathology; facing them honestly is the path to authentic living. These four givens are the single most testable item in the chapter (see table below).
- For Yalom the relationship is central, the therapist is fully **present** and transparent ("a fellow traveller"), and **here-and-now** work is the engine of change.

---

## PART FOUR: THE FOUR ULTIMATE CONCERNS (YALOM)

## 5.8 Yalom's Four Givens of Existence

| Ultimate concern (given) | The confrontation | How it generates distress when avoided | Healthy response |
|--------------------------|-------------------|----------------------------------------|------------------|
| **Death** | We will die; existence is finite, and so is everyone we love. The conflict is between the wish to continue to be and the awareness of inevitable death. | **Death anxiety**; symptoms and defences built to deny mortality (e.g., the belief in personal **specialness**, or in an **ultimate rescuer**). | Awareness of death gives life urgency and authenticity; it can be an "awakening experience" that reorders priorities. |
| **Freedom & Responsibility** | We are the uncaused authors of our own lives; beneath us is no ground, no fixed structure. With freedom comes total **responsibility** for our choices. | The **groundlessness** of freedom is dizzying; people flee responsibility (displacing it onto others, "I can't," avoidance, indecision = avoiding willing). | Owning one's freedom and authorship; taking responsibility; **willing** and deciding. |
| **Isolation (existential)** | No matter how close our relationships, there is an unbridgeable gap: we enter and leave existence alone. (Distinct from interpersonal or intrapersonal isolation.) | Clinging, fusing with others to deny aloneness; using relationships to avoid the self. | Confront aloneness directly so one can relate to others out of strength (love), not out of need. |
| **Meaninglessness** | The universe hands us no inherent meaning; we must **create** our own. The conflict: a meaning-seeking creature thrown into a universe with no built-in meaning. | The **existential vacuum**, emptiness, boredom, apathy, "what's the point." | **Engagement**, committed involvement in life creates meaning; meaning is a by-product of engagement, not something found by searching directly. |

> **Key Point:** Lock the four givens of Yalom in order and by name: **Death, Freedom (and Responsibility), Isolation, Meaninglessness.** Common distractors: "anxiety" and "guilt" are *consequences* of confronting the givens, not givens themselves; "death" is the most frequently tested. Yalom = **four ultimate concerns**; Frankl = **will to meaning / logotherapy**; May = **anxiety + the daimonic**.

## PART FIVE: ANXIETY, AUTHENTICITY, AND GUILT

## 5.9 Normal vs Neurotic Anxiety

A central existential reframe: **anxiety is not by itself pathological**. To exist as a free, finite, choosing being *is* to be anxious. The therapeutic question is not "how do we remove anxiety" but "is this anxiety normal (to be lived with and used) or neurotic (to be reduced)."

| | **Normal (existential) anxiety** | **Neurotic anxiety** |
|---|----------------------------------|----------------------|
| Proportion | **Proportionate** to the actual threat | **Disproportionate** to the threat |
| Repression | Need not be repressed; can be faced | Involves **repression** and blocking of awareness |
| Effect | **Constructive**, can be confronted creatively; spurs growth and the courage to be | **Destructive**, paralysing, restricts awareness and possibility |
| Source | Inherent in confronting the givens of existence (freedom, death, choice) | Defensive flight from the givens; over-reaction |

- **Existential (normal) anxiety** is the inevitable accompaniment of freedom: every genuine choice forecloses other possibilities (Kierkegaard's "dizziness of freedom"). The mature person **lives with** it rather than eliminating it.
- **Existential guilt** is likewise not (primarily) neurotic guilt over a transgression but the guilt of **unrealised possibility**, the sense that one has *not* lived authentically, has failed to actualise one's potential, has not honoured one's freedom. It can be a constructive call to live more fully.

## 5.10 Authenticity vs Bad Faith / Inauthenticity

- **Authenticity** = living in honest awareness of the givens of existence; owning one's freedom and responsibility; choosing and creating oneself rather than letting the world (or "the they," *das Man*) choose for you. The authentic person is the one with the **courage to be** despite anxiety.
- **Inauthenticity / bad faith** (Sartre's *mauvaise foi*) = the self-deception of denying one's freedom, "I had no choice," "this is just how I am," conformity to social roles to escape the burden of choice, treating oneself as a fixed object. Inauthenticity is the existential root of much psychological distress, and confronting the client's evasions of freedom is a core therapeutic move.

> **Key Point:** **Existential guilt** = guilt over **failing to fulfil one's potential / unlived life**, not guilt over a misdeed. **Bad faith** (Sartre) = denying one's freedom to escape responsibility = the opposite of **authenticity**.

## PART SIX: GOALS, RELATIONSHIP, AND TECHNIQUE

## 5.11 Goals of Therapy

Existential therapy does **not** aim primarily at symptom reduction, cure of a "disorder," or behaviour change. Its goals are:
1. **Expand self-awareness**, help the client become more fully aware of their existence, choices, and possibilities.
2. **Accept freedom and responsibility**, move the client from "I can't / I had to" to owning that they choose, and bear responsibility for, their life.
3. **Confront the givens**, help the client face death, freedom, isolation, and meaninglessness rather than flee them.
4. **Live authentically**, close the gap between how the client lives and an honest awareness of the human condition; replace bad faith with authentic choice.
5. **Discover or create meaning** (especially Frankl), find purpose through creative, experiential, and attitudinal values; fill the existential vacuum through engagement.
6. **Develop the courage to be**, to act, choose, and commit despite the anxiety that existence entails.

## 5.12 The Therapeutic Relationship: Presence and Authenticity

In existential therapy **the relationship is the primary therapeutic instrument**, not a backdrop to technique.
- **Presence.** The therapist is fully, genuinely *with* the client in the moment, not analysing from behind a screen but really *there*. Yalom's image is of two travellers on a journey.
- **Authenticity / genuineness.** The therapist is real and transparent, willing to be affected and to disclose appropriately, modelling authentic existence.
- **I–Thou encounter (Buber).** The meeting is subject-to-subject, mutual and reciprocal, not expert-to-object. This is the relational ideal.
- **Phenomenological understanding.** The therapist **brackets** their own theories and enters the client's subjective world on its own terms (Husserl).

> **Key Point:** For existential therapy the relationship is *the* method; **presence and authenticity** are valued above any technique. This is close to Rogers' core conditions, but note the distinction: Rogers' relationship cultivates the client's **actualising tendency** (an optimistic, growth-oriented view of human nature), whereas the existential relationship is the arena for **confronting the harsh givens of existence** (a more tragic, less rosy view of the human condition).

## 5.13 Techniques and the Deliberate Deemphasis of Technique

- Existential therapy is **deliberately light on technique**. May warned that focusing on technique reduces the client to an *object* to be manipulated (an I–It relation) and so betrays the entire approach. There is no fixed protocol, no manual, no set sequence.
- Therapists therefore **borrow techniques freely** from other approaches (Gestalt experiments, behavioural methods, dream work), using them only in the service of expanding awareness and confronting the givens, the *attitude*, not the technique, is what makes the work existential.
- **Confrontation** of avoidance, evasion, and bad faith, gently challenging the ways the client flees freedom, responsibility, and the givens.
- **Exploration of choice and responsibility**, helping the client see where they are choosing (even by "not choosing") and own it.
- **Frankl's logotherapy** is the major exception, supplying the only set of named, distinctive techniques: **paradoxical intention, dereflection, and Socratic dialogue** (see 5.6). Note that paradoxical intention is **shared** with Adlerian/strategic work, but as a *named logotherapy technique* it is attributed to **Frankl**.
- **Group work** (Yalom): the group is a "social microcosm" in which members confront isolation, responsibility, and authenticity in the here-and-now.

## PART SEVEN: APPLICATIONS, MULTICULTURAL ISSUES, AND CRITIQUE

## 5.14 Applications and Evidence

- Best suited to **life transitions, bereavement and grief, terminal illness, facing death, the "existential vacuum"/meaning crises, mid-life issues, and clients who are reflective and verbal**. Yalom's work with cancer and dying patients is paradigmatic; Frankl's logotherapy is applied to suffering, addiction, and meaninglessness.
- **Evidence base.** Because it resists manualisation and standardised technique, existential therapy has a **thin controlled-outcome evidence base**; support is more **conceptual and qualitative** than experimental. Its influence is felt strongly in **humanistic, meaning-centred, and emotion-focused** therapies and in the modern emphasis on the therapeutic relationship and "common factors."

## 5.15 Multicultural and Gender Considerations + Critique

- **Multicultural strengths:** themes of meaning, suffering, death, and freedom are **universal**; the phenomenological, non-imposing stance respects each person's own world; it does not pathologise. Useful for clients grappling with displacement, discrimination, or loss of meaning.
- **Multicultural limits:** the emphasis on the **autonomous individual, personal freedom, and self-determination** is rooted in **Western individualism** and may fit poorly with **collectivist** cultures where identity and choice are embedded in family and community, or with clients whose real constraints (poverty, oppression) make "you are free to choose" feel dismissive of genuine external barriers.
- **General criticisms:**
  - **Vague and hard to operationalise**; concepts (authenticity, being-in-the-world) are abstract and difficult to define or measure.
  - **Few specific techniques**; offers little concrete guidance to beginning therapists ("what do I actually *do*?").
  - **Weak empirical / outcome research** relative to CBT.
  - Demands an **articulate, intelligent, reflective, verbal** client; poorly suited to those in acute crisis, with severe disturbance, or who want concrete, directive help.
  - Can seem **pessimistic / heavy** (death, isolation, meaninglessness) and may underplay the past, the unconscious, and biology.

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **Existential therapy has no single founder.** It is a *movement* with philosophical roots; May, Frankl, and Yalom are its main therapeutic voices. A question naming "the founder of existential therapy" is usually a trap.
2. **"Existence precedes essence" = Sartre.** It means there is no fixed human nature; we create ourselves through choice. Opposite of Freudian/behavioural determinism.
3. **The will-to triad:** **will to pleasure = Freud; will to power = Adler (and Nietzsche); will to meaning = Frankl.** Most-tested attribution in the chapter.
4. **Logotherapy = Frankl** (the "third Viennese school," after Freud and Adler). *Logos* = meaning.
5. **Paradoxical intention vs dereflection (both Frankl):** paradoxical intention = *intend the feared symptom* (breaks anticipatory anxiety; for phobias/OCD); dereflection = *shift attention away from self/symptom* (counters hyper-reflection; for sexual dysfunction/insomnia).
6. **The four ultimate concerns = Yalom: Death, Freedom/Responsibility, Isolation, Meaninglessness.** "Anxiety" and "guilt" are *not* givens, they are consequences of confronting the givens.
7. **The three modes of being:** **Umwelt = physical/biological; Mitwelt = social; Eigenwelt = self/own-world.** The fourth, **Überwelt = spiritual**, and **Überwelt is Rollo May's addition**, the classic distractor.
8. **Normal vs neurotic anxiety (May):** normal = proportionate, faceable, growth-promoting; neurotic = disproportionate, repressed, paralysing. Existential therapy does *not* try to eliminate normal anxiety.
9. **Existential guilt = failing to fulfil one's potential / the unlived life**, not guilt over a transgression.
10. **The daimonic (May)** is *neither good nor evil*; it is natural energy that becomes destructive only when repressed. Health = *integrating* it, not suppressing it.
11. **I–Thou vs I–It = Buber.** I–Thou = genuine subject-to-subject encounter (the therapeutic ideal); I–It = treating the other as an object. (Shared with Gestalt's dialogic relationship.)
12. **Bracketing / epoché / phenomenology = Husserl.** Suspend assumptions to grasp the client's experience directly. **Dasein / being-in-the-world / being-toward-death = Heidegger.**
13. **Daseinsanalysis = Binswanger and Boss** (the Swiss psychiatrists who first applied Heidegger to therapy). Binswanger is associated with the *modes of being / world-design*.
14. **Rollo May** = the figure who **brought existentialism to American psychology** (editor of *Existence*, 1958); associated with **anxiety, the daimonic, intentionality, and the courage to be** (the latter phrase from **Paul Tillich**).
15. **Frankl's tragic triad = pain (suffering), guilt, death**; his three sources of meaning = **creative, experiential, and attitudinal values** (attitudinal = the stance toward unavoidable suffering, the "last of the human freedoms"). **Existential vacuum** = the emptiness left when the will to meaning is frustrated; a meaning-conflict neurosis = **noögenic neurosis**.

## Self-Test

1. Why does existential therapy have "no single founder," and which three figures are its main therapeutic voices?
2. State "existence precedes essence" and explain how it opposes both psychoanalytic and behavioural determinism. Who said it?
3. Name and define the three classic modes of being-in-the-world, plus the fourth one and who added it.
4. List Yalom's four ultimate concerns. For each, give the avoidant (unhealthy) response and the authentic response.
5. Distinguish **normal** from **neurotic** anxiety on at least three dimensions. Why does existential therapy refuse to simply eliminate anxiety?
6. What is the **daimonic** (May)? Why is repressing it dangerous, and what is the healthy response?
7. Contrast Frankl's **paradoxical intention** and **dereflection**: what does each do, and which clinical problem is each classically used for?
8. Give Frankl's three sources of meaning (the value triad). Which is the most distinctive, and why? What is the "tragic triad"?
9. What is **existential guilt**, and how does it differ from ordinary (neurotic) guilt over a misdeed?
10. Explain Buber's **I–Thou vs I–It** and why presence and authenticity matter more than technique in existential therapy.
11. Why is existential therapy "deliberately light on technique," and how does it justify borrowing methods from other approaches?
12. Give two multicultural strengths and two multicultural limitations of the existential approach, and two general criticisms.
