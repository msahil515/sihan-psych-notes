# Ch 7: Gestalt Therapy (Fritz Perls)

**Overview:** Gestalt therapy is an **experiential, humanistic, present-centred** approach founded by **Fritz (Frederick) Perls**, with **Laura Perls** and **Paul Goodman**. It fuses three streams: **Gestalt perceptual psychology** (the figure/ground organisation of experience), **psychoanalysis** (which Perls trained in and then revolted against), and **existential-phenomenological philosophy** (authenticity, the I-Thou meeting, here-and-now experience). Its central claim is that psychological health is a matter of **awareness**: the healthy person is fully aware of their needs and feelings in the present moment and can make flexible contact with the environment to meet those needs. Disturbance is not buried trauma to be excavated (as in Freud) but a **disturbance of awareness and contact in the now**. The work is therefore not to explain the past but to heighten present awareness through **experiments** that make the avoided experience vivid. Sharf places Gestalt with the **humanistic/experiential** family alongside existential and person-centred therapy: like them it is growth-oriented and present-focused, but unlike Rogers' purely non-directive stance, the Gestalt therapist is **active, confrontational, and frustrating**, designing experiments and blocking the client's habitual escapes from awareness.

---

## 7.1 Roots: Where Gestalt Therapy Comes From

Gestalt therapy is a deliberate synthesis. To answer "which influence gave Gestalt what," hold these four strands apart.

### Gestalt perceptual psychology (the academic root)
- **Gestalt** is German for "whole / configuration / form." The core slogan: **"the whole is different from the sum of its parts"** (often rendered "greater than"). Perception is organised, not a passive mosaic of sensations.
- Founders of the perceptual school: **Max Wertheimer** (the **phi phenomenon** of apparent motion, 1912, usually dated as the start of Gestalt psychology), **Wolfgang Köhler** (insight learning in apes), and **Kurt Koffka** (systematiser; brought Gestalt ideas to America).
- Key perceptual principle borrowed by therapy: **figure and ground**. At any moment one thing stands out vividly (the **figure**) against a less differentiated background (the **ground**). The Gestalt laws of organisation (proximity, similarity, closure, continuity, **Prägnanz** / good form) describe how we form figures.
- **Closure**: the mind completes incomplete forms. Perls extends this directly into the clinical idea of **unfinished business** (an incomplete emotional gestalt that the organism presses to close).

> **Key Point:** Gestalt *therapy* (Perls) is historically and conceptually distinct from Gestalt *perceptual psychology* (Wertheimer/Köhler/Koffka). Perls **borrowed the figure/ground and closure concepts** but the Gestalt academic school did not create a therapy. A classic trap: do not credit Wertheimer with founding Gestalt therapy.

### Field theory
- From **Kurt Lewin** (a Gestalt-influenced social psychologist): behaviour is a function of the **total field** at a given moment, B = f(P, E). Nothing is understood in isolation; the person and environment form one **field**.
- In Gestalt therapy this becomes the **organism/environment field**: the person can never be understood apart from the context they are embedded in right now.

### Phenomenology
- The method of attending to **immediate, subjective experience** as it presents itself, bracketing theory and interpretation. The Gestalt therapist asks **"what"** and **"how"** (what are you aware of now, how are you doing that), almost never **"why"** (which invites theorising and rationalisation).

### Existential influence
- Authenticity, personal responsibility, choice, and the **I-Thou** relationship (Martin Buber) and **dialogue**. Gestalt is an existential therapy that takes the encounter and present existence seriously.

> **Key Point:** Memorise the four roots as a set: **Gestalt perception (figure/ground) + field theory (Lewin) + phenomenology + existentialism**. Psychoanalysis is the fifth ingredient, but as the tradition Perls *reacted against* (he kept the idea of disowned material but rejected interpretation, the past-focus, and the blank-screen analyst).

## 7.2 The Founders

| Person | Role |
|--------|------|
| **Fritz (Frederick) Perls** (1893-1970) | Principal founder; German-born psychiatrist, trained in psychoanalysis (analysed by Wilhelm Reich, influenced by Reich's "body armour"). Charismatic, confrontational; later associated with **Esalen Institute** in California. Co-wrote *Ego, Hunger and Aggression* (1942/1947) and *Gestalt Therapy* (1951). |
| **Laura Perls** (Lore Posner, 1905-1990) | Fritz's wife and co-founder; trained in Gestalt psychology and existential philosophy. Brought the dialogic, relational, supportive emphasis; sustained the New York Institute and the relational tradition that Fritz's flashy individual style overshadowed. |
| **Paul Goodman** | Co-author of the foundational *Gestalt Therapy: Excitement and Growth in the Human Personality* (1951, with Perls and Ralph Hefferline); gave the theory much of its written intellectual structure, especially the contact/boundary theory. |

> **Key Point:** Three names co-found Gestalt therapy: **Fritz Perls, Laura Perls, Paul Goodman**. The seminal 1951 book is *Gestalt Therapy* by Perls, Hefferline & Goodman. If asked for "the founder" the answer is Fritz Perls; if asked who co-founded it, include Laura Perls and Goodman.

---

## 7.3 Holism and the Organism/Environment Field

- Gestalt theory is **holistic**: a person is an indivisible whole of mind, body, emotion, and behaviour, not a stack of separate faculties. Splitting "the rational mind" from "the irrational body" is itself pathological.
- The person exists only within an **organism/environment field**. There is no organism without an environment to contact; the **boundary** between them is where all psychological life happens.
- **Homeostasis / organismic self-regulation**: the organism continually works to maintain balance by sensing its dominant need, making it the figure, contacting the environment to meet it, and then letting it recede. The organism is trusted to know what it needs if awareness is not blocked.

---

## 7.4 Awareness and the Here-and-Now

- **Awareness** is *the* central concept. To be aware is to be in contact with one's own present experience: sensations, feelings, wants, and what one is doing. Awareness itself is curative; "awareness per se can be curative" because a fully aware organism self-regulates.
- The only real time is **now**. Perls: *"Nothing exists except the here and now."* The past survives only as it lives in present awareness (memory) and the future only as present anticipation.
- The therapist keeps returning the client to the present: "What are you aware of now?" "What are you feeling in your body as you say that?" Talking *about* the past in the past tense is shifted into a present re-enactment (e.g., speaking to the absent person now, in an empty chair).

> **Key Point:** Gestalt is radically **here-and-now / present-centred**. Even when "the past" is worked, it is brought into the present and experienced now, not interpreted as historical cause. Contrast Freud (past as cause, interpreted) and Beck/Ellis (present thoughts, but disputed/restructured cognitively rather than experienced).

---

## 7.5 Figure-Formation and Organismic Self-Regulation

- The **figure-formation process** (the "Gestalt cycle of experience" / cycle of need): the most pressing need of the moment emerges from the background to become the **figure**; the organism mobilises energy and makes **contact** with the environment to meet it; the need is satisfied; the gestalt **closes** and recedes into the ground; awareness then opens to the next emerging figure.
- A typical sequence: **sensation → awareness → mobilisation of energy/excitement → action → contact → satisfaction → withdrawal/closure**.
- **Healthy functioning** = figures form clearly, are met, and close cleanly, in rhythm. **Disturbance** = the cycle is interrupted at some point; needs do not form clearly, energy is not mobilised, contact is avoided, or the gestalt cannot close (leaving **unfinished business**).

> **Key Point:** "Organismic self-regulation" means the organism will naturally meet its needs in priority order *if awareness is unobstructed*. Neurosis is a self-imposed interruption of this natural cycle, not a structural deficit.

---

## 7.6 The Contact Boundary and Contact Functions

- **Contact** is the experience of meeting the environment (or another person) at the **boundary** while remaining a distinct self. Good contact requires both **connection** and **separation**: you meet the other *and* know where you end and they begin.
- **Contact functions** are the means of contact: the senses (looking, listening, touching, tasting, smelling), **moving**, and **talking**. Healthy contact uses these vividly and flexibly.
- The **contact boundary** is the meeting point of organism and environment, the place where all growth happens ("contact is the lifeblood of growth"). Withdrawal after contact is equally healthy; the rhythm of **contact and withdrawal** is normal.
- **Disturbance** occurs when the boundary is chronically blurred, rigid, or misplaced, producing the **boundary (contact) disturbances** below. These are habitual interruptions of contact that block awareness.

---

## 7.7 The Five Boundary (Contact) Disturbances

These are the classic **resistances to contact** or **interruptions of the contact cycle**. They are the single most-tested Gestalt content. Learn each by its one-line signature *and* its contrast with the others.

| Disturbance | Definition | Boundary problem | Everyday example |
|-------------|-----------|------------------|------------------|
| **Introjection** | Swallowing others' beliefs, standards, and values **whole**, without "chewing" (examining/assimilating) them. The "shoulds" you took in undigested. | Boundary pulled too far **in**; environment occupies the self. | "I must always put others first" accepted unquestioned from a parent. |
| **Projection** | Disowning a part of oneself and attributing it to the environment / others. | Boundary pushed too far **out**; self-parts located outside. | "Everyone here is angry at me" when *I* am the angry one. |
| **Retroflection** | Turning back onto oneself what one wants to do to (or get from) others; doing to yourself what you wish to do to another. | Boundary closes **in on the self**; the self becomes both agent and target. | Self-harm or self-criticism instead of expressing anger outward; biting one's own lip instead of "biting back." |
| **Deflection** | Avoiding direct contact by being vague, abstract, joking, intellectualising, changing the subject, or not looking at the person. | Contact is **diffused / turned aside**; energy dissipates. | Answering a feeling question with a theory; laughing off something painful. |
| **Confluence** | Merging with another so that **no boundary** is felt; no sense of where self ends and other begins. | Boundary **absent / erased**; no separation. | "We never disagree, we feel exactly the same" in an enmeshed couple. |

- Memory hooks: **Intro**ject = take *in*; **Pro**ject = throw *out*; **Retro**flect = bend *back* on self; **De**flect = turn *aside*; **Con**fluence = *flow together* (no boundary).
- Some texts (and Polster) add **egotism** (stepping back to observe/comment rather than spontaneously contacting) as a sixth, but for MET hold the **canonical five**.

> **Key Point:** **Retroflection vs introjection vs projection** is the favourite distractor cluster. Introjection = others' values *in* without chewing. Projection = my disowned trait *out* onto others. Retroflection = I do to *myself* what I'd do to others (or vice versa). Confluence = *no* boundary at all. Deflection = avoiding contact by diffusing it.

---

## 7.8 Unfinished Business and the Impasse

- **Unfinished business**: unexpressed feelings (resentment, grief, anger, guilt, longing) associated with past situations that were never brought to completion. By the principle of **closure**, the organism keeps pressing to finish the incomplete gestalt, so the feeling intrudes on the present, draining energy and distorting current contact.
- Common form: unexpressed **resentment** toward a significant figure (often a parent). The unfinished gestalt re-emerges as a present figure until it is expressed and closed (frequently worked via the **empty chair**).
- The **impasse** (the "stuck point" / *sick point*): the moment in growth where the person feels they have **no external support** left but does **not yet believe they have the internal resources** to support themselves. They feel stuck, lost, confused, "nowhere." The Gestalt therapist neither rescues (which would reinforce dependence on environmental support) nor abandons, but **stays with** the client through the impasse so they discover their own **self-support**.

> **Key Point:** **Unfinished business** is closed by *expression and completion in the present*, not by historical insight. The **impasse** is the pivot from **environmental support to self-support**; getting through it is the engine of growth.

---

## 7.9 The Five Layers of Neurosis

Perls described personality as five **layers** (an "onion") that must be peeled to reach authentic, self-supporting living. This is a high-frequency exam list. Keep the order and the names exact.

| # | Layer | What happens at this layer |
|---|-------|----------------------------|
| 1 | **Phony (cliché) layer** | Living in stereotyped, inauthentic roles and games; superficial social clichés ("How are you?" "Fine"). Reacting to others as if they were not real. |
| 2 | **Phobic layer** | As the phony games drop, the person meets the **fears** of being seen authentically; avoidance and resistance to the emotional pain they have been hiding from. |
| 3 | **Impasse layer** | The **stuck point**: external support is gone, internal support not yet trusted; feeling of being lost, empty, "nowhere," not knowing how to go on. |
| 4 | **Implosive (death) layer** | Becoming aware of how one has deadened and constricted oneself; feelings turned inward; a paralysed contraction, "playing dead." The energy is there but frozen/imploded. |
| 5 | **Explosive layer** | Breaking through into genuine, alive emotion: the energy that was bound is released into authentic **explosion** (into joy, grief, anger, or orgasm). The mark of reaching the **authentic self**. |

- Mnemonic for the order: **Phony → Phobic → Impasse → Implosive → Explosive** ("**P**lease **P**eel **I**n **I**ncreasing **E**nergy," or simply Ph-Ph-I-I-E).

> **Key Point:** The **impasse** sits at the *centre* of the five layers (layer 3) and is also the broader growth concept in 7.8. The sequence ends with the **explosive** layer, the breakthrough into authentic emotion. Do not swap implosive and explosive: **implosive = collapsed/deadened inward; explosive = released outward.**

---

## 7.10 Polarities and Topdog/Underdog

- The personality contains many **polarities** (opposites): strong/weak, active/passive, masculine/feminine. Health is the **integration** of polarities, not the suppression of one side; disowned poles get projected or retroflected.
- The most famous polarity is the internal split between **topdog** and **underdog**, the source of the classic self-torture game:
  - **Topdog**: the moralising, demanding, "righteous" tyrant. Speaks in **shoulds, oughts, musts**; bullies and threatens ("You should work harder, you ought to be ashamed"). Largely the voice of **introjected** parental standards.
  - **Underdog**: the defensive, manipulative, evasive saboteur. Plays victim, agrees and then doesn't deliver, makes excuses ("Yes, but...", "I'll try, but I can't"). Wins by passive resistance.
- The topdog/underdog war is endless and exhausting because neither side ever wins; it is worked directly in the **two-chair** dialogue so the client can hear both voices, own them, and move toward integration.

> **Key Point:** **Topdog = the shoulds/the tyrant (introjected morality); underdog = the evasive resister/victim.** It is an *internal* polarity (a split within the self), enacted in the **two-chair** technique. Topdog usually "should" you; underdog usually "yes-but" you.

## 7.11 Goals of Therapy

The aims are not symptom removal or insight into the past, but:

1. **Awareness** (the master goal): to expand and deepen present-moment awareness of self, feelings, body, and contact. Awareness is held to be inherently curative.
2. **Integration**: to re-own disowned and projected parts (the polarities), heal the splits (topdog/underdog), and become a coherent whole. Maturation is the journey from fragmentation to wholeness.
3. **Responsibility**: to "own" one's experience, choices, and behaviour as one's own ("**response-ability**", the ability to respond). The client moves from blaming the environment to authoring their own experience.
4. **From environmental support to self-support** (Perls' maturity formula): the mature person draws on their own resources rather than manipulating the environment for support.

> **Key Point:** If asked for the single overarching goal of Gestalt therapy, the answer is **awareness** (leading to integration, responsibility, and self-support). It is *not* unconscious insight (Freud) and *not* cognitive restructuring (Beck/Ellis).

---

## 7.12 The Therapeutic Relationship: I-Thou and Dialogue

- The relationship is an authentic, **I-Thou** (Buber) **dialogic** meeting between two real people, not a blank-screen transference relationship (Freud) and not a purely received empathy (Rogers).
- Core elements of the **dialogic relationship**:
  - **Presence**: the therapist is fully, genuinely present, and brings their own real responses into the room.
  - **Inclusion**: entering the client's experience as fully as possible while staying oneself (close to empathy, but the therapist remains a separate, contactful presence).
  - **Commitment to dialogue**: surrendering to *what emerges between* the two, rather than steering to a predetermined outcome.
- The therapist is **active and may be confrontational**, deliberately **frustrating** the client's habitual avoidances and demands for environmental support so that awareness sharpens and self-support is forced to emerge. (The therapist frustrates the *manipulation*, while supporting the *person*.)

> **Key Point:** Gestalt shares Rogers' valuing of a genuine relationship but is **directive and confrontational**, designing experiments and frustrating avoidance, whereas the person-centred therapist is **non-directive**. Both prize **authenticity/genuineness**; only Gestalt actively **frustrates** the client to provoke awareness.

## 7.13 Experiments and Techniques

Gestalt favours **experiments** (here-and-now "try this and notice what happens") over interpretation. The therapist designs an experiment to heighten awareness of whatever the client is avoiding. Key named techniques:

- **Empty-chair technique**: the client dialogues with an *absent person* (e.g., a parent) imagined in an empty chair, speaking to them directly in the present and often switching seats to answer back. Used heavily for **unfinished business**.
- **Two-chair technique**: the client gives voice to *two parts of the self* in dialogue, classically **topdog vs underdog**, switching chairs to embody each side, working toward integration. (Empty-chair often = a person; two-chair often = a polarity/part. The terms overlap, but the *function* differs: dialogue with another vs dialogue between parts of oneself.)
- **Exaggeration**: the client repeats and amplifies a gesture, posture, or phrase (a tapping foot, a small "I guess") until its suppressed meaning and feeling become conscious.
- **Dream work as parts of the self**: every element of a dream (people, objects, settings) is treated as a **projected, disowned part of the dreamer**. Rather than interpret the dream (Freud), the client **becomes** and **acts out** each element in the present and dialogues among them to re-own the projections.
- **"I take responsibility for..."**: the client appends this phrase to statements ("I feel tense in my shoulders... and I take responsibility for it") to convert passive, disowned experience into owned, authored experience.
- **Staying with the feeling**: when the client wants to flee an uncomfortable emotion, the therapist urges them to *stay with* and deepen it rather than escape, so the avoided gestalt can complete.
- **Making the rounds**: in a *group*, the client takes a theme or sentence ("I can't stand it when...") to each member in turn, experimenting with direct contact and risk.
- **Reversal**: the client deliberately enacts the *opposite* of their habitual behaviour (e.g., the timid person plays domineering) to contact and re-own a disowned polarity.
- **Language work**: shifting **"it" → "I"** (owning: "my hand is clenched" not "the hand clenches"), **questions → statements** (a question often hides a hidden statement), and removing qualifiers like "can't" (→ "won't") and "but" (→ "and") to expose responsibility and avoidance.
- **The Gestalt Prayer** ("I do my thing and you do your thing...") encapsulates the self-responsibility, anti-confluence ethos.

> **Key Point:** **Empty chair / two-chair, exaggeration, dream work as parts of the self, "staying with the feeling," reversal, making the rounds** are all signature **Gestalt/Perls** techniques. The empty chair is the single most identifiable. Do **not** confuse it with the **miracle question** (solution-focused / de Shazer) or with **systematic desensitisation** (Wolpe). Gestalt **acts out / enacts** dreams; Freud **interprets** them; Jung **amplifies** them.

## 7.14 Applications, Multicultural Issues, and Critique

**Used for / evidence.** Originally for over-controlled, constricted, "neurotic" clients; well suited to blocked emotion, unresolved grief, and experiential growth. The **empty-chair** and two-chair methods have the strongest empirical support; they are the direct ancestor of **Leslie Greenberg's Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT / process-experiential therapy)**, which has good outcome evidence.

**Multicultural considerations.**
- The **confrontational, emotionally expressive, here-and-now** style can clash with clients from cultures that value emotional restraint, indirectness, or deference to authority; pushing for open emotional expression may feel intrusive or shaming.
- The strong emphasis on **individual self-support and personal responsibility** can conflict with **collectivist** values where interdependence and family obligation are the norm (what Gestalt calls "confluence" may be culturally valued connectedness).
- Used flexibly and respectfully, the focus on present awareness and the body can translate across cultures; the rigidity is in the classic Perls *style*, not the core theory.

**Criticisms.**
- **Anti-intellectual / anti-theoretical**: Perls disparaged "explaining" and intellect ("lose your mind and come to your senses"), which limits cognitive understanding and theoretical rigour.
- Techniques can become **gimmicky, theatrical, or mechanical** in unskilled hands (empty chair as a parlour trick).
- The classic **confrontational, frustrating Perls style** risks overwhelming fragile clients and was sometimes charismatic showmanship; it is contraindicated for severely disturbed or poorly resourced clients.
- Historically **weak controlled outcome research** for the approach as a whole (though EFT has since strengthened the evidence for chair work).
- Heavily dependent on the therapist's skill, presence, and judgement; hard to manualise.

> **Key Point:** The empty-chair/two-chair work is the bridge from Gestalt to **Greenberg's Emotion-Focused Therapy**, and is the most evidence-supported Gestalt contribution. The main critiques are anti-intellectualism, gimmickry, and the risky confrontational style.

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **Founder is Fritz Perls**, with **Laura Perls** and **Paul Goodman** co-founding. The 1951 book *Gestalt Therapy* is Perls, **Hefferline & Goodman**.
2. **Gestalt therapy (Perls) ≠ Gestalt perceptual psychology (Wertheimer, Köhler, Koffka).** Perls *borrowed* figure/ground and closure; the perceptual school did not invent a therapy. Wertheimer's **phi phenomenon** is perception, not therapy.
3. The **five boundary disturbances**: introjection, projection, retroflection, deflection, confluence. **Retroflection** (turning back on the self) is the most common distractor against introjection and projection.
4. **Introjection = take in undigested; Projection = throw out onto others; Retroflection = do to self what you'd do to others; Confluence = no boundary; Deflection = avoid/diffuse contact.**
5. The **five layers of neurosis** in order: **phony → phobic → impasse → implosive → explosive.** Ends with **explosive** (breakthrough into authentic emotion). Do not reverse implosive/explosive.
6. **Topdog = shoulds/moralising tyrant (introjected); underdog = evasive saboteur/victim.** It is an **internal** split, worked with the **two-chair** technique.
7. **Empty chair** is *the* Gestalt technique. Do **not** assign it to person-centred, behaviour therapy, or solution-focused. The **miracle question** is de Shazer (SFBT), not Gestalt.
8. **Dreams in Gestalt are acted out / enacted as projected parts of the self**, NOT interpreted (Freud) and NOT amplified (Jung).
9. **Awareness in the here-and-now** is the master concept and master goal. Gestalt asks **"what" and "how," not "why."**
10. **Unfinished business** is closed by present expression/completion (often empty chair), not by historical insight. Rooted in the **closure** principle.
11. The **impasse** is the shift from **environmental support to self-support**; it is also **layer 3** of the five layers. Perls' maturity = moving from environmental to self-support.
12. **Organismic self-regulation / figure-formation**: the organism meets needs in priority order if awareness is unblocked; neurosis interrupts the contact cycle.
13. The relationship is **I-Thou dialogue** (Buber): present, inclusive, dialogic, and **actively confrontational/frustrating**, unlike Rogers' purely **non-directive** stance (both value genuineness/authenticity).
14. **Holism**: "the whole is different from the sum of its parts"; mind-body-emotion are one. **Field theory** (Lewin): organism/environment field; behaviour understood only in context.
15. Gestalt's chair work is the direct ancestor of **Greenberg's Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT)**, the most evidence-supported descendant.
16. Perls' anti-intellectual slogan: **"Lose your mind and come to your senses."** Critiques: anti-intellectualism, gimmickry, risky confrontation.
