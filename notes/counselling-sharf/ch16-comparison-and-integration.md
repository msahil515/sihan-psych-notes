# Ch 16: Comparison & Integration

**Overview:** This is the synthesis chapter. Chapters 2 to 15 each walked one theory through Sharf's recurring frame: originator, view of human nature / theory of personality, how problems develop, goals of therapy, techniques, the therapeutic relationship, evidence, and criticisms. Chapter 16 lines all of them up side by side so you can place any theory and predict its "which approach" answer in an exam. The single most useful exam skill is not recalling one theory in depth but telling rival theories apart: REBT vs Beck, Wolpe's desensitisation vs flooding, Bowen vs Minuchin vs Haley, miracle question vs empty chair. Learn each technique in its contrast pair, and learn where each theory sits on the master comparison axes. This chapter consolidates every theory, founder, signature technique, cross-theory trap, glossary term, and self-test you need for the MET 2026 and NIMHANS counselling-and-psychotherapy section.

The frame that organises the whole book: every theory can be located on five axes, and almost any "which approach" stem can be answered by reading the theory's position off them.

1. **View of human nature**: determined by the past (pessimistic) vs free and growth-oriented (optimistic); or neutral (a product of learning).
2. **Role of the past**: childhood causes vs the here-and-now vs the future.
3. **Directiveness**: therapist as expert / teacher / debater vs as a non-directive companion.
4. **The relationship**: a tool / working alliance, the cure itself, the site of the work (transference), or a collaboration.
5. **Insight vs action**: does change come from *understanding* or from *doing*?

## PART TWO: THEORIES GROUPED BY FORCE / PARADIGM

Sharf and NIMHANS both organise the field into broad "forces" or waves. A stem like "the third force in psychology refers to…" is answered directly from this grouping.

## 16.2 The four forces (plus the postmodern/systemic family)

### First force: psychodynamic
**Theories:** psychoanalysis (Freud), Jungian analytical psychology (Jung), Adlerian individual psychology (Adler).
**Defining stance:** behaviour is driven by unconscious forces and shaped by early childhood; change comes through **insight** into the past. The depth tradition. Freud is deterministic and instinct-driven; Jung de-sexualises libido and adds the collective unconscious; Adler is the most forward-looking and social of the three (a bridge toward humanism and cognitive therapy). This is the **first** systematic theory of psychotherapy and the tradition every later force reacts to.

### Second force: behavioural / cognitive-behavioural
**Theories:** behaviour therapy (Wolpe, Skinner, Bandura), REBT (Ellis), cognitive therapy (Beck), reality therapy (Glasser).
**Defining stance:** the focus is the **present**, behaviour is **learned** (and can be re-learned) or driven by **cognition** (and can be re-structured); the therapist is **directive**, change comes through **action** and **doing** plus (in the cognitive members) restructuring thought. Strongest empirical evidence base. The behavioural/cognitive-behavioural force arose as the scientific, measurable reaction to psychoanalysis.

### Third force: humanistic / experiential / existential
**Theories:** existential (May, Yalom, Frankl), person-centred (Rogers), Gestalt (Perls).
**Defining stance:** humans are **free**, **responsible**, and oriented toward **growth and self-actualisation**; the **present** and **subjective experience** matter; the **relationship** is central (the cure itself in Rogers). A reaction against both psychoanalytic determinism and behavioural mechanism. "Third force" most often points to Rogers / Maslow's humanistic psychology.

### Fourth force: multicultural / social-justice
**Theories / frameworks:** multicultural counselling (Sue & Sue), and feminist therapy is usually placed here too (and sometimes the transpersonal is named as a separate fourth/fifth force).
**Defining stance:** the client's **cultural, gender, and social-political context** is central; "pathology" may be a reasonable response to oppression; the relationship is **egalitarian**; the counsellor needs cultural **awareness, knowledge, and skills**. The most recent "force" in Sharf's and NIMHANS's framing.

### Postmodern / constructivist / systemic (the family of approaches outside the four-force scheme)
**Theories:** solution-focused brief therapy (de Shazer & Berg), narrative (White & Epston), and the family systems schools (Bowen, Minuchin, Haley, Satir).
**Defining stance:** reality is **socially constructed** through language and story (postmodern/constructivist), or the unit of treatment is the **system** rather than the individual (family systems). Brief, collaborative, non-pathologising (SFBT/narrative); systemic and pattern-focused (family therapy). Transactional Analysis sits as a structured neo-analytic / integrative approach.

> **Key Point:** Force mapping for fast recall: **1st = psychodynamic** (Freud, Jung, Adler), **2nd = behavioural/cognitive-behavioural** (behaviour therapy, REBT, Beck, reality), **3rd = humanistic-existential** (existential, person-centred, Gestalt), **4th = multicultural / social-justice** (Sue & Sue, feminist). Postmodern (SFBT, narrative) and systemic (family) are the newer streams outside the original three-force scheme.

## PART THREE: DIMENSIONS OF COMPARISON

The same theories arranged along each master axis. Memorise the *extremes* of each axis (they are the safest MCQ answers).

## 16.3 Insight vs action

- **Insight-based** (change through understanding): psychoanalysis, Jungian, Adlerian (insight into the style of life), existential (insight into one's freedom and avoidance).
- **Action / doing-based** (change through behaving differently): behaviour therapy, reality therapy, solution-focused, strategic family therapy.
- **Both (insight into thinking, then action)**: REBT and Beck's cognitive therapy (recognise the thought, then test/change it and act), and Gestalt (awareness *in action* in the present).

## 16.4 Directive vs non-directive

- **Highly directive** (therapist leads, teaches, prescribes): behaviour therapy, REBT (most confrontational), reality therapy, strategic family therapy.
- **Non-directive** (the extreme): person-centred (Rogers): no diagnosis, advice, or technique; the client leads.
- **In between / collaborative**: Beck's cognitive therapy (collaborative empiricism), Adlerian (egalitarian-but-teaching), solution-focused and narrative (collaborative, client-as-expert), feminist (egalitarian by principle).

## 16.5 Past vs present vs future focus

- **Past-focused**: psychoanalysis and Jungian (childhood / the deep past). Adlerian *uses* the past (early recollections) but aims **forward** toward goals.
- **Present / here-and-now**: Gestalt (radically present), person-centred, behaviour therapy, cognitive therapy, REBT, reality therapy.
- **Future-focused**: solution-focused (the preferred future / miracle), existential (creating future meaning), narrative (a re-authored future story), Adlerian (fictional finalism, pulled by future goals).

## 16.6 Deterministic vs free-will

- **Deterministic** (behaviour caused by forces outside present choice): psychoanalysis (the unconscious & the past), behaviour therapy (conditioning & environment); these are the two classic determinisms, one internal-historical, one external-environmental.
- **Free-will / self-determining**: existential (we create ourselves through choice), Adlerian (creative power of the self), person-centred (self-direction via the actualising tendency), reality/choice theory (we *choose* nearly all our behaviour), Gestalt, the postmodern approaches.
- **Soft / reciprocal**: cognitive and social-learning views: Bandura's reciprocal determinism (person, behaviour, and environment influence each other).

## 16.7 Individual vs systemic

- **Individual** (the person is the unit of treatment): all of psychoanalysis, Jungian, Adlerian, existential, person-centred, Gestalt, behaviour, REBT, Beck, reality, TA.
- **Systemic / relational** (the system is the unit; the "identified patient" is a symptom of the whole): all four family-systems schools (Bowen, Minuchin, Haley, Satir). Feminist therapy is individual-in-context (the social/political system is the backdrop). SFBT and narrative are often used with families too.

## 16.8 Length: long-term vs brief

- **Classically long-term / open-ended**: psychoanalysis (years, multiple sessions/week), Jungian, classical existential.
- **Inherently brief / time-limited**: solution-focused brief therapy (the briefest by design), strategic family therapy, REBT, much of behaviour therapy and Beck's CT (structured, manualised, often 12–20 sessions). Brief psychodynamic therapy is the time-limited modern descendant of psychoanalysis.

> **Key Point:** The "pure extremes" worth memorising for stems: most **deterministic & past-focused** = psychoanalysis; most **non-directive** = person-centred; most **brief & future-focused** = solution-focused; most **directive & confrontational** = REBT; the **relationship as the cure** = person-centred; the **system as the unit** = family therapy.

## PART FIVE: ROUTES TO INTEGRATION

## 16.10 How theories are combined

Most practising counsellors are not "pure" anything. Sharf distinguishes four routes to combining theories, a frequently tested four-way distinction.

| Route | What it means | Example |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Technical eclecticism** | Pick whatever techniques work best for this client, regardless of their parent theory; no requirement that the theories cohere | Arnold Lazarus's **multimodal therapy** (the **BASIC ID**) |
| **Theoretical integration** | Blend two or more theories into a new, coherent whole | Wachtel's **cyclical psychodynamics** (psychodynamic + behavioural) |
| **Common-factors approach** | Use what *all* effective therapies share (alliance, empathy, hope, a believable rationale + ritual) | Wampold's contextual model; the Dodo-bird tradition |
| **Assimilative integration** | Work from one "home" theory but import techniques from others as needed | A primarily psychodynamic therapist using behavioural homework |

- **BASIC ID** (Lazarus's multimodal assessment grid): **B**ehaviour, **A**ffect, **S**ensation, **I**magery, **C**ognition, **I**nterpersonal, **D**rugs/biology. The aim is to fit the therapy to the client, not the client to one theory.

> **Key Point:** The clean distinction for MCQs: **technical eclecticism** = mix *techniques* (Lazarus); **theoretical integration** = blend *theories* into something new; **common factors** = use what's *shared* by all (Wampold); **assimilative** = one home theory + borrowed techniques.

## PART SIX: WHAT NIMHANS ADDS BEYOND SHARF

## 16.11 High-yield extensions

- **Common factors & the Dodo-bird verdict (Wampold).** Across bona-fide therapies, much of the outcome is explained by **common factors** shared by all (the **therapeutic/working alliance**, empathy, expectancy/hope, a believable rationale + ritual, goal consensus) rather than by specific techniques. The "Dodo-bird verdict" (Rosenzweig; championed by Luborsky and **Bruce Wampold**): "everyone has won and all must have prizes," i.e. all bona-fide therapies achieve broadly comparable outcomes. The **working alliance** (**Bordin**: bond + goals + tasks) is the single most robust predictor of outcome.
- **Stages of change (Transtheoretical Model ; Prochaska & DiClemente).** **Precontemplation → contemplation → preparation → action → maintenance** (relapse possible). Match the intervention to the stage; "the client isn't resistant, they're precontemplative."
- **Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick).** A client-centred *but* directive method to resolve **ambivalence** and elicit **change talk**. Spirit = **PACE** (partnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation); core skills = **OARS** (Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, Summaries); "roll with resistance," develop discrepancy, support self-efficacy. Pairs naturally with the stages of change.
- **Multicultural counselling competencies (Sue & Sue), the "fourth force."** A competent counsellor has **awareness** (of own biases/worldview), **knowledge** (of the client's culture), and **skills** (culturally appropriate interventions). Watch for **cultural encapsulation**, **etic vs emic** perspectives, and the limits of imposing Western individualistic therapy.
- **Ethics of the counselling frame (Beauchamp & Childress / Kitchener).** Core principles: **autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice, fidelity** (and veracity). The frame: **informed consent**, **confidentiality** and its limits (**Tarasoff** duty to warn/protect; harm to self/others; abuse), clear **boundaries** (avoid **dual relationships**), competence, supervision. India: **RCI** registration for clinical psychologists; the **MHCA 2017** frame.

> **Key Point:** NIMHANS leans on the *integrative/meta* layer that Sharf treats lightly: the **alliance** as the top outcome predictor (Bordin), the **Dodo-bird** common-factors claim (Wampold), **stages of change** (Prochaska & DiClemente), **MI** (Miller & Rollnick, OARS), and **multicultural competence** (Sue & Sue). These appear as named-author stems.

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

The collected cross-theory confusions. Each one is a place the distractor lives. Learn both sides of every pair.

1. **REBT (Ellis) vs Cognitive Therapy (Beck): the most-tested pair.** Ellis = **A-B-C-D-E**, **irrational beliefs / musturbation**, **disputing**; **directive and confrontational**, therapist argues the belief is illogical. Beck = **automatic thoughts, cognitive distortions, schemas**, **collaborative empiricism**; **Socratic and gentler**, the client tests the thought as a **hypothesis** against evidence. REBT *predates* Beck. "Dispute the irrational belief" = Ellis; "test the automatic thought against the evidence" = Beck.
2. **Cognitive triad (negative self / world / future)** = **Beck** (depression), NOT Ellis.
3. **Systematic desensitisation (Wolpe) vs flooding.** Desensitisation = **graded** hierarchy **+ relaxation** (reciprocal inhibition), gentle and gradual. Flooding = **maximal** exposure, **no** hierarchy, **no** relaxation, anxiety left to extinguish. Same principle (exposure), opposite intensity. Systematic desensitisation = **Wolpe**.
4. **Token economy** = operant / **Skinnerian**. **Modeling / observational learning** = **Bandura**. **Aversion therapy** = pair the unwanted behaviour with an unpleasant stimulus. Don't cross-attribute these three.
5. **The core triad** (congruence/genuineness, unconditional positive regard, empathy) = **Rogers** (person-centred). "Six necessary and sufficient conditions," "fully functioning person," "actualising tendency" = Rogers. Never attribute UPR or empathy-as-the-cure to another theorist.
6. **"Necessary AND sufficient"** (the relationship is the whole cure) = **Rogers**. **"Necessary BUT NOT sufficient"** (the relationship matters but technique is also needed) = **Beck / CBT**. This stem-flip is a classic trap.
7. **Empty chair · top-dog vs under-dog · here-and-now awareness · unfinished business · figure/ground** = Gestalt / **Perls**.
8. **Miracle question · exception question · scaling question** = SFBT / **de Shazer & Berg**. The miracle question is the single most-tested item; it is **de Shazer**, NOT Gestalt's empty chair.
9. **Externalising the problem · re-authoring · unique outcomes** = Narrative / **White & Epston**. Both SFBT and narrative are postmodern/client-as-expert, but SFBT builds the **solution/future** while narrative re-writes the **story**.
10. **Logotherapy · will to meaning · existential vacuum · paradoxical intention · dereflection** = **Frankl**. The **four givens** (death/freedom/isolation/meaninglessness) = **Yalom**. **Rollo May** brought existentialism to American therapy.
11. **The three "wills":** **will to pleasure** = Freud · **will to power / superiority** = Adler · **will to meaning** = Frankl.
12. **WDEP · choice theory · total behavior · five needs (survival, love/belonging, power, freedom, fun)** = **Glasser** (WDEP formalised by **Wubbolding**). Glasser's five needs are NOT Maslow's hierarchy. Reality therapy is **present**-focused, not past.
13. **Jung vs Freud.** **Collective unconscious + archetypes + individuation + dream amplification** = **Jung**. **Personal unconscious only + free association + psychosexual stages** = **Freud**. Dreams: Jung uses **amplification** (link to myth), Freud uses **free association**.
14. **Adler vs Freud.** Adler = **inferiority/superiority complex, style of life, social interest (Gemeinschaftsgefühl), birth order, early recollections, encouragement**: social, goal-directed, future-oriented, egalitarian. Freud = instinct, sex, the deterministic past, the neutral analyst.
15. **Free association · dream analysis (manifest vs latent) · transference · interpretation of resistance** = **Freud** / psychoanalysis. The defining *technique* is free association; the defining *relationship mechanism* is the transference.
16. **Family-theorist swaps (a top NIMHANS trap).** **Genogram + differentiation + triangulation** = **Bowen** (multigenerational/insight). **Boundaries (enmeshed vs disengaged) + enactment + subsystems + joining** = **Minuchin** (structural). **Directives + paradox / prescribing the symptom** = **Haley** (strategic). **Family sculpting + placater/blamer/super-reasonable(computer)/irrelevant(distractor)** = **Satir** (experiential).
17. **"Identified patient · homeostasis · circular causality · feedback loops"** = **family systems** in general (any school).
18. **Parent–Adult–Child ego states · games · scripts · strokes · "I'm OK–You're OK" · life positions** = **TA / Berne**.
19. **Multimodal therapy / BASIC ID / technical eclecticism** = **Arnold Lazarus**. Don't confuse with Beck's CT or with general behaviour therapy.
20. **The four routes to integration:** **technical eclecticism** (mix techniques, Lazarus) vs **theoretical integration** (blend theories) vs **common factors** (use the shared ingredients, Wampold) vs **assimilative integration** (one home theory plus borrowed techniques).
21. **Dodo-bird verdict / common factors / the alliance** = **Wampold** (contextual model); the **working alliance = bond + goals + tasks = Bordin**, the top outcome predictor.
22. **Stages of change** (precontemplation → contemplation → preparation → action → maintenance) = **Prochaska & DiClemente** (Transtheoretical Model). **OARS + change talk + rolling with resistance** = Motivational Interviewing (**Miller & Rollnick**).
23. **Multicultural competencies = awareness, knowledge, skills** = **Sue & Sue** (the "fourth force").
24. **"The personal is political" · gender-role analysis · power analysis · egalitarian relationship** = **feminist therapy**.
25. **Paradoxical intention appears twice:** as a *named logotherapy* technique it is **Frankl**; it also appears in **Adlerian** and **strategic** (Haley, "prescribing the symptom") work. If the stem says "logotherapy," answer Frankl; if it says "family / strategic," answer Haley.

## Key Terms

Consolidated A-Z glossary across all theories. One line each, with the theory/founder in brackets. Cover the right column and self-test.

| Term | One-line definition (and who) |
|------|-------------------------------|
| **A-B-C-D-E** | Activating event → Belief → Consequence → Dispute → Effective new philosophy (Ellis, REBT). |
| **Active imagination** | Dialoguing with inner images / symbols (Jung). |
| **Actualising tendency** | Innate organismic drive to grow and enhance the self (Rogers). |
| **Archetypes** | Universal inherited images in the collective unconscious; persona, shadow, anima/animus, self (Jung). |
| **Automatic thoughts** | Rapid, plausible-feeling negative appraisals just below awareness (Beck). |
| **BASIC ID** | Multimodal grid: Behaviour, Affect, Sensation, Imagery, Cognition, Interpersonal, Drugs/biology (Lazarus). |
| **Boundaries (family)** | Rules of contact between subsystems; rigid → disengaged, diffuse → enmeshed (Minuchin). |
| **Choice theory** | We choose nearly all our behaviour to meet five basic needs (Glasser). |
| **Cognitive distortions** | Systematic thinking errors (all-or-nothing, overgeneralisation, catastrophising…) (Beck). |
| **Cognitive triad** | Negative view of self, world, and future, in depression (Beck). |
| **Collaborative empiricism** | Client + therapist test beliefs as hypotheses against evidence (Beck). |
| **Collective unconscious** | Inherited layer shared by all humanity; holds the archetypes (Jung). |
| **Common factors / Dodo-bird** | Shared ingredients (alliance, hope) drive outcome; all therapies roughly equal (Wampold). |
| **Conditions of worth** | "I'm valued only if…"; the root of incongruence (Rogers). |
| **Congruence** | Therapist genuineness; also a close real-self/ideal-self match (Rogers). |
| **Core triad** | Congruence, unconditional positive regard, empathy (Rogers). |
| **Defence mechanisms** | Unconscious ego strategies that reduce anxiety (Freud / Anna Freud). |
| **Dereflection** | Shift attention away from self/symptom (Frankl, logotherapy). |
| **Differentiation of self** | Separating intellect from emotion, self from family fusion (Bowen). |
| **Directives** | Tasks the therapist assigns between sessions (Haley, strategic). |
| **Disputing** | Actively challenging irrational beliefs (Ellis). |
| **Empty chair** | Dialogue with an absent person or a disowned part of the self (Perls, Gestalt). |
| **Enactment** | The family plays out the problem interaction in session (Minuchin). |
| **Exception question** | "When is the problem absent or less?" (de Shazer, SFBT). |
| **Externalising** | Treat the problem as separate from the person (White, narrative). |
| **Fictional finalism** | We are pulled by an imagined future goal (Adler). |
| **Five basic needs** | Survival, love/belonging, power, freedom, fun (Glasser). |
| **Flooding** | Intense, prolonged exposure with no hierarchy or relaxation (behaviour therapy). |
| **Four givens** | Death, freedom, isolation, meaninglessness (Yalom). |
| **Free association** | Say whatever comes, censorship off (Freud). |
| **Fully functioning person** | Open, present-living, self-trusting, free, creative (Rogers). |
| **Games** | Repetitive ulterior transactions paying off in bad feelings (Berne, TA). |
| **Genogram** | Multigenerational family map / diagram (Bowen). |
| **Identified patient** | The symptom-bearer expressing the system's dysfunction (family systems). |
| **Individuation** | Lifelong integration of the psyche into the self (Jung). |
| **Inferiority / superiority complex** | Paralysing self-doubt / over-compensation (Adler). |
| **Irrational beliefs** | Rigid absolutist demands (musts/shoulds) (Ellis). |
| **Logotherapy** | Therapy of meaning; the will to meaning is primary (Frankl). |
| **Manifest vs latent content** | The remembered dream vs its hidden meaning (Freud). |
| **Miracle question** | "If a miracle solved it overnight, what would you notice?" (de Shazer, SFBT). |
| **Modeling** | Learning a behaviour by observing a model (Bandura). |
| **Motivational interviewing** | Client-centred, directive resolution of ambivalence; OARS, change talk (Miller & Rollnick). |
| **Musturbation** | Rigid demands phrased as musts/shoulds (Ellis). |
| **Paradoxical intention** | Deliberately wish for the feared thing to break anticipatory anxiety (Frankl). |
| **Parent–Adult–Child** | The three ego states (Berne, TA). |
| **"Personal is political"** | Distress is rooted in social/gender power structures (feminist therapy). |
| **Power analysis** | Examining power inequality and how to gain personal power (feminist therapy). |
| **Prescribing the symptom** | Paradox: instruct the family to keep doing it (Haley, strategic). |
| **Reciprocal determinism** | Person, behaviour, and environment influence each other (Bandura). |
| **Reciprocal inhibition** | Relaxation displaces anxiety; basis of desensitisation (Wolpe). |
| **Re-authoring** | Building a new preferred life story (White & Epston, narrative). |
| **Scaling question** | "On 0–10, where are you?" (de Shazer, SFBT). |
| **Schema** | Deep core belief that generates automatic thoughts (Beck). |
| **Social interest (Gemeinschaftsgefühl)** | Feeling of kinship with humanity; the mark of health (Adler). |
| **Stages of change** | Precontemplation → contemplation → preparation → action → maintenance (Prochaska & DiClemente). |
| **Strokes** | Units of recognition exchanged between people (Berne, TA). |
| **Style of life** | Unique pattern built by ~age 5 to pursue goals (Adler). |
| **Systematic desensitisation** | Graded fear hierarchy paired with relaxation (Wolpe). |
| **Technical eclecticism** | Use whatever techniques work, regardless of theory (Lazarus). |
| **Token economy** | Earn tokens for target behaviour, exchange for reinforcers (operant/Skinner). |
| **Top-dog vs under-dog** | The "should" tyrant vs the evasive resister within (Perls). |
| **Total behavior** | Acting, thinking, feeling, physiology; we control the first two (Glasser). |
| **Transference** | Client projects feelings about early figures onto the analyst (Freud). |
| **Triangulation** | A third person is drawn in to stabilise two-person tension (Bowen). |
| **Unconditional positive regard** | Non-possessive acceptance / warmth (Rogers). |
| **Unfinished business** | Unexpressed past feelings intruding on the present (Perls). |
| **Unique outcomes** | Moments the person resisted the problem story (White, narrative). |
| **WDEP** | Wants, Doing, Evaluation, Plan (Glasser; formalised by Wubbolding). |
| **Working alliance** | Bond + goals + tasks; the top predictor of outcome (Bordin). |

## Self-Test

1. Name the defining *technique* and the defining *relationship* mechanism of psychoanalysis. **(Free association; the transference.)**

3. Match the three "wills": pleasure, power, meaning. **(Freud = pleasure; Adler = power; Frankl = meaning.)**

7. Empty chair and top-dog/under-dog belong to whom? **(Perls / Gestalt.)**
8. Give the two differences between systematic desensitisation and flooding. **(Desensitisation = graded hierarchy + relaxation, Wolpe; flooding = intense, no hierarchy, no relaxation.)**
9. In one line, distinguish REBT from Beck's cognitive therapy. **(Ellis disputes irrational beliefs, directive/confrontational; Beck tests automatic thoughts as hypotheses, collaborative/Socratic.)**

11. Expand WDEP and name Glasser's five needs. **(Wants, Doing, Evaluation, Plan; survival, love/belonging, power, freedom, fun.)**
