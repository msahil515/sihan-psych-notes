# Ch 6: Person-Centred Therapy (Carl Rogers)

**Overview:** Person-centred therapy is **Carl Rogers'** humanistic, **non-directive** approach, built on a radically **optimistic** view of human nature: every organism carries an innate **actualising tendency** toward growth, and given the right relational climate, the client will move toward health on their own. Psychological trouble does not come from instinctual conflict (Freud) or faulty learning (behaviourism) but from **incongruence**: a gap between the person's actual experience and the **self-concept** they have built up to satisfy **conditions of worth**. The cure is not technique, interpretation, or advice; it is a particular kind of **relationship**. Rogers reduced that relationship to **six necessary and sufficient conditions** for therapeutic change, three of which the therapist provides: the famous **core triad** of **congruence (genuineness)**, **unconditional positive regard (UPR)**, and **empathic understanding**. This chapter follows Sharf's recurring frame (originator, theory of personality, goals, the therapeutic relationship, techniques, applications, critique) but the load-bearing exam material is the **six conditions** and the **core triad** (a subset of those six). Person-centred therapy sits on the master axes as **present-focused, relationship-as-cure, non-directive, growth-oriented**, the polar opposite of the directive cognitive-behavioural group.

## PART ONE: BACKGROUND AND HUMANISTIC ROOTS

## 6.1 The Originator

- **Carl Ransom Rogers** (1902-1987), American psychologist. Trained originally in a directive, diagnostic, child-guidance tradition, he reacted against it and built an approach that placed faith in the client rather than the expert.
- He pioneered **recorded and transcribed therapy sessions**, the first to do so, opening psychotherapy to empirical research. Before Rogers, therapy process was private and unverifiable.
- Key works: *Counseling and Psychotherapy* (1942), *Client-Centered Therapy* (1951), *On Becoming a Person* (1961), and the pivotal 1957 paper **"The Necessary and Sufficient Conditions of Therapeutic Personality Change."**
- Late in life he applied the approach beyond the clinic to **encounter groups**, education, conflict resolution, and world peace (nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize).

> **Key Point:** Rogers gave humanistic psychotherapy its **empirical base**. The recorded session and the **Q-sort** are his methodological legacies; humanistic therapy is otherwise often criticised as untestable.

## 6.2 Humanistic Roots ("Third Force")

- Person-centred therapy is the flagship of **humanistic psychology**, the **"third force"** positioned against the two dominant forces of the mid-20th century: **psychoanalysis** (deterministic, past-driven, pessimistic) and **behaviourism** (mechanistic, environment-driven).
- Shared humanistic assumptions: humans are **basically good and trustworthy**, inherently growth-oriented, **free and self-determining**, and best understood holistically and from the **person's own subjective frame of reference (phenomenology)**.
- **Phenomenological** stance: what matters is not objective reality but the client's **perceived reality** (the **phenomenal field**). Behaviour is a response to the world *as perceived*, not as it is.
- Related figures: **Abraham Maslow** (hierarchy of needs, self-actualisation, peak experiences) is the other humanistic anchor; existential therapy (Ch 5) is the experiential cousin.

> **Key Point:** Humanistic = phenomenological + holistic + growth-oriented + free will. Trouble is a **blocked** growth tendency, never an inherently evil or merely conditioned organism.

## PART TWO: THEORY OF PERSONALITY

## 6.3 The Actualising Tendency and the Organismic Valuing Process

- The single motivating force in Rogers' theory is the **actualising tendency**: an innate, biological push in every organism to **maintain and enhance** itself, to develop all its capacities in ways that serve growth. It is **the** master motive (one drive, not many).
- **Self-actualising tendency**: as a self-concept emerges, a portion of the general actualising tendency is directed specifically at actualising the **self**. (In a healthy person these two are aligned; in a maladjusted person they conflict.)
- **Organismic valuing process (OVP)**: an inner, trustworthy "compass" by which the organism evaluates experience as good (enhancing) or bad (not enhancing) **based on the actualising tendency itself**. The infant naturally values food, warmth, and novelty positively and pain negatively, with no external rule book.
- The **fully functioning** person trusts the OVP. The maladjusted person has replaced it with **introjected conditions of worth** (other people's values swallowed whole), losing touch with their own organismic judgement.

> **Key Point:** **One** motivational construct: the **actualising tendency**. The **organismic valuing process** is the internal locus of evaluation; **conditions of worth** are what displace it. Restoring trust in the OVP is the implicit aim of therapy.

## 6.4 The Self-Concept, Ideal Self, and Self-Actualisation

- The **self / self-concept** is the organised, consistent set of perceptions the person holds about "I" or "me," built from interaction with the environment and especially with significant others. It is **learned**, not innate.
- The **ideal self** is the self-concept the person **would most like to possess** (the self they value most).
- **Self-actualisation** in Rogers' specific sense = the actualising tendency expressed through the realm of the self-concept; the movement toward realising one's perceived self.
- A large gap between **self-concept and ideal self** is one index of maladjustment; therapy typically **narrows** this gap (Rogers measured exactly this with the Q-sort).
- A second, deeper gap is between the **self-concept and the actual organismic experience**: this is **incongruence** (see 6.6).

## 6.5 Conditions of Worth and Conditional vs Unconditional Positive Regard

- Every person has a basic need for **positive regard** (love, warmth, acceptance, respect) from others, and a derived need for **positive self-regard**.
- When caregivers offer regard **conditionally** ("I love you *only when* you behave / achieve / feel the way I approve"), the child learns **conditions of worth**: internalised conditions under which they judge themselves worthy of regard.
- To keep the prized regard, the child begins to **deny or distort** experiences that conflict with those conditions (e.g., denying anger toward a parent because anger is "bad"). The self-concept is now built partly on **introjected** values rather than the OVP.
- **Conditional positive regard** breeds conditions of worth and incongruence. **Unconditional positive regard (UPR)**, prizing the person regardless of their behaviour or feelings, prevents conditions of worth from forming and, in therapy, helps dissolve them.

| Concept | Meaning | Effect |
|---------|---------|--------|
| **Need for positive regard** | Universal need for love/acceptance from others | Drives the child to seek approval |
| **Conditions of worth** | "I am worthy only if I meet condition X" | Self distorts experience to keep regard |
| **Conditional positive regard** | Regard given only when conditions met | Creates conditions of worth → incongruence |
| **Unconditional positive regard (UPR)** | Prizing regardless of behaviour/feelings | Prevents/undoes conditions of worth |

> **Key Point:** **Conditions of worth** are the developmental villain in Rogers' theory. They cause the self to **distort/deny** experience, producing incongruence. **UPR** is the antidote, both in childhood and in the therapy room.

## 6.6 Incongruence Between Self and Experience

- **Incongruence** = a discrepancy/mismatch between the **self-concept** and the **actual experience of the organism**. The person experiences something the self-concept cannot accept, so it is **denied** (kept out of awareness entirely) or **distorted** (admitted but in a falsified form).
- Incongruence is the **source of anxiety, defensiveness, and maladjustment** in this theory. The organism senses, **subceives** (perceives below awareness, "subception"), a threatening experience and mobilises defences before it reaches full awareness.
- **Threat** = the perception that an experience is inconsistent with the self-concept. **Defence** keeps the self-concept intact at the cost of awareness.
- **Congruence**, the goal state, = self-concept and experience **match**; the person is open to experience, undefended, and able to symbolise experience accurately in awareness.
- Contrast: **self-experience incongruence** (the internal gap) vs **incongruence between self and the therapist's behaviour** is not the term; the term always refers to self vs organismic experience.

> **Key Point:** **Incongruence (self vs experience)** is the Rogerian equivalent of "psychopathology." Distortion and denial are the two defensive operations; subception is the below-awareness detection of threat.

## 6.7 The Fully Functioning Person (Rogers' Characteristics)

The **fully functioning person** is Rogers' picture of optimal psychological health, the end-point of growth (a *process* of living, not a fixed state). Characteristics:

1. **Openness to experience** (the opposite of defensiveness): able to receive all experience, including feelings, without denial or distortion.
2. **Existential living**: living fully and freshly in the **present** moment, without imposing rigid preconceptions.
3. **Organismic trust**: trusting one's own inner experiencing (the OVP) as a guide to behaviour rather than external rules or others' judgements.
4. **Experiential freedom**: a felt sense of freedom and personal responsibility for one's choices.
5. **Creativity**: living creatively and adaptively, producing new ideas and adjusting to new conditions rather than conforming.

> **Key Point:** Memorise the five: **openness to experience, existential living, organismic trust, experiential freedom, creativity.** The fully functioning person trusts the **OVP**, not introjected conditions of worth. It is a direction of movement, not a destination.

## PART THREE: THEORY OF PSYCHOTHERAPY

## 6.8 Goals of Therapy

- Not symptom removal, advice, or problem-solving. The overarching aim is to free the client's own **actualising tendency** so growth resumes.
- Specific aims: **reduce incongruence**; help the client **drop conditions of worth**; help them **reconnect with the organismic valuing process** (move the locus of evaluation from outside to inside); increase **openness to experience** and **self-trust**; and move toward becoming a **fully functioning person**.
- The client moves from rigidity toward fluidity, from incongruence toward congruence, from an external to an **internal locus of evaluation**.

## 6.9 The SIX Necessary and Sufficient Conditions for Therapeutic Change

Rogers (1957) claimed that the following **six conditions**, if present and continuing over time, are **necessary and sufficient** for constructive personality change, and that **no other conditions are required**. Memorise and list them exactly:

| # | Condition | In plain terms |
|---|-----------|----------------|
| **1** | Two persons are in **psychological contact** | A relationship/minimal contact exists between the two |
| **2** | The **client is incongruent**, vulnerable or anxious | The client is in a state of incongruence (the reason they need help) |
| **3** | The **therapist is congruent / genuine** in the relationship | The therapist is integrated and real *within* the relationship |
| **4** | The therapist experiences **unconditional positive regard** for the client | Non-possessive warmth and acceptance |
| **5** | The therapist experiences **empathic understanding** of the client's internal frame of reference and tries to communicate this | Accurate empathy, communicated |
| **6** | The client **perceives**, at least minimally, the therapist's empathy and unconditional positive regard | Conditions 4 and 5 must actually *reach* the client |

- **Necessary**: change does not occur without them. **Sufficient**: nothing else (no technique, no diagnosis, no interpretation) is needed.
- The three the therapist *provides* (conditions **3, 4, 5**) are the **core conditions / core triad**.
- Condition **1** is the precondition (contact). Condition **2** describes the client's state (incongruence). Condition **6** is the requirement that the core conditions are **perceived/received** by the client; warmth and empathy that the client cannot perceive do nothing.

> **Key Point:** **SIX** conditions total, **necessary AND sufficient**, of which the therapist supplies **THREE** (the core triad: congruence, UPR, empathy). The core conditions are a **subset** of the six, not a separate list. This subset relationship is a classic exam trap.

> **Key Point:** Do not forget the bookend conditions: **(1) psychological contact**, **(2) client incongruent/anxious**, **(6) client perceives the therapist's empathy and UPR.** Students who only list the core triad miss half the answer.

## 6.10 The Three CORE Conditions (Explained Deeply)

The therapist-provided conditions, the **core triad**, the heart of person-centred practice:

| Core condition | Other names | Definition | What it is NOT |
|----------------|-------------|------------|----------------|
| **Congruence** | Genuineness, realness, transparency | The therapist is an integrated, authentic person *within* the relationship; their outer behaviour matches their inner experience; no professional façade | Not blurting every reaction; not a role or "blank screen" |
| **Unconditional positive regard** | UPR, acceptance, prizing, non-possessive warmth | Accepting and prizing the client as a person of worth **regardless** of their behaviour, feelings, or whether the therapist approves | Not approval of all behaviour; not conditional liking |
| **Empathic understanding** | Accurate empathy | Sensing the client's private, **internal frame of reference** as if it were one's own (the "as if" quality, without losing the "as if"), and communicating that understanding back | Not sympathy/pity; not identification (losing the "as if") |

- **Congruence (genuineness):** the most fundamental and, Rogers thought, the most important. The therapist does not hide behind a professional mask; what they feel, are aware of, and express are aligned. Congruence is **not** a licence to dump every passing reaction on the client; it is being real and undefended.
- **Unconditional positive regard (UPR):** a deep, non-judgemental **prizing** of the client. It is *unconditional*: it does not rise and fall with the client's behaviour, which is precisely what undoes the client's old conditions of worth. UPR ≠ liking everything the client does; it means valuing the person beneath the behaviour.
- **Empathic understanding:** entering the client's perceptual world **accurately and sensitively** and conveying that you have grasped it. The crucial qualifier is the **"as if"**: feeling the client's world *as if* it were your own, **without ever losing the "as if" quality** (that loss would be over-identification, not empathy). Empathy is contrasted sharply with **sympathy** (feeling sorry *for*) and **diagnosis** (categorising *about*).

> **Key Point:** Core triad = **congruence/genuineness + UPR + empathic understanding.** Of the three, Rogers regarded **congruence** as most basic. Empathy's signature qualifier is the **"as if"** quality. UPR is unconditional, which is what dissolves conditions of worth.

## 6.11 The Therapeutic Relationship

- The relationship **is** the therapy. There is no separate set of curative techniques operating alongside it; the climate created by the core conditions is itself the agent of change.
- The therapist is **non-expert**: they do not diagnose, advise, interpret, or steer. The client is the expert on their own experience and sets the direction.
- The core conditions are held to be **necessary and sufficient** (see 6.9), the strongest claim in the book about the relationship.

## PART FOUR: PROCESS, TECHNIQUE, AND APPLICATIONS

## 6.12 Rogers' Seven Stages of Process

Rogers' **Process Scale** describes a continuum along which a client moves during successful therapy, from rigidity/remoteness from experience toward fluidity and immediacy. The **seven stages**:

| Stage | Hallmark |
|-------|----------|
| **1** | Unwillingness to communicate about self; feelings unrecognised/unowned; rigidity; no desire to change. ("Stuck") |
| **2** | Talks about external topics; feelings described as not belonging to self; problems seen as external. |
| **3** | Talks about self as an object and about past feelings; emotion is distant ("there and then"). |
| **4** | Begins to describe deeper feelings; some present-tense feeling, but with mistrust/fear. |
| **5** | Feelings expressed freely **in the present**; experiences owned; growing acceptance; constructs loosen. |
| **6** | Previously stuck feelings experienced fully and immediately; physiological loosening; the self-concept becomes congruent with experiencing. (Often **irreversible**, a key change moment.) |
| **7** | New feelings experienced immediately and richly; **internal locus of evaluation**; living as a fluid process; the fully functioning person. |

> **Key Point:** Movement is from **fixity → fluidity**, from **external → internal locus of evaluation**, from feelings **denied/distant** to feelings **owned and present**. Stage 6 is the pivotal, often irreversible breakthrough; Stage 7 approaches the fully functioning person.

## 6.13 Techniques and the Deliberate De-emphasis of Technique

- Person-centred therapy is famously **anti-technique**. Rogers held that relying on techniques makes the therapist an expert *manipulating* the client and undermines genuineness; **techniques can never substitute for the attitudes** (the core conditions).
- What looks like technique is really the **expression of the core conditions**:
  - **Active listening** and **reflection of feeling** (mirroring back the felt meaning so the client hears and owns it). Rogers grew uneasy with "reflection" being taught as a mechanical trick; he preferred to see it as **testing/checking his understanding**.
  - **Accurate empathy** communicated moment to moment.
  - **Acceptance and warmth** (UPR in action).
  - **Genuineness/immediacy** (the therapist being real).
- No diagnosis, no advice, no interpretation, no questioning agenda, no homework. The client leads; the therapist follows the client's frame.

> **Key Point:** "Reflection of feeling" and "active listening" are the closest things to a technique, but Rogers insisted the **attitudes (core conditions) are primary** and technique without them is empty. Contrast with REBT/CBT, which are technique- and homework-heavy.

## 6.14 Encounter Groups

- In his later career Rogers extended the approach to **encounter groups** (intensive small groups, also called **basic encounter groups**), aimed at personal growth, openness, and improved communication among ordinarily healthy people rather than treating disorder.
- The same climate (genuineness, acceptance, empathy) is created among members; the facilitator is non-directive. Rogers documented a typical **process** the groups move through (initial milling and resistance → growing trust → expression of immediate feelings → therapeutic capacity within the group).
- This is the group-counselling application of person-centred principles.

## 6.15 The Evolution of the Name

The name changed three times, each shift signalling a deeper philosophical commitment:

1. **Non-directive therapy** (1940s): emphasised what the therapist does *not* do, no directing, advising, or interpreting; let the client lead. Risked reducing the approach to a passive technique (over-use of reflection).
2. **Client-centred therapy** (1951 onward): shifted emphasis from technique to the **client's frame of reference** and the centrality of the **relationship** and core conditions.
3. **Person-centred** (from the 1970s): broadened the approach beyond the clinical "client" to **education, groups, organisations, conflict resolution**, anywhere the conditions could foster growth in **persons**.

> **Key Point:** **Non-directive → client-centred → person-centred.** "Non-directive" is the earliest and stresses the absence of steering; "person-centred" is the latest and broadest (beyond therapy into education/peace work). The middle term "client-centred" is still used for the clinical application.

## 6.16 Used For / Evidence and Research Contributions

- Applied very broadly: individual counselling, the **common factors** movement, counsellor training (the core conditions are the backbone of basic counselling-skills training worldwide), education, and group work.
- Rogers' **research legacy** is large: he was first to **record and transcribe** sessions for study; he used the **Q-sort technique** (sorting self-descriptive statements) to measure the gap between **self and ideal self** before and after therapy (the gap narrows with successful therapy).
- The approach generated decades of process-outcome research and helped establish that the **therapeutic relationship/alliance** is one of the strongest predictors of outcome across therapies.
- **Emotion-focused therapy (EFT)** of **Leslie Greenberg** is a contemporary, more active descendant of the person-centred/experiential tradition.

## 6.17 Multicultural Considerations and Critique

**Multicultural strengths:** the emphasis on the client's own frame of reference, non-imposition of the therapist's values, and respect for the person fits a culturally sensitive stance well.

**Multicultural criticisms:** the values of **autonomy, individual self-actualisation, internal locus of evaluation, and open emotional expression** are culturally **Western/individualistic**; clients from **collectivist** cultures may expect, and prefer, an **expert who gives direction and advice**, and may read non-directiveness as the therapist being unhelpful or incompetent.

**General criticisms:**
- Too **passive / non-directive** for clients who want guidance, for crisis, and for **severe disorders** (psychosis, acute risk).
- The "**necessary and sufficient**" claim is **disputed**; most therapists today regard the core conditions as **necessary but not sufficient** (Beck explicitly says this in Ch 10).
- **Underplays technique**, assessment, the unconscious, the past, and the social/environmental context.
- Over-optimistic view of human nature; assumes a verbal, reflective, motivated client.

> **Key Point:** The single most testable critique: the core conditions are now widely held to be **necessary but NOT sufficient** (vs Rogers' original "necessary AND sufficient").

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **Core triad attribution.** Congruence/genuineness, **unconditional positive regard**, and **empathic understanding** = **Rogers (person-centred)**. Never attribute UPR or "empathy as the cure" to any other theorist.
2. **Six vs three.** There are **SIX** necessary and sufficient conditions in total; the therapist provides **THREE** (the core triad). The triad is a **subset** of the six. Distractors flip these numbers.
3. **Necessary *and* sufficient (Rogers) vs necessary *but not* sufficient (later/Beck).** Rogers claimed sufficiency; modern consensus (and Beck) say the conditions are necessary but **not** sufficient.
4. **The forgotten conditions.** Condition 1 = **psychological contact**; Condition 2 = the **client is incongruent/anxious**; Condition 6 = the **client perceives** the therapist's empathy and UPR. Questions love to ask for these "non-triad" conditions.
5. **UPR ≠ approval.** Unconditional positive regard means prizing the **person** regardless of behaviour, not approving of everything they do.
6. **Empathy vs sympathy.** Empathy = sensing the client's world **"as if"** it were one's own, *without losing the "as if."* Sympathy (feeling sorry for) is **not** empathy; losing the "as if" is over-identification, also not empathy.
7. **Actualising tendency.** Rogers posits **one** master motive (the actualising tendency). Not Maslow's hierarchy, not Glasser's five needs, not Adler's striving for superiority.
8. **Organismic valuing process vs conditions of worth.** OVP = the innate internal compass; **conditions of worth** = introjected external standards that displace it. The maladjusted person trusts conditions of worth; the fully functioning person trusts the OVP.
9. **Incongruence = self vs experience.** The Rogerian "pathology" construct is **incongruence** between self-concept and actual organismic experience, defended by **denial** and **distortion** (with **subception** detecting threat below awareness).
10. **Fully functioning person, the five.** Openness to experience, existential living, organismic trust, experiential freedom, creativity. (Not the same as Maslow's self-actualised-person list.)
11. **Name evolution order.** **Non-directive → client-centred → person-centred.** "Non-directive" is the **earliest** label; "person-centred" the **latest/broadest**.
12. **Non-directive = no diagnosis/advice/interpretation.** The therapist does **not** diagnose, advise, or interpret. Interpretation = psychoanalysis; disputing = REBT; the **miracle question** = solution-focused; the **empty chair** = Gestalt. Person-centred has **no** signature gimmick by design.
13. **Reflection of feeling** is the technique most associated with Rogers, but he subordinated it to the **attitudes**; he saw it as **checking understanding**, not a trick.
14. **Q-sort and recorded sessions** = Rogers' research methods; the Q-sort measures the **self vs ideal-self** gap.
15. **Third force.** Humanistic psychology is the **"third force"** (after psychoanalysis and behaviourism). Person-centred + Maslow are its anchors; the stance is **phenomenological**.
