# Ch 8: Behaviour Therapy

**Overview:** Behaviour therapy is not the creation of a single founder but a family of approaches built on **learning theory**: the assumption that behaviour, including symptomatic and maladaptive behaviour, is **learned** and can therefore be **unlearned** or relearned. There is no deep "personality" structure and no unconscious to interpret; a phobia is a conditioned fear response, a habit is a reinforced operant, and a skills deficit is something simply never learned. Change the learning history (the antecedents, the behaviour, the consequences) and you change the behaviour. The approach is rooted in three streams: **Pavlov's classical conditioning**, **Skinner's operant conditioning**, and **Bandura's social/observational learning**. Clinically it was crystallised by **Joseph Wolpe** (systematic desensitisation via reciprocal inhibition), **Hans Eysenck** (who coined and championed the term *behaviour therapy*), and **Arnold Lazarus** (later multimodal therapy). It is defined by a focus on **observable behaviour**, the **present** (not the distant past), **measurement**, **specific goals**, and **the strongest empirical evidence base** of the classical theories. The therapist is an active **teacher/trainer/consultant**, not a blank screen or a Rogerian companion.

---

## 8.1 The Three Roots of Behaviour Therapy

### Root 1: Pavlov and Classical (Respondent) Conditioning
- **Ivan Pavlov** (Russian physiologist, Nobel laureate for digestion work): discovered conditioning while studying salivation in dogs.
- A neutral stimulus (bell) repeatedly paired with an **unconditioned stimulus (UCS, food)** that automatically elicits an **unconditioned response (UCR, salivation)** comes to elicit a **conditioned response (CR)** on its own; the bell becomes a **conditioned stimulus (CS)**.
- Explains how **involuntary, reflexive, emotional** responses (fear, anxiety, nausea, arousal) get attached to previously neutral cues.
- **Watson & Rayner's "Little Albert" (1920)**: conditioned an 11-month-old to fear a white rat by pairing it with a loud noise (UCS); fear **generalised** to other furry objects. The experimental demonstration that phobias can be **learned**. (Watson = the father of behaviourism; "give me a dozen healthy infants...".)
- Clinical pay-off: phobias, anxiety, and conditioned aversions are treated by **counter-conditioning** and **exposure/extinction**.

### Root 2: Skinner and Operant (Instrumental) Conditioning
- **B. F. Skinner**: behaviour is controlled by its **consequences**. Behaviour that is **reinforced** increases; behaviour that is **punished** or **not reinforced** decreases.
- Concerns **voluntary, emitted** behaviour (operants) rather than reflexive respondents.
- Edward **Thorndike's Law of Effect** is the forerunner: responses followed by satisfying consequences are "stamped in".
- Clinical pay-off: token economies, contingency management, shaping of new skills, extinction of problem behaviour, applied behaviour analysis.

### Root 3: Bandura and Social/Observational Learning
- **Albert Bandura**: much human learning occurs **vicariously**, by **observing models** and the consequences of their behaviour, without direct reinforcement of the learner.
- **Bobo doll experiments**: children who watched an adult act aggressively toward an inflatable doll imitated that aggression; aggression rose further if the model was **rewarded** (vicarious reinforcement) and fell if the model was **punished** (vicarious punishment).
- Four processes for observational learning: **Attention → Retention → Reproduction (motor reproduction) → Motivation (reinforcement)**.
- **Self-efficacy**: a person's belief in their capability to perform a behaviour. Bandura argued that techniques work partly by **raising self-efficacy**. Four sources: **(1) performance/mastery accomplishments** (most powerful), **(2) vicarious experience**, **(3) verbal/social persuasion**, **(4) physiological/emotional arousal states**.
- **Reciprocal determinism**: person, behaviour, and environment all influence one another mutually (not one-directional environmental control).
- Bandura is the bridge from strict behaviourism toward the **cognitive** turn (his later work = social cognitive theory).

> **Key Point:** Classical conditioning = learning by **association** of stimuli; the response is **elicited** and **involuntary** (emotional/reflexive). Operant conditioning = learning by **consequences**; the response is **emitted** and **voluntary**. Observational learning = learning by **watching a model**, no direct reinforcement needed.

## 8.2 Wolpe and Reciprocal Inhibition

- **Joseph Wolpe** (South African psychiatrist) developed **systematic desensitisation** from his principle of **reciprocal inhibition**.
- **Reciprocal inhibition**: two incompatible (antagonistic) responses cannot occur at the same time. **Relaxation is physiologically incompatible with anxiety**; if relaxation is evoked in the presence of an anxiety-provoking stimulus, the relaxation **inhibits/displaces** the anxiety, weakening the bond between stimulus and anxiety.
- This is a form of **counter-conditioning**: a new, adaptive response (calm) is conditioned to a stimulus that previously evoked a maladaptive response (fear).
- Wolpe used **progressive muscle relaxation** (after Jacobson) as the competing response.

> **Key Point:** Reciprocal inhibition (Wolpe) is the theoretical engine of systematic desensitisation. The competing/incompatible response is **relaxation**; it cannot coexist with **anxiety**, so anxiety is "inhibited" out.

## 8.3 Classical Conditioning Principles

| Principle | Meaning | Clinical relevance |
|-----------|---------|--------------------|
| **Acquisition** | Initial learning of the CS-CR link through CS-UCS pairing | How a phobia is formed (e.g., dog + bite) |
| **Extinction** | CS presented repeatedly **without** the UCS → CR gradually weakens and disappears | Basis of **exposure** therapy (face the feared CS, no feared outcome follows) |
| **Spontaneous recovery** | A previously extinguished CR reappears after a rest interval | Why fears can return; need for repeated/overlearned exposure |
| **Generalisation (stimulus)** | Stimuli **similar** to the CS also evoke the CR | Little Albert fearing all furry things; phobia spreading |
| **Discrimination** | Learning to respond to the CS but **not** to similar stimuli | The opposite of generalisation; narrowing the fear |
| **Higher-order (second-order) conditioning** | A new neutral stimulus paired with an established CS becomes a CS itself (no UCS needed) | How elaborate fear networks build up |

> **Key Point:** **Extinction** in classical conditioning = the CS is presented **without** the UCS until the CR fades. This is exactly what therapeutic **exposure** does: confront the feared cue while the feared consequence does not occur.

## 8.4 Operant Conditioning Principles

### Reinforcement vs Punishment (the core 2×2)

| Contingency | Stimulus added/removed | Effect on behaviour | Example |
|-------------|------------------------|---------------------|---------|
| **Positive reinforcement** | **Add** a pleasant stimulus | **Increases** behaviour | Praise a child for homework → more homework |
| **Negative reinforcement** | **Remove** an aversive stimulus | **Increases** behaviour | Take aspirin to remove a headache → more aspirin-taking; seatbelt buzzer stops when buckled |
| **Positive punishment** | **Add** an aversive stimulus | **Decreases** behaviour | A scolding after misbehaviour → less misbehaviour |
| **Negative punishment** (response cost / omission) | **Remove** a pleasant stimulus | **Decreases** behaviour | Take away phone privileges → less rule-breaking; **time-out** |

> **Key Point (the #1 trap):** **Negative reinforcement is NOT punishment.** "Negative" means a stimulus is **removed**, not that the behaviour is "bad" or being discouraged. **All reinforcement INCREASES behaviour; all punishment DECREASES behaviour.** Negative reinforcement increases behaviour by removing something aversive (escape/avoidance). This single distinction is tested at almost every entrance exam.

### Schedules of Reinforcement

| Schedule | Rule | Response pattern | Resistance to extinction |
|----------|------|------------------|---------------------------|
| **Continuous (CRF)** | Reinforce **every** response | Fast acquisition | **Low** (extinguishes quickly) |
| **Fixed Ratio (FR)** | Reinforce after a **set number** of responses | High rate, post-reinforcement pause | Moderate |
| **Variable Ratio (VR)** | Reinforce after a **variable/average number** of responses | **Highest, steadiest** rate | **Highest** (gambling, slot machines) |
| **Fixed Interval (FI)** | Reinforce **first response after a set time** | "Scalloped" (rate rises near deadline) | Low-moderate |
| **Variable Interval (VI)** | Reinforce first response after a **variable/average time** | Slow, steady | High |

> **Key Point (schedule trap):** **Continuous reinforcement** produces the **fastest learning** but the **fastest extinction**. **Variable (intermittent) schedules**, especially **variable ratio**, are the **most resistant to extinction** (the **partial reinforcement extinction effect**). This is why intermittently rewarded behaviours (gambling, a child whose tantrums are *sometimes* given in to) are so hard to extinguish. Best practice: use continuous reinforcement to **establish** a behaviour, then thin to intermittent to **maintain** it.

### Other operant principles

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Shaping (successive approximations)** | Reinforce closer and closer approximations to a target behaviour the person cannot yet perform |
| **Chaining** | Link a sequence of discrete behaviours into a complex behaviour (forward or backward chaining), each step cueing the next |
| **Extinction (operant)** | **Withhold** the reinforcer that maintains a behaviour → behaviour declines (note the **extinction burst**: a temporary increase before the decline) |
| **Premack principle** | A **high-frequency/preferred** activity reinforces a **low-frequency/less-preferred** one ("Grandma's rule": eat your vegetables, then you get dessert) |
| **Discriminative stimulus (S^D)** | A cue signalling that reinforcement is available for a response |
| **Prompting & fading** | Add cues to evoke a behaviour, then gradually remove them |
| **Generalisation & discrimination** | Same logic as classical: transfer of a learned operant across settings, or restriction to specific cues |

> **Key Point:** Operant **extinction** = stop the **reinforcer**, not punish. Expect an **extinction burst** (behaviour worsens briefly) before it improves; warn caregivers so they do not "give in" and accidentally place the behaviour on a powerful **variable-ratio** schedule.

## 8.5 Behavioural Assessment: Functional Analysis (ABC)

- Behaviour therapy begins with a **behavioural assessment**, not a diagnosis or a history of childhood.
- **Functional analysis** identifies the **function** a behaviour serves by mapping its **ABCs**:
  - **A = Antecedents** (triggers/discriminative stimuli; what happens *before*)
  - **B = Behaviour** (the specific, observable, measurable target behaviour)
  - **C = Consequences** (what happens *after* that maintains the behaviour, e.g., attention, escape, tangible reward, sensory)
- Methods: behavioural **interview**, **direct observation**, **self-monitoring** (client records own behaviour), **rating scales**, **physiological measures**, **role-play / behavioural avoidance tests**.
- Establishes a **baseline** so change can be measured; assessment is **continuous** (ongoing, tied to treatment).
- The aim is a **clear operational definition** of the problem behaviour (frequency, intensity, duration, latency).

> **Key Point:** The **ABC functional analysis** is the signature assessment of behaviour therapy: figure out what **maintains** the behaviour (its consequence/function) before choosing an intervention. The same surface behaviour can serve different functions (attention vs escape) and needs different treatment.

---

## 8.6 Goals of Behaviour Therapy

- Goals are **specific, concrete, observable, and measurable**, negotiated collaboratively with the client.
- Stated as **behaviour to increase** (adaptive skills) and **behaviour to decrease** (maladaptive responses), not vague feeling-states.
- Progress is tracked against the **baseline** with objective data.
- Avoid global aims ("feel better", "more confident"); translate them into behavioural referents (e.g., "initiate one conversation per day", "ride the lift without leaving").

---

## 8.7 The Role of the Therapeutic Relationship

- A good relationship is **important but not curative in itself** (contrast with person-centred therapy, where the relationship is *necessary and sufficient*).
- The therapist is an active **teacher / trainer / consultant / coach** who forms a **working alliance**, builds collaboration, motivates, and **reinforces** progress.
- A positive relationship improves engagement, homework adherence, and the credibility of the rationale, but the **techniques** are seen as the active ingredient.

> **Key Point:** In behaviour therapy the relationship is a **means to an end / working alliance**, not the mechanism of change. (Same stance as REBT; opposite of Rogers, for whom the relationship *is* the cure.)

## 8.8 Techniques of Behaviour Therapy

| Technique | Conditioning root | What it does | Typical use |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Systematic desensitisation** (Wolpe) | Classical (counter-conditioning) | Pair a **graded anxiety hierarchy** with **relaxation** so calm replaces fear (reciprocal inhibition) | Phobias, specific anxiety |
| **Relaxation training** | Classical (response component) | Progressive muscle relaxation, breathing, imagery; the competing response | Anxiety, tension, adjunct to many techniques |
| **In-vivo exposure** | Classical (extinction) | Confront the **real** feared stimulus, graded, until anxiety extinguishes | Phobias, agoraphobia, OCD |
| **Imaginal exposure** | Classical (extinction) | Confront the feared stimulus **in imagination** when in-vivo is impractical | PTSD memories, hard-to-arrange fears |
| **Flooding** | Classical (extinction) | **Intense, prolonged** exposure to the **full-strength** feared stimulus, **no hierarchy, no relaxation**, until anxiety burns out | Phobias, OCD (intensive) |
| **Implosive therapy** (Stampfl) | Classical (extinction) | Like flooding but **in imagination** and using **exaggerated, psychodynamically themed** anxiety-laden imagery | Severe anxiety/phobia |
| **Aversion therapy** | Classical (counter-conditioning) | Pair the **unwanted behaviour** with an **unpleasant stimulus** (shock, nausea-inducing drug) so it becomes aversive | Addictions, paraphilias (controversial) |
| **Covert sensitisation** (Cautela) | Classical (imagined aversion) | Pair the unwanted behaviour with an **imagined** aversive consequence (no real shock) | Smoking, overeating, problem behaviours |
| **Token economy** | Operant | Earn **tokens** for target behaviours, exchange for backup **reinforcers** | Wards, classrooms, institutions |
| **Contingency contracting** | Operant | Written agreement specifying behaviours and their consequences (rewards/costs) | Families, couples, school, addictions |
| **Modeling** (Bandura) | Observational | Learn/strengthen a behaviour by **watching a model** (live, symbolic, participant) | Skills training, phobia reduction |
| **Behavioural rehearsal** | Operant + observational | **Practise** the target behaviour (often with role-play), get feedback, reinforce | Social/assertion skills |
| **Assertiveness / social-skills training** | Operant + observational | Instruction, modeling, rehearsal, feedback, reinforcement, homework to build interpersonal skills | Social anxiety, passivity, aggression |
| **Self-management / self-control** | Operant (self-applied) | Self-monitoring, self-reinforcement, stimulus control, goal-setting by the client | Habits, weight, study, self-directed change |
| **Biofeedback** | Operant (physiological) | Instruments give real-time feedback on bodily signals (HR, EMG, GSR) so the client learns voluntary control | Tension headache, hypertension, anxiety |

### Systematic desensitisation: the three steps
1. **Relaxation training** (usually progressive muscle relaxation).
2. **Construct the anxiety hierarchy**: rank feared scenes from least to most anxiety-provoking, often using **SUDs (Subjective Units of Distress, 0-100)**.
3. **Graded pairing**: while deeply relaxed, the client imagines (or, in-vivo, approaches) each hierarchy item from the bottom up, moving up only when each step evokes no anxiety, so **relaxation inhibits anxiety** at every level.

### Modeling: types
- **Live** modeling (a real person demonstrates), **symbolic** modeling (video/film), **participant modeling** (the model demonstrates and then guides the client through it; very effective for phobias), and **covert** modeling (imagined model).

> **Key Point (the classic exam pair):** **Systematic desensitisation** = **graded** hierarchy + **relaxation** (reciprocal inhibition), gentle and gradual, anxiety never allowed to peak. **Flooding** = **maximal** exposure, **no** hierarchy, **no** relaxation, anxiety deliberately peaks and then **extinguishes**. Same underlying mechanism (exposure/extinction), opposite intensity. **Implosion = flooding done in imagination** (with exaggerated imagery); **covert sensitisation = aversion done in imagination.**

## 8.9 Applied Behaviour Analysis (ABA)

- **Applied Behaviour Analysis** is the systematic application of **operant** principles to socially significant behaviour, descended directly from Skinner.
- Hallmarks: **functional analysis**, precise **operational definitions**, continuous **data collection**, **single-subject designs** (e.g., **ABAB / reversal**, multiple baseline) to demonstrate that the intervention (not chance) caused change.
- Core tools: reinforcement, shaping, chaining, prompting/fading, extinction, token systems, **differential reinforcement** (e.g., **DRO** = reinforce the *absence* of the behaviour; **DRA/DRI** = reinforce an alternative/incompatible behaviour).
- Best known for work with **autism spectrum disorder**, intellectual disability, and developmental settings, and in education and organisational settings (OBM).

> **Key Point:** ABA is the **operant** wing applied and measured rigorously, using **single-subject (within-subject) designs** like the **ABAB reversal** to prove the intervention caused the change.

## 8.10 The Third Wave (Link Forward)

- **First wave** = traditional behaviour therapy (this chapter): classical + operant conditioning, observable behaviour.
- **Second wave** = **cognitive** and **cognitive-behavioural** therapies: **Ellis's REBT** (Ch 9) and **Beck's Cognitive Therapy** (Ch 10) added **thoughts/beliefs** as targets, giving **CBT**.
- **Third wave** = acceptance- and mindfulness-based behavioural therapies: **ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy, Hayes)**, **DBT (Dialectical Behaviour Therapy, Linehan)**, **MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy)**, **FAP**. These emphasise **acceptance, mindfulness, values, and the function/context of thoughts** rather than directly changing thought *content*.

> **Key Point:** Behaviour therapy is the **first wave**. CBT/REBT (Ch 9-10) are the **second wave** (adding cognition). ACT and DBT are the **third wave** (adding mindfulness, acceptance, and context).

## 8.11 Multicultural Perspective and Critique

**Multicultural strengths**
- Concrete, **present-focused, action-oriented**, psychoeducational, and time-limited; fits clients who prefer practical problem-solving over insight or emotional exploration.
- Goals are **explicit and collaborative**, reducing hidden therapist values.
- **Self-efficacy** and skills-building generalise across cultural settings; strong fit with collectivist and pragmatic orientations.

**Multicultural cautions**
- The therapist's choice of "adaptive" target behaviour can impose **majority-culture norms** (e.g., what counts as appropriate **assertiveness** varies by culture and gender).
- Directiveness may suit some clients and alienate others.

**General criticisms**
- May **ignore feelings, insight, the relationship, and the underlying cause**; risk of treating the **symptom** while missing meaning.
- The older "behaviour explains everything / radical environmentalism" stance is **reductionist** and underplays cognition, emotion, and biology.
- Concerns about **manipulation/control** and, historically, the ethics of **aversion therapy**.
- Symptom **relapse / spontaneous recovery** if exposure is incomplete or reinforcement is withdrawn too soon.

**Strengths**
- The **strongest empirical/evidence base** of the classical theories; highly effective for **phobias, anxiety, OCD, habit and skills problems**, and in institutional/developmental settings.

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **Negative reinforcement is NOT punishment.** "Negative" = a stimulus is **removed**. Negative reinforcement **increases** behaviour (by removing something aversive); punishment **decreases** behaviour. This is the single most-tested distinction.

2. **All reinforcement increases behaviour; all punishment decreases it.** "Positive/negative" only tells you whether a stimulus was **added** or **removed**, never whether the behaviour is good or bad.

3. **Continuous vs intermittent schedules and extinction resistance.** Continuous reinforcement = **fastest acquisition but fastest extinction**; **variable-ratio (intermittent)** = **most resistant to extinction** (partial reinforcement extinction effect; the gambling schedule).

4. **Systematic desensitisation = Wolpe**, built on **reciprocal inhibition**, and uses a **graded hierarchy + relaxation**. Do not attribute it to Skinner or Bandura.

5. **Systematic desensitisation vs flooding:** SD is **graded + relaxation, anxiety never peaks**; flooding is **maximal, no hierarchy, no relaxation, anxiety peaks then extinguishes**. **Implosion = flooding in imagination**.

6. **Token economy = operant/Skinnerian** (not classical, not Bandura). **Modeling = Bandura/observational.** **Aversion therapy = classical counter-conditioning.**

7. **Covert sensitisation vs aversion therapy:** covert sensitisation uses an **imagined** aversive consequence; aversion therapy uses a **real** aversive stimulus.

8. **Reciprocal inhibition** = two **incompatible** responses cannot co-occur; **relaxation inhibits anxiety**. It is the *mechanism*; systematic desensitisation is the *technique*.

9. **Classical = involuntary/elicited/respondent (association of stimuli); operant = voluntary/emitted (consequences); observational = watching a model.** Pavlov / Skinner / Bandura respectively.

10. **Little Albert = Watson & Rayner** (classical conditioning of fear + generalisation). **Bobo doll = Bandura** (observational learning of aggression + vicarious reinforcement).

11. **Self-efficacy is Bandura's**, and its **most powerful source is performance/mastery accomplishments** (not verbal persuasion).

12. **Premack principle:** a **high-probability/preferred** behaviour reinforces a **low-probability** one (not the other way round). "Grandma's rule".

13. **Extinction burst:** when a reinforcer is withdrawn, the behaviour **temporarily increases** before it declines; do not mistake this for failure.

14. **The relationship in behaviour therapy is a working alliance / means to an end**, important but **not curative in itself** (contrast Rogers: necessary and sufficient).

15. **Shaping vs chaining:** shaping = reinforcing **successive approximations** to a new behaviour; chaining = **linking** an existing sequence of behaviours into a complex one.

16. **Wave sequence:** behaviour therapy = **first wave**; CBT/REBT (cognition) = **second wave**; ACT/DBT (mindfulness, acceptance) = **third wave**.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Classical conditioning** | Learning by association: a neutral stimulus paired with a UCS comes to elicit a CR (Pavlov) |
| **UCS / UCR / CS / CR** | Unconditioned stimulus/response (automatic) vs conditioned stimulus/response (learned) |
| **Operant conditioning** | Learning controlled by consequences of voluntary behaviour (Skinner) |
| **Observational (social) learning** | Learning by watching a model and the consequences of its behaviour (Bandura) |
| **Reciprocal inhibition** | Incompatible responses cannot co-occur; relaxation inhibits anxiety (Wolpe) |
| **Counter-conditioning** | Conditioning a new, adaptive response to a stimulus that evoked a maladaptive one |
| **Positive reinforcement** | Adding a pleasant stimulus to increase behaviour |
| **Negative reinforcement** | Removing an aversive stimulus to increase behaviour |
| **Positive punishment** | Adding an aversive stimulus to decrease behaviour |
| **Negative punishment (response cost)** | Removing a pleasant stimulus to decrease behaviour (e.g., time-out) |
| **Extinction** | Withholding the UCS (classical) or reinforcer (operant) so the response fades |
| **Extinction burst** | Temporary increase in behaviour when reinforcement is first withdrawn |
| **Spontaneous recovery** | Reappearance of an extinguished response after a rest interval |
| **Generalisation** | Responding to stimuli similar to the trained one |
| **Discrimination** | Responding only to the specific trained stimulus, not similar ones |
| **Schedules of reinforcement** | Rules for when responses are reinforced (FR, VR, FI, VI, continuous) |
| **Variable ratio** | Reinforcement after a variable number of responses; most resistant to extinction |
| **Shaping** | Reinforcing successive approximations to a target behaviour |
| **Chaining** | Linking discrete behaviours into a sequence |
| **Premack principle** | A preferred activity reinforces a less-preferred one |
| **Functional analysis (ABC)** | Mapping Antecedents, Behaviour, Consequences to find a behaviour's function |
| **Self-efficacy** | Belief in one's capability to perform a behaviour (Bandura) |
| **Reciprocal determinism** | Mutual influence of person, behaviour, and environment (Bandura) |
| **Systematic desensitisation** | Graded hierarchy + relaxation to extinguish anxiety (Wolpe) |
| **Anxiety hierarchy / SUDs** | Ranked list of feared scenes rated 0-100 in Subjective Units of Distress |
| **Flooding** | Intense, prolonged, full-strength exposure with no hierarchy or relaxation |
| **Implosive therapy** | Flooding conducted in imagination with exaggerated imagery (Stampfl) |
| **Aversion therapy** | Pairing an unwanted behaviour with a real aversive stimulus |
| **Covert sensitisation** | Pairing an unwanted behaviour with an imagined aversive consequence (Cautela) |
| **Token economy** | Earning tokens for target behaviour, exchangeable for backup reinforcers |
| **Contingency contracting** | Written agreement linking behaviours to consequences |
| **Modeling** | Learning by observing a model (live, symbolic, participant, covert) |
| **Behavioural rehearsal** | Practising a target behaviour, often by role-play, with feedback |
| **Biofeedback** | Instrument feedback enabling voluntary control of physiological responses |
| **Applied Behaviour Analysis (ABA)** | Rigorous, data-based application of operant principles; single-subject designs |
| **Differential reinforcement (DRO/DRA/DRI)** | Reinforcing absence of, or an alternative/incompatible, behaviour |
