# Ch 2: Psychoanalysis (Sigmund Freud)

**Overview:** Psychoanalysis is the first systematic theory of personality and psychotherapy, founded by **Sigmund Freud (1856-1939)**, a Viennese neurologist. It is the historical parent of every later "talk therapy"; almost every other approach (Jung, Adler, the humanists, even CBT) defines itself partly by reacting against it. Its central claims are that (1) behaviour is largely **determined** by unconscious forces and early-childhood experience (**psychic determinism**), (2) the mind is driven by **instincts/drives** (sex/libido and aggression), (3) the personality is structured into **id, ego, and superego** in continual conflict, (4) the ego defends itself against anxiety with **defence mechanisms**, and (5) cure comes from **insight**, that is, making the unconscious conscious. This chapter gives the full canonical treatment tested at NIMHANS and MET entrances.

---

## 2.1 Freud's Life and the Historical Context

- **Sigmund Freud (1856-1939)**: born in Freiberg, Moravia (now Czech Republic); trained as a **physician/neurologist** in Vienna; fled to London in 1938 after the Nazi annexation of Austria; died there in 1939 of cancer of the jaw.
- Early collaboration with **Josef Breuer** on hysteria. Breuer's patient **Anna O. (Bertha Pappenheim)** coined the phrase **"talking cure"** (and "chimney sweeping"); her case launched the cathartic method.
- Freud briefly used **hypnosis** and Breuer's **catharsis/abreaction** method, then abandoned hypnosis in favour of **free association** (the **"basic rule"**), the technical cornerstone of psychoanalysis.
- Studied under **Charcot** (Paris, Salpetriere) who used hypnosis with hysterical patients; this convinced Freud that symptoms had **psychological**, not purely organic, causes.
- 1900: *The Interpretation of Dreams* (introduced dream analysis; Freud's own self-analysis). 1901: *The Psychopathology of Everyday Life* (parapraxes / "Freudian slips"). 1905: *Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality* (psychosexual development).
- Key collaborators and breakaways: **Carl Jung** (broke 1913 over the sexual nature of libido), **Alfred Adler** (broke 1911 over the sexual emphasis), **Anna Freud** (his daughter; systematised ego defences and founded child psychoanalysis), **Melanie Klein** (object relations / child analysis).

> **Key Point:** Freud's data were almost entirely **case studies** of a small, non-representative sample (largely upper-middle-class Viennese women with hysteria). This is the root of the standard criticism that his theory is rich but **unfalsifiable** and built on a **biased sample**.

---

## 2.2 Drive (Instinct) Theory

- Freud held that mental life runs on **psychic energy** generated by **instincts (drives, German *Trieb*)**. Behaviour is fundamentally motivated by the discharge of this energy (the **pleasure principle**).
- **Eros (life instinct)**: drives toward survival, reproduction, pleasure, and creativity. Its energy is **libido**, the central form of psychic energy. (Freud increasingly treated libido as essentially **sexual**, broadly defined.)
- **Thanatos (death instinct)**: introduced later (1920, *Beyond the Pleasure Principle*); drives toward aggression, destruction, and a return to an inorganic, tensionless state. Its energy is sometimes called **mortido / destrudo**. Turned outward it becomes **aggression**; turned inward, self-destruction.
- **Cathexis**: the investment of psychic energy in an object, person, or idea. **Anticathexis**: energy the ego uses to block or restrain an unacceptable impulse.
- The system is **economic** (energy is finite and gets distributed), **dynamic** (forces in conflict), and **topographic/structural** (different regions and agencies).

> **Key Point:** The two basic drives are **Eros (life, libido)** and **Thanatos (death, aggression)**. "Will to **pleasure**" = Freud; "will to **power**" = Adler; "will to **meaning**" = Frankl. This contrast is a classic exam item.

---

## 2.3 The Topographic Model (Levels of Consciousness)

Freud's **first model of the mind** (pre-1923) divided mental life by **accessibility to awareness**.

| Level | Definition | Contents |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Conscious** | What you are aware of right now | Current thoughts, perceptions, feelings; a small fraction of mental life |
| **Preconscious** | Not currently in awareness but easily retrievable | Memories, stored knowledge you can call up at will (e.g., your phone number) |
| **Unconscious** | Inaccessible to ordinary awareness; reached only indirectly | Repressed wishes, traumatic memories, primitive drives, conflicts; the **largest and most important** region |

- The famous **iceberg analogy**: the conscious mind is the tip above water; the vast unconscious lies submerged.
- The unconscious is revealed only **indirectly**: through dreams, slips of the tongue (**parapraxes**), jokes, symptoms, and free association.

> **Key Point:** The topographic model (conscious/preconscious/unconscious) was Freud's **earlier** model. He later **added** (did not replace) the structural model (id/ego/superego). The two coexist: e.g., much of the ego and most of the superego operate **unconsciously**.

---

## 2.4 The Structural Model (id, ego, superego)

Freud's **second model** (from 1923, *The Ego and the Id*) divides the mind into three **agencies** by function.

| Structure | Principle | Process | Present from | Role |
|-----------|-----------|---------|--------------|------|
| **Id** | **Pleasure principle** | **Primary process** (illogical, timeless, wish-fulfilling imagery) | Birth | Reservoir of instinctual energy; seeks immediate gratification; entirely **unconscious** |
| **Ego** | **Reality principle** | **Secondary process** (logical, reality-testing, planning) | ~6-8 months | The **executive/mediator**; balances id, superego, and reality; mostly conscious/preconscious but partly unconscious |
| **Superego** | **Morality / ideals** | Internalised standards | ~3-5 yrs (resolution of Oedipus) | Conscience (punishes via guilt) + ego-ideal (rewards via pride); largely unconscious |

- **Id**: present at birth; demands immediate tension reduction; cannot tolerate frustration; amoral, irrational.
- **Ego**: develops out of the id to deal with the external world; uses **reality testing** and **defence mechanisms**; the seat of **anxiety**.
- **Superego**: develops from **identification** with parents and the resolution of the **Oedipus complex**; two parts:
  - **Conscience**: internalises punishments; produces **guilt** for wrongdoing.
  - **Ego-ideal**: internalises rewards/standards; produces **pride** when met.

> **Key Point:** The **ego is the mediator**, caught between the demands of the id (instincts), the superego (morality), and external reality. When the ego cannot reconcile these, it experiences **anxiety** and deploys **defence mechanisms**. The ego "serves three harsh masters."

---

## 2.5 Psychosexual Stages of Development

Freud proposed that the libido focuses on a different **erogenous zone** at each stage. **Fixation** (libido getting "stuck") results from **over-gratification or under-gratification** at a stage and shapes adult personality.

| Stage | Approx. Age | Erogenous Zone | Key Conflict / Task | Fixation Outcomes |
|-------|------------|----------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Oral** | 0-1 yr | Mouth (sucking, biting) | Weaning; dependency vs. independence | **Oral-receptive**: dependency, gullibility, smoking, overeating, optimism. **Oral-aggressive**: sarcasm, hostility, "biting" wit |
| **Anal** | 1-3 yrs | Anus (retention/elimination) | **Toilet training**; control | **Anal-retentive**: orderly, stingy, obstinate, rigid. **Anal-expulsive**: messy, destructive, disorganised |
| **Phallic** | 3-6 yrs | Genitals | **Oedipus / Electra complex**; identification; superego forms | Vanity, recklessness, or excessive modesty; sexual/identity difficulties |
| **Latency** | 6-puberty | (dormant) | Sublimation of sexual energy into school, friendships, skills | (No fixation stage proper; consolidation period) |
| **Genital** | Puberty onward | Genitals (mature) | Mature sexuality; love and work | Healthy adult if earlier stages resolved; the goal is the capacity **"to love and to work"** (*lieben und arbeiten*) |

### The Phallic Stage in Detail
- **Oedipus complex (boys)**: unconscious sexual desire for the mother and rivalry/hostility toward the father; fear of retaliation produces **castration anxiety**. Resolved by **repressing** the desire and **identifying** with the father (taking on his values, forming the superego).
- **Electra complex / penis envy (girls)**: Freud's parallel; the girl desires the father, blames the mother, and experiences **penis envy**. (The term "Electra complex" is **Jung's**, not Freud's; Freud wrote of the "female Oedipus complex.") Resolution and superego formation in girls were considered (by Freud) weaker, a much-criticised, sexist claim.

> **Key Point:** **Fixation** is caused by either **excessive gratification or excessive frustration** at a stage. The phallic-stage **Oedipus complex** is the engine of **superego formation** and **gender identification**. The mature endpoint (genital stage) is the ability **to love and to work**.

## 2.6 Anxiety

Freud (1926) distinguished **three types of anxiety** by the source of the threat. Anxiety is a **signal** to the ego that danger is near, triggering defences.

| Type | Source of Threat | Description |
|------|------------------|-------------|
| **Reality (objective) anxiety** | The external world | Fear of a real, external danger (a snake, a fire); the most adaptive form |
| **Neurotic anxiety** | The **id** | Fear that the ego will lose control of instinctual impulses and do something punishable |
| **Moral anxiety** | The **superego** | Fear of violating one's own conscience; experienced as **guilt or shame** |

> **Key Point:** Anxiety is the ego's distress **signal**. Reality anxiety = external; neurotic anxiety = fear of the **id's** impulses; moral anxiety = fear of the **superego** (guilt). Defence mechanisms are the ego's response to neurotic and moral anxiety.

---

## 2.7 Ego Defence Mechanisms

Defence mechanisms are **unconscious** strategies the ego uses to distort, deny, or falsify reality to reduce anxiety. They are **normal** in moderation but **pathological** when rigid or overused. **Anna Freud** systematised and extended them (*The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defence*, 1936). **Repression** is the **master/primary defence** underlying all others.

| Defence | Definition | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Repression** | The "master defence"; unconsciously pushing threatening thoughts/impulses out of awareness | No memory of a childhood trauma |
| **Denial** | Refusing to accept an external reality | A terminally ill patient insists nothing is wrong |
| **Projection** | Attributing one's own unacceptable impulses to someone else | "He hates me" (when really I hate him) |
| **Reaction formation** | Expressing the **opposite** of a threatening impulse | Being overly kind to someone you unconsciously despise |
| **Displacement** | Redirecting an impulse from a threatening target to a safer one | Yelling at the dog after being scolded by the boss |
| **Sublimation** | Channelling unacceptable impulses into **socially valued** activity (the only "healthy/mature" defence) | Aggression → becoming a surgeon or boxer |
| **Regression** | Reverting to behaviour from an earlier developmental stage under stress | An adult sulking or bed-wetting under stress |
| **Rationalization** | Inventing plausible but false reasons to justify behaviour | "I failed because the test was unfair" |
| **Intellectualization** | Dealing with an emotional threat in abstract, detached, intellectual terms | Discussing a diagnosis purely in clinical statistics |
| **Identification** | Taking on the qualities of another (esp. a feared/admired figure) to reduce anxiety | Boy identifies with father to resolve Oedipus complex |
| **Undoing** | Symbolically "cancelling out" an unacceptable act/thought with a ritual | Compulsive hand-washing after a "dirty" thought |
| **Introjection** | Taking in (internalising) others' values/standards as one's own | Adopting an aggressor's values |
| **Identification with the aggressor** | Adopting the behaviour of a threatening person to master fear | Abused child imitates the abuser |
| **Compensation** | Covering a weakness by excelling in another area | Poor athlete becomes a top scholar |
| **Fantasy** | Gratifying frustrated wishes in imagination | Daydreaming of success |

> **Key Point:** **Sublimation** is considered the **most mature/adaptive** defence (it channels impulses constructively). **Repression** is the **most basic** and underlies the others. **Reaction formation** = doing the **opposite**; **projection** = attributing **your** impulse to others. Distinguish **displacement** (new target) from **sublimation** (socially valued outlet) and **regression** (going backward in development).

## 2.8 Theory of Psychopathology

- Symptoms are **meaningful**, not random; they are **compromise formations** that simultaneously express and disguise an unconscious conflict.
- The core sequence: **unconscious conflict + unresolved early-childhood experience → anxiety → defence mechanisms → symptoms**.
- **Fixation** at, or **regression** to, an early psychosexual stage predisposes to particular adult pathologies (e.g., obsessional traits ↔ anal fixation).
- **Neurosis** arises when defences (especially repression) bind anxiety but at the cost of symptoms; the repressed conflict keeps pressing for expression (the **return of the repressed**).
- Behaviour is **overdetermined** (a single symptom has multiple unconscious causes) and follows **psychic determinism** (nothing in mental life is accidental, including slips and dreams).

> **Key Point:** In the psychoanalytic view, symptoms are not the problem to be removed directly; they are **surface signals** of an underlying unconscious conflict. Treat the conflict (via insight), not just the symptom.

---

## 2.9 Goals of Psychoanalysis

1. **Make the unconscious conscious** ("Where id was, there ego shall be" - *Wo Es war, soll Ich werden*). This is the defining goal.
2. **Strengthen the ego** so it is less driven by the id and superego and more by reality.
3. **Resolve unconscious conflicts** rooted in childhood that are re-enacted in the present.
4. Promote **insight** into the origins and meaning of one's behaviour; insight is the **engine of change**.
5. Achieve a **reorganisation of personality (structural change)**, not merely symptom relief; the mature aim is the capacity to **love and to work**.

---

## 2.10 The Analytic Relationship

- The analyst classically adopts a **neutral, "blank-screen" / anonymous** stance (the **rule of abstinence**), so the patient's reactions reflect their own inner world rather than the real analyst. The patient lies on the **couch** with the analyst out of sight.

### Transference
- **Transference**: the client **unconsciously redirects** feelings, attitudes, and wishes from important early figures (usually parents) **onto the analyst**.
- **Positive transference** (affection, idealisation) vs. **negative transference** (hostility, distrust).
- The **transference neurosis** is the re-creation, in the therapy relationship, of the patient's central childhood conflict; **analysing the transference is the core work** of psychoanalysis.

### Countertransference
- **Countertransference**: the analyst's **own** unconscious emotional reactions to the patient (often the analyst's unresolved conflicts triggered by the patient).
- Classically seen as an **obstacle** to be monitored and controlled (which is why analysts undergo a **training analysis**); modern views treat it as **useful diagnostic information** about the patient.

### Working Alliance
- **Working (therapeutic) alliance**: the rational, collaborative bond between the patient's reasonable ego and the analyst, distinct from transference; the realistic partnership that makes the work possible.

> **Key Point:** The single defining relational mechanism of psychoanalysis is **transference** (and its analysis). **Transference = patient onto analyst**; **countertransference = analyst onto patient**. Do not confuse the two on the exam.

## 2.11 Techniques

| Technique | What It Is | Purpose |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Free association** | Patient says whatever comes to mind, **without censorship** (the "basic rule") | The chief technique; bypasses ego defences to reveal unconscious material |
| **Dream analysis** | Interpreting dreams, the **"royal road to the unconscious"** | Access repressed wishes via dream symbolism |
| **Analysis of resistance** | Identifying and interpreting the patient's blocks to the process | Resistance signals where the most important material lies |
| **Interpretation** | The analyst explains the unconscious meaning of behaviour, dreams, resistance, transference | Promotes insight; must be **well-timed** and within the patient's tolerance |
| **Analysis of transference** | Interpreting the feelings transferred onto the analyst | The central curative work |
| **Analysis of parapraxes** | Examining "Freudian slips," forgetting, errors | Reveal unconscious wishes leaking through |
| **Working through** | Repeatedly re-examining a conflict across many sessions | Consolidates insight into lasting change |

### Dream Analysis: Manifest vs. Latent Content
- **Manifest content**: the dream as the dreamer **remembers and reports** it (the surface story).
- **Latent content**: the hidden, **unconscious meaning** (repressed wishes) behind the dream.
- **Dream work**: the process that disguises latent into manifest content, via **condensation** (combining many ideas into one image), **displacement** (shifting emotional emphasis), **symbolization**, and **secondary revision** (making the dream coherent).

### Resistance and Interpretation
- **Resistance**: anything (in or out of awareness) that **opposes the progress** of therapy: lateness, silence, "forgetting," changing the subject. Resistance is a clue to where defences are guarding important conflict.
- **Interpretation** should follow the rule: interpret **resistance before content**, and work from the **surface downward** (do not dive prematurely into deep unconscious material).

> **Key Point:** **Free association** = chief technique; **dream analysis** = "royal road to the unconscious"; **manifest** content (remembered) vs. **latent** content (hidden meaning). **Working through** is the slow, repetitive consolidation that turns intellectual insight into emotional, lasting change.

---

## 2.12 Stages / Course of Analysis

1. **Opening phase**: history-taking, establishing the **working alliance**, beginning free association, assessing suitability for analysis.
2. **Development of transference**: the patient begins to relate to the analyst as a key early figure; the **transference neurosis** unfolds.
3. **Working through**: the heart of analysis; repeated interpretation of resistance, transference, and conflict until insight becomes durable.
4. **Resolution / termination**: the transference is resolved, conflicts are worked through, the patient internalises the analytic function, and therapy ends.

- Classical analysis is **long and intensive**: typically **3-5 sessions per week** for several years, using the **couch**.

## 2.13 Brief Psychodynamic and Later Offshoots

- **Ego psychology** (Anna Freud, Heinz Hartmann): emphasises the ego's adaptive, "conflict-free" functions and defences.
- **Object relations theory** (Melanie Klein, D.W. Winnicott, W.R.D. Fairbairn, Margaret Mahler): focus on **internalised relationships** (mental representations of self and others) formed in infancy, rather than drives. Winnicott: "good-enough mother," transitional objects.
- **Self psychology** (Heinz Kohut): emphasises the development of a cohesive **self**, **empathy**, and **mirroring**; central to treating narcissistic disorders.
- **Interpersonal psychoanalysis** (Harry Stack Sullivan, Karen Horney): personality shaped by interpersonal relationships and culture.
- **Brief / time-limited psychodynamic therapy** (Malan, Sifneos, Davanloo, Mann; later **Luborjsky's** Core Conflictual Relationship Theme, **Strupp**): focused on a **central conflict**, time-limited (often ~12-40 sessions), more active therapist, stronger empirical/outcome base than classical analysis.
- **Relational psychoanalysis**: emphasises the **two-person field** (both analyst and patient co-create the relationship); rejects the strict blank-screen model.

> **Key Point:** Modern psychodynamic therapy is **briefer, more active, more focused**, and has a far better **evidence base** than Freud's classical method. **Object relations** = internalised relationships (Klein, Winnicott); **self psychology** = Kohut + empathy/narcissism.

## 2.14 Application, Evidence, Multicultural Issues, and Critique

### Application and Evidence
- Originally developed for **neurosis / hysteria**; now applied to personality disorders, relational and characterological problems.
- The classical evidence base is **case studies** (rich and detailed, but unrepeatable and hard to verify). **Modern brief psychodynamic therapy** has gathered controlled outcome support.

### Multicultural Considerations
- The neutral, non-directive, insight-oriented, individual-focused model may clash with clients from **collectivist** cultures who expect direction, advice, or family involvement.
- The theory was built on **Western, early-20th-century Viennese** assumptions; concepts like the Oedipus complex may not generalise across cultures.
- **Gender critique**: concepts such as **penis envy** and the claim of a weaker female superego are widely rejected as **androcentric/sexist** (challenged by Karen Horney, who proposed "womb envy" in reply).

### Criticisms
1. **Unfalsifiable / weakly testable**: many concepts cannot be empirically refuted (a key Popperian critique).
2. **Long, expensive, intensive**: years of multiple weekly sessions; inaccessible to most.
3. **Overemphasis on sexuality** and on the **deterministic past**; little attention to present/future or to free will.
4. Built on a **biased, non-representative sample** (Freud's patients).
5. **Little empirical support for the psychosexual stages** specifically.
6. **Deterministic and pessimistic** view of human nature.

> **Key Point:** The headline criticisms are **unfalsifiability**, **over-reliance on sexuality and the past**, **cost/length**, and a **biased sample**. Strengths: it discovered the **unconscious, defences, transference, and the developmental importance of early childhood**, all of which later therapies absorbed.

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **Topographic vs. structural model**: conscious/preconscious/unconscious is the **topographic** (earlier) model; id/ego/superego is the **structural** (1923) model. The structural model **added to**, did not replace, the topographic one.
2. **Id = pleasure principle + primary process**; **ego = reality principle + secondary process**; **superego = morality/ideals**. Match each agency to its principle exactly.
3. **Eros vs. Thanatos**: Eros (life) carries **libido**; Thanatos (death) carries **aggression**. "Will to pleasure" = Freud; "will to power" = Adler; "will to meaning" = Frankl.
4. **Order of psychosexual stages**: **O-A-P-L-G** (Oral, Anal, Phallic, Latency, Genital). Latency is **not** an erogenous-zone stage; it is a dormant/consolidation period.
5. **Oedipus complex** (boys, with **castration anxiety**) vs. **Electra complex / penis envy** (girls). Note: **"Electra complex" is Jung's term**, not Freud's; Freud said "female Oedipus complex." Superego forms from **resolving** the Oedipus complex via **identification**.
6. **Fixation** is caused by **either over-gratification OR under-gratification** at a stage, not just one.
7. **Three anxieties**: **reality** (external world), **neurotic** (id impulses), **moral** (superego/guilt). The source defines the type.
8. **Repression** is the **master/primary defence**; **sublimation** is the **only "mature/healthy"** defence. Do not confuse them.
9. **Reaction formation** = expressing the **opposite** impulse; **projection** = attributing **your own** impulse to **others**. Frequently swapped in MCQs.
10. **Displacement** (redirect to a safer target) vs. **sublimation** (redirect to a **socially valued** outlet) vs. **regression** (revert to an **earlier developmental stage**).
11. **Transference = client onto analyst**; **countertransference = analyst onto client**. The reverse is a classic trap.
12. **Dream analysis** = "**royal road to the unconscious**"; **manifest content** = the remembered dream; **latent content** = the hidden meaning. **Dream work** = condensation + displacement + symbolization + secondary revision.
13. **Free association** is the **basic rule / chief technique**; Freud **abandoned hypnosis** in favour of it. Anna O. (Breuer's patient) gave us the **"talking cure."**
14. **Working through** (not a single "aha") is what converts insight into **lasting structural change**; it is slow and repetitive.
15. **Anna Freud** systematised the **defence mechanisms** and founded **child analysis**; **Melanie Klein / Winnicott** = **object relations**; **Kohut** = **self psychology**. Don't attribute defences to Sigmund alone.
