# Ch 11: Reality Therapy / Choice Theory (Glasser)

**Overview:** Reality therapy is the clinical practice; **choice theory** is the explanatory psychology underneath it. Both were created by **William Glasser** (1925-2013), an American psychiatrist who rejected the prevailing psychoanalytic, medical-model account of distress. His core claim is radical and exam-friendly: we **choose** almost all of our behaviour, including the behaviour we ordinarily call "symptoms," and we choose it in a (usually ineffective) attempt to meet **five innate basic needs**. Therapy is therefore present-focused, action-oriented, and built on a warm but firm relationship; it refuses to dwell on the past, the unconscious, or diagnostic labels. The signature delivery system is the **WDEP** model (formalised by **Robert Wubbolding**): explore **W**ants, examine what the client is **D**oing, prompt self-**E**valuation, and build a committed **P**lan. This chapter walks the Sharf frame: originator → personality/human nature → goals → relationship → techniques → applications → critique, with the exam traps that MCQs are built on.

---

## 11.1 William Glasser and the Break with the Medical Model

- **William Glasser** (1925-2013), American psychiatrist, trained in classical psychoanalysis but grew disillusioned with it during the late 1950s.
- His formative clinical work was at the **Ventura School for Girls** (a California correctional facility) and at a Veterans Administration hospital. He found that focusing on the **past**, on **insight**, and on **diagnosis** did not help; focusing on **present behaviour** and **responsibility** did.
- Published **Reality Therapy** (1965), then **Schools Without Failure** (1969), and later reorganised the whole system around **control theory** (1980s), which he renamed **choice theory** in 1996 (*Choice Theory: A New Psychology of Personal Freedom*).
- Founded the **Institute for Reality Therapy** (later the **William Glasser Institute**). **Robert Wubbolding** became the principal systematiser and director of training, and is the source of the **WDEP** acronym and the plan criteria.

> **Key Point:** Reality therapy (the method, 1965) came **first**; choice theory (the underlying psychology) came **later** and was originally called **control theory**. Many MCQs test exactly this: "control theory" is the *old name* for choice theory, both Glasser.

### Rejection of the medical model
- Glasser rejected the idea that psychological problems are **diseases** to be **diagnosed** and **cured**. He argued the medical model is **disempowering**: it tells the client they are a passive victim of an illness rather than an active chooser.
- He was sharply critical of **DSM diagnosis** and of psychiatric medication for problems of living. To Glasser, most so-called mental illness (other than conditions with a clear organic/brain basis) is better understood as the **best choice the person could make at the time** to deal with a frustrating situation, usually an **unsatisfying relationship**.
- The single most common source of human misery, in choice theory, is a **failed or absent close relationship** (with a spouse, parent, child, employer). People disconnect, and the disconnection produces the symptom.

> **Key Point:** For Glasser, the root of almost all long-term psychological problems is a **present, unsatisfying relationship**, not a buried past trauma. The cure is reconnection plus more effective choices.

## 11.2 Choice Theory (formerly Control Theory)

- **Choice theory** holds that human beings are **internally motivated**. We are not pushed around by external stimuli (the behaviourist view) or by unconscious drives (the psychoanalytic view); we behave to satisfy **internal needs**.
- The slogan: **all behaviour is purposeful** and **all behaviour is chosen** (with the partial exception of feelings and physiology, which we control only indirectly; see Total Behaviour below).
- It is called **control theory** in its original form because the person is always trying to **control** their own behaviour so as to get what they want, i.e., to reduce the gap between the world they perceive and the world they want (their **quality world**).
- A crucial corollary: **we can only control our own behaviour, never anyone else's.** Trying to control other people (through criticising, blaming, complaining, nagging, threatening, punishing, bribing) Glasser called the **seven deadly habits**; they destroy relationships. He contrasted them with **seven connecting/caring habits** (supporting, encouraging, listening, accepting, trusting, respecting, negotiating differences).

> **Key Point:** Choice theory = **internal control psychology**. External events only give us **information**; they do not *make* us do or feel anything. This is the philosophical opposite of stimulus-response behaviourism.

---

## 11.3 The Five Basic Needs

All behaviour is an attempt to satisfy one or more of **five genetically encoded basic needs**. The first is physiological; the other four are psychological. They are **universal** but their **strength varies** from person to person, and they frequently **conflict** (e.g., freedom vs love/belonging).

| # | Need | Nature | What it drives | Everyday expression |
|---|------|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Survival** | Physiological | Self-preservation, health, security | Eating, sleeping, safety, saving money, shelter |
| 2 | **Love & Belonging** | Psychological | Connection, intimacy, being part of a group | Friendship, family, marriage, teamwork |
| 3 | **Power / Achievement** | Psychological | Competence, recognition, mastery, significance | Career success, being heard, winning, status |
| 4 | **Freedom / Independence** | Psychological | Autonomy, choice, room to move | Making one's own decisions, mobility, space |
| 5 | **Fun / Enjoyment** | Psychological | Pleasure, play, learning | Humour, hobbies, games, discovery |

- **Love & belonging is the central/most important** psychological need in Glasser's clinical thinking, because relationship failure is the most common cause of distress.
- Needs are **general**; the **specific pictures** of how we satisfy them are stored individually in the **quality world** (next section).
- Glasser added that **learning** is associated with the **fun** need: we are biologically rewarded (it "feels good") for learning, which is why fun is so prominent in his education work.

> **Key Point:** Do **not** confuse Glasser's five needs with **Maslow's hierarchy**. Glasser's needs are **not** hierarchically ranked and need **not** be satisfied in order; only **survival** is physiological, the rest are psychological and co-equal. This is a classic distractor.

## 11.4 The Quality World

- The **quality world** (Glasser's term, sometimes "all-we-want world") is a small, private mental **picture album** of the specific people, things, beliefs, and experiences that, from past experience, we have learned satisfy one or more of our basic needs.
- It is built from **birth onward** and continually edited. Each picture is something the person **wants** and feels good about.
- Behaviour is the constant attempt to make the **perceived world** match the **pictures in the quality world**. The gap between "what I want" (quality world picture) and "what I am getting" (perception) generates a **signal/urge** to act.
- The people most important to us are the pictures we most want to match. Therapy often works because the **therapist** can become a (temporary) picture in the client's quality world, which is why the **relationship/involvement** is built first.

> **Key Point:** The **quality world** holds the *specific* wants; the **five needs** are the *general* drives behind them. WDEP's "**W**" step is literally an exploration of the client's quality world.

---

## 11.5 Total Behaviour: the "Car" Metaphor

Glasser argued that what we loosely call "behaviour" is always **total behaviour**, made of **four inseparable components**:

| Component | Glasser's car part | Degree of control |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Acting** | Front wheel (steering) | **Direct** control |
| **Thinking** | Front wheel (steering) | **Direct** control |
| **Feeling** | Back wheel | **Indirect** control only |
| **Physiology** | Back wheel | **Indirect** control only |

- The metaphor: behaviour is a **car**. The **engine** is the basic needs; the **steering wheel** is what we want (quality world); the **front wheels** are **acting and thinking**; the **back wheels** are **feeling and physiology**.
- A car is **front-wheel drive**: the **front wheels (acting and thinking) steer the whole car.** The back wheels (feeling, physiology) follow wherever the front wheels point. They cannot be turned directly.
- Therefore: we **cannot directly change a feeling**, but we can **change what we are doing and thinking**, and the feelings and physiology will follow.

> **Key Point:** We have **direct control over acting and thinking** and only **indirect control over feeling and physiology**. The whole leverage of reality therapy is here: change the doing (the part you control), and the feeling changes too. This four-part model and the "two wheels you can steer" point are heavily tested.

---

## 11.6 Behaviour as Chosen and Purposeful: the "Behaving" Verbs

- Because all four components move together as one total behaviour, and because we steer with acting/thinking, Glasser insisted even our **misery is chosen**.
- He used a deliberately odd grammatical device: he turned **feeling-states into verbs**. Instead of "I am depressed" (a passive condition that happens to you), he wrote **"I am depressing"**, treating it as an **active verb**, something the person is **doing/choosing**.
- Other examples: **"angering," "anxietying," "headaching," "phobicking."** The point is rhetorical and therapeutic: language that implies you are the **author** of the behaviour restores a sense of choice and control.
- Why would anyone *choose* to depress? Glasser's answers: (1) it **keeps anger in check** (depressing is safer than raging); (2) it is a **cry for help** that gets others to care (meets belonging without asking directly); (3) it **excuses us** from doing something frightening or effortful. In each case the "depressing" is the best total behaviour the person can currently generate.

> **Key Point:** "Depressing" not "depression," "angering" not "anger." Verb form = the behaviour is an **active choice**, not a thing that befalls you. If an MCQ describes a therapist who reframes "I'm depressed" as "you are choosing to depress," that is **Glasser / reality therapy**.

---

## 11.7 Rejection of Mental-Illness Labels

- Following from the above, Glasser **refuses traditional diagnosis** for problems of living. He held that labels like "depression," "anxiety disorder," and most DSM categories describe **chosen total behaviours**, not diseases.
- Consequences for practice:
  - The therapist does **not** spend time history-taking for a diagnosis.
  - The therapist does **not** treat the client as a passive sufferer.
  - The therapist looks for the **unsatisfying relationship** behind the symptom and for **more effective choices**.
- Glasser acknowledged a small set of conditions with genuine organic/brain pathology, but argued that the vast majority of clinical presentations are **not brain diseases** and should not be medicalised.

> **Key Point:** Reality therapy is one of the few mainstream approaches that **explicitly rejects the DSM/medical model** and **diagnostic labelling**. This is a frequent "which approach rejects the disease model of mental illness?" answer.

## 11.8 Goals of Therapy

The goals follow directly from choice theory:

1. **Responsible need-satisfaction.** Help the client find **effective, responsible** ways to meet the five basic needs (especially love/belonging) **without interfering with others' ability to meet theirs.** Glasser's classic definition: **responsibility** = meeting your needs in a way that does **not deprive others** of meeting theirs.
2. **Regaining effective control** of one's own life by making better choices (the original "control theory" goal).
3. **Reconnecting / repairing relationships**, because the missing or failed relationship is usually the source of distress.
4. **Internal locus of control**: move the client from blaming the past, other people, or circumstances toward **owning their choices**.
5. **Teaching choice theory** itself, so the client can keep using it after therapy ends (psychoeducational element).

> **Key Point:** Glasser's **3 R's** of reality therapy: **Responsibility, Reality, and Right-and-wrong.** Clients are helped to face **reality**, accept **responsibility** for their choices, and evaluate behaviour against standards of **right and wrong** (i.e., does it work and is it harmful to others). This older "3 R" formulation is still examinable.

## 11.9 The Therapeutic Relationship: Involvement First

- Reality therapy is **warm, personal, and involved.** Glasser insisted the therapist must first build **involvement**: a genuine, caring, friendly connection so that the client experiences the therapist as someone who is **on their side**.
- The relationship is **necessary** but not the cure in itself (contrast Rogers, where the relationship *is* the cure). Once involvement is established, the focus shifts firmly to **present behaviour and choices.**
- The therapist is **firm but caring**: refuses to accept **excuses**, refuses to **punish** or criticise, and refuses to **give up** on the client. Optimistic and energetic in tone.
- Wubbolding stresses establishing a good environment through specific relational practices, sometimes summarised as the **"tonics"** (be courteous, determined, enthusiastic, genuine; use **AB** = **A**lways **B**e consistent, courteous, calm and confident, believing, etc.) versus the **"toxins"** (arguing, bossing, blaming, criticising, demeaning, excuse-accepting).

> **Key Point:** Sequence matters: **build involvement first, then work on present behaviour.** The therapist is a friendly but firm guide who **never accepts excuses, never punishes, and never gives up.**

## 11.10 The WDEP System (Wubbolding)

The **WDEP** system is **Robert Wubbolding's** formalisation of the procedures of reality therapy. It is the single most testable item in the chapter. Each letter is a cluster of strategies, not a rigid step, and the cycle is delivered inside the **involved, supportive relationship**.

| Letter | Stands for | The therapist's task | Signature question |
|--------|-----------|----------------------|--------------------|
| **W** | **Wants** | Explore the client's wants, needs, and **quality world** pictures; clarify the level of commitment to change. | "**What do you want?**" / "What would your life look like if it were the way you want?" |
| **D** | **Doing** (and **Direction**) | Examine **current total behaviour**, what the client is actually *doing*, and the overall **direction** their life is heading. Focus on actions, the part they control. | "**What are you doing?**" / "Where is this taking you?" |
| **E** | **Evaluation** | The client makes a **self-evaluation**: is the present behaviour actually **getting them what they want**? This is the **pivot/heart** of the method. | "**Is what you're doing getting you what you want?**" |
| **P** | **Planning** | Build a **specific, achievable plan** for more effective behaviour, and secure **commitment** to it. | "**What is your plan?**" / "When will you start?" |

- **W (Wants).** Helps the client articulate quality-world pictures and rank what they most want. Also assesses **commitment level** (from "I don't want to be here" up to "I'll do whatever it takes").
- **D (Doing / Direction).** Glasser deliberately asks about **doing**, not feeling, because doing is the controllable front wheel. "What are you *doing*?" not "How do you *feel*?"
- **E (Evaluation).** The **client**, not the therapist, judges whether the behaviour works. The therapist does **not** evaluate *for* the client; self-evaluation creates ownership and motivation. Without this step there is no leverage for change.
- **P (Planning).** Collaboratively design a plan that meets the plan criteria (next section), then get a firm **commitment**.

> **Key Point:** **WDEP = Wants, Doing/Direction, Evaluation, Planning**, and it is **Wubbolding's** formalisation of **Glasser's** reality therapy. The **E (self-evaluation by the client)** is the turning point of the whole method. Lock the order **W → D → E → P.**

## 11.11 Plan Criteria: SAMI2C3 (and SAMIC)

Good plans built in the **P** step meet a checklist of criteria. The simple version is **SAMIC**; Wubbolding's full version is **SAMI2C3** (also written **SAMIC³**).

| Letter | Criterion | Meaning |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **S** | **Simple** | Easy to understand; not elaborate. |
| **A** | **Attainable** | Realistic; the client can actually do it. |
| **M** | **Measurable** | Precise; you can tell whether it was done. |
| **I** | **Immediate** | Can be started right away (as soon as possible). |
| **I** | **Involved** (second I) | The therapist/counsellor is involved/invested in the plan (shows it matters). |
| **C** | **Controlled** | Carried out by the client **alone**, not dependent on others' behaviour. |
| **C** | **Committed to** (second C) | The client makes a firm commitment to do it. |
| **C** | **Continuous / Consistent** (third C) | Done repeatedly, not a one-off; the client keeps doing it. |

- The shorthand **SAMIC** = **S**imple, **A**ttainable, **M**easurable, **I**mmediate, **C**ontrolled (by the client). The expanded **SAMI2C3** adds the second **I** (Involved) and the second and third **C**'s (Committed, Continuous/Consistent).
- The most-tested feature: a plan must be **controlled by the client**; it must depend only on the client's **own** behaviour, never on someone else changing.

> **Key Point:** **SAMI2C3** = Simple, Attainable, Measurable, Immediate, Involved, Controlled, Committed, Continuous. A reality-therapy plan is **positive, specific, repeatable, and within the client's own control.**

## 11.12 Commitment, No Excuses, No Punishment

These three principles define the firm side of the relationship and follow logically from "behaviour is chosen."

- **Commitment.** A plan is worthless without a firm **commitment** to carry it out. The therapist secures an explicit "yes, I will do this." Commitment puts the client's word, and the relationship, behind the plan.
- **No excuses.** If the client does **not** carry out the plan, the therapist does **not** ask **why** (why-questions invite excuses and blame the past/others). Excuses let the client off the hook and undermine responsibility. Instead the therapist returns to **E** and **P**: "Okay, you didn't do it; do you still want what you said you wanted? What's the plan now?" The plan may simply need to be **revised** to be more workable.
- **No punishment.** The therapist never **punishes** or **criticises** failure to follow a plan. Punishment damages involvement and gives the client an external thing to react against, which **excuses** them from looking at their own choice. Natural consequences are allowed; imposed punishment is not.
- **Never give up.** Glasser's persistent optimism: the therapist keeps believing in the client's capacity to make better choices.

> **Key Point:** The triad **no excuses · no punishment · accept no excuses but never give up** is distinctive to reality therapy. When the plan fails, **do not ask "why," do not punish; revise the plan and recommit.** Asking "why" is explicitly discouraged because it elicits excuses.

## 11.13 Reality Therapy in Education and Lead Management

- Glasser applied choice theory to **schools** (*Schools Without Failure*, 1969; *The Quality School*, 1990). He argued schools fail children by **coercion** and by labelling them failures.
- The **Quality School** model: minimise coercion, eliminate failing grades for incomplete work (require it be redone to quality instead), make schoolwork relevant to students' **needs** (especially fun/learning, power/competence, belonging, freedom).
- **Lead management vs boss management** (Glasser's organisational application of choice theory):
  - **Boss management** = external control: the boss *drives* workers, sets the task without input, uses coercion, criticism, and fear. Produces compliance, not quality.
  - **Lead management** = internal control: the leader *engages* workers, involves them in defining quality work, models the task, creates a non-coercive environment, and facilitates self-evaluation. Produces commitment and higher quality.

> **Key Point:** **Lead management (good) vs boss management (coercive/bad)** is Glasser's workplace/school application of choice theory. The Quality School replaces failure and coercion with relevance, quality work, and self-evaluation.

## 11.14 Multicultural Considerations and Critique

### Multicultural / diversity
- **Strengths:** the focus on **present behaviour, responsibility, and choice** is concrete and time-limited, which suits clients who want **practical help** rather than open-ended insight. The emphasis on the client's **own wants and self-evaluation** is respectful of the client's values rather than the therapist imposing goals.
- **Cautions:** the heavy emphasis on **individual choice and personal responsibility** can clash with **collectivist** cultures and with clients facing real **systemic/environmental oppression** (poverty, discrimination). Telling a client they "choose" their misery can feel **blaming** and **culturally insensitive** when external constraints are genuine and severe. The de-emphasis of **emotion** may not fit clients who need their feelings validated first.

### Critique (Sharf's recurring "research/criticisms" frame)
- **Underplays the past, the unconscious, biology, and emotion.** Critics argue some problems *do* have organic/biological causes and that reality therapy's dismissal of biology and medication is overstated.
- **"You choose your misery" can be blaming** and dismissive of genuine mental illness and trauma.
- **Limited controlled research / weak empirical base** compared with CBT or behaviour therapy; much support is anecdotal or from education settings.
- **Oversimplified** view of human motivation (everything reduced to five needs and choice).
- **Strengths acknowledged:** clear, practical, easy to learn, time-efficient, widely used in **schools, corrections, addictions, and behaviour management**, and genuinely empowering for clients who can act on their choices.

> **Key Point:** The standard criticism cluster: **ignores the past/unconscious/biology/emotion**, the **"choice" framing can blame the client**, and the **research base is thin.** Its standard strengths: **practical, present-focused, empowering, easy to learn, strong in education/corrections/addictions.**

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **WDEP = Wants, Doing/Direction, Evaluation, Planning.** This is the single most-tested item. Lock the order **W → D → E → P** and the four words exactly.
2. **WDEP is Wubbolding's** formalisation; **reality therapy and choice theory are Glasser's.** A question may credit WDEP to Wubbolding specifically.
3. **Reality therapy (1965) came first; choice theory came later** and was **originally called control theory** (renamed in 1996). All Glasser.
4. **Total behaviour has four components: acting, thinking, feeling, physiology.** We have **direct control over acting and thinking only**; feeling and physiology are controlled **indirectly** (the front-wheel-drive car metaphor).
5. **The five basic needs: survival, love/belonging, power/achievement, freedom, fun.** Survival is the only **physiological** one; the rest are psychological and **not hierarchical.**
6. **Do NOT confuse Glasser's five needs with Maslow's hierarchy.** Glasser's needs are co-equal, not ranked, and need not be met in order.
7. **No punishment, no excuses, never give up.** When a plan fails, the therapist **does not ask "why"** (it invites excuses), does not punish, and instead **revises the plan and recommits.**
8. **"Depressing" not "depression":** the verb form signals the behaviour is an **active choice.** A therapist who reframes a feeling as a chosen verb = Glasser.
9. **Reality therapy is present-focused and rejects the past, the unconscious, and the medical/disease model of mental illness.** It rejects DSM-style diagnosis for problems of living.
10. **The relationship: build involvement first, then focus on present behaviour.** Warm but firm. Unlike Rogers, the relationship is **necessary but not sufficient/curative by itself.**
11. **Plan criteria = SAMI2C3** (Simple, Attainable, Measurable, Immediate, Involved, Controlled, Committed, Continuous); simple form **SAMIC**. A plan must be **controlled by the client alone.**
12. **The "E" (self-evaluation) is done by the CLIENT, not the therapist**, and is the pivot of the whole method: "Is what you're doing getting you what you want?"
13. **Responsibility** = meeting your needs **without depriving others** of meeting theirs (Glasser's definition). Linked to the older **3 R's: Responsibility, Reality, Right-and-wrong.**
14. **Lead management (good) vs boss management (coercive)** is Glasser's school/workplace application; the **Quality School** replaces failure and coercion.
15. **The quality world** = the private album of specific need-satisfying pictures; behaviour tries to match perception to these pictures. The **W** step explores it.
16. **Quality world vs the five needs:** the needs are the *general* drives; the quality world holds the *specific* wants. Do not swap them.
