# Ch 12: Solution-Focused Brief & Narrative Therapy

**Overview:** This chapter covers the two leading **postmodern / social-constructionist** therapies: **Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT)**, founded by **Steve de Shazer** and **Insoo Kim Berg**, and **Narrative therapy**, founded by **Michael White** and **David Epston**. Both reject the modernist assumption that a problem reflects a fixed, objective inner pathology waiting to be diagnosed and "fixed" by an expert. Instead they hold that **reality is constructed through language and conversation**, that the client (not the therapist) is the expert on their own life, and that the work of therapy is to change **meaning**. SFBT does this by ignoring the problem and **building a solution** out of the client's own exceptions and preferred future; Narrative does it by separating the person from the problem and helping them **re-author** a richer, preferred story. Both are brief, collaborative, resolutely **non-pathologising**, and a favourite NIMHANS/MET source of "match the technique to the founder" items.

---

## PART ZERO: THE POSTMODERN / SOCIAL-CONSTRUCTIONIST FOUNDATION

### From modernism to postmodernism

- **Modernism** (the worldview behind psychoanalysis, behaviourism, and most "first-generation" therapies) assumes there is a single **objective reality** and a **knowable truth**; the expert (therapist) discovers what is "really" wrong (the diagnosis, the unconscious conflict, the faulty schema) and corrects it.
- **Postmodernism** denies that any single account is privileged. There is no "view from nowhere"; every description of reality is shaped by language, culture, and the position of the observer.
- **Constructivism**: each individual actively **constructs** their own meaning/reality inside their own mind (the knower builds the known).
- **Social constructionism**: meaning is built **between** people, in **language and social interaction**, embedded in culture and history. This is the stronger emphasis in SFBT and especially Narrative therapy: problems and identities are **co-constructed in conversation**, so they can be **re-constructed in conversation**.

> **Key Point:** The single defining premise to memorise: **reality is constructed through language; there is no one true, expert-owned reality.** This is why both therapies make the **client the expert** and the therapist a curious, non-imposing collaborator. This stance is the opposite of the psychoanalyst-as-blank-screen-interpreter and the CBT-therapist-as-teacher.

### Shared family features (SFBT + Narrative)

- **Non-pathologising**: no DSM-style labelling of the person; the person is not their diagnosis.
- **Client as expert** on their own experience, values, and goals.
- **Brief and collaborative**; emphasis on **language** (the exact words used in the room matter).
- **Anti-deficit / competence-focused**: looks for strengths, resources, exceptions, and resistance to the problem rather than for what is broken.
- **Antecedents**: both grew out of the **Mental Research Institute (MRI, Palo Alto)** brief-therapy tradition and the indirect, utilisation-based work of **Milton Erickson**. SFBT also draws on **systems / family-therapy** thinking; Narrative draws on **Michel Foucault** (power/knowledge, discourse) and **Jerome Bruner** (narrative mode of thought).

> **Key Point:** SFBT and Narrative are cousins, not twins. SFBT builds the **solution / preferred future** and largely ignores the problem's content; Narrative engages the **problem story** in order to separate it from the person and **re-write** it. "Build the solution" = SFBT; "re-author the story" = Narrative.

## PART A: SOLUTION-FOCUSED BRIEF THERAPY (SFBT)

### A.1 Origins

- **Founders: Steve de Shazer and Insoo Kim Berg** (a husband-and-wife team) at the **Brief Family Therapy Center (BFTC), Milwaukee**, from the late 1970s onward.
- **Roots**: the **MRI brief-therapy** model (Palo Alto: Watzlawick, Weakland, Fisch) and **Milton Erickson's** strategic, future-oriented, utilisation approach. Other associated names: **Eve Lipchik, Bill O'Hanlon, Michele Weiner-Davis, Yvonne Dolan**.
- De Shazer's famous metaphor: the **"skeleton key"**: you do not need to understand the lock (the problem) to open the door; a key (solution) that already works can be used regardless of the problem's nature.

### A.2 Core philosophy: solutions, exceptions, the future

SFBT is built on a deliberate **shift of attention away from the problem and toward the solution.** Three pillars:

1. **Focus on solutions, not problems.** The detailed history and "cause" of the problem are treated as largely irrelevant; you do not need to analyse a problem to solve it.
2. **Focus on exceptions.** Problems are never present 100% of the time. The **exceptions** (times the problem is absent or less severe) already contain the seeds of the solution; the therapist hunts for them and amplifies them.
3. **Focus on the future.** The orientation is toward a concrete, **preferred future** the client wants, not toward the past.

De Shazer's three operating rules of thumb:

- **"If it ain't broke, don't fix it."**
- **"If it works, do more of it."**
- **"If it's not working, do something different."**

> **Key Point:** SFBT assumes **small change leads to bigger change** (a "ripple" / systems effect): one tiny step in the right direction reorganises the larger pattern, so the therapist deliberately negotiates **small, concrete, doable** goals.

### A.3 Goals

- Co-construct with the client a **clear, specific, concrete, behavioural, and realistic preferred future** ("What will you be doing instead?").
- Goals should be stated as the **presence of something** ("I'll be talking calmly with my son") rather than the **absence of the problem** ("I won't shout").
- Identify and **amplify existing exceptions and strengths** so the client does more of what already works.
- Goals are **small, achievable, and within the client's control**, framed in the client's own language.

### A.4 Signature questions and techniques

| Technique | What the therapist does / asks | Purpose |
|-----------|--------------------------------|---------|
| **Miracle question** | "Suppose tonight while you sleep a **miracle** happens and the problem that brought you here is solved, but you don't know it because you were asleep. When you wake tomorrow, **what would be the first small sign** that tells you the miracle has happened? What would you notice? What would others notice?" | Bypasses "how" and lets the client vividly **construct the preferred future** in concrete detail; the answer becomes the goal map. **(de Shazer's single most famous technique.)** |
| **Exception questions** | "Tell me about a time, even recently, when the problem **did not happen** or was **less of a problem**. What was different then? What were you doing?" | Surfaces the client's own already-existing solutions to do **more of**. |
| **Scaling questions** | "On a scale of **0 to 10**, where 10 is the day after the miracle and 0 is the worst it's been, where are you today? What tells you you're at a 4 and not a 2? **What would one point higher (a 5)** look like?" | Makes progress measurable, breaks change into **tiny next steps**, tracks gains, assesses motivation/confidence. |
| **Coping questions** | "This sounds incredibly hard. **How have you managed to cope** and keep going? How do you get through the day?" | Used with overwhelmed/hopeless clients; uncovers hidden **resilience and resources** when no exceptions seem available. |
| **Formula First-Session Task (FFST)** | At the end of session one: "Between now and next time, **observe what happens in your life that you want to keep happening**, so you can tell me about it." | A standard homework that primes the client to **notice exceptions and positives**. |
| **Presession change question** | "What's gone better between making the appointment and arriving today?" | Highlights that change is **already underway** before therapy "officially" starts. |
| **"What else?" / amplifying** | Keeps gently asking "What else?" after each positive | Thickens the description of the preferred future and exceptions. |
| **Compliments** | Genuine, evidence-based affirmations of the client's efforts and strengths | Build cooperation, reinforce competence, set up the task. |

> **Key Point:** The **miracle question** is the most-tested SFBT item in entrance exams. If a stem describes asking the client to imagine waking up to find the problem gone, the answer is **de Shazer / SFBT**, NOT Gestalt's empty chair, NOT Adler's "the question."

### A.5 The break, the message, and compliments

A classic SFBT session has a distinctive structure: after the conversation, the therapist takes a **break / consulting break** (sometimes consulting a team behind a one-way mirror), then returns to deliver an **intervention message** with three parts:

1. **Compliments**: affirm the client's strengths and what is already working.
2. **A bridge**: a rationale linking the compliments to the task.
3. **A task / suggestion**: usually small, often an observation or "do more of what works" task.

### A.6 Types of therapeutic relationship (the de Shazer / Berg typology)

SFBT classifies the **client-therapist relationship** (not the client's "personality") by how the client relates to the goal. This determines the kind of task you can give.

| Relationship type | Description | Appropriate response |
|-------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Visitor** (visitor-type) | The client does not see a problem or is "sent" by someone else (court, spouse, school); no complaint and no goal of their own. | **No task** about change; offer compliments, build rapport, accept their view. Pushing tasks here backfires. |
| **Complainant** | The client sees and describes a problem but does not yet see themselves as part of the solution; expects someone/something else to change. | **Observation tasks** (notice exceptions); reflection, not action tasks yet. |
| **Customer** (customer-type) | The client sees a problem **and** is ready to do something about it; wants to work and act. | **Action / behavioural tasks** ("do more of what works"); the relationship that supports active homework. |

> **Key Point:** In SFBT these labels describe a **relationship/stance toward the goal**, not a fixed trait, and a client can move from visitor → complainant → customer within or across sessions. A common trap pairs the wrong **task** with the wrong relationship: never give an **action task to a visitor.**

### A.7 The therapeutic relationship and the role of the therapist

- **Collaborative, respectful, client-as-expert.** The therapist **asks** rather than **prescribes**.
- The therapist adopts a stance of **"leading from one step behind"**, guiding the conversation toward solutions while letting the client define the destination and the meaning.
- Emphasis on the client's **language**: the therapist mirrors the client's own words and presupposes change ("**When** things improve…" rather than "**if**").

### A.8 Applications, brevity, and evidence

- Genuinely **brief** (often a handful of sessions; sometimes single-session).
- Broad applicability: schools, social-service agencies, couples and families, substance abuse, workplaces.
- Growing empirical base (meta-analyses are generally favourable), though early studies were methodologically weak.

### A.9 Critique of SFBT

- May be **too quick / superficial**; can **gloss over** real pathology, severe mental illness, trauma, and **emotion** (criticised for sidelining feelings).
- The standardised questions can feel **formulaic or gimmicky** if applied mechanically.
- Ignoring the problem may not suit **complex, chronic, or risk** situations (suicidality, abuse) where assessment is essential.
- Thin on theory of personality (deliberately so), which some see as a weakness.

## PART B: NARRATIVE THERAPY

### B.1 Origins

- **Founders: Michael White (Australia, Dulwich Centre, Adelaide) and David Epston (New Zealand).** Key text: *Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends* (1990).
- **Influences**: **Michel Foucault** (knowledge is bound up with **power**; "expert" discourses can subjugate people), **Jerome Bruner** (humans think in a **narrative mode**, organising experience as story), and the broader social-constructionist movement.

### B.2 Core philosophy: we live by stories

- People make sense of their lives by organising experience into **stories / narratives**. The story we hold becomes the lens through which new experience is filtered.
- A **dominant story** that is **problem-saturated** can trap a person: it selectively recruits problem-confirming events and screens out competence, so the person comes to believe the problem **is** who they are (e.g., "I am a depressed/failing/angry person").
- These stories are shaped by wider **cultural discourses** (about gender, success, normality, etc.) that can be oppressive.

> **Key Point:** The slogan to memorise: **"The person is not the problem; the problem is the problem."** Distress lives in the **story and its surrounding discourse**, not as an essential trait of the person. Therapy de-couples the two.

### B.3 Goals

- **Separate the person from the problem** so the person can take a position toward it.
- **Deconstruct** the problem-saturated dominant story and the cultural assumptions feeding it.
- **Identify exceptions** to the problem story (unique outcomes) and **re-author** a richer, "thicker," preferred story rooted in the client's **own values, intentions, and competence**.
- Strengthen the new story by giving it an **audience** and a documented record.

### B.4 Signature techniques

| Technique | What it involves |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Externalising the problem** | Through language, talk about the problem as a **separate entity acting on** the person, often by **naming** it ("the Anger," "Sneaky Poo," "the Depression," "the Voices"). "When did **the Anger** first try to take over?" rather than "Why are you so angry?" Separates identity from problem. |
| **Mapping the influence of the problem** | Trace how far the problem has spread into the person's life and relationships (its **effects/influence**) **and** how far the **person's influence over the problem** extends. A two-way map. |
| **Deconstruction** | Question and unpack the taken-for-granted **dominant story** and the cultural/discursive assumptions ("Whose idea of 'success' is this? Who benefits from you believing it?"). Inspired by Foucault. |
| **Unique outcomes / "sparkling moments"** | Find **exceptions** (moments the person resisted, escaped, or stood up to the problem) that the dominant story ignored. These are the foundation of the new story. |
| **Re-authoring (re-storying)** | Build a new, preferred narrative from the unique outcomes, asking **landscape-of-action** questions (what did you do?) and **landscape-of-identity/meaning** questions (what does that say about what you value, what kind of person you are?). |
| **Therapeutic documents and letters** | The therapist writes **letters, certificates, declarations, lists** summarising and celebrating the client's gains, extending therapy beyond the room and **thickening** the new story (Epston was famous for this). |
| **Outsider witnesses / definitional ceremony** | Recruit an **audience** (significant others, a reflecting team, past clients) to witness, retell, and acknowledge the person's preferred story in a **"definitional ceremony,"** which makes the new identity socially real (drawn from Barbara Myerhoff). |
| **Re-membering conversations** | Deliberately re-engage the "club of one's life," revising the membership and influence of important figures who support the preferred identity. |

> **Key Point:** **Externalising** is the signature first move of Narrative therapy. If a stem describes the therapist encouraging the client to **name the problem and treat it as an external opponent** ("How long has 'the Fear' been bossing you around?"), the answer is **White & Epston / Narrative**.

### B.5 The therapeutic relationship: the not-knowing stance

- **Collaborative, curious, and respectful.** The therapist is **NOT the expert** on the client's life.
- The hallmark stance is **"not-knowing"** (a term associated with Anderson & Goolishian and shared across postmodern therapies): the therapist comes with genuine curiosity rather than expert assumptions, letting the client be the authority on meaning.
- White described the therapist as **"de-centred but influential"**: not the centre of attention (the client's story and knowledge are central), yet actively shaping the conversation through skilful questioning.
- The therapist is an **investigative co-author / co-editor** of the new story.

### B.6 Applications and evidence

- Used in family work, child therapy ("Sneaky Poo" for encopresis), grief, trauma and abuse narratives, eating disorders, and **oppression-linked** problems (gender, culture, marginalisation), reflecting its social-justice leaning.
- Evidence is largely **qualitative / case-based**; far fewer controlled trials than SFBT or CBT.

### B.7 Critique of Narrative therapy

- **Hard to evaluate empirically** (qualitative, individualised); weak RCT base.
- Can be **intellectually demanding / abstract** and verbally loaded, and may not fit clients who are concrete, very young, or in crisis.
- May **underweight biological and severe illness** (psychosis, major depression) by treating problems primarily as stories.
- Risk that the therapist's **political/social values** drive the deconstruction.

## SFBT vs NARRATIVE: AT-A-GLANCE COMPARISON

| Dimension | **Solution-Focused Brief Therapy** | **Narrative Therapy** |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Founders** | **Steve de Shazer & Insoo Kim Berg** (Milwaukee, BFTC) | **Michael White & David Epston** (Australia / NZ) |
| **Intellectual roots** | MRI brief therapy; Milton Erickson | Foucault (power/discourse); Bruner (narrative); social constructionism |
| **What it attends to** | The **solution** and the **future**; largely ignores the problem | The **problem story** and its surrounding discourse, to re-write it |
| **Master move** | **Build the solution** from exceptions and the preferred future | **Separate person from problem**, then **re-author** the story |
| **Signature techniques** | Miracle question, exception questions, scaling questions, coping questions, formula first-session task, compliments | Externalising, deconstruction, unique outcomes / sparkling moments, re-authoring, letters/documents, outsider witnesses |
| **Slogan** | "If it works, do more of it"; small change → big change | **"The person is not the problem; the problem is the problem."** |
| **Relationship stance** | Collaborative, client-as-expert; "lead from one step behind" | Collaborative, **not-knowing**, **de-centred but influential** |
| **View of the problem** | An obstacle to bypass; content largely irrelevant | A separable entity / oppressive story to be re-storied |
| **Length** | Very brief (often a few sessions) | Brief-to-moderate; document-extended |

> **Key Point:** Both are postmodern, non-pathologising, and client-as-expert. The cleanest discriminator: **SFBT looks FORWARD to a constructed solution; Narrative looks AT the story to re-write it.** Miracle/scaling = SFBT; externalising/re-authoring = Narrative.

## MET / NIMHANS Exam Traps

1. **Miracle question = Steve de Shazer / SFBT.** The single most-tested item in this chapter. Not Gestalt's empty chair, not Adler's "the question," not the existentialists.
2. **Scaling questions ("0 to 10, where are you now?") = SFBT.** A favourite distractor pairs it with CBT; scaling here is solution-building, not symptom rating.
3. **Exception questions and coping questions = SFBT** (de Shazer & Berg). Exceptions = times the problem is absent; coping questions = used with hopeless/overwhelmed clients.
4. **"The person is not the problem; the problem is the problem" = Narrative (White & Epston).** Pin this slogan to White, not de Shazer.
5. **Externalising the problem (naming it, talking about it as a separate entity) = Narrative.** Often the trap answer is "Gestalt" because it sounds experiential; wrong, it's White & Epston.
6. **Unique outcomes / sparkling moments = Narrative.** Note the parallel: Narrative's "unique outcomes" ≈ SFBT's "exceptions," but the **term** maps to the **founder**: unique outcomes = White; exceptions = de Shazer.
7. **Re-authoring / re-storying, therapeutic letters/documents, outsider witnesses / definitional ceremony = Narrative.** Epston is especially associated with **letters and documents**.
8. **de Shazer & Berg vs White & Epston**: do not cross the founders. Berg & de Shazer = SFBT (Milwaukee); White & Epston = Narrative (Australia/NZ).
9. **Relationship types in SFBT = visitor, complainant, customer.** A trap pairs the wrong **task** with each: no action task for a **visitor**; observation tasks for a **complainant**; action tasks for a **customer**.
10. **Both therapies are POSTMODERN / SOCIAL-CONSTRUCTIONIST and "client-as-expert."** If a stem says "reality is constructed through language" or "the client is the expert," it is pointing at SFBT/Narrative, not psychoanalysis/CBT.
11. **Roots = MRI brief therapy + Milton Erickson** (both); **Foucault + Bruner** specifically behind **Narrative**. "Influenced by Foucault" → Narrative.
12. **Formula First-Session Task (FFST) = SFBT** ("notice what you want to keep happening").
13. **"Not-knowing stance" and "de-centred but influential" = Narrative / postmodern therapist stance** (de-centred-but-influential is White's phrase). Contrast with the analyst-as-expert.
14. **Skeleton key metaphor = de Shazer / SFBT** (you don't need to understand the lock/problem to open the door).
15. **Critique split**: SFBT is criticised for being **too quick and ignoring emotion/pathology**; Narrative is criticised for being **hard to test, abstract, and underweighting biology**.

## Key Terms

- **Postmodernism**: rejection of a single objective truth; all knowledge is perspective- and language-bound.
- **Social constructionism**: meaning is built **between** people through language and culture.
- **Constructivism**: each individual builds their own internal reality.
- **Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT)**: de Shazer & Berg; build solutions from exceptions and the preferred future.
- **Miracle question**: imagining the problem solved overnight, then describing the first signs.
- **Exception question**: identifying times the problem is absent or lessened.
- **Scaling question**: rating progress/confidence on a 0–10 scale and naming the next small step.
- **Coping question**: eliciting how an overwhelmed client has managed to keep going.
- **Formula First-Session Task (FFST)**: homework to notice what one wants to keep happening.
- **Visitor / Complainant / Customer**: SFBT relationship types defined by stance toward the goal.
- **Compliments / break / message**: the affirm-bridge-task structure of an SFBT session.
- **Skeleton key**: de Shazer's metaphor; a working solution opens the door regardless of the lock (problem).
- **Narrative therapy**: White & Epston; re-author the dominant problem story.
- **Dominant / problem-saturated story**: the constraining account a person lives by.
- **Externalising**: linguistically separating the problem from the person, often by naming it.
- **Deconstruction**: unpacking taken-for-granted dominant stories and discourses (Foucault).
- **Unique outcomes / sparkling moments**: exceptions to the problem story; basis for re-authoring.
- **Re-authoring (re-storying)**: building a preferred narrative via landscape-of-action and landscape-of-identity questions.
- **Therapeutic documents / letters**: written records that thicken and extend the new story.
- **Outsider witnesses / definitional ceremony**: an audience that retells and validates the preferred story.
- **Re-membering conversations**: revising the "club of life" to favour identity-supporting figures.
- **Not-knowing stance**: therapist's genuine curiosity, refusing the expert position.
- **De-centred but influential**: White's description of the narrative therapist's role.
