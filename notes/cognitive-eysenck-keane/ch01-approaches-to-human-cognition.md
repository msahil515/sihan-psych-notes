# Ch 1: Approaches to Human Cognition

**Overview:** Cognitive psychology studies internal processes (attention, perception, memory, language, reasoning) by observing behaviour; the broader field also incorporates brain structure and activity. Four major approaches have developed: experimental cognitive psychology, cognitive neuropsychology, cognitive neuroscience, and computational cognitive science. These approaches increasingly converge, and the ideal strategy is **converging operations** across methods.

---

## 1.1 Origins and the Information-Processing Approach

- **1956: the founding year** of cognitive psychology. Three landmark events at MIT:
  - **Noam Chomsky** presented his theory of language.
  - **George Miller** (1956) described "the magic number seven" in short-term memory capacity.
  - **Newell and Simon** introduced the **General Problem Solver** (Newell et al., 1958).
  - **Bruner et al.** (1956) published the first cognitive analysis of concept formation.
- The **information-processing approach** modelled the mind on the computer: a stimulus enters, internal processes transform it, a response is produced.
- **Bottom-up processing**: processing driven directly by the stimulus input (data-driven).
- **Top-down processing**: processing driven by the individual's prior knowledge, expectations, and context (concept-driven). Classic demonstration: the phrase "Paris in the spring" contains a repeated "the" that top-down expectation causes most readers to miss.
- **Serial processing**: one process completes before the next begins (early assumption of the information-processing model).
- **Parallel processing**: two or more processes occur simultaneously. More common in well-practised tasks (e.g., an experienced driver steers, changes gears, and monitors traffic simultaneously, whereas a novice cannot).
- **Task impurity problem**: most cognitive tasks recruit multiple processes, complicating interpretation of findings.
  - Solution: **latent-variable analysis** (Miyake et al., 2000) extracts the common factor shared by several tasks requiring the same underlying process (e.g., inhibition extracted from Stroop, anti-saccade, and stop-signal tasks).
  - **Articulatory suppression** (Baddeley et al., 1975): participants repeated digits 1-8 during word-list presentation; this abolished the **word-length effect**, supporting verbal rehearsal as the mediating process.

## 1.2 Approach 1: Experimental Cognitive Psychology

### Core Features
- Studies healthy participants under controlled laboratory conditions using behavioural measures (reaction time, accuracy).
- Source of most theories and tasks adopted by the other three approaches.
- Has strongly influenced social, clinical, and developmental psychology.

### Strengths
1. First systematic approach to human cognition; enormous accumulated empirical base.
2. Enormously flexible: applicable to any aspect of cognition.
3. Source of nearly all major cognitive theories (Coltheart, 2011 noted, controversially, that cognitive neuroscience has so far added little to cognitive theorising).
4. Provided tasks and analytical frameworks for cognitive neuropsychology and computational modelling.

### Limitations
1. **Ecological validity**: laboratory behaviour may not reflect everyday cognition. Wachtel (1973) called the fixed stimulus sequence the **implacable experimenter** problem.
2. Behavioural measures (speed and accuracy) provide only **indirect evidence** about internal processes; cannot easily distinguish serial vs. parallel processing.
3. Theories often expressed in verbal terms only, allowing "wiggle room" (Murphy, 2011): vague predictions that are hard to falsify.
4. **Paradigm specificity**: findings obtained with one task often fail to generalise even to apparently similar tasks (Meiser, 2011).
5. Lack of an overarching theoretical framework or **cognitive architecture**.

## 1.3 Approach 2: Cognitive Neuropsychology

### Core Features
- Studies brain-damaged patients (who have suffered **lesions**: structural brain damage from injury or disease) to understand normal cognition.
- Goal: understand the **functional architecture of the mind**, not primarily the brain (Coltheart, 2010).
- Key historical case: **KF** (Shallice and Warrington, 1970) had severely impaired short-term memory but intact long-term memory, challenging the then-dominant view that long-term storage requires prior short-term processing.
- **HM (Henry Molaison)**: severely impaired long-term episodic memory but intact short-term memory and motor-skill learning; implicated the hippocampus in long-term memory consolidation.

### Four Theoretical Assumptions (Coltheart, 2001)

| Assumption | Content | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| **Modularity** | The cognitive system consists of numerous fairly independent, domain-specific modules | Human cognition is more interactive and flexible than pure modularity implies |
| **Anatomical modularity** | Each module is located in a specific brain region | Neuroimaging often shows same frontal areas activated across diverse complex tasks (Duncan & Owen, 2000; Yarkoni et al., 2011 across 3,000+ studies) |
| **Uniformity of functional architecture** | The cognitive architecture is essentially the same across individuals | Little convincing evidence; large individual differences complicate inference from single patients |
| **Subtractivity** | Brain damage removes/impairs modules but does not add or reorganise them | Recovery often involves compensatory strategies and neural plasticity (Cus et al., 2011) |

### Dissociations

- **Dissociation**: a patient performs normally on task X but is impaired on task Y. Risk: may simply reflect task-complexity differences rather than different modules.
- **Double dissociation**: Patient A is impaired on X but not Y; Patient B is impaired on Y but not X. Eliminates task-complexity explanation; strong (though not definitive) evidence for separate modules.
  - Classic example: amnesic patients show impaired long-term memory / intact short-term memory; KF showed the reverse pattern.
- **Association**: impairment on both X and Y co-occurs (defines a **syndrome**). Risk: anatomical adjacency can cause associated deficits that share no common mechanism (e.g., Gerstmann's syndrome: finger agnosia, acalculia, agraphia, left-right disorientation).

### Single-Case vs. Case-Series Studies

- Historical emphasis on **single-case studies** (because each patient is unique; rare patterns are hard to replicate in groups).
- Trend toward **case-series studies**: several patients with similar impairments are tested individually and variation across them is assessed (Lambon Ralph et al., 2011). Richer data; allows identification of outliers.

### Strengths
1. Double dissociations have yielded strong evidence for major processing distinctions (e.g., short-term vs. long-term memory; regular vs. irregular word reading routes).
2. Can establish **causal** links between brain damage and cognitive performance.
3. Revealed unexpected complexities in language and reading.
4. Transformed memory theory.

### Limitations
1. Patients may develop **compensatory strategies** that mask the original deficit.
2. Modularity assumption appears too strong: human cognition is flexible and highly interconnected.
3. Minimises the interconnectedness (parallel, distributed) of cognitive processes.
4. Excessive reliance on single-case studies.
5. Historically insufficient engagement with brain-level data from cognitive neuroscience.

## 1.4 Approach 3: Cognitive Neuroscience

### Brain Organisation Basics
- The cortex is divided into four lobes per hemisphere: **frontal** (separated from parietal by the **central sulcus**), **parietal**, **temporal** (separated from parietal/frontal by the **lateral fissure**), **occipital**.
- Directional terms: **dorsal/superior** (top), **ventral/inferior** (bottom), **rostral/anterior** (front), **posterior** (back), **lateral** (side), **medial** (middle).
- **Brodmann areas (BAs)**: map of 52 cortical areas based on cytoarchitecture (cell-type distributions across layers). BA17 = primary visual cortex.
- **Brain network organisation** (Bullmore and Sporns, 2012): the brain achieves a near-optimal trade-off between **cost control** (short, cheap connections) and **efficiency** (integrative long-distance connections). Contains tight local **modules** plus long-distance **hubs** (e.g., anterior cingulate). IQ correlates with global network efficiency (Van den Heuvel et al., 2009: r = +0.75 in medial PFC).

### Neuroscience Techniques: Comparison Table

| Technique | What is measured | Spatial resolution | Temporal resolution | Invasive? | Establishes causation? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Single-unit recording** | APs in individual neurons (micro-electrode 1/10,000 mm) | Extremely fine (single cell) | Excellent (ms) | Yes (usually animals; rarely humans with implanted electrodes) | No |
| **ERP/EEG** | Scalp electrical potentials; averaged across trials | Poor (skull distorts fields) | Excellent (ms) | No | No |
| **PET** | Positron emission from radioactive tracer (labelled water); blood flow/metabolism | Moderate (5-10 mm) | Very poor (30-60 s) | Slightly (injection) | No |
| **fMRI** | BOLD signal (blood oxygen level-dependent contrast); deoxyhaemoglobin concentration | Very good (~1 mm) | Poor-moderate (2-3 s) | No | No |
| **efMRI** | Event-related BOLD signal per stimulus/response type | Very good (~1 mm) | Poor-moderate (2-3 s) | No | No |
| **MEG** | Magnetic fields from neural electrical activity (SQUID device) | Reasonably good | Excellent (ms) | No | No |
| **TMS / rTMS** | Disruption of neural processing via brief magnetic pulse (~1 cubic cm) | Moderate | Excellent (ms, can be timed precisely) | No (but skull-surface only) | Yes ("virtual lesion") |

### Key Technique Details

**Single-unit recording**
- Micro-electrode inserted into brain to detect activity of a single neuron (sensitivity: 1 millionth of a volt).
- Invasive; almost never used with humans. Exception: Quiroga et al. (2005) recorded from medial temporal lobe neurons in epileptic patients implanted for seizure mapping. One neuron responded specifically to pictures of **Jennifer Aniston** but not other famous people (caution: only a tiny fraction of neurons were sampled).

**Event-Related Potentials (ERPs)**
- Same stimulus presented repeatedly; EEG segments time-locked to stimulus onset are averaged to cancel random noise, yielding a clean waveform.
- Waveform components labelled by polarity (P = positive, N = negative) and peak latency in ms: e.g., **N400** (negative peak ~400 ms, associated with semantic incongruity).
- Excellent temporal resolution; poor spatial resolution.
- Best suited to simple stimuli and basic processes (target detection); limited for complex cognition.

> **Key Study:** ERPs used by Thorpe et al. (1996) showed that natural scene categorisation (animal vs. non-animal) produced differential EEG responses within ~150 ms, demonstrating the remarkable speed of visual categorisation.

**PET**
- Radioactively labelled water injected; accumulates in active regions.
- Spatial resolution: 5-10 mm. Temporal resolution: 30-60 s (very poor).
- Largely replaced by fMRI.

**fMRI**
- Active neurons consume oxygen; oxyhaemoglobin converts to deoxyhaemoglobin, altering local magnetic field (BOLD signal).
- Spatial resolution: ~1 mm. Temporal resolution: ~2-3 s.
- Limitations: indirect measure of neural activity; BOLD distorted near sinuses/oral cavity; noisy scanner; claustrophobia (43% of participants at least "a bit upset," Cooke et al., 2007); movement artefacts; poor ecological validity.

> **Key Study:** Wagner et al. (1998) used efMRI during word learning. Words later recognised showed greater brain activity during encoding than words later forgotten, demonstrating a neural correlate of successful memory formation.

**MEG**
- Measures brain magnetic fields using SQUID (superconducting quantum interference device).
- Skull is virtually transparent to magnetic fields, so MEG is less spatially distorted than EEG.
- Temporal resolution: ms level. Spatial resolution: reasonably good.
- Extremely expensive; 35% of participants found it "a bit upsetting" (Cooke et al., 2007).

**TMS / rTMS**
- A coil (often figure-of-eight shaped) placed near the skull; brief (<1 ms) magnetic pulse induces electrical stimulation in ~1 cm³ of underlying cortex, usually inhibiting processing.
- **rTMS**: repeated pulses in rapid succession; most widely used form.
- **Key advantage**: can establish that a brain region is **necessary** for task performance (causal inference), unlike correlational neuroimaging.
- Can also time when a region is critical: Cracco et al. (1999) found letter-detection most impaired when TMS hit occipital cortex 80-100 ms post-stimulus.
- Limitations: complex effects (sometimes enhances performance if it inhibits an area that normally inhibits another area); cannot reach deep or muscle-covered brain areas; occasional seizure risk; precise area affected can be unclear.

### Cognitive Neuroscience: Strengths
1. Great variety of techniques offering excellent temporal or spatial resolution (each technique's strength compensates another's weakness).
2. Can study both **functional specialisation** (what area does what) and **functional integration** (coordinated network activity).
3. TMS allows causal inference.
4. Rich neuroimaging data allow re-analysis under different theoretical frameworks (self-correcting).
5. Has resolved previously intractable theoretical disputes: e.g., Wild et al. (2012) showed visual-auditory congruence modulates **primary auditory cortex**, supporting early integration of top-down knowledge in speech perception. Kosslyn (2005) showed ~two-thirds of areas active in visual perception are also active in visual imagery.

### Cognitive Neuroscience: Limitations
1. **Correlational, not causal** (except TMS): activation co-occurs with a task but does not prove necessity.
2. **Reverse inference**: inferring a cognitive process from a brain activation pattern is problematic because most regions participate in multiple processes (e.g., amygdala activates for fear, disgust, happiness, anger, sadness; Lindquist et al., 2012).
3. **"Blobology"**: single activated voxel clusters labelled as "the love area" etc.; oversimplification.
4. **Underpowered studies**: typically n < 20; many true effects missed; many false positives (~15% false positive rate, Yarkoni et al., 2010). Classic demonstration: Bennett et al. (2009) found "significant" brain activation in a dead salmon without correcting statistical thresholds.
5. Bridging problem: psychological concepts (attention, planning) may not map neatly onto brain activation patterns (Harley, 2012).
6. **Neuroimaging illusion**: brain images give an unwarranted sense of explanatory depth; 3D-looking images rated as more convincing (Keehner et al., 2011).
7. Ecological validity concerns: fMRI participants lie supine in noisy, claustrophobic conditions. Scanner context impairs long-term memory (Gutchess and Park, 2006).
8. Resting brain activity is high: cognitive tasks add less than 5% to resting-state activity (Raichle, 2010); some tasks even show decreased activity in certain regions.

## 1.5 Approach 4: Computational Cognitive Science

### Core Distinction
- **Computational modelling**: programs that simulate human cognitive processes (the goal is psychological fidelity).
- **Artificial intelligence**: programs that produce intelligent outputs regardless of whether they mirror human processes (e.g., Deep Blue considered 200 million chess positions per second, far exceeding any human).

### Why Computational Models?
- Force precise specification of theoretical assumptions, eliminating "wiggle room" of verbal theories (Murphy, 2011).
- Programs must be internally consistent; implementing a theory as code often reveals hidden assumptions.
- Enable quantitative predictions: the pattern of operation-time differences across materials should correlate with participants' reaction time differences.
- Enable "lesioning" the model to simulate brain damage and compare output to patient data (Dell and Caramazza, 2008).

### Connectionist Models
- Networks of simple **neuron-like units (nodes)** with weighted connections; interconnected in layers.
- **Input layer** encodes the stimulus; **hidden units** (intermediate layer); **output layer** produces response.
- Knowledge is represented in a **distributed** fashion across many units (parallel distributed processing, Rumelhart et al., 1986).
- Units excite or inhibit each other; a unit fires if the weighted sum of inputs exceeds its threshold.
- **Backpropagation (BackProp)**: the primary learning rule. The network compares actual output to the correct output, calculates error, and back-propagates adjustments to the weights. The network learns without being explicitly programmed.
  - Caveat: little or no neural evidence of backpropagation in the human brain (Mayor et al., 2014).
- **Localist connectionist models** (alternative): single nodes represent specific concepts. Examples: Coltheart et al.'s (2001) reading model; TRACE model (McClelland and Elman, 1986); Dell (1986) speech production model.
- Evidence cuts both ways: some knowledge appears locally stored (Quiroga et al., 2005, Jennifer Aniston neuron), suggesting not all representation is distributed (Bowers, 2009).

### Production Systems
- Consist of many **"If...then" production rules** and a **working memory** holding currently active information.
- If a working-memory state matches the IF-part of a rule, the THEN-part is executed.
- Conflict resolution: if two rules are triggered, a selection strategy picks one.
- Newell and Simon (1972) first formalised production systems in the **General Problem Solver**.

### ACT-R (Adaptive Control of Thought-Rational)
- Developed by J.R. Anderson et al. (2004); a **cognitive architecture** (domain-general; covers many aspects of cognition).
- Combines computational cognitive science with cognitive neuroscience by linking modules to specific brain regions:

| ACT-R Module | Function | Proposed Brain Location |
|---|---|---|
| **Retrieval module** | Maintains retrieval cues for long-term memory access | Inferior ventrolateral prefrontal cortex |
| **Imaginal module** | Transforms problem representations | Posterior parietal cortex |
| **Goal module** | Tracks intentions; controls information processing | Anterior cingulate cortex |
| **Procedural module** | Selects next action via production rules | Head of caudate nucleus (basal ganglia) |

- A central production system monitors buffers and coordinates across modules.
- Limitation: hard to test comprehensively; de-emphasises dorsolateral PFC; does not capture network-level dynamics.

### Nested Incremental Modelling
- A new model builds on predecessors' strengths, eliminates their weaknesses, and accounts for additional data (e.g., Perry et al.'s (2007) CDP+ reading model improved on earlier dual-process models).

### Strengths of Computational Cognitive Science
1. Precise specification of theoretical assumptions.
2. Cognitive architectures offer potential overarching frameworks.
3. Models can be lesioned to simulate patient data.
4. Computational cognitive neuroscience increasingly models neuroimaging data.
5. Emphasis on parallel processing fits with functional neuroimaging findings.

### Limitations of Computational Cognitive Science
1. **Bonini's paradox**: as a model grows more complete and realistic, it becomes as difficult to understand as the real system it represents.
2. Hard to falsify (especially flexible architectures).
3. **Overfitting**: models that fit one dataset may not generalise to others (Ziegler et al., 2010).
4. Most models de-emphasise **motivational and emotional factors** (ACT-R's goal module is a partial exception; Norman (1980) distinguished Pure Cognitive System vs. Regulatory System).
5. Poor code-sharing practices make models hard to evaluate, replicate, or extend (Addyman and French, 2012).

## 1.6 Comparing the Four Approaches

| Criterion | Experimental Cog. Psych. | Cog. Neuropsychology | Cog. Neuroscience | Computational Cog. Sci. |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary data source** | Behaviour (RT, accuracy) in healthy adults | Cognitive performance in brain-damaged patients | Brain imaging + behaviour | Computer simulation of cognition |
| **Causal inference** | Limited (correlational) | Yes (damage causes deficits) | Mostly no (except TMS) | Via model lesioning |
| **Precision** | Moderate | Moderate | High (rich data) | Highest (explicit formalisation) |
| **Ecological validity** | Moderate concern | High concern | High concern (fMRI) | Not applicable |
| **Key methodological risk** | Paradigm specificity | Compensatory strategies; modularity assumption | Reverse inference; false positives | Overfitting; Bonini's paradox |
| **Major contribution** | Theory engine for the field | Revealed distinct memory/language systems | Temporal and spatial brain localisation | Formal testing and architecture |

- **Converging operations**: using multiple methods to study the same question. When methods agree, confidence is higher. When they disagree, further research is needed.

> **Key Study:** Diana et al. (2007) combined cognitive neuropsychology and fMRI on recognition memory. Double dissociation (neuropsychology): hippocampal damage impairs **recollection** but spares familiarity; perirhinal damage shows the reverse. Neuroimaging: recollection activates hippocampus more than perirhinal cortex; familiarity shows the reverse. Converging evidence strongly supports distinct neural substrates for recollection vs. familiarity.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Year 1956 and why it matters**: Miller (1956) on "magic number 7"; Chomsky on language; Newell and Simon on General Problem Solver; Bruner et al. on concept formation. These collectively mark the birth of cognitive psychology.

2. **Bottom-up vs. top-down processing**: bottom-up = stimulus-driven; top-down = knowledge/expectation-driven. "Paris in the spring" demonstration. Both operate in parallel in normal cognition.

3. **Serial vs. parallel processing**: early information-processing models assumed serial; modern understanding emphasises parallel (especially in practised tasks).

4. **Four assumptions of cognitive neuropsychology** (Coltheart, 2001): modularity, anatomical modularity, uniformity of functional architecture, subtractivity. Know each assumption AND its problem.

5. **Dissociation vs. double dissociation**: single dissociation is ambiguous (could reflect task difficulty differences); double dissociation is stronger evidence for separate modules. Classic example: KF vs. amnesic patients (short-term vs. long-term memory).

6. **Gerstmann's syndrome** as an example of why associations/syndromes can be misleading (anatomical adjacency, not shared mechanism).

7. **Spatial vs. temporal resolution of techniques**: ERP/MEG = excellent temporal, poor-moderate spatial; fMRI = excellent spatial (~1 mm), poor temporal (2-3 s); PET = moderate spatial, very poor temporal (30-60 s); single-unit recording = finest spatial, excellent temporal but invasive.

8. **TMS is the only technique that establishes causation** (virtual lesion); all neuroimaging techniques (PET, fMRI, MEG) provide only correlational evidence.

9. **BOLD signal**: what fMRI measures; reflects deoxyhaemoglobin concentration; indirect measure of neural activity; 2-3 s lag.

10. **Reverse inference problem**: a brain region can be active during many different processes; you cannot reliably infer a specific cognitive process from activation in that region. Yarkoni et al. (2011): DLPFC and anterior cingulate activated in 20% of 3,489 studies.

11. **Dead salmon study** (Bennett et al., 2009): without correcting statistical thresholds, false-positive "brain activation" was found in a dead salmon, illustrating the false-positive problem in neuroimaging.

12. **Connectionist networks**: distributed representation; nodes connected with weighted links; learn via backpropagation; but backpropagation has no clear neural evidence (Mayor et al., 2014).

13. **ACT-R modules and brain locations**: retrieval (inferior ventrolateral PFC), imaginal (posterior parietal), goal (anterior cingulate), procedural (head of caudate nucleus). Combines computational and neuroscience approaches.

14. **Bonini's paradox**: more complete models become harder to understand than the real system itself.

15. **Converging operations**: the recommended strategy when each method has distinct strengths and limitations; agreement across methods provides stronger evidence.

16. **Coltheart (2011) controversy**: claimed cognitive neuroscience has so far made no difference to cognitive theorising (this is a provocation worth knowing for exam discussion questions).
