# Ch 6: Cognition, Emotion & Consciousness

**Overview:** Two-way relationship between cognition and emotion. How cognitive appraisals shape emotional experience; how emotion shapes attention, memory, judgement and decision making; cognitive biases in anxiety and depression and their clinical relevance; and major theories of consciousness including global workspace theory. Source: Eysenck and Keane (7th ed.), Part V, Chapters 15 and 16.

---

## 6.1 Structure of Emotion and Key Definitions

- **Affect**: broad term encompassing both emotions and moods.
  - **Emotions**: intense, brief, caused by a specific event (e.g., passing an exam).
  - **Moods**: less intense, longer-lasting, cause often unclear.
  - **Valence**: dimension running from very negative to very positive affect.

- **Two main structural views:**
  - **Categorical approach** (Izard, 2007): distinct basic emotions (happiness, anger, fear, disgust, sadness).
  - **Dimensional approach** (Barrett and Russell, 1998; Watson and Tellegen, 1985): two uncorrelated dimensions. Barrett and Russell: **misery-pleasure** (valence) and **arousal-sleep**. Watson and Tellegen: **positive affect** and **negative affect**. Both approaches describe the same 2D space.

- **Bottom-up vs. top-down in emotion** (Ochsner et al., 2009): bottom-up (aversive photos responded to naturally) activates **amygdala** and occipital/temporal/parietal areas. Top-down (reinterpreting neutral photos as aversive) activates **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)**, medial PFC, and anterior cingulate. Negative affect in bottom-up linked most strongly to **amygdala** activation; in top-down to **medial PFC**.

## 6.2 Appraisal Theories of Emotion

> **Key Study:** Schartau et al. (2009): participants trained in positive cognitive appraisal ("silver lining") before viewing distressing films showed reduced horror, distress, and galvanic skin response relative to a watch-only control group. Demonstrates that appraisals causally influence emotional reactions.

- **Core assumption** (Brosch, 2013): emotional responses are elicited as the organism evaluates the relevance of environmental changes for its well-being.
- Each emotion is elicited by its own **specific appraisal pattern** (Scherer and Ellsworth, 2009):
  - **Anger**: blame attributed to another person; situation appraised as offering control and power.
  - **Sadness**: situation appraised as permitting very little control.
  - **Guilt**: self-blame.
- **Key finding** (Fontaine et al., 2013, 27 countries): emotions correctly classified from appraisal features alone in **71%** of cases; other components (bodily sensations, action tendencies) added only marginal improvement.

### Lazarus's Primary and Secondary Appraisal

- **Primary appraisal**: is this situation relevant to my well-being? Is it harmful, threatening, or challenging?
- **Secondary appraisal**: what can I do about it? Evaluation of coping resources and options.
- Smith and Lazarus (1993): manipulation of appraisals (e.g., blame external vs. internal) reliably shifted emotional state (anger vs. guilt). However, appraisal manipulations accounted for under 30% of variance in emotion ratings (Parkinson, 2001).

### Automatic vs. Controlled Appraisal

- Smith and Kirby (2001, 2009): distinguish **reasoning-based appraisal** (slow, deliberate) from **memory-activation-based appraisal** (fast, automatic). Both can produce emotional reactions.
- Ohman and Soares (1994): snake and spider phobics showed physiological responses to phobia-relevant stimuli presented **subliminally** (below conscious threshold). Emotional processing can occur without conscious appraisal.
- Winkielman et al. (2005): thirsty participants exposed to subliminal happy faces drank approximately **twice as much** liquid as those exposed to subliminal angry faces. Affective reactions can be **unconscious**.
- **Affective blindsight** (Tamietto and de Gelder, 2010): brain-damaged patients without primary visual cortex can still discriminate emotional stimuli; facial emotional reactions occurred **200-300 ms faster** for non-consciously perceived expressions.

### Evaluation of Appraisal Theories

| Strength | Limitation |
|----------|-----------|
| Appraisal manipulation alters emotion (Schartau et al., 2009) | Explains under 30% of emotion-rating variance |
| Explains individual differences in emotion | Neglects bottom-up and social processes |
| Distinguishes automatic vs. conscious appraisal | Appraisals and emotions blur; causality unclear |
| Cross-cultural support (Fontaine et al., 2013) | Focuses on current situation; ignores past/future emotions |

## 6.3 Emotion Regulation

> **Key Study:** Webb et al. (2012): meta-analysis of 306 experimental comparisons. **Cognitive change strategies** (e.g., reappraisal) had a **moderate** effect on reducing negative affect; response modulation (suppression) had a **small** effect; attentional deployment overall had a **non-significant** effect (but distraction specifically was effective).

- **Emotion generation**: immediate, spontaneous emotional response to a situation.
- **Emotion regulation** (Gross, 2013, p. 359): "requires the activation of a goal to up- or down-regulate either the magnitude or duration of the emotional response." It overrides the spontaneous response.

### Gross's Process Model

Five major strategy families, operating at different points before/after full emotional response:

| Strategy | Stage | Type | Example |
|----------|-------|------|---------|
| **Situation selection** | Earliest | Antecedent-focused | Avoiding social events (social anxiety) |
| **Situation modification** | Early | Antecedent-focused | Asking a friend to accompany you |
| **Attentional deployment** | Middle | Antecedent-focused | Distraction; focusing on neutral thoughts |
| **Cognitive change (reappraisal)** | Late | Antecedent-focused | "Most people won't judge me harshly" |
| **Response modulation (suppression)** | After response | Response-focused | Hiding angry feelings; expressing anger |

- **Antecedent-focused strategies** occur before full emotional response; **response-focused strategies** occur after.
- **Distraction** (attentional deployment): less cognitively demanding; effective early; "nips it in the bud."
- **Reappraisal** (cognitive change): cognitively demanding; more effective if used early while emotional intensity is low; produces long-lasting benefits (Sheppes and Gross, 2011).

### Reappraisal vs. Suppression: Key Differences

| Dimension | Reappraisal | Suppression (Response Modulation) |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Timing | Antecedent; early | Response-focused; late |
| Cognitive load | High | Moderate |
| Effectiveness on negative affect | Moderate-high | Small |
| Brain activity | Increases medial PFC activation; reduces amygdala | Less prefrontal engagement |
| Clinical outcome | Beneficial: reduces anxiety and depression | Associated with social phobia, eating disorders |
| Rumination/avoidance | Not linked | Avoidance increases symptoms (Aldao et al., 2010) |

- **Aldao et al. (2010)** meta-analytic review: **acceptance, problem solving, and reappraisal** reduce anxiety and depression symptoms; **rumination and avoidance** increase them.
- **Troy et al. (2013)**: reappraisal ability reduces depression only when stress is **uncontrollable**; with controllable stress, reappraisal may increase depression (problem-solving is preferable).

### Neural Basis of Emotion Regulation

- Successful strategies involve activation in **prefrontal cortex** causing reduced **amygdala** activation and reduced negative affect (Ochsner and Gross, 2008).
- Kohn et al. (2014) meta-analysis: three-stage neural network:
  1. **Emotion evaluation network**: centred on **ventrolateral PFC (VLPFC)**; initiates appraisal.
  2. **Initiation-of-regulation network**: centred on **DLPFC**; processes regulation.
  3. **Execution-of-regulation network**: superior temporal gyrus, supplementary motor area, angular gyrus; generates regulated emotional state.
- Individuals with strongest **prefrontal-amygdala connectivity** show most effective reappraisal (S.-H. Lee et al., 2012).

### Implicit Emotion Regulation

- Explicit strategies that are practised repeatedly can become **implicit** (automatic) processes.
- Mauss et al. (2007): priming reappraisal-relevant words implicitly reduced anger during provocation.
- Williams et al. (2009): implicit and explicit reappraisal equally effective in controlling physiological reactivity in anxiety-provoking situations.

> **Clinical Implication (CBT link):** Emotion regulation research directly underpins CBT. Reappraisal corresponds to cognitive restructuring; suppression is a maladaptive strategy targeted in therapy. Rumination and avoidance perpetuate anxiety and depression.

## 6.4 Affect and Cognition: Attention and Memory

### Affect and Breadth of Attention

- **Easterbrook (1959)**: increasing arousal/anxiety **narrows** the range of cues processed ("tunnel vision"). High negative affect predicts impaired peripheral attention.
- **Fredrickson and Branigan (2005)**: positive emotions **broaden** attention and the array of thoughts and actions in mind.
- **Harmon-Jones et al. (2011)**: valence alone insufficient; **motivational intensity** is the key factor:

| Affect type | Motivational Intensity | Attentional Effect |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| High-intensity negative (fear, disgust) | High | Narrowing |
| Low-intensity negative (sadness) | Low | Broadening |
| High-intensity positive (desire to win money) | High | Narrowing |
| Low-intensity positive (relaxed pleasure) | Low | Broadening |

- **Gable and Harmon-Jones (2010)**: disgust (high motivational intensity) narrowed attention; sadness (low) broadened it. Positive high-intensity produced narrowing; low-intensity positive produced broadening.
- **Weapon focus effect** (see Chapter 8): eyewitness emotional arousal narrows attention to the weapon, impairing peripheral memory.

### Mood-Congruent Memory

- **Mood congruity** (Hills et al., 2011; Miranda and Kihlstrom, 2005): emotionally toned material is learned and retrieved best when its affective value **matches** the learner's current mood state.
- Mechanism: consistent with Tulving's (1979) **encoding specificity principle**: memory retrieval is enhanced when there is overlap between information at encoding and retrieval.
- Effect is **stronger for positive** than negative mood (Rusting and DeHart, 2000): individuals in negative mood are motivated to reduce it via emotion regulation, reducing access to negative memories.
- Lewis et al. (2005): brain regions activated during learning of positive/negative stimuli were **reactivated** when participants were tested in a matching mood state (subgenual cingulate for positive; posteriolateral orbitofrontal cortex for negative).

### Mood-State-Dependent Memory

- **Mood-state-dependent memory** (Kenealy, 1997): memory is better when mood at **retrieval matches** mood at **learning**. Effect found for free recall but not cued recall.
- **Eich's "do-it-yourself" principle** (Eich, 1995): mood-dependency is greatest when effortful processing is required at learning and/or retrieval. Eich and Metcalfe (1989): mood-dependent effect was **4 times greater** in a generate (effortful) vs. read (passive) condition.

### Amygdala and Emotional Memory

- The **amygdala** is a critical hub: connected to 90% of cortical areas; strongly influences memory for emotional material.
- Murty et al. (2010) meta-analysis: good long-term memory for emotional material associated with greater amygdala and temporal lobe activation at learning.
- **Urbach-Wiethe disease** (bilateral amygdala destruction): Cahill et al. (1995) found patient BP had poor recall for emotional story events that healthy controls recalled well. Siebert et al. (2003): memory impairment greater for emotional than neutral pictures in these patients.
- Adolphs et al. (2005): amygdala required for **focused attention on important (gist) information** in emotional contexts, consistent with Easterbrook's hypothesis.

### Affect and Judgement/Decision Making

- Angie et al. (2011): anxiety, sadness, anger, and positive affect each produce different and specific patterns of effects on judgement and decision making.

| Mood | Optimism Bias | Risk-Taking | Processing Style |
|------|--------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Anxiety** | Reduced (pessimistic) | Decreased (risk-averse) | Analytic |
| **Sadness** | Slightly reduced ("depressive realism") | Increased (motivated for reward) | Analytic |
| **Anger** | Increased (optimistic) | Increased | Heuristic/shallow |
| **Positive** | Increased or unchanged | Decreased (preserve mood) | Heuristic/shallow |

- **Anger vs. anxiety** (Lerner and Keltner, 2001): fearful participants were risk-averse; angry participants were risk-seeking on the Asian disease problem.
- **Affect-as-information**: people use their current emotional state as information in making judgements, even when the emotion is incidental (unrelated to the judgement task).
- **Somatic marker hypothesis** (Damasio, 1994): bodily arousal responses (somatic markers) mark emotional events and influence decision making. Dunn et al. (2010) Iowa gambling task: individuals high in **interoception** (heartbeat detection accuracy) made superior decisions using bodily signals.
- **Moral dilemmas** (Greene et al., 2008): **personal moral dilemmas** (e.g., footbridge problem) trigger emotional (affective) processing via **VMPFC**; **utilitarian judgements** depend on **DLPFC** and anterior cingulate. 90% choose to divert trolley vs. 10% push the person. Anti-anxiety drugs shifted judgements toward utilitarian (Perkins et al., 2013).

## 6.5 Anxiety, Depression and Cognitive Biases

> **Clinical Relevance:** Understanding cognitive biases is central to Beck's cognitive model and CBT. Attentional bias and interpretive bias are primary targets of cognitive bias modification (CBM) techniques with clinical applications.

- **Comorbidity**: anxiety and depression frequently co-occur; important to distinguish their specific bias profiles.
- **Anxiety vs. depression** (Eysenck et al., 2006): anxiety is primarily associated with **future threats**; depression with **past losses**. Worry is the central symptom of generalised anxiety disorder; **rumination** is characteristic of depression.

### The Four Cognitive Biases

| Bias | Definition | Anxiety | Depression |
|------|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Attentional bias** | Selective attention to threat stimuli when presented with neutral stimuli | Strong (both subliminal and supraliminal; rapid engagement + slow disengagement) | Moderate; reduced attention to positive stimuli |
| **Interpretive bias** | Interpreting ambiguous stimuli/situations in a threatening way | Strong | Moderate |
| **Explicit memory bias** | Retrieving more negative than positive/neutral information on conscious recall tests | Weak (recall only, not recognition) | Strong |
| **Implicit memory bias** | Superior performance for negative information on non-conscious memory tests | Absent (Mitte, 2008) | Small but significant (Phillips et al., 2010) |

- **Williams et al. (1997)** theoretical framework: anxiety facilitates **perceptual** processing of threat (hence attentional and implicit bias). Depression facilitates **conceptual** processing of internally generated failure/loss material (hence explicit memory bias and rumination).

### Attentional Bias: Methods and Findings

**Dot-probe task** (MacLeod et al.): two stimuli appear simultaneously; dot replaces one; faster detection when dot replaces the threatening stimulus = attentional bias.

**Emotional Stroop task**: slower colour-naming of emotional words than neutral words = attentional bias; but note: rapid threat avoidance may produce opposite effects (Chajut et al., 2010).

- **Bar-Haim et al. (2007)** meta-analysis: strong attentional bias in anxiety with both subliminal and supraliminal stimuli; comparable magnitude across all anxiety disorders and high trait-anxious healthy individuals.
- **Rudaizky et al. (2014)**: high trait anxiety associated with **facilitated attentional engagement** with negative stimuli AND **slowed attentional disengagement**; these two biases are **uncorrelated**, suggesting separate vulnerability mechanisms.
- **Cisler and Koster (2010)**: attentional bias partially due to a largely **automatic threat-detection mechanism** (subliminal); disengagement difficulty due to poor **attentional control**.
- **Depression**: Peckham et al. (2010) meta-analysis: clear attentional bias on dot-probe; marginal on emotional Stroop. Armstrong and Olatunji (2012) eye-tracking: depressed (but not anxious) individuals showed **delayed fixation** on positive stimuli and shorter gaze duration on them.

> **Key Study:** Bar-Haim et al. (2007) meta-analysis: attentional bias for threat found consistently across all anxiety disorders and high trait-anxious healthy individuals, with both subliminal and supraliminal threat stimuli, confirming the role of both preattentive and controlled attentional processes.

### Interpretive Bias

- Trait anxiety correlated **+0.60** with threatening homophone interpretations (Eysenck et al., 1987; e.g., writing "die" vs. "dye" for an auditorily presented homophone).
- Patients with **generalised anxiety disorder** more likely to interpret ambiguous sentences in a threatening way (Eysenck et al., 1991).
- Walsh et al.: interpretive bias in high trait anxiety limited to **interpersonal/social** threats, not physical or health threats.
- Calvo and Castillo (1997): interpretive bias present at **1,250 ms** but not 500 ms, suggesting **strategic** as well as automatic components.
- Moser et al. (2012): social phobia and major depression showed larger **N4 ERP component** (400 ms) to positive resolutions of ambiguous scenarios, indicating interpretive bias occurring within 400 ms.

### Memory Biases

- **Explicit memory bias in depression** (Williams et al., 1997; Rinck and Becker, 2005): strong and well-replicated. Depressed individuals recall more negative than positive material.
- **Implicit memory bias**: small but significant in depression (Phillips et al., 2010); absent in anxiety (Mitte, 2008) contrary to Williams et al.'s prediction.
- **Anxiety + memory**: Mitte (2008) meta-analysis: anxiety associated with memory bias on **recall** tests but **not recognition** tests (recall requires more extensive retrieval; more scope for emotional influence).

### Beck's Schema Theory

> **Clinical Link (CBT):** Beck's cognitive model posits that clinical anxiety and depression are maintained by negative automatic thoughts and underlying maladaptive **schemas** (organised cognitive structures). Schemas bias all stages of information processing, producing selective attention, negative interpretations, and enhanced recall of schema-consistent material. CBT targets schema change through cognitive restructuring (reappraisal) and behavioural experiments.

- **Cognitive vulnerability hypothesis** (Beck and Dozois, 2011; Williams et al., 1997): individuals possessing cognitive biases are more vulnerable to developing anxiety disorders or depression when they encounter negative life events.
- Lewinsohn et al. (2001): adolescents with strong **interpretive bias** were much more likely to develop major depressive disorder after experiencing many negative life events; those with low interpretive bias were not affected.
- Cohen et al. (2012): children with interpretive bias (pessimistic consequences of negative events) showed greater increase in depressive symptoms after real negative events over 7 weeks.

### Attentional Control Theory (Eysenck et al., 2007)

> **Clinical Link:** Eysenck's attentional control theory explains why anxious patients show impaired performance even with no reduction in accuracy: efficiency declines while effectiveness is maintained, at the cost of extra cognitive effort.

- **Core claim**: anxiety reduces available **working memory** capacity, impairing **attentional control** in two ways:
  1. **Impaired inhibitory control**: reduced ability to suppress task-irrelevant threatening stimuli (negative attentional control).
  2. **Impaired shifting ability**: reduced ability to shift attention optimally within and between tasks (positive attentional control).
- Calvo et al. (2012): high-anxiety individuals showed delayed inhibitory control relative to low-anxiety individuals (threat distractors).
- **Joormann's impaired cognitive control model of depression**: depression associated with deficient inhibitory control, causing difficulty disengaging from negative stimuli. Prolonged engagement leads to elaborative processing and enhanced memory for negative material (explaining explicit memory bias and rumination).

### Combined Cognitive Bias Hypothesis

- Hirsch et al. (2006): cognitive biases do not operate in isolation; they **interact and influence each other**.
- White et al. (2011): training to increase **attentional bias** also increased **interpretive bias**.
- Amir et al. (2010): training to reduce interpretive bias made it easier to **disengage** attention from threat.
- Salemink et al. (2010): training to produce negative interpretations led to more negative-toned **memory** of ambiguous scenarios.

## 6.6 Cognitive Bias Modification (CBM)

> **Clinical Relevance:** CBM offers a focused, mechanistic alternative or adjunct to CBT, allowing causal testing of cognitive vulnerability hypotheses.

- **Definition** (Hallion and Ruscio, 2011): "an experimental paradigm that uses training to induce maladaptive or adaptive cognitive biases."
- **Two main targets**: attentional bias and interpretive bias.

### Methods

| Target | Procedure |
|--------|----------|
| **Attentional bias modification (ABM)** | Modified dot-probe: probe always replaces the **neutral** stimulus; participants learn to direct attention away from threat |
| **Interpretive bias modification** | Homograph (one spelling, two meanings, e.g., "choke") followed by a word fragment completable only with a positive/neutral word; trains positive/benign interpretations |

### Effectiveness

- **Hakamata et al. (2010)**: correlation of **+0.75** between reduction in attentional bias and reduction in anxiety across studies; effects larger in patient groups; enduring changes to trait anxiety levels.
- **Hallion and Ruscio (2011)** meta-analysis: CBM reduced attentional and interpretive biases; reduction greater for **interpretive** bias; CBM significantly reduced **anxiety** (but not depression) after a stressor. Most studies targeted only one bias; combined training likely more effective.
- **Haeffel et al. (2012)**: attentional bias modification targeting depression-relevant adjectives (failure, worthless) reduced depressive symptoms; treated participants persisted longer on difficult tasks.
- **Generalisation caveat**: reductions in laboratory conditions do not guarantee generalisation to everyday life; almost exclusively self-report outcome measures used (MacLeod and Mathews, 2012).

## 6.7 Consciousness

### Definitions and Distinctions

- **Conscious content**: information of which we are aware at any given moment (perceptions, thoughts, feelings, self-awareness).
- **Conscious level**: state of consciousness; runs from coma through to alert wakefulness.
- **Access consciousness** (Block, 2012): contents can be reported and used by other cognitive processes.
- **Phenomenal consciousness**: raw, private subjective experience; richer than what can be reported.
- **The hard problem** (Chalmers, 2007): why and how physical processing gives rise to subjective experience. "The easy problems" involve integration, discrimination, access to internal states, and deliberate control of behaviour.

### Functions of Consciousness

1. **Perception of the environment.**
2. **Social communication and understanding others' mental states** (Humphrey, 1983): same machinery computes awareness in oneself and in others (temporo-parietal junction; Graziano and Kastner, 2011). TMS to temporo-parietal junction impairs switching between self- and other-representations (Sowden and Catmur, 2014).
3. **Controlling action** (contested). Baumeister et al. (2011): implementation intentions create "instant habits" aiding goal achievement. Gollwitzer and Sheeran (2006) meta-analysis: specific implementation intentions significantly increased goal achievement over general intentions.
4. **Thinking beyond the here-and-now**: Kane et al. (2007): conscious thoughts wander 30% of the time.
5. **Integrating information** (Tononi and Koch, 2008): consciousness is extraordinarily informative because it rules out enormous numbers of alternative possible states.

- **Illusion of free will** (Wegner, 2003): conscious intention is inferred from **priority** (thought precedes action), **consistency** (thought is consistent with action), and **exclusivity** (no obvious alternative cause). Wegner and Wheatley (1999): participants falsely believed they caused the cursor to stop when they heard the relevant word 1-5 seconds beforehand.
- **Libet et al. (1983)**: **readiness potential** occurred **350 ms** before conscious awareness of intention to move; conscious awareness preceded actual movement by ~200 ms.
- **Soon et al. (2008)**: frontopolar cortex (BA10) and parietal cortex brain activity predicted participants' left/right finger decisions **up to 7 seconds** before conscious awareness.

### Assessing Consciousness and Disorders of Consciousness

| State | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Coma** | No wakefulness; no conscious awareness |
| **Vegetative state** | Wakefulness without awareness; no purposeful response or language |
| **Minimally conscious state** | Some evidence of conscious awareness |

- **Owen et al. (2006)**: vegetative-state patient (23-year-old, road accident) showed same brain activity patterns as healthy controls when imagining playing tennis (supplementary motor area) or navigating her house.
- **Monti et al. (2010)**: vegetative-state patient answered yes/no questions via imagery (tennis vs. house navigation) with correct brain activation patterns.
- **Coleman et al. (2009)**: neuroimaging signs of consciousness predicted **behavioural recovery at 6 months**, even when initial behavioural assessment showed no signs.
- **Recurrent processing and consciousness** (Lamme, 2010): **feedforward sweep** (0-100/150 ms, V1 to higher areas) is necessary but not sufficient for consciousness. **Recurrent processing** (feedback from higher to lower areas) is required. Masking and TMS disrupting recurrent processing impair conscious perception (Fahrenfort et al., 2007; Koivisto et al., 2011).

### Global Workspace Theory (GWT)

**Baars (1988)** and **Baars and Franklin (2007)** (behavioural emphasis); **Dehaene and Naccache (2001); Dehaene and Changeux (2011)** (neural emphasis, global neuronal workspace theory).

**Key assumptions:**

1. Much processing occurs via **numerous specialised unconscious processors** operating in parallel across distributed brain areas. Early processing is similar whether or not a stimulus is consciously perceived.
2. Consciousness involves **integrating information** from multiple processors into a **global workspace**; this produces "ignition" (widespread, synchronised brain activity), making information globally available.
3. Key brain areas: **DLPFC**, **anterior cingulate**, and parietal regions; activity is widespread and relatively long-lasting.
4. **Attention and consciousness are closely linked**: attention resembles choosing a television channel; consciousness resembles the picture on the screen (Baars, 1997). Attention is necessary but not always sufficient for conscious awareness.

**Evidence:**
- Lamy et al. (2009): early ERP components unaffected by conscious awareness; late wave **P3** (400-600 ms) was far more widespread in the presence of conscious awareness.
- Melloni et al. (2007): only consciously perceived words produced **synchronised neural activity** across frontal, parietal, and occipital areas (40-182 ms).
- Gaillard et al. (2009): epileptic patients with implanted electrodes; unmasked (conscious) vs. masked (unconscious) stimuli; **frontal cortex** showed far greater sustained activation (from ~350 ms) under conscious conditions.
- **King et al. (2013)**: EEG (wSMI measure) in vegetative, minimally conscious, conscious brain-damaged, and healthy groups. Dramatic increases in integrated brain activity with increasing levels of conscious awareness.
- **Del Cul et al. (2009)**: prefrontal cortex damage patients required longer masking delays for conscious perception; magnitude of effect proportional to prefrontal damage. Establishes **causal** role of PFC in consciousness.

> **Key Study:** Melloni et al. (2007): compared brain activity for consciously vs. non-consciously perceived words. Non-consciously perceived words showed substantial EEG activity, suggesting thorough processing. Only consciously perceived words elicited synchronised neural activity spanning frontal, parietal, and occipital areas, strongly supporting the global workspace prediction of widespread integration as the hallmark of consciousness.

**Evaluation of GWT:**

| Strength | Limitation |
|----------|-----------|
| All major assumptions supported | Focuses narrowly on visual consciousness |
| Explains vegetative-state findings | Integrated activity may precede or follow consciousness (causality unclear) |
| Links to attention well-specified | Neglects self-awareness and self-knowledge aspects |
| Cross-modal evidence (auditory, visual) | Prefrontal activation also occurs during some unconscious processes |

### Is Consciousness Unitary? Split-Brain Evidence

- **Split-brain patients**: corpus callosum (250 million axons) cut to contain epileptic seizures. Normal daily life largely intact because patients move their eyes to ensure both hemispheres receive visual information.
- **Sperry (1968)**: two separate consciousnesses; right hemisphere is a "second conscious entity running in parallel."
- **Gazzaniga (2013)**: single consciousness based in the **left hemisphere interpreter**; right hemisphere has limited self-awareness. Paul S. case: left hemisphere confabulated explanations for right-hemisphere-driven actions ("The chicken claw goes with the chicken and you need a shovel to clean out the chicken shed").
- **Bayne's switch model (2008, 2010)**: single stream of consciousness alternates between hemispheres based on attentional resources allocation.
- **ERP evidence**: Hesselmann et al. (2013); GH study (Verleger et al., 2011): pronounced **P3** component to stimuli presented to left hemisphere; absent or markedly reduced P3 to right-hemisphere stimuli, suggesting **right hemisphere has limited conscious access**.
- **Overall conclusion**: left hemisphere interpreter plays the dominant role in conscious awareness; right hemisphere has basic self-awareness and emotional processing but very limited language-mediated consciousness.

| **Appraisal** | Cognitive evaluation of a situation's relevance to well-being |

| **Secondary appraisal** | Evaluating available coping resources and options |
| **Emotion generation** | Immediate, spontaneous emotional response to a situation |
| **Emotion regulation** | Deliberate processes to alter magnitude or duration of emotional response |

| **Cognitive reappraisal** | Reinterpreting emotional meaning of a situation to reduce negative affect |
| **Response modulation** | Altering emotional/expressive response after the emotion has occurred |
| **Suppression** | Inhibiting outward expression of emotion (response modulation subtype) |
| **Distraction** | Disengaging attention from emotional content to reduce affect |
| **Mood congruity** | Better learning/recall when material's valence matches current mood |
| **Mood-state-dependent memory** | Better memory when mood at retrieval matches mood at learning |
| **Encoding specificity principle** | Memory depends on overlap between encoding and retrieval contexts |
| **Amygdala** | Brain structure in temporal lobe; strongly associated with fear and emotional memory |
| **Trait anxiety** | Personality dimension; individual differences in susceptibility to anxiety |
| **Comorbidity** | Presence of two or more mental disorders simultaneously |
| **Attentional bias** | Selective attention to threat stimuli presented alongside neutral stimuli |
| **Interpretive bias** | Tendency to interpret ambiguous stimuli in a threatening way |
| **Explicit memory bias** | Retrieving more negative than neutral/positive information on conscious tests |
| **Implicit memory bias** | Better performance for negative information on non-conscious memory tests |
| **Dot-probe task** | Paradigm measuring attentional bias: faster detection of probe at attended location |
| **Emotional Stroop task** | Slower colour-naming of emotional vs. neutral words as index of attentional bias |
| **Attentional control theory** | Eysenck et al. (2007); anxiety reduces working memory capacity and impairs inhibitory and shifting control |
| **Cognitive vulnerability hypothesis** | Cognitive biases increase susceptibility to anxiety/depression after negative life events |
| **Cognitive bias modification (CBM)** | Training to reduce attentional and/or interpretive bias; clinical applications |
| **Homograph** | Word with one spelling but two or more meanings (used in CBM training) |
| **Beck's schema theory** | Maladaptive cognitive schemas bias processing at all levels; target of CBT |
| **Access consciousness** | Consciousness whose contents can be reported and used by other cognitive processes |
| **Phenomenal consciousness** | Raw, private subjective experience |
| **Hard problem of consciousness** | Why and how physical processing gives rise to subjective experience (Chalmers, 2007) |
| **Vegetative state** | Brain-damaged state with wakefulness but no behavioural signs of awareness |
| **Recurrent processing** | Feedback from higher to lower brain areas; associated with conscious visual perception (Lamme) |
| **Feedforward sweep** | Rapid (0-150 ms) processing from V1 to higher areas; not sufficient for consciousness |
| **Global workspace theory** | Baars (1988); consciousness involves widespread integrated brain activity in a global workspace |
| **Global neuronal workspace theory** | Dehaene and Naccache (2001); neural version of GWT; emphasises DLPFC and anterior cingulate |
| **Ignition** | Sudden widespread synchronised brain activation marking the onset of conscious awareness |
| **Split-brain patients** | Patients with corpus callosum severed; test of unitary vs. multiple consciousness |
| **Interpreter (Gazzaniga)** | Left-hemisphere system that constructs coherent explanations of events |
| **Somatic marker hypothesis** | Damasio (1994); bodily arousal markers tag emotional events and influence decision making |
| **Interoception** | Sensitivity to internal bodily stimuli; predicts quality of somatic marker use |
| **Optimism bias** | Tendency to believe one is more likely than others to experience positive events |
| **Affective blindsight** | Discrimination of emotional stimuli without conscious perception in brain-damaged patients |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Appraisal theories**: primary appraisal (is this event relevant to me? Is it threatening?) and secondary appraisal (what can I do?). Lazarus's distinction is classic. Fontaine et al. (2013): appraisal features alone correctly classified emotions in 71% of cases across 27 countries.

2. **Automatic vs. conscious appraisal**: Ohman and Soares (1994): subliminal phobia-relevant stimuli produce physiological responses. Winkielman et al. (2005): subliminal happy vs. angry faces doubled amount drunk. Emotions can be non-conscious.

3. **Gross's process model**: five strategies; antecedent-focused (situation selection, modification, attentional deployment, reappraisal) vs. response-focused (response modulation/suppression). **Reappraisal is superior to suppression** in reducing negative affect and clinical symptoms.

4. **Reappraisal vs. suppression outcomes** (clinical exam favourite): reappraisal reduces negative affect moderately; associated with PFC activation and amygdala reduction; correlated with well-being and working memory capacity. Suppression has small effect; associated with social phobia and eating disorders. Aldao et al. (2010): rumination and avoidance worsen anxiety/depression; reappraisal, acceptance, and problem-solving help.

5. **Mood congruity vs. mood-state-dependent memory**: congruity = valence of material matches mood; dependence = mood at retrieval matches mood at learning. Both explained by encoding specificity. Both stronger with positive than negative mood. Eich's "do-it-yourself" principle: effortful processing required.

6. **Affect and attention**: motivational intensity is the critical variable (Harmon-Jones et al., 2011), not just valence. High intensity narrows attention; low intensity broadens it. Applies to both positive and negative affect.

7. **Cognitive bias profiles in anxiety vs. depression** (very high-yield clinical topic):
   - Anxiety: strong attentional bias, strong interpretive bias, weak explicit memory bias, absent implicit memory bias.
   - Depression: moderate attentional bias (to negative and away from positive), moderate interpretive bias, strong explicit memory bias, small implicit memory bias.
   - Anxiety = future threat orientation; depression = past loss and internal focus.

8. **Attentional bias methods**: dot-probe task (faster probe detection when dot replaces threatening stimulus) and emotional Stroop task (slower colour-naming of emotional words). Eye-tracking provides continuous, more direct assessment.

9. **Attentional control theory (Eysenck et al., 2007)**: anxiety reduces working memory capacity, impairing inhibitory control (inability to suppress threat) and shifting ability. Explains why anxious individuals are inefficient even when accuracy is maintained.

10. **Beck's schema theory**: maladaptive schemas bias all information processing stages. Cognitive vulnerability hypothesis: biases + negative life events predict onset of anxiety/depression (Lewinsohn et al., 2001; Cohen et al., 2012). Foundation of CBT.

11. **Cognitive bias modification**: dot-probe modified (probe always replaces neutral) reduces attentional bias. Homograph training reduces interpretive bias. Hakamata et al. (2010): r = +0.75 between reduction in attentional bias and reduction in anxiety. More effective for anxiety than depression.

12. **Hard problem vs. easy problems of consciousness** (Chalmers, 2007): hard = subjective experience; easy = discrimination, integration, access, control.

13. **Global workspace theory**: Baars; Dehaene (global neuronal workspace). Key: (a) early processing same for seen/unseen stimuli; (b) consciousness associated with widespread synchronised brain activity (ignition); (c) key areas: DLPFC, anterior cingulate, parietal cortex; (d) attention is necessary (but not sufficient) for consciousness. P3 ERP (400-600 ms) is the electrophysiological marker.

14. **Vegetative state and neuroimaging**: Owen et al. (2006): tennis/house imagery paradigm. Monti et al. (2010): yes/no answers via imagery. Some vegetative-state patients show neuroimaging evidence of conscious awareness and language comprehension without behavioural signs.

15. **Split-brain consciousness**: Gazzaniga's interpreter hypothesis (left hemisphere dominant; single consciousness). Sperry's dual consciousness hypothesis. ERP evidence (Hesselmann et al., 2013): P3 present for left-hemisphere stimuli, absent for right-hemisphere stimuli. Left hemisphere interpreter confabulates explanations for right-hemisphere-driven actions (Paul S. chicken/shovel case).

16. **Libet et al. (1983)**: readiness potential 350 ms before conscious awareness of intention; 200 ms between awareness and movement. Soon et al. (2008): decision predicted 7 seconds before conscious awareness from frontopolar cortex activity. These findings are important but limited (artificial, trivial tasks; does not rule out role of consciousness).
