# Ch 5: Thinking, Reasoning & Decision Making

**Overview:** Part IV covers three interrelated domains: (1) problem solving and expertise (Ch 12), (2) judgement and decision making (Ch 13), and (3) reasoning and hypothesis testing (Ch 14). Running theme: humans are neither fully rational nor hopelessly irrational; we are bounded rational agents who use heuristics, mental models, and dual processes to navigate an uncertain world.

---

## 5.1 Problem Solving: Foundations

### Definitions
- **Problem solving** requires: (1) two states exist; (2) agent is in one and wants the other; (3) gap-bridging is non-obvious; (4) solution is a consciously guided multi-step process (Goel, 2010).
- **Well-defined problems:** initial state, permissible moves, and goal state all clearly specified (e.g., chess, Tower of Hanoi).
- **Ill-defined problems:** underspecified on one or more dimensions (e.g., "become happier"). Most everyday problems are ill-defined; most lab research uses well-defined problems.
- **Knowledge-rich problems:** require extensive domain knowledge (e.g., chess problems). **Knowledge-lean problems:** all necessary information is in the problem statement; minimise individual differences.

> **Key Study (Goel & Grafman, 2000):** Patient PF had right prefrontal damage, IQ = 128, and performed well on well-defined lab tasks but failed catastrophically on ill-defined everyday problems due to impaired preliminary planning.

## 5.2 Gestalt Approach: Insight, Fixedness, and Mental Set

### Insight
**Insight** = "any sudden comprehension, realisation, or problem solution that involves a reorganisation of the elements of a person's mental representation" (Kounios & Beeman, 2014). Accompanied by the "aha experience". Gestaltists contrasted:
- **Reproductive thinking:** systematic reuse of past experience.
- **Productive thinking:** novel restructuring of the problem.

**Warm feelings study (Metcalfe & Wiebe, 1987):** On non-insight problems, "warmth" ratings rose progressively; on insight problems, warmth remained flat then spiked immediately before solution.

> **Key Study (Bowden et al., 2005):** Using the **Remote Associates Test** (three words, find a common associate), insight trials (vs. non-insight) activated the **right anterior superior temporal gyrus** (processes distant semantic relations). The right hemisphere integrates weakly active, distant associations; the left hemisphere handles strongly connected associations.

**Incubation (Wallas, 1926):** Resting away from a blocked problem can facilitate solution. Meta-analysis (Sio & Ormerod, 2009): effects found in 73% of studies, larger with multiple-solution creative problems, larger after long preparation times. Wagner et al. (2004): after sleep, 59% found a mathematical short-cut vs. only 25% without sleep.

### Representational Change Theory (Ohlsson, 1992)
Impasse occurs because problem is misrepresented. Three routes to change:
1. **Constraint relaxation** (remove implicit rules about what is permissible).
2. **Re-encoding** (reinterpret a feature of the problem).
3. **Elaboration** (add new information to the representation).

> **Key Study (Knoblich et al., 1999):** Matchstick arithmetic problems (Roman numerals). Problems requiring constraint relaxation on operators (e.g., + or =) were far harder than those changing values, confirming that habitual arithmetic rules block solutions.

Ollinger et al. (2014) extension: even after insight, an efficient **search process** is still needed (shown with the nine-dot problem). The nine-dot problem is not solved by insight alone.

### Functional Fixedness
**Functional fixedness** = inflexible focus on the usual function of an object.

> **Key Study (Duncker, 1945) -- Candle Problem:** Participants given candle, matches, and tacks in a box had to mount the candle on a wall. Most failed to see the box as a potential candle platform (fixated on it as a container). More correct solutions when the box was presented empty (less cue to "container" function).

Chrysikou et al. (2013): TMS reducing left prefrontal activity facilitated generation of **uncommon** object uses, supporting the role of cognitive control in producing functional fixedness.

### Mental Set (Einstellung)
**Mental set** = tendency to apply a previously successful strategy even when it is inappropriate.

> **Key Study (Luchins, 1942) -- Water-Jar Problems:** Participants trained on complex three-jar solutions were told to find 25 quarts (trivially solved by A - C). Only 36% solved it vs. 95% of naive participants. Even **chess experts** show Einstellung (Bilalić et al., 2008a): most failed to find a 3-move solution when a familiar 5-move solution was available, and eye-movement data showed their attention stayed on features relevant to the familiar solution.

## 5.3 Information-Processing Approach: Newell and Simon

**Newell and Simon (1972)** reframed problem solving as search through a **problem space** (all states that can arise within a problem). Key distinctions:
- **Algorithm:** exhaustive, guaranteed solution; computationally expensive.
- **Heuristic:** rule of thumb; fast, frugal, often (not always) accurate.

### Heuristics Identified by Newell and Simon
| Heuristic | Description | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **Means-ends analysis** | Note current-goal difference; form sub-goal to reduce it; select operator | Fails when every correct move requires moving away from goal (Sweller & Levine, 1982: only 10% solved such a maze vs. 100% without goal info) |
| **Hill climbing** | Always move to state closer to goal | Gets stuck on local maxima ("trapped on a hill") |
| **Progress monitoring** (MacGregor et al., 2001) | Monitor rate of progress; switch strategy if too slow | Illusion of progress delays strategy switch |

### General Problem Solver (GPS)
GPS = Newell and Simon's (1972) computer simulation implementing means-ends analysis. Demonstrated that heuristic search could produce intelligent behaviour without exhaustive look-ahead.

### Planning and Prefrontal Cortex
- Dagher et al. (1999): **Tower of London** task -- complex moves activated **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)**.
- Left DLPFC: problem **representation** (Ruh et al., 2012). Right DLPFC: **planning** itself.
- Prefrontal damage patients perform worse on moves requiring planning ahead or moving contrary to the goal (Goel & Grafman, 1995; Colvin et al., 2001).

### Cognitive Miser
**Cognitive miser** (Kahneman, 2003): someone economical with thinking effort. Differs from Newell and Simon's view: heuristics used not because of capacity limits but because of unwillingness to expend effort. The **Cognitive Reflection Test** (Frederick, 2005) measures this -- most Harvard/Princeton students fail at least one of three questions (bat-and-ball: 5 cents; machines-and-widgets: 5 minutes; lily-pads: 47 days).

## 5.4 Analogical Problem Solving

**Analogy** = comparison between two domains emphasising structural similarity. Three types of similarity (Chen, 2002):
1. **Superficial (surface):** solution-irrelevant details match.
2. **Structural:** causal/relational structure matches -- most important for transfer.
3. **Procedural:** concrete solution steps match.

> **Key Study (Gick & Holyoak, 1980) -- Radiation Problem:** A tumour must be destroyed with rays without damaging surrounding tissue. Unprompted, only 10% solved it. When told about the structurally analogous "general and fortress" story (army converges on fortress by multiple small roads), 80% solved it -- but only 40% when not told the story was relevant. Superficial similarity to the problem boosted spontaneous recall to 88% (Keane, 1987).

**Reception vs. Production paradigm:** In reception, people tend to pick analogies based on surface similarity. In production (generating one's own analogies), people favour **structural** similarity (Blanchette & Dunbar, 2000; Dunbar & Blanchette, 2001 on molecular biologists).

**Improving analogy use:** Explicitly comparing two structurally similar problems before the target problem increases retrieval of the fortress story (Kurtz & Loewenstein, 2007: 54% vs. 38%). Forming a concrete mental model of the first problem also aids transfer (Day & Goldstone, 2011).

**Brain:** Analogical reasoning engages **rostrolateral prefrontal cortex (BA10)** throughout, plus inferior frontal gyrus and DLPFC for encoding and mapping; less activation at response stage. Temporal lobes provide semantic content. Frontal patients are disproportionately impaired when relevant semantic or perceptual distractors compete (Krawczyk et al., 2008).

## 5.5 Expertise

**Expertise** = very high knowledge and performance in a domain through many years of systematic practice.

### Chess Expertise: De Groot and Chase & Simon
> **Key Study (de Groot, 1965):** Brief presentation of real game positions. Chess masters recalled 91% vs. 43% for weaker players. Crucially, there was **no difference** for random positions, showing superior memory reflects stored patterns, not general memory ability.

**Chunking (Chase & Simon, 1973):** Experts perceive chess positions as **meaningful chunks** rather than individual pieces, allowing rapid encoding and retrieval.

**Template theory (Gobet):** Experts store **templates** (abstract schematic structures with a fixed core and variable slots). Each template covers ~10+ pieces. Templates allow:
- Rapid recognition of known positions.
- Good (though not perfect) recall of random positions (because some patterns appear by chance).

Evidence for templates:
- Experts recall positions using ~2 large templates (max size 13-15 for masters vs. 6 for novices) (Gobet & Clarkson, 2004).
- Blitz chess performance (5 minutes total) correlates +0.78 to +0.90 with normal chess performance, implying template-based knowledge dominates rapid play (Burns, 2004).
- Charness et al. (2001): Within the first second of viewing, experts fixated tactically relevant pieces 80% vs. 64% for intermediates.

**Limitation:** Slow search processes matter too. Moxley et al. (2012): final chosen move was much stronger than first-mentioned move for both experts and tournament players, indicating strategic deliberation. Bilalić et al. (2008a): no candidate masters found a shorter (3-move) solution when familiar 5-move solution was available -- template knowledge can itself cause mental set.

### Medical Expertise
- Experts rely more on **fast, (quasi-)automatic pattern recognition**; novices rely more on slow, analytic feature-by-feature assessment.
- Eye-tracking: experts fixate task-relevant areas faster, with fewer but longer fixations, and larger saccades (Gegenfurtner et al., 2011 meta-analysis: 5 consistent differences from novices).
- Kulatunga-Moruzi et al. (2004): dermatologists performed **better without** verbal descriptions of cases; verbal information interfered with their visual strategy.
- Mamede et al. (2010): analytic thinking enhanced expert diagnosis for **complex** (not simple) cases, but helped novices not at all.
- Medical knowledge is more visual and narrowly focused than chess templates (chess requires planning future moves; diagnosis mainly involves pattern-to-stored-knowledge matching).

### Brain Plasticity
- London taxi drivers must learn 25,000 streets (takes ~3 years). Experienced drivers have **greater grey matter volume in posterior hippocampus** (Woollett et al., 2009). This is causal: only those who qualified showed the increase (Woollett & Maguire, 2011). Trade-off: smaller anterior hippocampus and worse learning of new associations.
- Juggling training: 5% increase in grey and white matter in visual motion area over 6 weeks (Scholz et al., 2009).
- Musical training: structural changes in motor cortex and auditory cortex even in 6-year-olds after 15 months training (Hyde et al., 2009); changes largest in children and young adults.

**Plasticity** = changes in brain structure and function dependent on experience that affect behaviour.

### Deliberate Practice (Ericsson)
**Deliberate practice** (Ericsson & Towne, 2010): four components: (1) appropriate difficulty; (2) informative feedback; (3) opportunity to repeat; (4) opportunity to correct errors.

**Long-term working memory (Ericsson & Kintsch, 1995):** experts bypass limited working memory capacity by rapidly encoding domain-relevant information directly into LTM via knowledge structures. Example: SF (Ericsson & Chase, 1982) increased digit span from 7 to 80 over two years by chunking digits into running-time associations.

**Support for deliberate practice:** Correlation between total practice hours and chess skill always >+0.50 (Campitelli & Gobet, 2011 review). International masters averaged >25,000 hours.

**Limits of deliberate practice (Hambrick et al., 2014):** Deliberate practice accounts for only **34% of chess variance** and **29.9% of music variance**. Some players achieve master level with only 3,000 hours; others need 23,600. Intelligence matters -- no chess master had verbal IQ below 110 or non-verbal IQ below 115 (Grabner et al., 2007). Conclusion: deliberate practice is **necessary but not sufficient**; talent sets a ceiling.

## 5.6 Judgement: Heuristics and Biases (Kahneman and Tversky)

### Bayes' Theorem and Base-Rate Neglect
**Base-rate information** = relative frequency of an event in a population. Bayes' theorem prescribes how new evidence should update prior probabilities (prior odds x likelihood ratio = posterior odds).

> **Key Study (Kahneman & Tversky, 1972) -- Taxi-cab Problem:** 85% Green cabs, 15% Blue cabs; witness identifies Blue but is wrong 20% of the time. Bayesian answer: 41% probability it was Blue. Most participants ignored base rates and answered 80%.

**Representativeness heuristic:** judging category membership by how typical the target is of the category (ignoring base rates).

> **Key Study (Tversky & Kahneman, 1983) -- Linda Problem (Conjunction Fallacy):** Linda is described as a former feminist activist. Is it more probable she is (a) a bank teller, or (b) a bank teller active in the feminist movement? Most choose (b) -- this is the **conjunction fallacy** (P(A and B) cannot exceed P(A)). Reduction of about two-thirds when expressed in frequency format (Fiedler, 1988). Persists even with explicit clarification.

**Availability heuristic (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974):** estimating frequency/probability by the ease with which examples come to mind. Dramatic or publicised events (e.g., murder) are overestimated relative to quiet ones (e.g., suicide) (Lichtenstein et al., 1978). Overridden by deliberate thought when participants were not under cognitive load (Oppenheimer, 2004). Under cognitive load, famous names were chosen 80% of the time (Oppenheimer & Monin, 2009).

**Anchoring and adjustment:** initial anchor value biases final estimates even when irrelevant (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974). Not directly addressed in Ch 13 in detail but listed as one of the three major heuristics alongside representativeness and availability.

**Affect heuristic (Pachur et al., 2012):** gauge dread/fear to infer prevalence. Recall of direct personal experience was the best predictor of judged death frequencies; media coverage was the weakest predictor.

### Evaluation of Kahneman and Tversky
Strengths: established the field; applicability outside psychology (economics, law). Weaknesses: heuristics vaguely defined; limited theoretical precision; detached from emotional/social reality; neglects that problems sometimes provide misleading information rather than revealing biased processing.

### Support Theory (Tversky & Koehler, 1994)
More explicit descriptions of an event increase its subjective probability. Mechanism: (1) draws attention to less obvious sub-events; (2) overcoming memory limits. Redelmeier et al. (1995): expert doctors assigned higher probability to residual diagnoses when five specific diagnoses (vs. two) were listed first. Limitation: explicit descriptions can **decrease** probability when they direct attention to low-probability causes (Sloman et al., 2004).

### Natural Frequencies (Gigerenzer and Hoffrage, 1999)
Evolutionary history equipped us to process natural sampling frequencies, not probabilities. Presenting problems in frequency format substantially improves accuracy (Hoffrage et al., 2000: advanced medical students performed much better on diagnostic tasks in frequency format). However, Evans et al. (2000): advantage of frequency formats disappears when problem structure is equally transparent in both formats.

### Fast-and-Frugal Heuristics (Gigerenzer)
**Recognition heuristic:** if one of two objects is recognised and the other is not, choose the recognised one. Used in 92% of trials when recognition is valid (Goldstein & Gigerenzer, 2002). Meta-analysis: correlation of +0.64 between heuristic usage and its validity (Pachur et al., 2012).
**Take-the-best strategy:** rank cues by validity; stop searching after the first discriminating cue. Less-is-more effect: simpler hiatus heuristic (only recent customers are active) correctly categorised 83% vs. 75% for a complex model (Wubben & Wangenheim, 2008).

## 5.7 Dual-Process / System 1 and System 2

**Kahneman's (2003) dual-process theory:**
| Feature | System 1 | System 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Fast | Slow |
| Effort | Effortless | Effortful |
| Processing | Parallel, automatic | Serial, controlled |
| Consciousness | Not introspectable | Consciously monitored |
| Emotion | Often emotionally charged | Relatively neutral |

System 1 generates intuitive answers (e.g., via representativeness); System 2 evaluates and may correct them. Correct answers on Linda problem took ~40% longer than incorrect ones, consistent with System 2 involvement (De Neys, 2006).

**De Neys' (2012) Logical Intuition Model:** not purely serial. Both heuristic and logical information can be processed intuitively (in parallel). If they conflict, deliberate System 2 processing is triggered. Explains why people sometimes feel conflict even when they give the heuristic answer.

## 5.8 Decision Making Under Risk

### Expected Utility Theory (von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944)
Expected utility = probability x utility. People should maximise expected utility. Prescriptive normative theory.

### Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979, 1984)
Two core assumptions:
1. **Reference point:** current state defines gains and losses.
2. **Loss aversion:** people are far more sensitive to losses than equivalent gains. Typical ratio: need to win ~twice what you might lose to accept a 50:50 bet.

Value function (see shape in Kahneman & Tversky 1984): concave for gains (diminishing sensitivity), convex for losses (also diminishing sensitivity but steeper slope for losses). Also predicts **overweighting of low-probability events** (explains lottery buying).

> **Key Study (Tversky & Kahneman, 1981) -- Asian Disease Problem (Framing Effect):** 600 expected deaths. Gain frame (200 saved certain vs. 1/3 chance all 600 saved): 72% chose certain gain. Loss frame (400 die certain vs. 2/3 chance 600 die): 78% chose risky option. Same expected outcomes; framing changed preference. Explained by loss aversion (certain losses are more aversive than equivalent certain gains).

**Sunk-cost effect:** continuing to invest in a failing course of action because resources have already been spent ("throwing good money after bad"). Example: Dawes (1988) -- many participants said a couple should continue to a resort despite feeling unwell, just to avoid "wasting" a $100 deposit.

**Certainty effect:** people prefer certain outcomes over probabilistic ones even when expected values are equal.

**Real-world evidence for loss aversion:**
- Pope & Schweitzer (2011): 2.5 million golf putts -- par putts (avoiding bogey = loss) more successful than same-length birdie putts (extra gain); 94% of golfers including Tiger Woods showed loss aversion.
- Expert poker players bet more aggressively after big losses (Smith et al., 2009).
- Financial professionals showed risk aversion for gains, risk seeking for losses (Abdellaoui et al., 2013) but were less loss-averse than students.

**Limitations of prospect theory:** Overweighting of rare events fails when decisions are based on personal experience rather than descriptions (Hertwig et al., 2004 -- description-experience gap). Loss neutrality sometimes occurs (Yechiam & Hochman, 2013 attentional model). Individual differences (e.g., narcissism, self-esteem) strongly influence risk preference but are de-emphasised.

### Bounded Rationality (Simon, 1957)
Decision making constrained by environmental and cognitive limits (limited attention, small STM). Leads to **satisficing** (choosing the first option meeting minimum requirements) rather than maximising. Satisficers are happier, more optimistic, and less regretful than maximisers (Schwartz et al., 2002).

### Omission Bias and Status Quo Bias
- **Omission bias:** preference for inaction even when action yields better outcomes (e.g., parents accept higher disease risk than vaccination risk). Even expert pulmonologists chose suboptimal strategies when inaction was an option (Aberegg et al., 2005). Explained by anticipated regret.
- **Status quo bias:** preference for maintaining current state; mistaken rejection of status quo activates medial PFC and insula (Nicolle et al., 2011).

### Somatic Marker Hypothesis (Damasio)
Not covered extensively in Ch 13, but emotional/brain evidence converges: ventromedial PFC damage leads to elevated risk-seeking for both gains and losses (Weller et al., 2007); amygdala damage eliminates loss aversion (De Martino et al., 2010 -- "cautionary brake"). Emotion regulation (Sokol-Hessner et al., 2013) reduced loss aversion via decreased amygdala response.

**Shiv et al. (2005):** Patients with damage to emotion-related brain areas (amygdala, OFC, insula, somatosensory cortex) invested in 84% of rounds vs. 58% for healthy controls on a gambling task where investing was always rational. Anxiety caused excessive loss aversion in healthy individuals.

## 5.9 Reasoning and Hypothesis Testing

### Wason's 2-4-6 Task (Hypothesis Testing)
Participants told "2-4-6" fits a rule; must discover the rule by generating triples and receiving yes/no feedback. Correct rule: any ascending numbers. Only 21% correct on first attempt; 28% never discovered the rule (Wason, 1960).

Common explanation: **confirmation bias** -- people only test triples consistent with their hypothesis (e.g., "even numbers increasing by 2"). More accurate label: **positive testing strategy** (not inherently biased -- optimal for narrow hypotheses, poor for broad ones like "ascending numbers").

Key: most real-world hypotheses are sparse (apply to <20% of instances), so positive testing is usually optimal. The 2-4-6 task is unusual because the target rule is very general.

**Cowley & Byrne (2005):** 62% abandoned another person's hypothesis vs. only 25% who abandoned their own -- confirmation bias is stronger for self-generated hypotheses.

**Tweney et al. (1980):** Giving participants two complementary rules (DAX triples vs. MED triples) increased success rate to >50% from 21% because positive testing of one rule constituted effective testing of the other.

**Real scientists:** Often use "confirm early, disconfirm late" (Manktelow, 2012). Bell showed confirmation bias in telephone research. Molecular biologists attributed inconsistent results to methods 88% of the time; only changed theory in 12% (Fugelsang et al., 2004). After a second replication of inconsistency, theory change rose to 61%.

## 5.10 Deductive Reasoning

### Conditional Reasoning (If-Then)
Based on **propositional logic**. Key inference forms:

| Inference Form | Form | Valid? | Acceptance Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Modus ponens (MP)** | If P then Q; P; therefore Q | Yes | Very high (~100%) |
| **Modus tollens (MT)** | If P then Q; not-Q; therefore not-P | Yes | Much lower (~60-80%) |
| **Affirming the consequent (AC)** | If P then Q; Q; therefore P | No (invalid) | Moderate -- often wrongly accepted |
| **Denying the antecedent (DA)** | If P then Q; not-P; therefore not-Q | No (invalid) | Moderate -- often wrongly accepted |

People perform far better on MP than MT. Invalid AC and DA are often accepted because people interpret "if P then Q" as "if and only if P then Q" in natural language.

**Markovits et al. (2013):** Two strategies: (1) statistical (intuitive, probability-based); (2) counterexample (demanding, logic-based). Counterexample used 49% of trials with unlimited time but only 1.7% with limited time.

### Wason Selection Task
Four cards: R, G, 2, 7. Rule: "If there is an R on one side, there is a 2 on the other." Which cards to turn?

Correct answer: **R** (to check the rule holds) and **7** (to check the rule is not violated). Most people choose R and 2. The 2 card is irrelevant (finding R on the back only says the rule might hold, not that it does hold; finding any other letter is also consistent with the rule).

**Matching bias:** people tend to select cards named in the rule regardless of logical relevance (Evans et al., 1993).

Improving performance:
- **Deontic rules** (obligations/permissions, e.g., "if you have paid, you may take the item"): Sperber & Girotto (2002) -- 68% correct vs. ~10% with standard indicative rule.
- **Personal motivation to disprove the rule:** Dawson et al. (2002) -- 38% correct for participants who believed the rule implied early death for themselves vs. 9% for non-motivated participants.

### Syllogistic Reasoning
**Syllogism:** two premises + conclusion; must assess whether conclusion follows necessarily.

**Belief bias (Klauer et al., 2000):** tendency to accept invalid but believable conclusions and reject valid but unbelievable ones. Stronger when only heuristic processing is used. Reduced by instructions emphasising logical reasoning (Evans, 2000); absent in those high in working memory capacity who use more analytic processing.

**Matching bias in syllogisms (Stupple et al., 2013):** conclusions matching premises in surface features accepted more often, regardless of validity.

**Difficulty:** Ceraso & Provitera (1971): performance improved substantially when premises were stated unambiguously (no implied converses).

## 5.11 Theories of Deductive Reasoning

### Mental Model Theory (Johnson-Laird, 1983, 2010)
People construct **mental models** (iconic representations of possibilities) from premises, generate conclusions, then search for counterexamples.

Core assumptions:
- Mental models represent what is **true** (Principle of Truth) -- false possibilities are ignored, conserving working memory.
- Problems requiring **multiple models** are harder.
- Working memory limits constrain the number of models.

Illusory inferences: When the principle of truth prevents considering what is false, people reliably draw incorrect conclusions (Legrenzi et al., 2003 -- only correct on ~10% of illusory problems vs. very high on control problems).

Evidence for WM involvement: Copeland & Radvansky (2004) -- 86% correct with one-model premises vs. 31% with three-model premises; WM capacity correlated +0.42 with reasoning performance.

**Limitations:** People rarely search for counterexamples (Newstead et al., 1999: average number of conclusions considered was 1.05-1.12 even with multiple-model problems). Theory underspecifies how background knowledge is used. Meta-analysis (Khemlani & Johnson-Laird, 2012): best predictive accuracy (95%) of 7 theories, but weak at ruling out responses people never give.

### Heuristic-Analytic Theory (Evans, 2006)
Dual-process account of reasoning:
- **Heuristic processes (System 1):** rapid; use context, task features, and background knowledge to construct a single mental model (singularity principle); most probable/relevant interpretation selected (relevance principle).
- **Analytic processes (System 2):** slow, effortful; evaluate the model; accept if adequate without full testing (satisficing principle).

Analytic processes more likely when: task instructions stress logic; participant is intelligent; sufficient time available; need to justify reasoning.

Evidence: Belief bias stronger with time pressure (Evans & Curtis-Holmes, 2005). Demanding secondary task impairs performance on conflict (belief-bias) problems but not non-conflict ones (De Neys, 2006). High WM capacity correlated with less belief bias.

**De Neys' (2012) Logical Intuition Model** (updated): heuristic and logical responses processed in parallel at the intuitive level; deliberate System 2 processes triggered only when there is a conflict. More parsimonious than serial model.

## 5.12 Informal Reasoning and Human Rationality

### Informal Reasoning
**Informal reasoning** uses knowledge, experience, and probabilities rather than binary logic. Key differences from deductive reasoning:
- Content matters (expert vs. lay source changes persuasiveness).
- Contextual factors are relevant.
- Statements are probably true, not definitely true or false.
- Goals often include persuasion, not just truth-seeking.

**Myside bias (Stanovich & West, 2007):** evaluate arguments based on consistency with prior beliefs. Extent unrelated to cognitive ability or IQ (suggesting System 2 is not routinely engaged).

**Bayesian informal reasoning (Hahn & Oaksford, 2007):** argument strength influenced by (1) prior conviction, (2) positivity vs. negativity of belief, (3) strength of evidence. A Bayesian model predicted data well. Even slippery slope arguments vary in strength depending on probability and negativity of the hypothesised outcome (Corner et al., 2011).

### Are Humans Rational?

**Evidence supporting limited rationality:**
- Base-rate neglect on taxi-cab and Linda problems, even after clarification.
- Conjunction fallacy persists with explicit instructions.
- Medical experts show biases (Redelmeier et al., 1995; Schwartz et al., 2004).
- Performance rarely improves with financial incentives (Camerer & Hogarth, 1999 -- 74 studies).
- **Dunning-Kruger effect:** less skilled individuals overestimate their abilities; incompetent people lack the metacognitive skills to recognise their own errors (Dunning, 2011). Both 0% and 100% scorers on the Wason selection task estimated 80-90% correct.

**Evidence supporting rationality:**
- Heuristics allow rapid, approximately correct judgements -- adaptive in a changing world.
- Base-rate information is used when causal structure is explicit (Krynski & Tenenbaum, 2007).
- Many "errors" disappear when frequency formats are used (Gigerenzer & Hoffrage, 1999).
- Laboratory tasks use binary logic irrelevant to everyday uncertainty.
- Accountability and social context explain some apparent biases (Tetlock, 2002).

**Bounded rationality (Simon, 1957):** "Human rational behaviour is shaped like scissors whose blades are the structure of task environments and the computational capabilities of the actor." We are as rational as our environment and our minds permit. Satisficing over maximising is adaptive.

**Stanovich's (2012) Tripartite Model:**
- **Autonomous mind (Type 1):** fast, automatic heuristics.
- **Algorithmic mind (Type 2, System 2):** contains mindware (rules, strategies); overrides Type 1 if invoked; limited by fluid intelligence.
- **Reflective mind:** monitors goals and beliefs; decides whether to engage the algorithmic mind.

**Dysrationalia** (Stanovich, 2009): intelligent people failing to think rationally because they are cognitive misers -- they have the mindware but the reflective mind does not trigger its use.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Well-defined problem** | Initial state, permissible moves, and goal state all clearly specified |
| **Ill-defined problem** | Underspecified; goal or solution path ambiguous |
| **Knowledge-lean / knowledge-rich** | Problems solvable without / only with domain expertise |
| **Insight** | Sudden reorganisation of mental representation leading to solution |
| **Incubation** | Rest period away from an unsolved problem, facilitating later solution |
| **Representational change theory** | Insight requires constraint relaxation, re-encoding, or elaboration to overcome impasse |
| **Functional fixedness** | Inability to see uses of an object beyond its typical function (Duncker candle problem) |
| **Mental set (Einstellung)** | Perseverating with a familiar but suboptimal strategy (Luchins water jars) |
| **Problem space** | All possible states of a problem (Newell and Simon) |
| **Algorithm** | Exhaustive procedure guaranteeing solution |
| **Heuristic** | Fast, frugal rule of thumb |
| **Means-ends analysis** | Sub-goal creation to reduce gap between current and goal state |
| **Hill climbing** | Move toward the goal state at each step; prone to local maxima |
| **Progress monitoring** | Assess progress rate; switch strategy if too slow |
| **Cognitive miser** | Individual who avoids effortful thinking even when capable |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Well-defined vs. ill-defined problems:** Clinical relevance -- Goel & Grafman (2000) patient PF had IQ 128 but failed everyday ill-defined problems after right prefrontal damage.

2. **Duncker's candle problem:** Classic demonstration of functional fixedness; empty box condition produced more correct solutions. Generic-parts technique (McCaffrey, 2012) reduces fixedness.

3. **Luchins' water-jar problems:** 95% of naive vs. 36% of trained participants solved the easy final problem; mental set from prior complex solutions blocked simple approach. Chess experts show same vulnerability (Bilalić et al., 2008a).

4. **Newell and Simon:** Problem space, means-ends analysis (most important heuristic), General Problem Solver; DLPFC involved in planning; means-ends analysis counterproductive in mazes designed to require moving away from goal (Sweller & Levine, 1982: only 10% solved).

5. **De Groot (1965) chess study:** Masters 91% vs. weaker players 43% for real positions; no difference for random positions -- proves stored patterns (chunks/templates), not memory ability. Chase and Simon added the chunking explanation; Gobet developed template theory.

6. **Deliberate practice (Ericsson):** Necessary but not sufficient; accounts for only ~34% of chess variance (Hambrick et al., 2014); SF's digit span increased 7 to 80 via LTM chunking of running times.

7. **London taxi drivers:** Greater posterior hippocampal volume (Woollett et al., 2009); causal evidence from Woollett & Maguire (2011); trade-off with anterior hippocampus (worse novel association learning).

8. **Representativeness heuristic and conjunction fallacy:** Linda problem -- feminist bank teller chosen over bank teller by most; incorrect even after clarification (Tversky & Kahneman, 1983). P(A and B) cannot exceed P(A).

9. **Availability heuristic:** Media-publicised deaths (murder) overestimated vs. quieter deaths (suicide) (Lichtenstein et al., 1978). Under cognitive load, famous names chosen 80% (Oppenheimer & Monin, 2009).

10. **Prospect theory core predictions:** Reference point + loss aversion; framing effect (Asian disease problem: 72% chose certain gain in gain frame, 78% chose risky option in loss frame); sunk-cost effect; certainty effect; overweighting of rare events (lottery).

11. **Loss aversion real-world evidence:** Golf putts (Pope & Schweitzer, 2011 -- par putts more likely to succeed than birdie putts; 94% of golfers show effect); poker experts; financial professionals.

12. **Wason 2-4-6 task:** Only 21% correct on first try; confirmation/positive testing bias; positive testing is usually optimal for narrow hypotheses but fails here because rule is maximally general. Performance improved with DAX/MED framing (Tweney et al., 1980).

13. **Wason selection task (R-G-2-7):** Correct answer = R and 7. Most choose R and 2 (matching bias). Improved by deontic rules (68% correct: Sperber & Girotto, 2002) and personal motivation to disprove (38% vs. 9%: Dawson et al., 2002).

14. **Conditional reasoning forms:** MP always valid, always high acceptance. MT valid, low acceptance. AC and DA invalid, but frequently accepted. Know all four names and patterns.

15. **Belief bias:** Accept invalid-believable; reject valid-unbelievable conclusions. Stronger with time pressure, lower WM capacity, high cognitive load. Reduced by logic-emphasis instructions. Right inferior frontal cortex inhibits heuristic-biased responses (Tsujii & Watanabe, 2009).

16. **Mental model theory:** Iconic representations; principle of truth; multiple models harder (WM limits); illusory inferences occur when principle of truth prevents correct inference (Legrenzi et al., 2003 -- ~10% correct on illusory problems). Best of 7 theories in meta-analysis (Khemlani & Johnson-Laird, 2012: 95% predictive accuracy).

17. **Dual-process theories applied to reasoning:** Evans' heuristic-analytic theory (singularity, relevance, satisficing principles). De Neys' logical intuition model adds parallel intuitive processing and conflict-triggered deliberate processing.

18. **System 1 vs. System 2:** Memorise defining features of both. Kahneman (2003) quotes are exam-standard definitions.

19. **Are humans rational?** Bounded rationality (Simon) -- scissors metaphor. Dysrationalia (Stanovich) -- intelligent cognitive misers. Dunning-Kruger effect -- 0% and 100% scorers both estimated 80-90% correct on Wason task. Classical logic inappropriate normative standard (Oaksford & Chater, 2009 probabilistic framework).

20. **Somatic marker / emotional factors in decision making:** Ventromedial PFC and amygdala damage -> risk-seeking. Amygdala = "cautionary brake" for loss aversion. Patients with emotion-area damage invested in 84% vs. 58% of rounds in Shiv et al. (2005) -- better performance due to reduced emotional loss aversion.
