## 2.1 Vision and the Brain

### From Eye to Cortex
- Retina contains two receptor types: **cones** (colour, detail, foveal) and **rods** (dim light, peripheral; ~125 million in outer retina).
- **Retinal ganglion cells** are the output neurons; one ganglion cell receives from few cones but hundreds of rods.
- Main pathway: **retina-geniculate-striate pathway** (retina → lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) → V1 → V2).
- **Retinotopy**: retinal receptor cells map point-to-point onto surface of visual cortex.
- At the **optic chiasma**, axons from nasal (inner) halves cross; each hemisphere receives the contralateral visual field.
- Two channels within the pathway:
  - **Parvocellular (P) pathway**: colour and fine detail; mostly cone input.
  - **Magnocellular (M) pathway**: movement; mostly rod input.

### Visual Cortical Areas (Zeki's Functional Specialisation Theory)
| Area | Primary function |
|------|-----------------|
| V1, V2 | Early processing; contain cells for colour and form |
| V3, V3A | Form (especially shapes in motion); not colour |
| V4 | Colour (also some spatial/shape); damage → **achromatopsia** |
| V5 / MT | Motion; damage → **akinetopsia** (LM case: frozen fluid in pouring) |
| MST | Motion; involved in visually guided walking |
| Inferotemporal (IT) cortex | Object and face recognition (ventral stream endpoint) |

- **Achromatopsia**: brain damage to V4 region; loss of colour perception with intact form and motion (Bouvier & Engel, 2006 meta-analysis).
- **Akinetopsia**: damage to V5/MT; stationary objects normal, motion severely impaired (Zihl et al., 1983 - patient LM).
- **Binding problem**: how features processed in separate areas (colour, form, motion) are integrated into coherent percepts. Selective attention and synchronised neural firing both contribute.
- **Lateral inhibition**: activity in one neuron inhibits neighbours; increases edge contrast (basis of simultaneous contrast illusion).

> **Key Study - Zeki (1993):** Identified functional specialisation in macaque and human visual cortex. Found V5/MT cells respond exclusively to motion direction but not colour; V4 cells respond to colour. Limitation: specialisation is less absolute than claimed; many areas respond to multiple features (Heywood & Cowey, 1999).

## 2.2 Two Visual Systems: Perception and Action (Milner & Goodale)

### Core Model
- **Milner & Goodale (1995, 2008)**: Two visual systems serving different functions.

| Feature | Ventral stream ("what" / vision-for-perception) | Dorsal stream ("how" / vision-for-action) |
|---------|------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| Terminus | Inferotemporal cortex | Posterior parietal cortex |
| Coding | **Allocentric** (object-centred) | **Egocentric** (body-centred) |
| Temporal persistence | Sustained | Short-lasting |
| Conscious access | Usually yes | Usually no |
| Speed | Slower | Faster |
| Foveal dependence | Higher | Lower |
| Associated pathway | P/parvocellular | M/magnocellular |

- **Allocentric coding**: independent of observer's viewpoint.
- **Egocentric coding**: relative to the observer's body.

### Supporting Evidence
- **Optic ataxia** (dorsal damage - posterior parietal): poor visually guided reaching/grasping despite intact visual perception (Perenin & Vighetto, 1988). Patient VK: planning intact but grasping impaired.
- **Visual form agnosia** (ventral damage): cannot identify object shapes but can guide actions accurately. Classic patient: **DF** (James et al., 2003 - lateral occipital complex damage). DF could not identify drawings but grasped objects accurately.
- **Double dissociation**: optic ataxia vs. visual form agnosia supports two-system model.

> **Key Study - Goodale & Milner (1992):** DF viewed a circular block with a slot. She could not orient a card perceptually to match the slot, yet accurately inserted the card with a hand movement. Demonstrates intact dorsal (action) stream with impaired ventral (perception) stream.

### Visual Illusions and the Two Systems
- Milner & Goodale predicted action-based responses (pointing, grasping) should be largely immune to visual illusions processed by the vision-for-perception system.
- Bruno et al. (2008) meta-analysis: Muller-Lyer illusion effect = 22.4% with perceptual tasks, only 5.5% with rapid pointing (action task). Supports two-systems model.
- **Hollow-face illusion** (Króliczak et al., 2006): strong illusion effect when drawing target position (perception); near-zero with rapid flicking movement (action).
- Caveat: grasping (unlike pointing) shows larger illusion effects (~9-11%) because grasping involves planning (ventral stream contribution).

### Limitations of Two-Systems Model
1. Two systems interact extensively; not truly independent (Cloutman, 2013).
2. Visually guided action requires ventral stream when memory, planning, or object knowledge is needed.
3. Double dissociation not clean: DF has some action deficits; optic ataxia patients can have perceptual impairments too (Pisella et al., 2009; Himmelbach et al., 2012).

## 2.3 Colour Vision

### Trichromacy Theory
- Three cone types: short-wavelength (blue), medium-wavelength (yellow-green), long-wavelength (orange-red).
- Most colours seen by differential activation of cone types.
- **Dichromacy**: missing one cone class. Types: **deuteranopia** (green cones missing), **protanopia** (red cones missing), **tritanopia** (blue cones missing).

### Opponent-Process Theory (Hering, 1878)
- Three opponent channels: **red-green**, **blue-yellow**, **achromatic (black-white)**.
- Explains **negative afterimages** (prolonged red viewing → green afterimage) and some forms of colour deficiency.

### Dual-Process Theory (Hurvich & Jameson, 1957)
- Combines trichromacy and opponent processes. Cone signals feed into opponent channels. The three channels: achromatic, blue-yellow, red-green.

### Colour Constancy
- **Colour constancy**: tendency to perceive an object's colour as stable despite changes in illumination.
- Factors contributing to constancy:
  - **Local colour contrast** (Land's retinex theory, 1986): compare wavelength reflectance of adjacent surfaces.
  - **Cone-excitation ratios** (Foster & Nascimento, 1994): ratio of cone responses stays nearly invariant across illuminant changes.
  - **Chromatic adaptation**: visual sensitivity decreases to a given illuminant over time.
  - **Object familiarity**: Hansen et al. (2006) - bananas look yellowish even when physically grey.
- V4 neurons in monkeys respond to actual surface colour rather than to reflected wavelengths (Zeki, 1983; Kusunoki et al., 2006 - colour constancy neurons).

## 2.4 Depth Perception

### Monocular (Pictorial) Cues
| Cue | Mechanism |
|-----|-----------|
| **Linear perspective** | Parallel lines appear to converge with distance |
| **Texture gradient** | Rate of change of texture density with slant (Gibson, 1979) |
| **Shading** | Light/shadow indicates 3D form |
| **Interposition** | Nearer object occludes farther one |
| **Familiar size** | Known object size informs distance estimate |
| **Blur** | Blur increases away from fixation point |
| **Motion parallax** | Near objects move opposite/fast; far objects same/slow with head movement |

### Binocular and Oculomotor Cues
- **Vergence**: eyes turn inward more for close objects.
- **Accommodation**: lens thickens to focus on close objects.
- **Binocular disparity**: slight difference in images from two eyes; produces **stereopsis** (binocular depth). Very powerful close up; diminishes rapidly beyond 20 m.

### Cue Integration
- Jacobs (2002): reliable cues weighted more heavily; weights adjust flexibly.
- Small cue conflicts → additive combination; large conflicts → selection of one dominant cue.

### Size Constancy
- Objects perceived as same size despite changing retinal image.
- Perceived distance is the main driver (Ames room illusion: perceived distance distorted → size distorted).
- **Body size effect** (Van der Hoort et al., 2011): perceived body size influences apparent object sizes.

## 2.5 Perception Without Awareness

### Blindsight
- **Blindsight**: ability to perform visual tasks in the absence of conscious awareness in patients with V1 damage.
- Classic patient: **DB** (Weiskrantz) - could detect and locate objects, discriminate motion and orientation, reported NO conscious experience; confirmed with forced-choice tests.
- Brain regions mediating blindsight: **superior colliculus**, lateral geniculate nucleus projections to BA18/19, bypassing V1.
- Sub-types (Danckert & Rossetti, 2005):
  1. **Action-blindsight**: grasping/pointing in blind field (dorsal stream).
  2. **Attention-blindsight**: vague awareness of presence; some dorsal stream use.
  3. **Agnosopsia** (Type 1): no awareness; some form/wavelength discrimination; ventral stream partial use.
- Ko & Lau (2012): blindsight patients may over-conservatively deny awareness due to prefrontal damage affecting detection criterion.

### Subliminal Perception
- **Subjective threshold**: patient denies seeing.
- **Objective threshold**: chance-level forced-choice performance.
- Naccache et al. (2002): masked digits (29 ms) influenced response times to visible target digits; no conscious awareness of primes.
- Sandberg et al. (2010): **Perceptual Awareness Scale** most sensitive measure; perceptual awareness is often graded.
- Neuroimaging: face/house identity decodable from brain patterns even for unseen stimuli (Rees, 2007 - ~90% accuracy).

## 2.6 Pattern Recognition and Perceptual Organisation (Ch 3)

### Pattern Recognition Theories
- **Template theory**: percept matched to stored exact template. Limitation: inflexible to variation in size/orientation.
- **Feature theory**: stimuli represented by sets of features (e.g., "A" = two straight lines + crossbar). Feature detectors (Hubel & Wiesel, 1962): simple cells (bar/edge orientation), complex cells (moving contours), end-stopped cells (length-sensitive).
- **Prototype theory**: stimuli compared to average or idealised category member. More flexible than templates.
- **Navon (1977)** global precedence: global structure (forest) processed before local features (trees). Low spatial frequencies (coarse, magnocellular, fast) precede high spatial frequencies (fine, parvocellular, slow).

### Gestalt Laws of Perceptual Organisation
- **Law of Pragnanz** (Prägnanz): simplest possible perceptual organisation is perceived.
- **Proximity**: elements close together are grouped.
- **Similarity**: similar elements are grouped.
- **Good continuation**: elements forming smooth or straight lines are grouped.
- **Closure**: missing parts of a figure are filled in.
- **Uniform connectedness** (Palmer & Rock, 1994): connected regions with uniform properties form single units. Dominates over proximity and similarity.
- **Figure-ground segregation**: figure = distinct form, in front, contour belongs to figure. Convex, small, surrounded, symmetrical regions tend to be figure.
- Limitation: Gestaltists underestimated learning/experience (Barense et al., 2012 - amnesic patients show no familiarity effect in figure-ground). Bayesian approaches (Kersten et al., 2004) better account for role of prior knowledge.

## 2.7 Object Recognition

### Marr's Computational Theory (1982)
Three successive representations:
1. **Primal sketch**: 2D description of intensity changes, edges, contours, blobs.
2. **2.5-D sketch**: depth and orientation of visible surfaces (observer-centred/viewpoint-dependent).
3. **3-D model representation**: object-centred, viewpoint-invariant.

Influence: inspired computational modelling; distinguished viewpoint-dependent vs. viewpoint-invariant representations.
Limitation: no one has generated 3-D models as stipulated; too computationally demanding; underemphasises top-down processes.

### Biederman's Recognition-by-Components (RBC) Theory (1987)
- Objects decomposed into ~36 **geons** (geometric ions): basic shapes (blocks, cylinders, spheres, arcs, wedges).
- Geons identified via five **non-accidental properties** of edges: **curvature, parallel edges, cotermination, symmetry, collinearity**. These are invariant across viewpoints.
- Geons + spatial relations → matched to stored structural models.
- Predicts: object recognition is **viewpoint-invariant**.
- Biederman & Gerhardstein (1993): 135-degree rotation produced equal priming as same view (for familiar objects).

> **Key Study - Biederman (1987):** Identified geons as the structural primitives of object recognition. A cup = arc (handle) connected to side of cylinder. Predicted viewpoint-invariance via stable geon representations.

Limitations:
1. Viewpoint-invariance applies to categorisation, not identification (Hamm & McMullen, 1998 - identification is viewer-centred).
2. Edge-based extraction insufficient in cluttered scenes; surface/colour information matters (Sanocki et al., 1998).
3. Too bottom-up; top-down processes (orbitofrontal, Bar et al., 2006) important, especially for degraded stimuli.

### Inferotemporal (IT) Cortex
- Crucial for object recognition; activation correlates 90% with monkey's perception vs. 20% for V1 (Blake & Logothetis, 2002).
- Some IT neurons: high **selectivity** (respond to one object), high **invariance/tolerance** (respond regardless of orientation, size).
- Quiroga et al. (2005): single medial temporal lobe neuron responded to Jennifer Aniston but not her with Brad Pitt. Interpreted as sparse population coding.

## 2.8 Face Recognition

### Face vs. Object Processing
- Face recognition involves **holistic processing**: integrating whole-face configuration, not feature by feature; faster and more parallel.
- **Face inversion effect**: faces much harder upside-down; effect smaller for other objects (McKone et al., 2007). Evidence for holistic face-specific processing.
- **Part-whole effect** (Farah, 1994): face parts recognised better in whole-face context than in isolation; not found for houses.
- **Composite face effect** (Richler et al., 2011): can't ignore mismatched bottom half when judging top half; shows holistic integration.
- **Expertise hypothesis** (Gauthier & Tarr, 2002): holistic processing generalises to any domain of expertise; "fusiform expertise area" not face-specific.

### Prosopagnosia
- **Prosopagnosia**: acquired or developmental inability to recognise faces ("face blindness"); object recognition largely intact.
- Patient GG (Busigny et al., 2010a, 2010b): normal performance on birds, boats, cars, chairs; severely impaired only on faces. Confirms face-specific deficit.
- Prosopagnosics fail face inversion and composite face effects (GG, Van Belle et al., 2011) - deficient holistic processing.
- **Covert recognition**: PS (Simon et al., 2011) showed no conscious recognition of familiar faces but had fusiform face area activation to them.

> **Key Study - Bruce & Young (1986) Model:** Eight-component sequential model of face processing. Structural encoding → face recognition units → person identity nodes → name generation. Predicts: (1) familiarity accessed before occupation before name; (2) facial identity and expression processed via separate routes. Young et al. (1985) diary study: 1,008 face-recognition failures - never named a face without knowing anything else.

### Fusiform Face Area (FFA)
- In ventral temporal cortex; typically more activated for faces than all other object categories (Downing et al., 2006).
- Damaged in most prosopagnosia; but occipital face area (OFA) also often damaged (Gainotti & Marra, 2011 - face network, not single area).
- FFA also activated by objects of expertise (Bilalić et al., 2010 - chess experts; McGugin et al., 2012 - car expertise).

## 2.9 Visual Imagery

### Kosslyn's Perceptual Anticipation Theory
- Visual images are **depictive representations** organised spatially like percepts, formed in early visual cortex (**visual buffer** in BA17/18).
- Images depend on top-down activation from long-term memory (shapes: inferior temporal lobe; spatial: posterior parietal cortex).
- Neuroimaging: imagery activates ~2/3 of areas activated by perception (Kosslyn & Thompson, 2003); facilitatory/interfering effects between imagery and perception tasks (Pearson et al., 2008 - binocular rivalry; Baddeley & Andrade, 2000 - dual-task interference).

### Pylyshyn's Propositional Theory
- Imagery involves **tacit knowledge** (inaccessible to awareness), not depictive/pictorial representations. No need for early visual cortex activation.

### Evidence
- **Similar to perception**: Ganis et al. (2004) - extensive overlap in frontal and parietal activation for imagery and perception.
- **Different from perception**: S.-H. Lee et al. (2012) - imagined objects not classifiable in V1; perceived objects classifiable throughout visual hierarchy. Brain-damaged patients show double dissociations: some with intact perception but impaired imagery (left temporal damage), others with impaired perception but intact imagery (V1 damage, Anton's syndrome).
- Slezak (1991, 1995): observers could not see new objects when mentally rotating images, but could when drawing from memory - images less flexible than percepts.

## 2.10 Direct Perception (Gibson)

### Core Ideas (Ch 4)
- **Gibson (1950, 1966, 1979)**: ecological approach. Perception is direct, not mediated by representations or inference.
- **Optic array**: structural pattern of light reaching the eye from the whole environment.
- **Optic flow**: changes in the optic array caused by observer movement or environmental movement. Provides information about **heading**.
- **Focus of expansion**: the point toward which an observer moves appears stationary; surroundings appear to expand away from it.
- **Invariants**: properties of the optic array remaining constant as observer moves. Allow reliable perception.
- **Affordances**: potential uses of objects perceived directly (e.g., ladder affords climbing). Gibson (1979): affordances exist in environment; don't require mental mediation.
- **Tau** (Lee, 1976): retinal image size / rate of expansion = time to contact with approaching object. Drivers brake to hold tau-dot constant.

> **Key Study - Lee (1976):** Proposed tau as the optical variable specifying time to contact. Savelsbergh et al. (1993) - deflating ball shifts peak grasp closure by 30 ms (theory predicted 230 ms); tau is used but not exclusively.

Limitations: optic flow insufficient for curved paths; ignores top-down and stored knowledge; dismisses internal representations.

## 2.11 Planning-Control Model (Glover, 2004)

Two sequential (often overlapping) systems for motor action:

| Feature | Planning system | Control system |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| Timing | Before movement initiation | During movement execution |
| Information used | Goals, context, object identity, cognitive processes | Spatial properties (size, shape, orientation) only |
| Speed | Slow | Fast |
| Conscious | Yes | No |
| Brain area | Inferior parietal lobe, prefrontal cortex, basal ganglia | Superior parietal lobe, cerebellum |
| Visual illusions | Influenced | Not influenced |

- **Ideomotor apraxia**: inferior parietal damage; planning deficits.
- **Optic ataxia**: superior parietal damage; control deficits (patient IG - OK with stationary targets, fails when target jumps location).
- Glover et al. (2012) neuroimaging: practically no overlap between planning and control brain networks.

## 2.12 Biological Motion and Mirror Neurons

### Biological Motion
- **Johansson (1973)**: **point-light displays** (lights on actors' joints in darkness) perceived as human motion within 200 ms with just 6 lights; gender identifiable 85% of the time (Runeson & Frykholm, 1983).
- Partly innate: newborns (1-3 days) prefer biological over non-biological motion point-light displays (Simion et al., 2008 - even chicken displays).
- **Superior temporal sulcus (STS)**: key area for biological motion. rTMS to STS reduces biological motion sensitivity (Grossman et al., 2005). Grey matter volume in STS correlates with biological motion but not non-biological motion (Gilaie-Dotan et al., 2013a).
- Why biological motion is special: only motion type humans can both perceive and produce; heavy social use; rich emotional information.

### Mirror Neuron System
- Discovered by Gallese et al. (1996): ~17% of F5 premotor neurons in monkeys fired both when monkey performed an action AND when it observed the same action.
- Human mirror neuron areas: **inferior frontal gyrus (IFG)** and **inferior parietal lobule (IPL)** (Molenberghs et al., 2012a meta-analysis).
- TMS to mirror neuron areas impairs imitation (Mengotti et al., 2013).
- Umiltà et al. (2001): mirror neurons fired even when goal of action was hidden (but known from context). Meaning-sensitive.
- Caveat (Williams, 2013): mirror neurons reflect action understanding, not motor copying; complex action understanding also involves prefrontal cortex (Lingnau & Petris, 2013).

## 2.13 Change Blindness and Inattentional Blindness

- **Change blindness**: "surprising failure to detect a substantial visual change" (Jensen et al., 2011).
- **Inattentional blindness**: failure to notice an unexpected, fully visible object when attention is occupied.

> **Key Study - Simons & Chabris (1999):** Gorilla study. Observers counted ball passes; 50% failed to notice a gorilla on screen for 9 seconds. Detection improved to 83% when attended team's colour matched gorilla (black).

- **Change blindness blindness**: observers overestimate their ability to detect changes (Levin et al., 2002 - 78% thought they'd notice a disappearing scarf; none did).
- Five processes required for change detection: (1) attention to location, (2) encode pre-change, (3) encode post-change, (4) compare representations, (5) conscious recognition of discrepancy.
- Changes are typically concealed by: saccades, temporal gaps, or eyeblinks (eliminate motion transient).
- Hollingworth & Henderson (2002): fixating the to-be-changed object is necessary but not sufficient (60% of fixated objects still missed).
- **Inattentional blindness causes**: attentional set (similar-to-task objects more likely detected), processing resources (Richards et al., 2012 - high working memory capacity → less inattentional blindness).

## 2.14 Focused Auditory Attention

### Dichotic Listening and the Cocktail Party Problem
- **Cherry (1953)**: dichotic listening task; shadowing one ear. Unattended channel: physical changes (tone) detected; language, reverse speech unnoticed. Cocktail party solved via physical cues (voice sex, intensity, location).
- **Moray (1959)**: own name detected on unattended channel (special significance). Little memory for 35-repetition unattended words.

### Models (Early vs. Late Selection)
| Model | Theorist | Filter location | Unattended processing |
|-------|----------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Filter model** | Broadbent (1958) | Early (physical features) | Almost none |
| **Attenuation model** | Treisman (1964) | Flexible; physical → semantic | Graded; high-importance words pass |
| **Late selection** | Deutsch & Deutsch (1963) | Late (after full analysis) | All stimuli fully analysed; importance determines response |

- Evidence supports Treisman's middle position: unattended words do receive some semantic processing (Bentin et al., 1995 - ERP evidence) but less than attended words.
- Li et al. (2011): weight-related words on unattended channel caused more shadowing errors in women dissatisfied with their weight - meaning modulates unattended processing.

## 2.15 Focused Visual Attention

### Spotlight, Zoom Lens, Multiple Spotlights
- **Spotlight model** (Posner, 1980): attention illuminates a small area; can be directed anywhere.
- **Zoom-lens model** (Eriksen & St James, 1986): attentional area adjustable in size; confirmed by LaBerge (1983) probe study and Müller et al. (2003) neuroimaging.
- **Multiple spotlights** (Awh & Pashler, 2000): split attention to non-adjacent areas; supported by brain imaging showing dual activation peaks with suppression in between.

### Space-Based vs. Object-Based Attention
- **Object-based attention**: O'Craven et al. (1999) - attended face activates face areas more even when spatially overlapping with house.
- **Egly et al. (1994)**: within-object invalid cues produce faster detection than between-object invalid cues equidistant from target.
- **Inhibition of return**: reduced probability of re-attending recently attended location/object.
- Space-based and object-based attention coexist and interact (Hollingworth et al., 2012).

### Dorsal vs. Ventral Attention Networks (Corbetta & Shulman, 2002)
| Network | Type | Brain areas | Function |
|---------|------|-------------|----------|
| **Dorsal** | Top-down, goal-directed | Superior parietal lobule, intraparietal sulcus, frontal eye field | Voluntary, endogenous, expectation-based |
| **Ventral** | Bottom-up, stimulus-driven | Temporo-parietal junction, inferior frontal gyrus, supramarginal gyrus (right hemisphere) | "Circuit-breaking"; unexpected salient/task-relevant stimuli |

- TMS to right temporo-parietal junction impairs stimulus-driven (ventral) attention (Chica et al., 2011).
- Neglect patients: damage to ventral network disrupts both networks (right hemisphere bias).

## 2.16 Disorders of Visual Attention

### Neglect and Extinction
- **Neglect (spatial neglect)**: unawareness of stimuli on contralesional side (typically left visual field, right-hemisphere damage). Types:
  - **Egocentric/subject-centred neglect**: neglect of left visual field.
  - **Allocentric/object-centred neglect**: neglect of left side of each object regardless of position.
- **Extinction**: failure to detect contralesional stimulus when ipsilesional stimulus simultaneously present. Brain areas: temporo-parietal junction, intraparietal sulcus.
- Both neglect and extinction: unattended stimuli still receive some processing (McGlinchey-Berroth et al., 1993 - semantic priming from left-field words in neglect patients).
- Reducing neglect: alertness training (Thimm et al., 2009); prism adaptation (Rossetti et al., 1998) - 10-degree rightward prisms, then correction reduces neglect.

### Balint's Syndrome
- **Simultanagnosia**: inability to perceive more than one object at a time.
- **Optic ataxia**: poor visually guided reaching (see section 2.2).
- **Ocular apraxia**: difficulty directing eye movements voluntarily.

## 2.17 Visual Search

### Feature Integration Theory (Treisman & Gelade, 1980)
- **Stage 1**: preattentive, parallel processing of basic features (colour, size, line orientation) across the visual field.
- **Stage 2**: serial, attentional processing to bind features into objects. Attention = "glue" binding features.
- **Feature search** (e.g., blue letter in red letters): fast, flat set-size function = **pop-out** (parallel).
- **Conjunction search** (e.g., blue letter T among red Ts and blue Ls): slow, steep set-size function (serial).
- **Illusory conjunction**: without focused attention, features from different objects combine randomly (Friedman-Hill et al., 1995 - patient with spatial localisation problems produced many illusory conjunctions).

> **Key Study - Treisman & Gelade (1980):** Feature targets pop out in parallel regardless of display size (slope ~0 ms/item); conjunction targets require serial search (slope ~20-40 ms/item). Established distinction between preattentive and attentive processing.

### Beyond Feature Integration Theory
- Duncan & Humphreys (1989): search also depends on target-distractor similarity and distractor-distractor similarity.
- Thornton & Gilden (2007): 72% of visual search tasks involve parallel processing; serial only for complex tasks.
- **Texture tiling model** (Rosenholtz et al., 2012): information in peripheral vision determines search difficulty, not attention per se. Central vision clear; periphery represented as texture tiles.
- **Dual-path model** (Wolfe et al., 2011): selective pathway (capacity-limited, object recognition) + non-selective pathway (gist of scene guides search). Real-world search efficient because scene knowledge reduces functional set size.

## 2.18 Divided Attention and Automatic Processing

### Dual-Task Performance
- Task similarity is key: same modality (Treisman & Davies, 1973) or same response modality (McLeod, 1977) → more interference.
- **Multiple-resource theory** (Wickens, 1984/2008): independent processing resources (modality, processing stage, processing code, response type). Tasks use different resources → better time-sharing.
- **Threaded cognition** (Salvucci & Taatgen, 2008): multiple goal-threads run simultaneously; competition when threads need same resource; no central executive (greedy/polite resource claiming).
- Practice (Spelke et al., 1976 - Diane and John): extensive training can allow near-perfect dual-task performance; may reflect automation of component processes.

### Automatic vs. Controlled Processing
- **Automatic processing**: fast, parallel, not capacity-limited, triggered by familiar stimuli, not requiring conscious attention, hard to modify once learned.
- **Controlled processing**: slow, serial, capacity-limited, requires attention, flexible and modifiable.
- **Stroop effect** (Stroop, 1935): naming ink colour is slowed when the colour word names a different colour (e.g., RED in blue ink). Reading is automatic; colour naming is controlled. Interference occurs because automatic reading produces a competing response.
- Practice → controlled processes become automatic (e.g., learning to drive a car).

### Load Theory (Lavie, 2005, 2010)
- **High perceptual load** reduces distractor processing (processing resources fully occupied by task).
- **High cognitive load** (working memory demands) increases susceptibility to distraction (reduces cognitive control).
- Limitation: perceptual load effects not found in auditory modality; human faces not ignored even at high load.

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Ventral vs. dorsal stream**: "what" vs. "how/where"; inferotemporal vs. posterior parietal; allocentric vs. egocentric coding; ventral = sustained, conscious; dorsal = transient, unconscious. Optic ataxia = dorsal damage; visual form agnosia = ventral damage.

2. **Milner & Goodale two-systems model**: the best evidence is the double dissociation between DF (visual form agnosia: no object recognition but accurate grasping) and optic ataxia patients (intact perception but impaired reaching). Illusions affect perception (22%) more than pointing (5.5%).

3. **Colour vision**: trichromacy (3 cone types) + opponent processes (3 channels: R-G, B-Y, achromatic) = dual-process theory. Negative afterimages explained by opponent processes. Achromatopsia = V4 damage. Colour constancy: cone-excitation ratios are key; chromatic adaptation and familiarity also contribute.

4. **Marr vs. Biederman**: Marr (1982): primal sketch → 2.5-D → 3-D model (observer-centred to object-centred). Biederman (1987): geons as structural primitives; non-accidental edge properties; predicts viewpoint-invariance (supported for categorisation, not identification).

5. **Gestalt laws**: Pragnanz (simplicity), proximity, similarity, good continuation, closure, figure-ground, uniform connectedness (Palmer & Rock). Limitations: underestimated learning; mostly 2D stimuli; no adequate explanations.

6. **Face recognition - Bruce & Young model**: familiarity → occupation → name (never name without other info). Separate routes for identity and expression. Prosopagnosia = impaired holistic processing; FFA usually damaged. Expertise hypothesis (Gauthier & Tarr): FFA = "fusiform expertise area."

7. **Focused auditory attention**: Broadbent (early filter, physical features) vs. Treisman (attenuation, flexible) vs. Deutsch-Deutsch (late selection, full analysis). Evidence favours middle ground: some semantic processing of unattended channel, less than attended; own name detected. Cocktail party solved by physical cues + top-down.

8. **Feature integration theory**: preattentive parallel feature processing → attentive serial binding. Feature search = pop-out (flat slope); conjunction search = serial (steep slope). Illusory conjunctions occur without attention.

9. **Change blindness vs. inattentional blindness**: Change blindness involves memory comparison; inattentional blindness is attention-based (task-irrelevant unexpected object). Gorilla study (50% miss 9-second gorilla). Five processes needed for change detection; any failure → blindness.

10. **Neglect and extinction**: Neglect = right-hemisphere damage, left visual field ignored; egocentric and allocentric forms. Extinction = contralesional stimulus not detected when ipsilesional stimulus present. Both: some unconscious processing preserved. Corbetta & Shulman: ventral attention network damaged; dorsal network intact but under-resourced.

11. **Visual search**: Feature vs. conjunction search. Texture tiling model - peripheral information more important than previously thought. Dual-path model - scene knowledge (gist) guides search by reducing functional set size.

12. **Automatic vs. controlled processing**: Stroop effect as classic example of automation. Practice converts controlled to automatic. Load theory: high perceptual load reduces distractor processing automatically; high cognitive load reduces cognitive control → more distraction.

13. **Biological motion and mirror neurons**: Johansson (1973) point-light displays. STS crucial for biological motion. Mirror neurons: IFG and IPL; fire for both performing and observing actions; support imitation and action understanding. Exaggerated claims: complex goal understanding requires prefrontal cortex.

14. **Planning-control model (Glover, 2004)**: Planning (inferior parietal, slow, context-sensitive, cognitive) precedes control (superior parietal, fast, spatial properties only). Explains visual illusion immunity in online control but not planning. Glover et al. (2012): virtually no overlap in brain networks.

15. **Gibson's direct perception**: optic flow, focus of expansion, invariants, affordances, tau. Radical anti-representationist stance largely incorrect - top-down and memory processes are essential, especially in complex/degraded environments.
