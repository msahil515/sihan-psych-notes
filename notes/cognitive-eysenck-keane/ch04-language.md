## 9.1 Reading: Word Recognition

### Phonological Processing in Reading
- **Strong phonological model** (Frost, 1998): phonological representation is a mandatory, automatic product of reading. Supported by masked phonological priming studies (Rastle & Brysbaert, 2006 meta-analysis).
- **Weak phonological model** (Coltheart et al., 2001): phonological processing is slow and not essential for word identification.
- Evidence for strong model: Van Orden (1987) showed readers accept "ROWS" as a flower because it is homophonic with "rose" (phonological mediation). TMS to supramarginal gyrus at 80-120 ms disrupts phonological task (Sliwinska et al., 2012), proving phonology begins fast enough to influence recognition.
- Evidence phonology is not essential: patient PS could define printed words accurately despite failing to produce correct phonology (Hanley & McDonnell, 1997).

### Interactive Activation Model (McClelland & Rumelhart, 1981)
- Three levels: **feature level** (bottom), **letter level** (middle), **word level** (top).
- Bottom-up activation: detected feature activates letter units containing it; identified letter activates word units containing it in that position.
- Top-down activation: activated word units send activation back down to their letter-level units (feedback loop).
- Accounts for the **word superiority effect**: target letter detected more accurately when the string forms a word than when it does not, because top-down word-to-letter activation boosts the correct letter (~200 ms after word onset; Martin et al., 2006).
- **Orthographic neighbours** (words differing by one letter from target): facilitate recognition if less frequent than target, inhibit if more frequent (Chen & Mirman, 2012).

> **Key Study:** McClelland & Rumelhart (1981) - Interactive activation model showing how parallel bottom-up and top-down processes interact at feature, letter, and word levels to explain the word superiority effect and neighbourhood effects.

**Limitations:** ignores meaning; ignores phonology; over-rigid letter-position assignment (contradicted by the "Cambridge email" transposition reading); built only for four-letter words.

### Semantic Priming
- **Semantic priming**: prior semantically related word (prime) speeds recognition of target (Meyer & Schvaneveldt, 1971; e.g., NURSE primes DOCTOR).
- Two mechanisms: rapid automatic spreading activation (facilitatory, short-lived) AND slower controlled expectancy-based effects (Neely, 1977).
- Context can influence word processing within 200 ms, predicting upcoming words (DeLong et al., 2005 using N400 ERP: article "an" before unpredictable noun showed larger N400 than "a" before predictable noun).
- **N400**: negative ERP component peaking ~400 ms; larger when word meaning mismatches context.

## 9.2 Reading Aloud: Dual-Route Cascaded Model (DRC)

### Architecture (Coltheart et al., 2001)
Both routes start with **orthographic analysis** (letter/word identification).

| Route | Name | Process | Clinical Pattern |
|-------|------|---------|-----------------|
| Route 1 | Non-lexical | **Grapheme-phoneme conversion** (GPC rules) | Used by **surface dyslexics** |
| Route 2 | Lexical + semantic | Orthographic input lexicon → semantic system → phonological output lexicon | Used by **phonological dyslexics** |
| Route 3 | Lexical only | Orthographic input lexicon → phonological output lexicon (bypasses semantics) | Also phonological dyslexic route |

- **Cascade**: activation passes from one level to the next before processing is complete (unlike threshold models).
- Computational model tested on 7,981 one-syllable words: 99% accuracy; 7,000 non-words: 98.9% accuracy.

### Reading Disorders
| Disorder | Core Problem | Pattern |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Surface dyslexia** | Damaged Routes 2/3; relies on Route 1 | Reads regular words well; **regularisation errors** on irregular words (e.g., PINT → "pint to rhyme with hint"); reads non-words well. Patient KT: 100% non-words, 81% regular, 41% irregular (McCarthy & Warrington, 1984). |
| **Phonological dyslexia** | Damaged Route 1 | Reads familiar words (regular and irregular) >90%; very poor on non-words (<60%). |
| **Deep dyslexia** | Routes 1 impaired + right-hemisphere reading | Cannot read non-words; makes **semantic reading errors** (e.g., reads "ship" as "boat"); DRC places this outside its scope; linked to right-hemisphere reading (patient NI after left hemispherectomy: Patterson et al., 1989). |

> **Key Study:** Coltheart et al. (2001) DRC model - computational implementation successfully simulating surface dyslexia, phonological dyslexia, and the speed advantages of the lexical route for words vs. the serial length-dependent non-lexical route for non-words.

### Connectionist Triangle Model (Plaut et al., 1996; Harm & Seidenberg, 2004)
- Three interacting systems: **orthography** (spelling), **phonology** (sound), **semantics** (meaning).
- Two pathways from spelling to sound: direct orthography-to-phonology; and indirect via semantics.
- Key concept: **consistency** (not regularity) determines speed. Consistent words/non-words (neighbours always share pronunciation) are named faster than inconsistent ones.
- Surface dyslexia attributed to semantic system damage; phonological dyslexia to general phonological impairment.
- Jared (2002): naming times affected more by consistency than regularity, supporting triangle model over DRC.
- Neuroimaging (Taylor et al., 2013 meta-analysis): words > non-words in anterior fusiform gyrus, angular/middle temporal gyri (lexicon + semantics); non-words > words in posterior fusiform, inferior parietal, inferior frontal gyrus (GPC areas).

## 9.3 Eye-Movement Research in Reading

### Basic Facts
- Eyes move in rapid jerks: **saccades** (20-30 ms, ~8 letters long).
- **Fixations** last 200-250 ms; information extracted only during fixations.
- **Regressions** (eyes moving backwards): 10% of saccades.
- Content words fixated 80% of the time; function words only 20%.
- **Perceptual span**: 3-4 letters left of fixation, up to 15 letters right (in English).
- **Spillover effect**: fixation on a word is longer when preceded by a rare word.

### E-Z Reader Model (Reichle et al., 1998; Rayner et al., 2012)
Six assumptions:
1. Readers check **familiarity** (word frequency) of currently fixated word.
2. Completion of frequency check signals initiation of **eye-movement programme** to next word.
3. Second stage of lexical access: semantic + phonological form retrieved.
4. Completion of stage 2 signals **covert attention shift** to next word.
5. Both stages faster for **common** words than rare ones.
6. Both stages faster for **predictable** words in context.

- Explains spillover: rare word leaves less time for parafoveal preview of next word.
- Explains word-skipping: common/predictable words whose lexical access completes during prior fixation are skipped.
- Key finding: word frequency influences brain activity within 110 ms (Reichle et al., 2011 ERPs).
- Debate: **SWIFT model** (Engbert et al., 2005) argues for parallel processing. Evidence of **parafoveal-on-foveal effects** (characteristics of next word affect fixation on current word) is inconsistent with strict serial E-Z Reader, but effects are small (Schotter et al., 2012).

> **Key Study:** Reichle et al. (1998) E-Z Reader model - two-stage lexical access model explaining how word frequency and predictability control fixation durations and word skipping during reading.

## 9.4 Speech Perception: Core Problems

Three classic problems listeners face:
1. **Segmentation**: speech is nearly continuous with few silences; hard to identify word boundaries. (Lieberman 1963: words spliced out of sentences recognised only 50% of time.)
2. **Coarticulation**: production of each phoneme influenced by preceding and following phonemes, so the acoustic signal for the same phoneme varies by context.
3. **Invariance problem**: same word can be pronounced very differently across speakers, rates, and contexts.

### Mattys et al. (2005) Hierarchical Cue Model
Three tiers (listeners prefer highest available):
- **Tier 1 (lexical)**: syntax, word knowledge.
- **Tier 2 (segmental)**: coarticulation, allophony.
- **Tier 3 (metrical)**: syllable stress (initial syllable stressed in most English content words).

## 9.5 Categorical Perception
- Sounds intermediate between two phonemes are perceived as one or the other, not as intermediate: **categorical perception**.
- Boundary between phoneme categories is sharp; within-category differences are minimised.
- Not unique to speech: musicians show categorical perception for musical chords (Locke & Kellar, 1973), suggesting it reflects domain expertise.
- Brain: differences in activation between the two stimuli in a pair amplified when they cross the phoneme boundary (Raizada & Poldrack, 2007).

---

## 9.6 McGurk Effect
- McGurk & MacDonald (1976): video of lips saying "ba" + audio saying "ga" = listeners hear "da" (fusion/blending of visual and auditory signals).
- Demonstrates that speech perception integrates **audiovisual information**.
- Top-down context modulates the effect: stronger when the target word is in a semantically congruent sentence (Windmann, 2004).
- Not entirely automatic: found even when listeners are aware of temporal mismatch (Soto-Faraco & Alsius, 2009).

## 9.7 Context Effects in Speech Perception

### Phonemic Restoration Effect
- Warren & Warren (1970): phoneme replaced by a cough is "restored" by sentence context. Listeners report hearing the appropriate word (wheel/heel/meal/peel depending on sentence ending).
- Two processes: **sensory repair** (unconscious auditory cortex suppression of noise onset/offset) and **subjective continuity** (top-down expectation; Shahin et al., 2009, 2012).

### Ganong Effect
- Ganong (1980): ambiguous initial phoneme is categorised as whichever phoneme produces a real word (lexical identification shift).
- Both perceptual (superior temporal gyri) and post-perceptual decision processes (frontal/midline) involved (Myers & Blumstein, 2008).

### Interactionist vs. Autonomous Accounts
- **Autonomous**: context only affects post-perceptual decision stages; no effect on primary auditory cortex.
- **Interactionist**: context can influence early acoustic processing, including primary auditory cortex.
- Evidence favours **interactionist** position especially for degraded speech (Wild et al., 2012; Sohoglu et al., 2014). Context effects are smaller with clear, unambiguous speech.

## 9.8 Theories of Speech Perception

### Motor Theory (Liberman et al., 1967)
- Listeners recover the speaker's **articulatory gestures** (not the acoustic signal directly); motor mimicry provides a more stable signal.
- Links to **mirror neuron system**: same neurons fire for performing and observing an action.
- TMS to motor lip area disrupts speech perception (Möttönen et al., 2013).
- Motor areas facilitate speech perception mainly under **demanding/noisy conditions** (D'Ausilio et al., 2012).
- Limitation: patients with Broca's aphasia (motor speech impaired) can still perceive speech accurately (Hickok et al., 2011); left-hemisphere anaesthesia abolishing motor speech did not abolish word-picture matching (Hickok et al., 2008).
- Dual-stream model: **ventral "what" stream** (superior/middle temporal gyri) is primary for comprehension; **dorsal "how" stream** (premotor, Broca's area) has limited auxiliary role.

### TRACE Model (McClelland & Elman, 1986)
- Connectionist model with three levels: **features, phonemes, words**.
- Within-level connections are **inhibitory** (competition); between-level connections are **facilitatory** and **bidirectional** (bottom-up and top-down).
- Accounts for: word superiority effect in phoneme monitoring, Ganong effect (top-down word-to-phoneme activation), phonemic restoration, categorical perception (mutual inhibition sharpens boundaries).
- Limitations: exaggerates top-down effects; ignores orthographic influences on speech perception; ignores non-phonemic cues such as syllable duration.

> **Key Study:** McClelland & Elman (1986) TRACE model - bidirectional interactive network at feature, phoneme, and word levels; explains Ganong effect, word superiority in phoneme monitoring, and phonemic restoration through top-down activation.

### Cohort Model (Marslen-Wilson & Tyler, 1980; revised Marslen-Wilson, 1990)
- Upon hearing a word onset, all matching words become active: the **cohort**.
- Competition: words eliminated as input arrives (mismatch) or due to context.
- **Uniqueness point**: moment at which only one cohort word remains.
- Revised model: cohort membership is graded (not all-or-none); initial cohort may include phonologically similar words.
- Context influences the **integration stage** (after word identification) in the revised model.
- Evidence: Zwitserlood (1989) showed context does not prevent activation of contextually inappropriate cohort competitors.
- Limitation: strongly predictive context can influence word processing even before the uniqueness point (Van Petten et al., 1999).

## 9.9 Cognitive Neuropsychology of Speech Perception

Ellis & Young (1988) framework for spoken word repetition: five components (auditory analysis system, auditory input lexicon, semantic system, speech output lexicon, phoneme response buffer) linked by three routes.

| Condition | Damaged Component | Symptom Pattern |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|
| **Pure word deafness** | Auditory analysis system | Severely impaired speech perception; intact speech production, reading, writing, non-speech sounds |
| **Word meaning deafness** | Route 2 pathway (input lexicon to semantics disconnected) | Recognises words vs. non-words; repeats words but not non-words; no comprehension of spoken words; intact written comprehension |
| **Transcortical sensory aphasia** | Semantic system | Can repeat words and sentences; severe auditory and reading comprehension impairment |
| **Deep dysphasia** | General phonological impairment affecting all routes | Semantic errors when repeating; very poor word and non-word repetition; may read aloud adequately |

## 10.1 Parsing: Garden-Path Model (Frazier & Rayner, 1982)

- **Parsing**: analysis of syntactical/grammatical structure of sentences.
- **Garden-path model**: listeners/readers are "led up the garden path" by ambiguous sentences.

Two serial stages:
1. Only syntactic information used; applies two principles:
   - **Minimal attachment**: prefer the grammatical structure with fewest nodes (simplest).
   - **Late closure**: attach new words to the current phrase or clause if grammatically permissible.
2. If the initial structure is incompatible with later information, reanalysis occurs.

Classic examples:
- "The horse raced past the barn fell." (very hard: late closure causes wrong parse)
- "Since Jay always jogs a mile seems like a short distance." (re-analysis required after "jogs")

Limitations: does not account for early semantic effects (Trueswell et al., 1994: animacy of noun influenced parsing immediately); misleading prosody disrupts late closure adherence (Pauker et al., 2012); visual context can override initial parse (Spivey et al., 2002, visual world paradigm).

---

## 10.2 Constraint-Based Model (MacDonald et al., 1994)

- All information sources (syntactic, semantic, world knowledge, verb bias) available **immediately** from the outset.
- Competing syntactic analyses activated in **parallel**; the analysis best supported by all constraints is most highly activated.
- **Verb bias**: verbs favour particular syntactic structures (e.g., "read" typically takes a direct object). Constraint-based model predicts sentence ambiguity resolution is faster when sentence structure matches verb bias (Wilson & Garnsey, 2009).
- Evidence: readers process both object and subject analyses simultaneously even when adopting only one (Cai et al., 2012).
- Limitation: hard to falsify; does not specify precisely which constraints matter most and when.

### Unrestricted Race Model (Van Gompel et al., 2000)
- Uses all information sources (like constraint-based) but selects ONE syntactic structure; if disconfirmed, extensive reanalysis follows (like garden-path).
- Van Gompel et al. (2001): ambiguous sentences processed faster than either unambiguous structure because no reanalysis needed.

### Good-Enough Representations (Ferreira et al., 2002)
- Readers/listeners often form incomplete, heuristic, "good-enough" representations rather than full accurate parses.
- **NVN strategy**: the first noun is typically treated as the agent. Causes errors with passive sentences (e.g., "The mouse was eaten by the cheese" sometimes interpreted as mouse eating the cheese; Ferreira, 2003).
- **Moses illusion**: "How many animals did Moses put on the ark?" (It was Noah) most people answer "two."

## 10.3 Pragmatics

- **Pragmatics**: meaning minus semantics; intended vs. literal meaning; often requires inference.
- **Figurative language**: metaphor, irony, idiom. More cognitively demanding than literal language; recruits inferior frontal gyrus (BA45/46/47/13) more than literal processing (Bohrn et al., 2012 meta-analysis).
- **Standard pragmatic model** (Grice, 1975): literal meaning accessed first → if inadequate in context → search for figurative meaning. Proved inadequate: metaphors are sometimes understood as quickly as literal statements (Gibbs, 2013).
- **Kintsch's (2000) predication model**: argument (first noun, e.g., lawyers) selects features of predicate (second noun, e.g., sharks) that are relevant and inhibits irrelevant features. High working memory capacity individuals process metaphors 23% faster (Chiappe & Chiappe, 2007).

### Common Ground (Grice; Keysar et al., 2000)
- **Common ground**: shared knowledge between speaker and listener. Facilitates communication.
- **Egocentric heuristic** (Keysar et al., 2000): when it is attentionally demanding to compute common ground, listeners default to their own knowledge perspective. Cultural difference: Chinese individuals show faster correction of egocentric processing than Americans (Wu et al., 2013).
- **Cooperative Principle** (Grice, 1967): speakers should be relevant, as informative as necessary, truthful, and clear (four maxims).
- **Audience design**: speakers tailor what they say to the listener's needs.

## 10.4 Discourse Inferences

### Types of Inference
| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Logical inferences** | Derived from word meanings alone | Widow → female |
| **Bridging (backward) inferences** | Establish coherence with prior text | "Ken drove to London / The car overheated" → he drove in a car |
| **Elaborative (forward/predictive) inferences** | Add details using world knowledge | Charlotte brushed away bees → predictive inference: she got stung |

### Theoretical Positions
- **Constructionist approach** (Bransford et al., 1972): numerous elaborative inferences drawn during reading; readers construct a complete mental model.
- **Minimalist hypothesis** (McKoon & Ratcliff, 1992): only two automatic inference types: local coherence and readily available information. Most elaborative inferences made at recall, not during reading.
- **Search-after-meaning theory** (Graesser et al., 1994): inference drawing is flexible; depends on the reader's goals. Those reading for mastery draw many more inferences than those proofreading.
- Consensus: bridging and causal inferences typically drawn automatically; predictive inferences depend on context strength, working memory capacity, and reading skill. High-working-memory individuals draw more elaborative inferences (Barreyro et al., 2012).

### Anaphor Resolution
- Simple bridging inference: identifying pronoun referent ("he" → Fred or John?).
- Facilitated by: gender cues, expected referent order, working memory capacity (Nieuwland & van Berkum, 2006b).

## 10.5 Discourse Comprehension: Theoretical Approaches

### Schema Theory (Bartlett, 1932)
- **Schemas**: organised knowledge packets (scripts, frames) guiding comprehension and memory in a top-down fashion.
- Bransford & Johnson (1972): "Washing clothes" passage incomprehensible (2.8 idea units recalled) without title; 5.8 units with title.
- Schemas cause three memory errors: **rationalisation** (culturally distorting), **levelling** (omitting unfamiliar detail), **sharpening** (embellishing selected detail).
- Bartlett (1932) "War of the Ghosts": schema-driven distortions increase over time (Bergman & Roediger, 1999).
- Limitation: hard to test; vague about when schemas activate; often erroneously predicts more errors than observed (Steyvers & Hemmer, 2012).

### Kintsch's Construction-Integration Model (1998)
Three levels of representation built during reading:
1. **Surface representation**: verbatim text (rapid forgetting).
2. **Propositional textbase**: propositions (statements true or false) derived from text.
3. **Situation model (mental model)**: a model of the described world; integrates text with world knowledge; shows no forgetting over four days (Kintsch et al., 1990).

Construction phase: inefficient bottom-up process; many irrelevant propositions activated.
Integration phase: spreading activation selects coherent clusters; top-down contextual information weeds out irrelevant propositions.

### Event-Indexing Model (Zwaan et al., 1995)
Readers monitor five situational dimensions for updating their situation model:
1. **Protagonist** (central character)
2. **Temporality** (time)
3. **Causality**
4. **Spatiality** (location)
5. **Intentionality** (character's goals)

Discontinuity in any dimension triggers more processing effort and reading time. Best applicable to narrative texts; less applicable to expository texts.

## 11.1 Speech Planning

- Speakers typically speak at 2-3 words/second (~150 words/minute).
- Planning reduces errors but is cognitively costly; speakers use **preformulation** (~70% of speech is repeated word combinations) and **underspecification** (simplified expressions).
- Planning scope: often at the **clause level** (word-exchange errors usually involve same clause); also at **phrase level** (Martin et al., 2004: complex initial phrases delay speech onset).
- Flexibility: more planning when sentences are simple and cognitive load is low (Wagner et al., 2010). Under time pressure, partial pre-speech planning continues during articulation (Ferreira & Swets, 2002).

## 11.2 Speech Errors

| Error Type | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Spoonerism** | Initial letters of two words switched | "hissed all my mystery lectures" |
| **Freudian slip** | Error revealing unconscious desires | Sexual spoonerisms under arousal (Motley, 1980) |
| **Semantic substitution** | Correct word replaced by similar-meaning word | "tennis bat" for "tennis racquet"; 99% same part of speech |
| **Morpheme-exchange error** | Inflections stay, stems swap | "He has already trunked two packs" |
| **Word-exchange error** | Two words in sentence swapped | "I must send a wife to my email" |
| **Anticipatory error** | Sound/word spoken too early | "a Tanadian from Toronto" |
| **Perseveratory error** | Sound/word repeated from earlier | "beef needle" for "beef noodle" |
| **Number-agreement error** | Singular verb with plural subject or vice versa | "The government have made a mess" |

- **Lexical bias effect**: speech errors more likely to form real words than non-words.
- **Mixed-error effect**: incorrect word is related to the target in both meaning and sound (evidence for interactive processing across semantic and phonological levels).

### Error Detection
- **Perceptual loop theory** (Levelt, 1983): speakers detect errors by monitoring their own speech through the comprehension system.
- **Conflict-based theory** (Nozari et al., 2011): detection occurs via conflict monitoring within the production system itself; error detection ability correlates with speech-production quality (r = -0.59 for semantic errors), not comprehension (r = -0.01).

## 11.3 Theories of Speech Production

### Dell's (1986) Spreading-Activation Theory
- Four levels: **semantic, syntactic, morphological, phonological**.
- Processing is **parallel** and **interactive**: activation spreads in both directions among levels.
- **Insertion rules**: most-activated node in the appropriate syntactic category is selected; selected node's activation resets to zero.
- Predicts: mixed-error effect (semantic + phonological factors jointly influence selection), lexical bias effect (words more activatable than non-words), anticipatory errors, exchange errors.
- Practice increases proportion of anticipatory vs. perseveratory errors (Dell et al., 1997): skilled speakers plan further ahead.

> **Key Study:** Dell (1986) spreading-activation theory - interactive connectionist model predicting mixed-error effect (incorrect word resembles target in both meaning and sound), lexical bias effect, and exchange errors.

### Levelt et al.'s (1999) WEAVER++ Model
- **Feedforward** (strictly meaning-to-sound, no feedback) and **discrete** (each stage completed before next begins).
- Three levels:
  - **Lexical concepts** (highest)
  - **Lemmas**: abstract words with syntactic and semantic but not phonological information.
  - **Word forms**: morphemes and phonemic segments.
- Processing sequence: conceptual preparation (~200 ms) → lemma retrieval (~75 ms) → phonological code retrieval (~20 ms/phoneme, ~50-55 ms/syllable) → phonetic encoding + articulation (~600 ms total from stimulus onset; Indefrey, 2011 meta-analysis).
- **Tip-of-the-tongue (TOT) state**: lemma retrieved (meaning + syntax accessed) but phonological form inaccessible. Evidence: Italian TOT participants guess grammatical gender correctly 85% of the time (Vigliocco et al., 1997).
- Evidence against strict feedforward: phonologically related distractors speed picture naming (Meyer & Damian, 2007), contrary to WEAVER++ prediction. Serial processing only observed under high cognitive load (Mädebach et al., 2011).

**Comparison:**

| Feature | Spreading-Activation (Dell) | WEAVER++ (Levelt) |
|---------|----------------------------|-------------------|
| Processing direction | Interactive; bidirectional | Feedforward; unidirectional |
| Stages | Parallel | Serial (discrete) |
| Evidence base | Speech error data | Reaction time / picture naming |
| Interaction between levels | High | Minimal |

## 11.4 Cognitive Neuropsychology: Aphasia

### Broca's vs. Wernicke's Aphasia
| Feature | **Broca's Aphasia** | **Wernicke's Aphasia** |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Speech fluency | Non-fluent, slow | Fluent, effortful-seeming (but meaningless) |
| Syntax | Impaired (**agrammatism**) | Relatively preserved |
| Comprehension | Relatively intact | Severely impaired |
| Brain region | BA44/45, inferior frontal gyrus | Posterior BA22, superior temporal gyrus |
| Key feature | Content words preserved, function words/endings dropped | Jargon, semantic paraphasias, neologisms |

Note: the distinction is oversimplified; many patients have damage to both areas; symptoms vary widely.

### Anomia
- **Anomia**: impaired ability to name objects; occurs in virtually all aphasic patients.
- Two possible loci (WEAVER++):
  1. **Semantic level**: incorrect lemma selected; naming errors semantically related to target.
  2. **Phonological level**: lemma correct but phonological form inaccessible; resembles magnified tip-of-the-tongue state (Kay & Ellis, 1987, patient EST).
- ERP evidence: semantic group shows ERP abnormalities 100-250 ms; phonological group 300-450 ms (Laganaro et al., 2008).
- Incorrect phonological cues worsen naming performance (Soni et al., 2009), supporting interactive models over WEAVER++.

### Agrammatism
- **Agrammatism**: speech with content words but missing function words and word endings; grammatically incorrect sentences.
- Associated with damage to Broca's area (BA44/45), but many exceptions.
- Grodzinsky & Friederici (2006) three phases of syntactic processing:
  1. Local phrase structures (frontal operculum, anterior superior temporal gyrus).
  2. Dependency relations - who does what to whom (Broca's area, BA44/45).
  3. Syntactic-lexical integration (posterior superior temporal gyrus/sulcus).

### Jargon Aphasia
- **Jargon aphasia**: fluent speech; reasonable grammar; severe word-finding difficulties; produces **neologisms** (made-up words); deficient self-monitoring.
- Deficient self-monitoring central: masking own speech increases jargon (Sampson & Faroqi-Shah, 2011). Therapy improving phonological processing reduces neologisms (Bose, 2013).

## 11.5 Speech as Communication

- **Cooperative Principle** (Grice, 1967): speakers and listeners cooperate for mutual understanding.
- Four maxims: relevance, quantity (informative enough), quality (truthful), manner (clear).
- **Audience design**: speakers tailor utterances to the listener's knowledge and needs.
- **Syntactic priming**: speakers reproduce syntactic structures they recently heard, even when content is different and without conscious awareness (Pickering & Ferreira, 2008).
- **Monitoring and adjustment model** (Horton & Keysar, 1996): speakers initially plan utterances without considering the listener's perspective, then monitor and correct for common ground.

## 11.6 Writing and Spelling

### Writing Processes (Hayes & Flower, 1986; Chenoweth & Hayes, 2003)
1. **Planning/Proposing**: generating and organising ideas (30% of writing time).
2. **Translating/Sentence generation**: converting plan to sentences (50%).
3. **Transcribing**: converting to written text (requires processing resources; articulatory suppression disrupts this).
4. **Revising/Evaluating**: monitoring and revising (20%; expert writers revise 40% more than novices).

Writers switch processes ~8 times/minute on average; episodes average 7.5 s (generation) to 2.5 s (revision). Central executive is the most important working memory component for writing.

### Spelling: Dual-Route Model (Rapp & Dufor, 2011)
| Route | Process | Used For |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Lexical route** | Phonological lexicon → semantic system → orthographic long-term memory (orthographic lexicon) → graphemic buffer | Familiar words (regular AND irregular) |
| **Non-lexical route** | Phoneme-to-grapheme conversion rules | Non-words; regular/unfamiliar words |

Both routes converge on **orthographic working memory (graphemic buffer)** before writing/typing.

### Spelling Disorders (Dysgraphias)
| Disorder | Damaged Route | Pattern |
|----------|--------------|---------|
| **Phonological dysgraphia** | Non-lexical route impaired | Good spelling of familiar words; very poor non-word spelling; reflects general phonological deficit (Cholewa et al., 2010). |
| **Surface dysgraphia** | Lexical route impaired | Relies on GPC rules; good regular word spelling; poor irregular word spelling; some success with non-words. Patient MK: 92% regular, 52% irregular (Macoir & Bernier, 2002). |

- Routes interact: lexical route influences non-word spelling (Martin & Barry, 2012: writing /vi:m/ as VEAM or VEME depending on recently seen rhyming word).

## Linguistic Relativity (Whorf Hypothesis)
- **Whorfian/linguistic relativity hypothesis**: language shapes thought; speakers of different languages perceive and categorise the world differently.
- Weak version: language influences (does not determine) perception and cognition. Moderate support (e.g., colour perception, spatial reasoning differ across languages).
- Strong version (linguistic determinism): language determines thought. Not supported.
- Relevance to cognitive psychology: dispute whether language-based categories constrain or merely facilitate certain cognitive processes.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Lexical decision task** | Deciding rapidly whether a letter string is a word |
| **Naming task** | Pronouncing a visually presented word aloud as rapidly as possible |
| **Phonological processing** | Processing the sounds of words; obligatory in reading per strong model |
| **Word superiority effect** | Target letter detected more accurately in words than in non-words or letter strings |
| **Orthographic neighbours** | Words differing by one letter from a target word |
| **Semantic priming** | Faster recognition of a word when preceded by a semantically related word |
| **N400** | Negative ERP component peaking ~400 ms; reflects semantic mismatch |
| **Interactive activation model** | McClelland & Rumelhart (1981); feature-letter-word levels with bidirectional activation |
| **Dual-route cascaded model** | Coltheart et al. (2001); lexical and non-lexical routes for reading aloud |
| **Grapheme** | Basic unit of written language corresponding to a phoneme |
| **Phoneme** | Basic unit of spoken language |
| **Surface dyslexia** | Regularisation errors on irregular words; Route 1 dependent |
| **Phonological dyslexia** | Cannot read non-words; Routes 2/3 dependent |
| **Deep dyslexia** | Cannot read non-words; semantic reading errors; right-hemisphere reading |
| **Triangle model** | Orthography-phonology-semantics interactive system (Plaut et al., 1996) |
| **Saccades** | Rapid eye movements between fixations (~20-30 ms, ~8 letters) |
| **Perceptual span** | Effective field of view during reading (15 letters right of fixation in English) |
| **Spillover effect** | Longer fixation on word following a rare word |
| **E-Z Reader model** | Two-stage lexical access model of eye movements (Reichle et al., 1998) |
| **Segmentation** | Dividing continuous speech into phonemes and words |
| **Coarticulation** | Production of a phoneme influenced by surrounding phonemes |
| **Categorical perception** | Intermediate sounds heard as one phoneme or another, not as intermediate |
| **McGurk effect** | Seeing "ba" + hearing "ga" = perceiving "da"; audiovisual speech fusion |
| **Phonemic restoration effect** | Deleted phoneme replaced by cough is heard as the appropriate phoneme |
| **Ganong effect** | Ambiguous phoneme heard as whichever variant makes a real word |
| **Motor theory of speech** | Listeners mimic articulatory gestures to recognise spoken words (Liberman et al., 1967) |
| **TRACE model** | Connectionist bidirectional model of speech perception (McClelland & Elman, 1986) |
| **Cohort model** | Word recognition by progressive elimination of word-initial cohort (Marslen-Wilson) |
| **Uniqueness point** | Moment at which only one word remains consistent with the acoustic signal |
| **Pure word deafness** | Impaired speech perception; intact speech production, reading, non-speech sounds |
| **Word meaning deafness** | Cannot understand spoken words; can repeat them; intact written comprehension |
| **Parsing** | Analysis of syntactic/grammatical structure of sentences |
| **Minimal attachment** | Prefer grammatical structure with fewest syntactic nodes (Frazier) |
| **Late closure** | Attach new words to current phrase if grammatically permissible (Frazier) |
| **Garden-path model** | Frazier & Rayner (1982); two-stage serial syntactic-first parsing |
| **Constraint-based model** | MacDonald et al. (1994); all information sources available immediately and in parallel |
| **Verb bias** | Imbalance in the frequency with which a verb occurs with different syntactic structures |
| **Pragmatics** | Study of intended (not literal) meaning in context |
| **Common ground** | Shared knowledge and beliefs between speaker and listener |
| **Egocentric heuristic** | Interpreting speech based on one's own knowledge rather than shared knowledge |
| **Bridging inferences** | Inferences establishing coherence between current and preceding text |
| **Elaborative inferences** | Inferences adding world-knowledge details to text |
| **Minimalist hypothesis** | McKoon & Ratcliff (1992); few automatic elaborative inferences drawn during reading |
| **Schema** | Organised knowledge packet guiding comprehension and memory (Bartlett) |
| **Situation model** | Mental model of the described world constructed during reading (Kintsch) |
| **Spreading activation** | Activation of a node spreads to related nodes (Dell, 1986) |
| **Lemma** | Abstract word with syntactic and semantic but not phonological features (Levelt) |
| **Lexicalisation** | Process of translating a word's meaning into its sound representation |
| **Tip-of-the-tongue state** | Lemma accessed, phonological form inaccessible |
| **Mixed-error effect** | Incorrect spoken word related to target in both meaning and sound |
| **Lexical bias effect** | Speech errors tend to form words not non-words |
| **Spoonerism** | Initial letters/sounds of two words transposed |
| **Freudian slip** | Speech error revealing unconscious desires |
| **Aphasia** | Severe language comprehension and/or production impairment due to brain damage |
| **Broca's aphasia** | Non-fluent speech, agrammatism, relatively intact comprehension; BA44/45 |
| **Wernicke's aphasia** | Fluent but semantically empty speech, severe comprehension impairment; BA22 |
| **Anomia** | Impaired object naming; occurs in virtually all aphasic patients |
| **Agrammatism** | Speech lacking grammatical structure; content words preserved, function words/endings omitted |
| **Jargon aphasia** | Fluent, grammatical speech with severe word-finding difficulty; neologisms; poor self-monitoring |
| **Syntactic priming** | Speaker reproduces syntactic structure of recently heard utterance |
| **Audience design** | Tailoring what and how speakers say something to their listener's needs |
| **Phonological dysgraphia** | Brain damage causing inability to spell non-words; familiar word spelling intact |
| **Surface dysgraphia** | Brain damage causing errors on irregular words; uses GPC rules |
| **Graphemic buffer** | Orthographic working memory storing letter representations before writing |
| **Orthographic lexicon** | Long-term memory store of word spellings |
| **Linguistic relativity** | Hypothesis that language influences or determines thought (Whorf) |

## Likely Exam Points

1. **Word superiority effect** is explained by the interactive activation model's top-down activation from the word level to the letter level. Occurs ~200 ms after word onset.

2. **Dual-route cascaded model** (Coltheart et al., 2001): Route 1 (non-lexical, grapheme-phoneme conversion) is used by **surface dyslexics**; Routes 2/3 (lexical) are used by **phonological dyslexics**. Deep dyslexia involves semantic errors and right-hemisphere reading.

3. **Surface dyslexia vs. phonological dyslexia**: surface dyslexics regularise irregular words (e.g., PINT → "pint rhymes with hint"); phonological dyslexics read regular and irregular words well but fail on non-words.

4. **Triangle model** emphasises word **consistency** (not regularity) and the role of semantics in reading. Jared (2002): consistency matters more than regularity for naming times.

5. **E-Z Reader model**: frequency check (stage 1) initiates saccade programming; completion of lexical access (stage 2) triggers attention shift. Explains word skipping (short/common/predictable words) and spillover effect.

6. **Three problems in speech perception**: segmentation, coarticulation, invariance problem.

7. **Categorical perception**: intermediate sounds heard as one phoneme or another; not unique to speech (musicians show it for chords). Explained by TRACE model via mutual inhibition between phoneme units.

8. **McGurk effect** (McGurk & MacDonald, 1976): audio "ga" + visual "ba" = perceived "da." Demonstrates mandatory audiovisual integration in speech perception.

9. **Phonemic restoration effect** (Warren & Warren, 1970): deleted phoneme + noise = listener hears the word-appropriate phoneme. Top-down effect; greater for real words than non-words (Samuel, 1981).

10. **TRACE model**: three levels (features, phonemes, words); between-level connections facilitatory and bidirectional; within-level connections inhibitory. Explains Ganong effect, word superiority in phoneme monitoring, categorical perception.

11. **Cohort model** uniqueness point: moment only one word remains in cohort. Revised model: cohort membership is graded; context operates at integration stage. Limitation: strongly constraining context can influence processing before uniqueness point.

12. **Garden-path model** principles: **minimal attachment** and **late closure**. Classic garden-path sentence: "The horse raced past the barn fell." Limitation: semantic factors influence initial parse (Trueswell et al., 1994).

13. **Constraint-based model** vs. garden-path: all sources (semantic, syntactic, verb bias) used from the outset vs. syntax-only initially. Verb bias (Wilson & Garnsey, 2009) supports constraint-based.

14. **Three inference types**: logical (word meanings only), bridging/backward (coherence with prior text), elaborative/forward (world knowledge adds details). Minimalist hypothesis: few automatic elaborative inferences; search-after-meaning: flexible depending on reader's goals.

15. **Kintsch's construction-integration model**: three levels (surface, propositional textbase, situation model). Situation model shows no forgetting over four days (Kintsch et al., 1990). Bottom-up construction stage followed by top-down integration stage.

16. **Dell's spreading-activation theory**: parallel, interactive; explains mixed-error effect and lexical bias effect. **WEAVER++** (Levelt et al., 1999): feedforward, discrete stages; lemma (syntax + semantics, no phonology) selected before phonological encoding. TOT state = lemma retrieved, phonological form inaccessible.

17. **Broca's aphasia**: non-fluent, agrammatic speech, relatively intact comprehension; BA44/45 (inferior frontal gyrus). **Wernicke's aphasia**: fluent but semantically empty speech, severe comprehension impairment; BA22 (superior temporal gyrus).

18. **Anomia**: two loci per WEAVER++ (semantic = wrong lemma selected; phonological = lemma correct, form inaccessible). Nearly universal in aphasia.

19. **Agrammatism**: content words preserved, function words and word endings omitted; associated with Broca's area damage; may reflect processing limitation rather than lost competence.

20. **Spelling dual routes**: lexical route (orthographic lexicon; familiar words including irregular) vs. non-lexical route (GPC rules; non-words and regular unfamiliar words). **Phonological dysgraphia**: cannot spell non-words (non-lexical route damaged). **Surface dysgraphia**: poor on irregular words, some non-word success (lexical route damaged).

21. **Linguistic relativity** (Whorf): weak version (language influences thought) receives moderate support; strong version (language determines thought) is not supported.
